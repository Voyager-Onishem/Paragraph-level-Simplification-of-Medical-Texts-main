Slow versus fast subcutaneous heparin injections for prevention of bruising and site pain intensity

  • Mina Mohammady
  • Maryam Radmehr

    • Background

    Heparin is an anticoagulant medication that is usually injected subcutaneously. Subcutaneous administration of heparin may result in complications such as bruising, haematoma, and pain at the injection site. One of the factors that may affect pain, haematoma, and bruising is injection speed. Several studies have been carried out to determine if speed of injection affects the amount of pain and bruising where the injection is given; however, the results of these studies have differed, and study authors have not reached a clear final conclusion. This is the second update of a review first published in 2014.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of duration (speed) of subcutaneous heparin injection on pain and bruising at the injection site in people admitted to hospitals or clinics who require treatment with unfractionated heparin (UFH) or low molecular weight heparin (LMWH). We also looked at haematoma at the injection site.

    Search methods

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist searched the Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and CINAHL databases and the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform and ClinicalTrials.gov trials registers to 22 June 2020. We undertook reference checking of included studies to identify additional studies.

    Selection criteria

    We searched for randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing the effects of different durations of subcutaneous injection of heparin on pain, bruising, and haematoma at the injection site.

    Data collection and analysis

    For this update, two review authors independently selected studies and extracted data via Covidence software and assessed methodological quality using Cochrane's risk of bias tool. The primary outcomes of interest were pain intensity at injection site and size and incidence of bruising. The secondary outcomes of interest were size and incidence of haematoma at injection site. We calculated the odds ratio (OR), mean difference (MD), or standardised mean difference (SMD) with corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We assessed the certainty of the evidence using GRADE criteria.

    Main results

    We identified one new study for this update, resulting in a total of five included studies with 503 participants who received subcutaneous injections of LMWH into the abdomen. Given the nature of the intervention, it was not possible to blind participants and caregivers (personnel) in any of the included studies. Two studies described blinding of outcome assessors. Overall, the methodological quality of included studies was moderate. The duration of the fast injection was 10 seconds, and the duration of the slow injection was 30 seconds in all included studies.

    Four studies reported site pain intensity after each injection at different time points. Two studies assessed site pain intensity immediately after each injection; meta‐analysis showed no evidence of a difference in site pain intensity immediately after slow injection when compared to fast injection (MD −1.52, 95% CI −3.56 to 0.53; 140 participants; low‐certainty evidence). Meta‐analysis of three studies indicated that site pain intensity may be slightly reduced 48 hours after the slow heparin injection compared to fast injection (MD −1.60, 95% CI −2.69 to −0.51; 103 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    Five studies assessed bruise size at 48 hours, and two studies assessed bruise size at 60 hours. Meta‐analysis showed there may be a reduction in bruise size 48 hours (SMD −0.54, 95% CI −1.05 to −0.02; 503 participants; 5 studies; very low‐certainty evidence) and 60 hours (SMD −0.49, 95% CI −0.93 to −0.06; 84 participants; 2 studies; low‐certainty evidence) after slow injection compared to fast injection. There was no evidence of a difference in bruise size 72 hours after slow injection compared to fast injection (SMD −0.27, 95% CI −0.61 to 0.06; 140 participants; 2 studies; low‐certainty evidence).

    Three studies evaluated incidence of bruising and showed there may be a reduction in bruise incidence 48 hours (OR 0.39, 95% CI 0.26 to 0.60; 444 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and 60 hours (OR 0.25, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.65; 84 participants; 2 studies; low‐certainty evidence) after slow injection compared to fast injection. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to risk of bias concerns, imprecision, and inconsistency.

    None of the included studies measured size or incidence of haematoma.

    Authors' conclusions

    Administering medication safely and enhancing patient comfort are the main aims of clinical nurses. In this review, we identified five RCTs that evaluated the effect of subcutaneous heparin injection duration on pain intensity, bruise size and incidence. We found that pain may be slightly reduced 48 hours after slow injection. Similarly, there may be a reduction in bruise size and incidence after slow injection compared to fast injection 48 and 60 hours postinjection. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence for all outcomes to low or very low due to risk of bias concerns, imprecision, and inconsistency. Accordingly, new trials with a more robust design, more participants, and a focus on different injection speeds will be useful in strengthening the certainty of the available evidence.

    • Does the speed of injection make a difference in the amount of pain and bruising in people receiving heparin injections?

    Background

    Heparin is a drug used to help stop blood from clotting. It comes in two forms: unfractionated heparin (UFH) and low molecular weight heparin (LMWH). LMWH has a longer half‐life (the time it takes for the concentration of a drug in the body to be reduced by 50%) and fewer side effects compared to UFH. Heparin is usually given by injection just underneath the skin, going into the layer of fat under the skin so that it is released slowly into the body. This type of injection can sometimes cause bruising and pain at the site where the needle is inserted. Bruising occurs when the small blood vessels and capillaries break and bleed under the skin and lead to discolouration of the skin. This bleeding can sometimes worsen and cause a swelling, known as a haematoma, that occurs when blood collects in the injury site. Several studies have been carried out to find out if the speed of injection affects the amount of pain and bruising at the site where the injection is given. Study investigators think that slow injection of heparin allows time for tissue under the skin to accommodate the injected volume, resulting in reduced pressure, capillary bleeding, and site pain intensity, thereby minimising the likelihood of bruising at the injection site. However, the results of studies differ, and study authors have not reached a clear final conclusion.

    Study characteristics and key results

    We searched for studies that investigated the effects of speed of injection on the amount of pain and bruising where the injection is given (current to 22 June 2020). Five studies met our inclusion criteria and were included in the review. The included studies took place in Thailand, Turkey, Italy, and China, and enrolled a total of 503 people (312 female and 191 male participants). All study participants received LMWH, whilst no study used UFH.

    Study investigators injected heparin into the abdomen of participants. Participants could watch the injection being given and knew whether it was fast (10 seconds long) or slow (30 seconds long).

    We found that pain may be slightly reduced 48 hours after slow injection compared with fast injection. Similarly, there may be smaller bruise sizes and fewer bruises with the slow injection 48 and 60 hours after the injection. None of the included studies measured the number or size of haematomas.

    Certainty of the evidence

    We graded the certainty of the evidence as low or very low because we found only a small number of published studies that reported on our review question, and the studies were small and had contradicting results. The fact that participants knew whether they had received a fast or a slow injection may also have affected the results because this knowledge may have led them to use different techniques (i.e. applying cold, heat, or massage) to relieve the pain and bruising based on the intervention received.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Administering medication safely and enhancing patient comfort are the main aims of clinical nurses. The nurse researchers have a leadership role to achieve the best evidence in safe medication administration, and it is essential for nurse researchers to prepare guidelines according to evidence‐based practices for nursing staff to minimise the side effects for patients who need multiple or long‐term subcutaneous heparin injections. The results of this research therefore have clinical importance for nursing practice. We identified five randomised controlled trials in this review that evaluated the effect of subcutaneous heparin injection duration on pain intensity and bruise size and incidence. We found that pain may be slightly reduced 48 hours after slow injection. Similarly, there may be a reduction in bruise size and incidence following slow injection compared to fast injection after 48 and 60 hours postinjection. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence for all outcomes to low or very low due to risk of bias concerns, imprecision, and inconsistency.

    Implications for research

    The results of this review suggest that slow injection may be associated with lower pain intensity 48 hours postinjection and a reduction in bruise size and incidence of bruising 48 and 60 hours postinjection compared to fast injection. However, new trials with a more robust design, more participants, and a focus on different injection speeds will be useful in strengthening the certainty of these results. In addition, trials are needed to evaluate other aspects that might impact adverse outcomes (bruising, haematoma, and pain at the injection site), for example different types of heparin given (unfractionated heparin (UFH) or low molecular weight heparin (LMWH)), needle gauge, retractable needles, drug volume injected, cold applied to the injection site, injection site and injection technique used, and different injectors and their experience.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Heparin is an anticoagulant medication that is well known for its anticoagulant and antithrombotic pharmacological effects (Hao 2019). Different forms of heparin are available, including unfractionated heparin (UFH) and low molecular weight heparin (LMWH), which may be administered by continuous infusion, subcutaneous (SC), or intravenous injection (Hodgson 2007; Zhu 2019). LMWH has several advantages over UFH. It has better bioavailability after subcutaneous injection and a longer half‐life, which gives a more predictable anticoagulant response compared to UFH (Weitz 2009). Approximately 12 million hospitalised patients in the United States receive heparin each year (Cuker 2018).

    Description of the intervention

    Subcutaneous administration of heparin is frequently carried out in various healthcare settings by clinical nurses (Amaniyan 2020). Subcutaneous injection is normally chosen when slow and continuous absorption of a drug (e.g. insulin, heparin) is needed. The drug is injected into fat and connective tissue underlying the dermis, where less blood flow results in a slower absorption rate. Suitable sites for subcutaneous injections are often the umbilical region of the abdomen and the lateral sides of the arms and thighs (Hunter 2008). Subcutaneous administration of heparin may cause adverse outcomes such as bruising, haematoma, and pain at the injection site (Zaki 2020). Bruising occurs when the capillaries break and bleed under the skin and lead to discolouration of the skin. In some cases bleeding worsens and causes a swelling, known as a haematoma, which occurs when blood collects in the bleeding area (Urakov 2020). The incidence of haematoma and bruising at the injection site has been reported to be 23% and 53.6% at injection sites, respectively (Cortés 2009; Poursafar 2019).

    How the intervention might work

    Adverse drug reactions are a relatively common occurrence and can cause harm to patients. Side effects associated with subcutaneous administration of heparin such as bruising and haematoma at the injection sire may affect patients' body image and self‐esteem (Jareño‐Collado 2018). Nurses should apply techniques that minimise the side effects of subcutaneous injections, including site pain, haematoma, and bruising. Bruises usually persist for up to a week, and then disappear on their own accord and without any treatment (Urakov 2020). A haematoma, depending on its size, takes longer to fully absorb the blood. It should be noted that frequent and widespread occurrence of bruising and haematoma at the injection site limits the choice of the next injection area for patients and nurses.

    It can be argued that slow versus fast injection of heparin might significantly reduce pain, haematoma, and bruising at the injection site. Slow administration of heparin allows time for subcutaneous tissue to accommodate the injected volume, resulting in reduced pressure, capillary bleeding, and site pain, and minimising the likelihood of other damage (Chan 2001).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Some patients receive subcutaneous heparin for a lengthy time period from the time of hospital admission until after hospital discharge (Delate 2012). With daily heparin injections for several weeks, the risk of extensive bruising in addition to pain at the injection site is high. Consequently, strategies that can reduce pain and bruising are considered important for patients and healthcare providers. Administering medication safely and enhancing patient comfort are the main aims of clinical nurses (Sendir 2015). Several studies have explored the effects of factors such as changing injection site (Ciftci 2016), cold application (Mohammady 2020), syringe size, needle gauge (Robb 2002), and injection volume and air bubble in the syringe (Chan 2001), on the incidence of bruising, haematoma, and pain at the injection site. Other studies have explored the effect of duration of the injection on the incidence of bruising and pain (Dadaeen 2015; Dehghani 2012; Jueakaew 2019). Some investigators have recommended that subcutaneous heparin injections be given slowly to reduce bruising and pain, but other investigators have reported no significant differences between the two techniques in terms of bruising and pain (Balci Akpinar 2008; Chenicek 2004; Pourghaznein 2014). Several studies have compared slow versus fast subcutaneous injection of heparin, but researchers have reported variable results, and no clear final conclusion regarding the exact speed of heparin injection could be reached (Chenicek 2004; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008). This controversy highlights the importance of conducting a systematic review to explore the effect of different durations of heparin injection on complications at the injection site.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of duration (speed) of subcutaneous heparin injection on pain and bruising at the injection site in people admitted to hospitals or clinics who require treatment with unfractionated heparin (UFH) or low molecular weight heparin (LMWH). We also looked at haematoma at the injection site.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in the review. We excluded quasi‐RCTs (studies using a quasi‐random method of allocation such as alternation, date of birth, or case record number, etc.) and quasi‐experimental studies.

    Types of participants

    We included studies in which participants were males and females 18 years of age or older who were admitted to hospitals or clinics and treated with subcutaneous injections of heparin including LMWH and UFH. We excluded trials involving people treated with other anticoagulant drugs because of the possible effects of these agents on bruising and haematoma.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies in which the intervention consisted of slow subcutaneous administration of heparin (LMWH or UFH) compared to fast subcutaneous administration of heparin. We considered an injection speed of 20 or more seconds as a slow injection, and an injection speed of less than 20 seconds as a fast injection.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Pain intensity at injection site (measured at injection site by any scale, including visual analogue scale (VAS), numerical rating, McGill scales, descriptive or other pain scales)

    • Size of the bruise at the injection site (measured by diameter at the widest point; or area measured by millimetric measuring papers)

    • Incidence of bruising at the injection site (the number of bruises larger than 2 mm2)

    Secondary outcomes

    • Size of haematoma at the injection site (measured by diameter at the widest point; or area measured by millimetric measuring papers)

    • Incidence of haematoma at the injection site (the number of haematomas larger than 2 mm2)

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist conducted systematic searches of the following databases for RCTs and controlled clinical trials without language, publication year, or publication status restrictions:

    • Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register via the Cochrane Register of Studies (CRS‐Web searched on 22 June 2020);

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) Cochrane Register of Studies Online (CRSO 2020; Issue 5);

    • MEDLINE (Ovid MEDLINE Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE Daily and Ovid MEDLINE) (searched from 1 January 2017 to 22 June 2020);

    • Embase Ovid (searched from 1 January 2017 to 22 June 2020);

    • CINAHL EBSCO (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature) (searched from 1 January 2017 to 22 June 2020);

    • AMED Ovid (Allied and Complementary Medicine) (searched from 1 January 2017 to 22 June 2020).

    The Information Specialist modelled search strategies for other databases on the search strategy designed for CENTRAL. Where appropriate, they were combined with adaptations of the Highly Sensitive Search Strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying RCTs and controlled clinical trials (as described in Chapter 6 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions) (Lefebvre 2011). Search strategies for the major databases are provided in Appendix 1.

    The Information Specialist searched the following trials registries on 22 June 2020:

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (who.int/trialsearch);

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov).

    Searching other resources

    For this update, we reviewed the reference list of the newly included study to identify other potentially relevant studies.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    For this update, we imported search results into Covidence software (Covidence), and two review authors (MM, MR) independently assessed the titles and abstracts of the studies retrieved by the searches to determine eligibility for inclusion in the review. If it was not possible to include or exclude a study on the basis of the title or abstract, we obtained the full version of the article. The same two review authors (MM, MR) then independently assessed the full papers to determine if they met the inclusion criteria. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion or by consultation with the third review author (LJ) if necessary. We listed all articles excluded after full‐text assessment in the Characteristics of excluded studies table along with the reasons for their exclusion. We illustrated the study selection process in a PRISMA diagram (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    We used Covidence software to extract data using a structured data extraction form. Two review authors (MM, MR) independently extracted data from the included studies, resolving any disagreements by discussion. We collected the following data from the included studies: study design; method of randomisation; method of concealment of allocation; blinding; details of participants; details and duration of interventions; main inclusion and exclusion criteria; outcomes; methods of measuring pain, bruising, and haematoma; and methods of performing statistical analysis and reporting results. We then entered the data into Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2019).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MM, MR) independently assessed risk of bias of the included studies. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion. We used the Cochrane risk of bias tool as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions when assessing risk of bias (Higgins 2011).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We calculated the mean difference (MD) and 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs) for continuous outcomes (pain intensity, bruise and haematoma size). As the study investigators measured bruise size using different scales, we calculated the standardised mean difference (SMD) and 95% CIs in order to pool data. We calculated odds ratio (OR) with 95% CIs for dichotomous outcomes (bruise and haematoma incidence).

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cross‐over trials were eligible for inclusion in this review because LMWH has a temporary effect, and the half‐life of heparin is four hours after administration, therefore the first administration of heparin has no effect on formation of bruises at the second injection. We did not expect to find cluster‐randomised trials, and we identified none for inclusion in this review. The individual participant was the unit of analysis.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted trialists to obtain missing information when needed. We received clarification of information from the authors of Jueakaew 2019 and Sendir 2015. One study reported pain intensity as median (min and max), and the authors provided data as mean and standard deviation (Jueakaew 2019). All of the included studies provided an adequate description of individual study withdrawals and dropouts. When dropouts were not equal between groups, we carried out sensitivity analysis to investigate the impact of including these data.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We visually inspected forest plots and determined the I2 and Chi2 statistics to evaluate heterogeneity amongst the included studies. We used the following categories to indicate heterogeneity:

    • less than 30%: low heterogeneity;

    • 30% to 59%: moderate heterogeneity;

    • 60% to 74%: substantial heterogeneity;

    • 75% or greater: high heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    It is recommended that the test for funnel plot asymmetry to detect publication bias be used when at least 10 studies are included in the review (Higgins 2011). We included only five studies and therefore determined that it was not appropriate to prepare a funnel plot.

    Data synthesis

    We summarised study outcomes using narrative and quantitative methods. We applied a fixed‐effect model for meta‐analyses unless heterogeneity was indicated (I2 > 40%), in which case we used a random‐effects model for data synthesis. We used MD or SMD (with 95% CI) to pool continuous outcomes and OR (with 95% CI) for dichotomous outcomes.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We presented results of outcomes subgrouped according to the different measures used by trialists to record size (mm or mm2), and by different time points of assessment. We planned to perform subgroup analyses according to participants' sex, age, etc.; however, data were insufficient to do so. We will include these subgroup analyses in future updates if appropriate.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We carried out sensitivity analyses for the primary outcomes to assess the effect of attrition bias on effect size. We planned to perform a sensitivity analysis to assess the possible influence of the exclusion of unpublished studies on effect size, if sufficient studies met our inclusion criteria.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a summary of findings table using GRADEpro GDT software to summarise the evidence derived when investigators compared fast versus slow subcutaneous heparin injections in participants requiring heparin (summary of findings Table 1) (GRADEpro GDT). We included the outcomes of pain intensity, bruise size, bruise incidence, haematoma size, and haematoma incidence, as described in Types of outcome measures. We used the GRADE system to assess the certainty of evidence as high, moderate, low, or very low, using the criteria of within‐study risk of bias, inconsistency, directness of evidence, imprecision, and publication bias (Atkins 2004).

    Included studies

    Following addition of the newly included study (Jueakaew 2019), this review update includes a total of five studies (503 participants) (Jueakaew 2019; Palese 2013; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008; Zhao 2016). The included studies were conducted in Thailand (Jueakaew 2019), Italy (Palese 2013), Turkey (Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008), and China (Zhao 2016).

    Four studies were published in English (Jueakaew 2019; Palese 2013; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008), and one in Chinese (Zhao 2016). Four studies were small, including between 19 and 100 participants. The largest study involved 300 participants (Palese 2013). Three trials included more females (Jueakaew 2019; Palese 2013; Sendir 2015); one included more males (Zhao 2016); and one included equal numbers of males and females (Zaybak 2008).

    All studies included participants who received subcutaneous injections of LMWH; no studies included participants given UFH. In all of the included studies subcutaneous injections were performed into the abdomen. Only one trial reported the injected drug volume (0.4 mL) (Palese 2013).

    The included studies enrolled participants who were hospitalised in orthopaedics units (Palese 2013; Sendir 2015), cardiology units (Zhao 2016), vascular surgery clinics (Jueakaew 2019), and neurology, orthopaedics, and cardiology units (Zaybak 2008).

    Sendir 2015 randomised participants to three groups: group A (injection duration 10 seconds); group B (injection duration 30 seconds and 5‐minute dry cold local application); and group C (injection duration 30 seconds). We did not consider results from group B, as injection duration 30 seconds and 5‐minute dry cold local application was not an objective of this review (Sendir 2015).

    Zhao 2016 divided participants into six groups. Four groups investigated the effects of different injection durations and different pressing time on the injection site (Zhao 2016). We did not consider results from these four groups, as these were not objectives of our review. We included the group that injected heparin for 10 seconds without pressing as the control group, and the group that injected heparin for 30 seconds without pressing as the intervention group (Zhao 2016).

    Two studies used an applied self‐controlled design whereby every participant received the slow injection into one side of the abdomen (as intervention) and the fast injection into the other side of the abdomen (as control) (Palese 2013; Zaybak 2008).

    Three studies applied a parallel design whereby investigators randomly allocated participants to different groups (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zhao 2016).

    Two studies reported the source of funding (Jueakaew 2019; Zaybak 2008).

    For details of the included studies, see Characteristics of included studies.

    Excluded studies

    For this 2020 update, we excluded five additional studies (Ahmadi 2016; Bijani 2016; IRCT2015032721547N1; Li 2018; Poursafar 2019), for a total of 26 excluded studies. Reasons for exclusion of studies are as follows:

    • quasi‐randomised design (nine studies) (Ahmadi 2016; Babaie Asl 2008; Balci Akpinar 2008; Chan 2001; Dadaeen 2015; Dehghani 2012; Gholam Nezhad 2004; Sanagoo 2011; Tehrani Neshat 2005);

    • non‐randomised design (four studies) (Bijani 2016; Deng 2009; Nair 2008; Uzun 2016);

    • investigators used other anticoagulant drugs during the study (two studies) (Chenicek 2004; Rahmani 1999);

    • researchers compared 10‐second heparin injection versus 30‐second heparin injection plus air lock (one study) (Fathi 2014);

    • study authors compared 10‐second heparin injection versus 10‐second injection and waiting for 10 seconds before withdrawing the needle (two studies) (Li 2018; Rahmani 2013);

    • the heparin injection duration was less than 20 seconds in all comparison groups (two studies) (IRCT2015032721547N1; Pourghaznein 2014);

    • there was no control group for comparison (one study) (Poursafar 2019).

    The remaining excluded studies compared various techniques of heparin injection but did not explore the effects of injection duration on study outcomes. Two of these studies compared two techniques (McGowan 1990; Wooldridge 1988); one study compared three different techniques (Vanbree 1984); and two studies compared four techniques of heparin injection (Avsar 2013; Jesús Gómez 2005).

    For details of the excluded studies, see Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Allocation

    Three studies adequately described methods of random sequence generation and were therefore at low risk of bias (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zhao 2016). Two of these studies used a computerised randomisation program to generate the random sequence (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015), whilst the other study used a table of random numbers (Zhao 2016). However, methods of allocation concealment were unclear in these studies (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zhao 2016).

    Two studies gave every participant a slow injection into one side of the abdomen and a fast injection into the other side of the abdomen (Palese 2013; Zaybak 2008), therefore it was not possible to randomise participants into two separate groups. However, participants could instead be randomised into intervention or control groups according to treatment order. Each participant was given one of the two injection techniques (injection duration of 10 or 30 seconds) for the first injection and the second technique 12 hours later. For each individual, the technique to be used first (injection duration of 10 or 30 seconds) was identified randomly using a randomised sequence. Neither of these studies described the methods of sequence generation and allocation concealment used (Palese 2013; Zaybak 2008). The authors of Palese 2013 responded to a request for information regarding the randomisation method used. They stated that the random sequence was generated by computer, therefore this study was judged to be at low risk of bias (Palese 2013). Zaybak 2008 was at an unclear risk of selection bias as details were insufficient to assess otherwise.

    Blinding

    Given the nature of the intervention, it was not possible to blind participants and caregivers in any of the included studies, therefore they were all assessed as at high risk of performance bias. Two studies described blinding of outcome assessors and were assessed as at low risk of detection bias (Palese 2013; Zhao 2016). We assessed two studies as at unclear risk of detection bias because, although the study authors mentioned assessor blinding, they provided no description of assessor blinding for outcomes (Jueakaew 2019; Zaybak 2008). The Sendir 2015 investigators reported that they used unblinded methods, resulting in a judgement of high risk of detection bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    All of the included studies provided an adequate description of individual study withdrawals and dropouts. Three studies were at low risk of attrition bias as no issues were detected (Jueakaew 2019; Palese 2013; Zaybak 2008). Sendir 2015 and Zhao 2016 reported 6.25% and 10% dropouts in one group, respectively, with no described intention‐to‐treat analysis, and were therefore assessed as at high risk of attrition bias.

    Selective reporting

    We found only one study protocol, and there was no selective outcome reporting (Jueakaew 2019). We did not have access to individual study protocols for the other included studies; however, the study reports suggest that investigators reported all expected outcomes, therefore we assessed these studies as at low risk of reporting bias (Palese 2013; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008; Zhao 2016).

    Other potential sources of bias

    Four studies did not provide clear descriptions of some aspects of heparin injection (e.g. heparin temperature, syringe size, injection volume, air bubble in the syringe) (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008; Zhao 2016). These factors may have affected study outcomes, therefore we judged these studies as being at unclear risk of other bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Slow versus fast subcutaneous heparin injection for prevention of bruising and site pain intensity

    Pain intensity

    Four studies evaluated pain intensity at injection site over different time points after each injection. One study reported pain intensity immediately after injection and at 48, 60, and 72 hours after each injection (Sendir 2015). Another study reported pain intensity immediately after injection (Zaybak 2008). The third study reported pain intensity at 48 hours after each injection (Zhao 2016), and the fourth reported it at 48 and 60 hours (Jueakaew 2019). Palese 2013 did not report pain intensity.

    We detected substantial heterogeneity at some time points for this outcome, so we pooled results of different studies using a random‐effects method. We pooled data according to different time points reported in the included studies, therefore numbers of studies and participants at each time point were small.

    Meta‐analysis of data from two trials showed no clear differences in site pain intensity immediately after slow injection compared to fast injection (mean difference (MD) −1.52, 95% confidence interval (CI) −3.56 to 0.53; 140 participants; 2 studies; I2 = 73%; P = 0.15; low‐certainty evidence) (Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008). See Analysis 1.1.

    Meta‐analysis of data from three RCTs showed that site pain intensity may be slightly reduced 48 hours after slow injection compared to fast injection (MD −1.60, 95% CI −2.69 to −0.51; 103 participants; 3 studies; I2 = 50%; P = 0.004; low‐certainty evidence) (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zhao 2016). See Analysis 1.1.

    Two RCTs reported pain intensity at 60 hours after injection (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015). Meta‐analysis of data showed no clear differences in site pain intensity at 60 hours post‐slow injection compared to fast injection (MD −0.97, 95% CI −2.11 to 0.17; 84 participants; 2 studies; I2 = 0%; P = 0.09). See Analysis 1.1.

    Only one study reported pain intensity at 72 hours after injection (Sendir 2015). This study described no clear differences in site pain intensity at 72 hours after each injection between groups (MD −0.80, 95% CI −1.70 to 0.10; 40 participants; 1 study; P = 0.08; low‐certainty evidence). See Analysis 1.1.

    We detected no subgroup differences between different time points (P = 0.7). Two studies were at high risk of attrition bias (Sendir 2015; Zhao 2016), but we were not able to investigate this with sensitivity analysis as planned due to the limited number of studies reporting at each time point.

    Size of bruise

    Three studies used millimetric measuring papers or a device to measure the area of the bruise and recorded it as square millimetres (mm2) (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008). Two studies measured the largest diameter of the bruise and recorded it as millimetres (mm) (Palese 2013; Zhao 2016).

    All five studies reported bruise size 48 hours after injection. Heterogeneity was high for this outcome (I2 = 81%), so we pooled results of different studies using the random‐effects method. We used standardised mean difference (SMD) to pool data from these different outcome measures.

    Meta‐analysis revealed that 48 hours after injection there may be a reduction in bruise size after slow injection compared to fast injection (SMD −0.54, 95% CI −1.05 to −0.02; 503 participants; 5 studies; P = 0.04; very low‐certainty evidence). See Analysis 1.2. No subgroup differences where detected (P = 0.44). This possible effect on bruise size was lost when we excluded studies at high risk of attrition bias in sensitivity analysis (SMD −0.14, 95% CI −0.32 to −0.05; 444 participants; 3 studies; P = 0.15).

    Two studies assessed bruise size after 60 hours. Heterogeneity was low for this outcome (I2 = 0%), so we pooled results of different studies using the fixed‐effect method. Results showed that there may be a reduction in bruise size after slow injection compared to fast injection (SMD −0.49, 95% CI −0.93 to −0.06; 84 participants; 2 studies; P = 0.03; low‐certainty evidence) (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015). See Analysis 1.3.

    Two studies assessed bruise size after 72 hours (Sendir 2015; Zaybak 2008). Heterogeneity was low for this outcome (I2 = 0%), so we pooled results of these studies using the fixed‐effect method. We found no clear difference between slow and fast injections of heparin (SMD −0.27, 95% CI −0.61 to 0.06; 140 participants; 2 studies; P = 0.11; low‐certainty evidence). See Analysis 1.4.

    Incidence of bruising

    Three studies evaluated bruise incidence at 48 hours after each injection (Jueakaew 2019; Palese 2013; Zaybak 2008). Two studies reported bruise incidence 60 hours after each injection (Jueakaew 2019; Sendir 2015). Heterogeneity was low for this outcome (I2 = 0%), so we pooled the results of these studies using the fixed‐effect method.

    Meta‐analysis revealed that there may be a reduction in bruise incidence at 48 hours (odds ratio (OR) 0.39, 95% CI 0.26 to 0.60; 444 participants; 3 studies; P < 0.001; low‐certainty evidence) and 60 hours post‐slow injection (OR 0.25, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.65; 84 participants; 2 studies; P = 0.004; low‐certainty evidence). We detected no subgroup differences (P = 0.40). See Analysis 1.5. One study was at high risk of attrition bias (Sendir 2015), but the limited number of studies reporting at 60 hours after injection precluded sensitivity analysis.

    Size of haematoma

    None of the included studies reported haematoma size.

    Incidence of haematoma

    None of the included studies reported incidence of haematoma.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    See summary of findings Table 1.

    Some patients receive subcutaneous heparin for a lengthy period from hospital admission to after hospital discharge. With daily heparin injections for several weeks, the risk of extensive bruising in addition to pain at the injection site is high. Consequently, patients and healthcare providers consider strategies that can reduce pain and bruising to be important. For this reason, we sought to evaluate the effectiveness of subcutaneous heparin injection duration on pain, bruising, and haematoma. This systematic review incorporated data from five trials enrolling a total of 503 participants to assess the effects of duration of subcutaneous heparin injection on pain intensity, bruise size and incidence, and haematoma size and incidence at the injection site.

    Results of this review update show that slow injection may slightly reduce pain intensity after 48 hours compared to fast injection (MD −1.60, 95% CI −2.69 to −0.51; 103 participants; low‐certainty evidence). This reduction was not detected immediately after injection (low‐certainty evidence), nor at 60 or 72 hours after injection (low‐certainty evidence). We were not able to carry out sensitivity analysis due to the limited number of studies reporting at each time point.

    Results also showed that bruise size may be reduced at 48 hours after slow injection compared to fast injection (SMD −0.54, 95% CI −1.05 to −0.02; 503 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). This effect was lost following sensitivity analysis excluding studies at high risk of attrition bias, therefore the results must be interpreted with caution. In addition, two studies assessed bruise size at 60 hours. Results showed that there may be a reduction in bruise size after slow injection compared to fast injection (SMD −0.49, 95% CI −0.93 to −0.06; 84 participants; low‐certainty evidence). No evidence of a difference to bruise size was detected at 72 hours after injection (low‐certainty evidence).

    Bruise incidence may be reduced at 48 hours (OR 0.39, 95% CI 0.26 to 0.60; 444 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and 60 hours (OR 0.25, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.65; 84 participants; low‐certainty evidence) after slow injection compared to fast injection.

    None of the included studies measured haematoma size or incidence after injection.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    All of the included studies injected heparin subcutaneously into the abdomen; however, some excluded studies injected heparin into the arm (Babaie Asl 2008; Balci Akpinar 2008). It should therefore be considered that pain and bruising at the injection site could differ depending on whether heparin is injected into the arm, thigh, or abdomen.

    Similarly, all of the included studies used low molecular weight heparin (LMWH); it is possible that outcomes could have been different had unfractionated heparin (UFH) been used.

    Numerous biological factors are involved in the incidence and severity of bruising, including aging, taking other types of anticoagulant and antiplatelet medications, bleeding disorders due to liver disease, vitamin K deficiency, an inherited condition, blood vessel defects, and platelet disorders; the studies included in this review tried to control for these confounders by excluding these patients as well as by using random assignments. To minimise any confounding effect, we excluded studies in which investigators also treated participants with other types of anticoagulant drugs because of the possible effects of these agents on bruising and haematoma.

    It should be noted that the included studies used different measures for the assessment of abnormal coagulation status when excluding patients (i.e. thrombocytopenia or abnormal activated partial thromboplastin time (APTT), etc.), and that participants were chosen from different clinical settings such as vascular, neurology, orthopaedic, and cardiology units, where heparin may have been used as short and long term, which in turn may have affected bruising and pain intensity in the injection site.

    In addition, the included studies may have used different injection protocols such as needle gauge, syringe size, heparin volume injected, injection technique used, experience of injector and number of the injectors, etc.; however, these were not clearly described. It is therefore important to consider that along with injection speed, additional factors may also affect pain intensity and bruising at the injection site. The included studies provided no clear description of how or which analgesic medications were used.

    Quality of the evidence

    See summary of findings Table 1.

    All of the studies included in this review were randomised controlled trials. We assessed the overall methodological quality of the included studies as moderate. The nature of the studies did not permit blinding of participants and researchers, and it should be noted that the fact that participants knew whether they had received a fast or a slow injection may also have affected the results because this knowledge may have led them to use different techniques (i.e. applying cold, heat, or massage) to relieve the pain and bruising based on the intervention received. However, two trials blinded outcome assessors (Palese 2013; Zhao 2016).

    Pooling of data from all included studies in the meta‐analyses was impossible, as some studies reported outcomes at different time points or used different measurements, thus reducing the numbers of available studies and participants for each outcome. One study addressed only bruise size (Palese 2013). The other included studies addressed all of our primary outcomes.

    We assessed the overall certainty of the evidence using the GRADE approach. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence for all reported outcomes (except bruise size after 48 hours) to low because of risk of bias concerns (performance and attrition bias or performance bias alone); the available data were from only a few studies with small numbers of participants (imprecision); and heterogeneity was detected across studies (inconsistency). We downgraded the outcome of bruise size at 48 hours to very low certainty as concerns about risk of bias were increased following sensitivity analysis. See summary of findings Table 1.

    Potential biases in the review process

    None of the review authors were involved in any of the included or excluded trials. Furthermore, none of the review authors had any conflicts of interest. To minimise the possibility of bias, we tried to locate eligible RCTs by conducting a broad and comprehensive search of several national and international databases. However, there may be some studies published in journals that are not indexed in common literature databases, so the possibility remains that we did not identify all eligible studies. In addition, two trained review authors independently performed study selection, data collection, and risk of bias and GRADE assessment of the included studies. We followed Cochrane processes as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    A published systematic review evaluated the effects of heparin injection duration on injection pain intensity and bruising (Yi 2016). Overall, the findings of that review were in line with the findings of our review. Yi and colleagues reported that slow heparin injection reduced pain intensity and bruise size and incidence at 48 and 60 hours after each injection (Yi 2016). Only 30% of the studies overlapped when we compared the two reviews; this was due to the fact that in addition to RCTs, Yi and colleagues also included quasi‐experimental studies, and limited their search strategies to studies published only in Chinese and English (Yi 2016).
