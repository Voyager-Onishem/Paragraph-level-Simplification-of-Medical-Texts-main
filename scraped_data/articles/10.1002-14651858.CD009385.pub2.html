Exercise training to improve exercise capacity and quality of life in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases

  • Marita T Dale

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español

    • Background

    Non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases, such as asbestosis and silicosis, are similar to other chronic respiratory diseases and may be characterised by breathlessness, reduced exercise capacity and reduced health‐related quality of life. Some non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases are a global health issue and very few treatment options, including pharmacological, are available. Therefore, examining the role of exercise training is particularly important to determine whether exercise training is an effective treatment option in non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of exercise training for people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases compared with control, placebo or another non‐exercise intervention on exercise capacity, health‐related quality of life and levels of physical activity.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), MEDLINE/PubMed, EMBASE, CINAHL, PEDro and AMED (all searched from inception until February 2015), national and international clinical trial registries, reference lists of relevant papers and we contacted experts in the field for identification of suitable studies.

    Selection criteria

    We included only randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that compared exercise training of at least four weeks duration with no exercise training, placebo or another non‐exercise intervention.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane. Two review authors independently assessed study eligibility and risk of bias, and extracted data. We employed the GRADE approach to assess the overall quality of evidence for each outcome and to interpret findings. We synthesized study results using a random‐effects model based on the assessment of heterogeneity. We conducted subgroup analyses on participants with dust‐related interstitial lung diseases (ILDs) and participants with asbestos related pleural disease (ARPD).

    Main results

    Two RCTs including a combined total of 40 participants (35 from one study and five from a second study) met the inclusion criteria. Twenty‐one participants were randomised to the exercise training group and 19 participants were randomised to the control group. The included studies evaluated the effects of exercise training compared to a control group of no exercise training in people with dust‐related ILDs and ARPD. The exercise training programme in both studies was in an outpatient setting for an eight‐week period. The risk of bias was low in both studies. There were no reported adverse events of exercise training. Following exercise training, six‐minute walk distance (6MWD) increased with a mean difference (MD) of 53.81 metres (m) (95% CI 34.36 to 73.26 m). Improvements were also seen in the domains of health‐related quality of life: Chronic Respiratory Disease Questionnaire (CRQ) Dyspnoea domain (MD 2.58, 95% CI 0.72 to 4.44); CRQ Fatigue domain (MD 1.00, 95% CI 0.11 to 1.89); CRQ Emotional Function domain (MD 2.61, 95% CI 0.74 to 4.49); and CRQ Mastery domain (MD 1.51, 95% CI 0.29 to 2.72). Improvements in exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life were also evident six months following the intervention period: 6MWD (MD 52.68 m, 95% CI 27.43 to 77.93 m); CRQ Dyspnoea domain (MD 3.03, 95% CI 1.41 to 4.66); CRQ Emotional Function domain (MD 5.57, 95% CI 2.34 to 8.81); and CRQ Mastery domain (MD 2.66, 95% CI 1.08 to 4.23). Exercise training did not result in improvements in the Modified Medical Research Council (MMRC) dyspnoea scale immediately following exercise training or six months following exercise training. The improvements following exercise training were similar in a subgroup of participants with dust‐related ILDs and in a subgroup of participants with ARPD compared to the control group, with no statistically significant differences in treatment effects between the subgroups.

    Authors' conclusions

    The evidence examining exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases is of very low quality. This is due to imprecision in the results from the small number of trials and the small number of participants, the indirectness of evidence due to a paucity of information on disease severity and the data from one study being from a subgroup of participants, and inconsistency from high heterogeneity in some results. Therefore, although the review findings indicate that an exercise training programme is effective in improving exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in the short‐term and at six months follow‐up, we remain unsure of these findings due to the very low quality evidence. Larger, high quality trials are needed to determine the strength of these findings.

    • Exercise training for dust‐related respiratory diseases

    Research question
    Exposure to harmful dusts or particles (such as asbestos), which may occur at work or outside of the work setting, can lead to respiratory diseases. These are called dust‐related respiratory diseases and include diseases such as asbestosis. People with dust‐related respiratory diseases may experience decreases in exercise capacity and quality of life. Therefore the research question for this review was: does exercise training improve exercise capacity and quality of life in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases?

    Why the review is important
    There are very few treatments available for people with dust‐related respiratory diseases. Exercise training in other chronic respiratory diseases helps to improve exercise capacity and quality of life. However, exercise training for people with dust‐related respiratory diseases has not been examined well.

    Studies we found
    We included two studies with a total of 40 people (35 from one study and five from a second study). Of these people, 21 participated in exercise training and 19 did not participate in exercise training. All people were men and they were between 55 and 86 years old. Both exercise training programmes included cycling and walking and one programme also included strength training exercises. In both studies, training lasted for eight weeks with people attending two to three sessions per week.

    Key results
    Immediately following exercise training, people walked an average of 53.81 metres further in a six‐minute walk test than those who did not complete exercise training. Six months following exercise training, people walked 52.68 metres further in a six‐minute walk test than those who did not complete exercise training. These improvements in exercise capacity were similar for people with dust‐related interstitial lung disease and people with asbestos related pleural disease. Quality of life also improved more in people who exercised compared to those who did not. No one reported experiencing any unwanted effects due to exercise training.

    Quality of evidence
    The quality of evidence was very low because there were only two studies and 40 people. Therefore, it is likely that these findings will change with more studies in the future. We need bigger studies that can confirm the findings of this Cochrane review.

    • Implications for practice

    The evidence examining exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases is very low quality, with most data obtained from one study. Although data within this Cochrane review demonstrate that exercise training results in improved functional exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life immediately following exercise training and at six months follow‐up in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases, we remain unsure of these findings due to the very low quality evidence. The included studies did not report any adverse events with exercise training.

    Implications for research

    Larger high‐quality RCTs are required to increase the number of studies and participants included in future updates of this review and to provide a more accurate estimate of the effect size of change in exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life by narrowing the 95% CIs. To detect a difference between groups of 30 m (SD 77 m) in 6MWD following exercise training, 110 participants per group are required to give statistical power of 80% with a 5% significance level. However this sample size does not account for any loss to follow‐up or participant drop‐out. Larger studies are also required to determine the effects of disease severity (including severe disease), supervision and programme duration on exercise training. Furthermore, research is required to determine the MIDs for changes in measures of exercise capacity, health‐related quality of life and levels of physical activity in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases. Future studies should continue to report any adverse events of exercise training and report data on compliance, hospitalisations and survival in this patient population.

    Background

  • English

    • Description of the condition

    Non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases comprise a broad spectrum of diseases and include both dust‐related interstitial lung diseases (ILDs) and dust‐related pleural diseases, resulting from inhalation of dusts or particles, such as asbestos, crystalline silica and coal. Some of the resulting diseases are a global health issue, in particular asbestos‐related diseases. Despite asbestos being banned in 55 countries worldwide, some asbestos‐related diseases are now pandemic (Stayner 2013). Furthermore, many nations, predominantly developing countries, continue to mine or use hazardous materials such as asbestos (Le 2011; Ramazzini 2011; Stayner 2013). Therefore the impact of these materials is likely to be felt for many generations to come.

    Non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases may affect the lung interstitium or pleura. In dust‐related ILDs, a person inhales the dusts or particles into their lungs, which may induce an inflammatory reaction that results in fibrosis (Begin 1983). Frequently, there is a long latency period from dust exposure to the development of disease, which can be up to 50 years after initial exposure (Welch 2009). Depending on the inhaled dust or particle, resulting diseases (which are often fibre or particle dose‐related) include asbestosis, silicosis and coal‐workers' pneumoconiosis (Lapp 1992; Mossman 1998). These diseases are often chronic and slowly progressive (American Thoracic Society 2004; Petsonk 2008; Wagner 1997).

    Dust‐related pleural disease, for example asbestos related pleural disease (ARPD), may be characterised by diffuse pleural thickening of the visceral pleura, rounded atelectasis or pleural effusion, the result of the inhalation of asbestos fibres. The resulting diffuse pleural thickening may be extensive and may cover a lobe of the lung or the whole lung, may be unilateral or bilateral, and may obliterate lobar fissures and the costophrenic angles. The rounded atelectasis is the result of infolding of the thickened visceral pleura, resulting in collapse of the adjacent lung parenchyma. Asbestos‐related pleural effusions may precede the development of diffuse pleural thickening. These diseases are often characterised by an appreciable latency period, usually 15 to 20 years from initial exposure to dust to the development of disease (American Thoracic Society 2004; Miles 2008).

    Non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases may result from occupational, domestic or environmental exposure to dusts. A diverse range of occupations were, or continue to be, at risk of developing these diseases following exposure to hazardous materials and include plumbers, electricians, carpenters, construction workers, miners, iron and steel foundry workers, shipyard workers, jeans sandblasters and fire fighters (American Thoracic Society 2004; Bakan 2011; Markowitz 1991; Wagner 1997). It is estimated that 125 million people worldwide are exposed to asbestos in the occupational setting alone (WHO 2006).

    Non‐occupational exposure includes exposure to asbestos due to asbestos emissions from asbestos‐product manufacturing facilities (Kurumatani 2008) or exposure to ‘do‐it‐yourself home‐renovators' (Park 2013). In the domestic setting, para‐occupational exposure has been well documented in families of asbestos workers when workers' contaminated clothes were brought into the home (American Thoracic Society 2004; Goswami 2013). Exposure to hazardous materials may also occur in the environmental setting, including exposure to silica following volcanic eruptions (Searl 2002) or asbestos exposure in residents living near natural deposits (Pan 2005). The number of people worldwide exposed to asbestos and other dusts in non‐occupational settings is unknown.

    Symptoms of non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases are similar to other chronic respiratory diseases, and are not unique to dust‐related respiratory diseases (American Thoracic Society 2004; Petsonk 2008; Wagner 1997). They include shortness of breath at rest or on exertion. These symptoms may result in the spiral of disability, commonly seen in other chronic respiratory diseases.

    There are very few treatment options for people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases. Following diagnosis of disease, withdrawal from further exposure is imperative, as well as assessment of functional impairment, management of concurrent respiratory diseases, immunisation (influenza and pneumococcal pneumonia), smoking cessation and regular monitoring. No prophylactic pharmacological management is available to prevent progression of these diseases (American Thoracic Society 2004; Miles 2008; Wagner 1997). More recently, the role of exercise training has been examined.

    Description of the intervention

    We included exercise training which was supervised or unsupervised, in an inpatient, outpatient or home‐based setting in the literature searches. We included all modes of exercise and programmes where the duration was four or more weeks.

    The control group included no exercise training, sham exercise training (placebo) or another non‐exercise intervention.

    How the intervention might work

    Chronic respiratory diseases often result in breathlessness, reduced functional exercise capacity and reduced health‐related quality of life. These symptoms may contribute to physical deconditioning, which in turn increases the ventilatory requirement in ventilatory‐limited patients. This is often referred to as the 'vicious circle' of deconditioning‐induced respiratory problems (Cooper 1995).

    Exercise training may break this cycle of physical deconditioning, resulting in improved muscle function, exercise capacity, symptoms and ultimately health‐related quality of life (Chang 1999; Ries 1995). Whether exercise training promotes sustained increases in physical activity is unclear (Ng 2012). There is substantial evidence that exercise training is an effective strategy for the management of chronic respiratory diseases with a recent respiratory society statement from the United States of America and Europe recommending all people with chronic respiratory diseases be considered for participation in exercise training as the key component of pulmonary rehabilitation (Spruit 2013).

    The pathophysiology resulting in exercise limitation in chronic respiratory diseases is complex. Exercise limitation may be due to pulmonary, cardiovascular or musculoskeletal factors, or a combination of these factors (Cooper 2001). Physiologically, exercise training in people with chronic respiratory diseases may affect cardiovascular or musculoskeletal function. Exercise training aims to improve oxygen extraction at the muscle, resulting in lower lactate production and hence lower ventilation for a given submaximal work rate after training (Casaburi 1991; Maltais 1996; Markovitz 1998). Exercise training does not usually improve resting pulmonary function.

    It is widely accepted that lower limb endurance training at higher intensity for at least 30 minutes three days per week is more likely to elicit physiological changes in people with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) (Casaburi 1991). Other modes of training, such as upper limb exercise training, may improve upper limb exercise capacity (Martinez 1993). The optimal training intensities in other chronic respiratory diseases is unclear.

    The primary goal of exercise training is to restore the patient to the highest possible level of function (Ries 1995; Spruit 2013) and this Cochrane review focused on patient‐important outcomes of exercise capacity, health‐related quality of life, symptoms of breathlessness and levels of physical activity.

    Why it is important to do this review

    With very limited treatment options available for people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases, the question of whether exercise is an effective treatment option is particularly important. Exercise training has been shown to be effective in people with ILDs (Dowman 2014) and in people with COPD (McCarthy 2015). However, ILDs incorporate a wide spectrum of both rapidly progressing and chronic respiratory diseases and the pathophysiology of COPD differs markedly from both dust‐related interstitial and pleural diseases. Therefore, the effects of exercise training in non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases cannot be assumed from findings in other chronic respiratory diseases.

    • Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Studies considered for inclusion in this review were randomised controlled trials (RCTs) or quasi‐RCTs in which exercise training of at least four weeks duration was compared with no exercise training, placebo or another non‐exercise intervention. We also considered cross‐over and cluster RCTs for inclusion.

    Types of participants

    We included studies conducted with adults diagnosed with a non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory disease. Dust‐related respiratory diseases encompassed both interstitial and pleural respiratory diseases due to the inhalation of dusts or particles, from either occupational or non‐occupational exposure. Diseases included:

    • Dust‐related ILDs:

      • asbestosis;

      • silicosis;

      • pneumoconiosis; including coal‐workers', mixed‐dust and hard metal;

    • ARPD.

    If data were available, we recorded an indication of the stage of disease.

    We excluded studies from this systematic review if they had included participants with a malignant dust‐related respiratory disease, such as mesothelioma or asbestos‐related cancer, or if they had discrete parietal pleural plaques as their only manifestation of dust exposure. We also excluded studies if participants did not have dust‐related respiratory diseases or if participants had mixed‐disease.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies examining exercise training which might consist of endurance or strength training of the lower limbs, upper limbs or a combination of upper and lower limb exercise training or an alternate form of muscle training. The intervention setting could be any inpatient, outpatient or home‐based rehabilitation programme. The exercise training had to be compared with a control group of no exercise training, placebo or another non‐exercise intervention. We recorded the specific nature of the training (intensity, frequency, duration, type) wherever possible.

    Types of outcome measures

    We collected data on outcome measures immediately following the intervention period and, if available, at six and 12 months following the intervention period.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Exercise capacity measured by a field walking test, a cardiopulmonary exercise test or a constant work rate exercise test;

    2. Health‐related quality of life measured by generic or respiratory disease‐specific quality of life questionnaires.                                         

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Pulmonary function: spirometry, static lung volumes and diffusing capacity for carbon monoxide;       

    2. Dyspnoea;

    3. Level of physical activity;

    4. Adverse events;

    5. Compliance;

    6. Hospitalisation;

    7. Survival.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We identified trials by searching the following databases: the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (2015, Issue 2), MEDLINE through PubMed (from inception until 6th February 2015), EMBASE through embase.com (from inception until 6th February 2015), CINAHL (from inception until 13th February 2015), AMED (from inception until 13th February 2015) and PEDro (from inception until 13th February 2015). We did not use any language restrictions. All searches were based on the MEDLINE search strategy as reported in our protocol (Dale 2011). We present the original search strategy in Appendix 1. However, we modified this strategy to improve sensitivity by including a greater number of search terms as outlined in Appendix 2. We have presented the PEDro search strategy in Appendix 3 and the EMBASE search strategy in Appendix 4.

    Searching other resources

    We screened reference lists of all primary studies and review articles for additional references. We contacted authors of identified trials and asked them to identify other published and unpublished studies. We also contacted known researchers and experts in the field to assist with identification of other published or unpublished studies. We also screened national and international clinical trial registers. 

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (MD and JA) independently coded studies identified in the literature searches for relevance by examining titles, abstract and keywords fields as follows:

    1. Include: study categorically meets all review criteria;

    2. Unclear: study appears to meet some review criteria but insufficient information available to categorically determine relevance;

    3. Exclude: study does not categorically meet one or more inclusion criteria.

    Two review authors (MD and JA) used a full text copy of studies in categories 1 and 2 to decide on study inclusion. We resolved disagreements by consensus. If a disagreement could not be resolved, we referred to a third review author (ZM) for resolution. We kept a full record of decisions and simple agreement and calculated kappa statistics.

    One review author (TT) and a person unrelated to this Cochrane review examined a study conducted by the remaining four members of our review team (MD, ZM, PB and JA) to determine whether the study met the inclusion criteria. This prevented any conflict of interest.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (MD and JA) extracted data independently by using a specifically developed data collection form. The primary review author (MD) entered data into Review Manager (RevMan), and performed random checks on accuracy. Data included characteristics of included studies (methods, participants, interventions, comparisons and outcomes) and results of the included studies. We asked authors of included studies to verify the data extracted for their study and to provide details of missing data if applicable.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MD and JA) independently rated the risk of bias of included trials using the following criteria as described in the Cochrane Handbook of Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

      1. Sequence generation;

      2. Allocation sequence concealment;

      3. Blinding of participants and personnel;

      4. Incomplete outcome data;

      5. Selective outcome reporting;

      6. Other potential sources of bias (i.e. other biases that do not fit into the above categories, for example, recruitment bias in cluster RCTs).

    We conducted a 'Risk of bias' assessment for each included study using the above criteria. We assigned a judgement relating to the risk of bias for each study; either 'low', 'high' or 'unclear' risk of bias if there was not enough information to make a clear judgement. We referred to a third review author (ZM) to resolve disagreements. We contacted study authors to seek clarification if it was difficult to make a judgment on a particular 'Risk of bias' item. We generated a 'Risk of bias' summary using Review Manager (RevMan) to present the judgements for all studies.

    We judged a study to be low risk overall when the study provided a description of sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of outcome assessment, complete outcome data, no selective outcome reporting and no other potential sources of bias. When studies did not report blinding of participants or personnel, we still rated them as having a low risk of bias as it is not possible to blind either participants or personnel due to the nature of the intervention. Conversely, we considered a study to have a high overall risk of bias if we judged that any of the aforementioned criteria were at high risk of bias.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For continuous variables we recorded the mean change from baseline or the mean post‐intervention values and 95% confidence intervals (CI) for each group. In the included studies, continuous outcomes were not measured using different scales. However, in future updates if required, we will express continuous outcomes using different scales as a standardised mean difference (SMD) with 95% CI. Likewise, there were no dichotomous outcome measures used in this Cochrane review. For future updates with dichotomous outcomes, where possible we will record the number of participants with each outcome event by allocated treated group to allow an intention‐to‐treat (ITT) analysis. 

    Unit of analysis issues

    A priori, we specified we would include cross‐over trials but we would only use data from the first period of the trial, prior to the cross‐over. For cluster RCTs that had not accounted for the clustering effect, we specified we would calculate the design effect according to the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook of Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011) and we would adjust the analyses at the level of the individual accordingly. No cross‐over or cluster RCTs met the inclusion criteria of this systematic review. However, we will use the aforementioned methods for future updates of this Cochrane review.

    Dealing with missing data

    Where possible, we extracted data from ITT analyses. We also contacted trial authors if data were missing from included studies. For this Cochrane review we did not perform last observation carried forward (LOCF). For future reviews, if the original trial authors did not perform ITT analyses and sufficient raw data are available, we will perform LOCF before entering data into Review Manager (RevMan), to limit attrition bias.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    Two review authors (MD and JA) assessed clinical heterogeneity, and we only conducted meta‐analyses where both agreed that study participants, interventions and outcomes were sufficiently similar. We combined only exercise training programmes of similar duration, mode and intensity. We specified that if these features were similar, we would combine supervised and unsupervised exercise training. However, data were only available for supervised exercise training. We combined all dust‐related respiratory diseases to determine an overall effect size as resulting symptoms are similar. For outcomes, different measures of exercise capacity were not combined. If the follow‐up times were at similar time points data were combined. We assessed statistical heterogeneity using the I² statistic (Higgins 2003). We considered a value of less than 25% as low statistical heterogeneity and up to 75% as moderate statistical heterogeneity. We considered a value of greater than 75% as high statistical heterogeneity, and did not conduct meta‐analysis if such high statistical heterogeneity was found.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We explored possible causes of bias on the analysis including publication bias, methodological quality and true heterogeneity.

    Data synthesis

    We used the Cochrane statistical package Review Manager (RevMan) for statistical analyses. One review author (MD) entered statistical data into Review Manager (RevMan) and a second review author (JA) checked the data. We performed a pooled quantitative analysis where trials were found to be clinically homogeneous. We used a random‐effects model in the data analysis. This was based on a post‐hoc review of the clinical diversity of participants and interventions resulting in heterogeneity. For future review updates, we will use either a fixed‐effect or random‐effects model depending on our assessment of heterogeneity. The small number of studies prevented the creation of funnel plots to assess publication bias.

    For assessments of the overall quality of evidence for each outcome, we employed the GRADE (Grading of Recommendations, Assessment, Development and Evaluation) approach to interpret findings (Schünemann 2013). The GRADE approach specifies four levels of quality (high, moderate, low and very low). For findings that included pooled data from RCTs only, the quality of data started at 'high quality'. We downgraded the evidence from 'high quality' by one level for serious (or by two for very serious) study limitations (risk of bias) based on the following factors:

    • Risk of bias;

    • Indirectness of evidence (indirect population, intervention, control and outcomes);

    • Serious inconsistency (subgroup analysis, heterogeneity and inconsistent results);

    • Imprecision of effect estimates (wide CIs, small participant numbers, small number of trials included in meta‐analysis);

    • Publication bias.

    Data from observational studies started at 'low quality' (Schünemann 2013).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We conducted subgroup analyses to explore possible sources of heterogeneity. We conducted the following subgroup analyses:

    1. The effect of exercise training on exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in dust‐related ILDs;

    2. The effect of exercise training on exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in ARPD.

    A priori, additional subgroup analyses were specified however there were insufficient data to perform the following:

    • The effect of exercise training on exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in people with early disease compared with people with advanced disease;

    • The effect of exercise training of less than four weeks duration, four to eight weeks duration and longer than eight weeks duration on exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases;

    • The effect of supervised exercise training compared to unsupervised exercise training on exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life in dust‐related respiratory diseases.

    If sufficient data are available for future updates we will conduct these subgroup analyses.

    Sensitivity analysis

    The small number of included studies prevented a sensitivity analysis to analyse the effect of risk of bias. If more studies are identified in the future, we will conduct a sensitivity analysis and perform a separate analysis with only those studies included that have a low risk of bias for at least three of the following domains: allocation concealment, assessor blinding, the number of participant drop‐outs, random allocation and the use of ITT analysis on results.

    Results of the search

    We identified 4892 studies from the initial search of the databases, including duplicates. From this list, based on titles and abstracts, we attempted to retrieve 21 full text articles and one abstract. However, we were unable to obtain one of the full text articles (Jastrzbska 2005). One study is ongoing (Dowman Ongoing). Of the remaining 20 studies, we reached agreement between the author team for all studies (kappa = 1, indicating excellent agreement). From these 20 studies, two studies met the inclusion criteria of this Cochrane review. Common reasons for excluding studies were studies not being RCTs, cross‐over or cluster RCTs or participants not having dust‐related respiratory diseases. We have listed the reasons for exclusion of studies in the 'Characteristics of excluded studies' table. See Figure 1 for the PRISMA study flow diagram.

    Included studies

    Two studies met the inclusion criteria. Full details of these studies can be found in the 'Characteristics of included studies' table.

    Study design

    Both included studies were parallel RCTs conducted in Australia (Dale 2014; Holland 2008).

    Participants

    The sample size of the included studies was 35 participants (Dale 2014) and five participants (Holland 2008). All participants were male with an age range of 55 to 86 years. Of the 35 participants in Dale 2014, 24 participants had ARPD, five had asbestosis, two had combined asbestosis and ARPD, three had silicosis and one had mixed‐dust pneumoconiosis. This study provided information on the diagnostic procedure for dust‐related respiratory diseases which included an occupational history, clinical examination by a physician, radiological findings on chest X‐ray and computed tomography scans confirming dust‐related pleural or interstitial disease, or both, and lung function tests (Dale 2014). The five participants from the Holland 2008 study were a subgroup of participants from a larger cohort of mixed ILDs and we included them in this Cochrane review as they had a diagnosis of asbestosis. The remaining participants with ILDs in the Holland 2008 study did not have a dust‐related respiratory disease and we obtained data specific to participants with asbestosis from the study authors. No information on the diagnostic procedure for people with asbestosis was provided within the Holland 2008 study. No indication of the disease severity of participants was provided in either study. Participants were excluded from Dale 2014 if they had mesothelioma, discrete parietal pleural plaques as their only manifestation of dust exposure, cardiovascular, neurological or orthopaedic conditions limiting exercise performance, or were on long term oxygen therapy, could not understand English or had participated in a pulmonary rehabilitation programme within the previous 12 months. Participants were excluded from Holland 2008 if they had a history of syncope on exertion, any comorbidities which precluded exercise training (such as severe orthopaedic or neurological deficits or unstable cardiovascular disease) or had participated in a pulmonary rehabilitation programme within the previous 12 months. Based on the randomisation procedure in the Holland 2008 study, three participants with asbestosis were randomised to exercise training and two participants were randomised to control. Based on the randomisation procedure in the Dale 2014 study, seven participants with dust‐related ILDs and 11 participants with ARPD were randomised to exercise training and four participants with dust‐related ILDs and 13 participants with ARPD were randomised to control.

    Interventions

    In both included studies the intervention was exercise training, two or three times per week, for eight weeks in the outpatient setting. The Dale 2014 study used aerobic exercise training only and the Holland 2008 study used a combination of aerobic exercise training and resistance training.

    Control

    The control group in the Dale 2014 study continued with their usual medical management and did not receive any intervention while the control group in the Holland 2008 study received weekly telephone calls for general health advice and support.

    Outcomes

    The primary outcome for both included studies was functional exercise capacity measured by a six‐minute walk test (6MWT) (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Health‐related quality of life measured by the CRQ was a primary outcome in Dale 2014 and a secondary outcome in Holland 2008. Both studies also used another quality of life questionnaire, either generic or disease‐specific, to measure health‐related quality of life; the Medical Outcomes Short Form 36 (SF‐36) (Holland 2008) or the St George's Respiratory Questionnaire (SGRQ) (Dale 2014). Secondary outcomes in one or both studies were peak exercise capacity measured by an incremental cycle test to peak work capacity (Dale 2014; Holland 2008); endurance exercise capacity measured by a constant work rate test (Dale 2014), dyspnoea measured by the MMRC scale (Holland 2008) and physical activity levels measured by a physical activity monitor (Dale 2014). Both studies reported adverse events and compliance and provided pulmonary function data in the baseline characteristics. We obtained additional raw data on MMRC dyspnoea from Dale 2014 and pulmonary function from the authors of both Dale 2014 and Holland 2008 to include these outcomes within this Cochrane review.

    Follow‐up

    Both studies measured outcomes at baseline and immediately following eight weeks of exercise training or control. Some outcomes were measured again after a further 26 weeks (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). We have provided full details in the 'Characteristics of included studies' table.

    Baseline characteristics

    Combining data from both studies, at baseline, lung function in the control group was mean (standard deviation (SD)) FEV1 81 (18)% predicted and FVC 86 (20)% predicted and in the exercise training group FEV1 was 78 (23)% predicted and FVC 87 (22)% predicted. At baseline, six‐minute walk distance (6MWD) was mean (SD) 460 (82) m in the control group and 464 (79) m in the exercise training group. Although no data on disease severity were provided in either study, based on mean lung function and 6MWT data, and that most participants were not on long term oxygen therapy, the participants could be considered to have mild to moderate dust‐related respiratory diseases.

    Excluded studies

    The most common reasons for excluding studies were study design not being RCT, cluster RCT or quasi‐RCT and participants not having dust‐related respiratory diseases. See Characteristics of excluded studies for further information.

    Allocation

    Both included studies reported random allocation to groups. Dale 2014 reported group allocation was determined by a person independent of the recruitment process through a computer‐generated randomisation programme. Holland 2008 reported the allocation sequence was computer‐generated and performed by an individual unrelated to the study, and concealed using envelopes.

    Blinding

    Both included studies reported the use of a blinded assessor for all outcome measures immediately following the intervention period and six months following the intervention period (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Due to the nature of the intervention, no participants in either of the two studies were blinded to the intervention.

    Incomplete outcome data

    The two studies reported dropouts and loss to follow‐up (Dale 2014; Holland 2008) with both studies having minimal drop‐outs. In the Dale 2014 study, one participant did not complete follow‐up testing at eight weeks. At 26 weeks, an additional one participant did not complete the health‐related quality of life questionnaires, three participants did not complete the 6MWT and four participants did not complete the physical activity measurements. In the Holland 2008 study, all participants with dust‐related ILDs completed follow‐up testing at eight and 26 weeks. Both studies performed data analysis according to the ITT principle.

    Selective reporting

    Both included studies documented outcome measures that were reported in the specified methods (Dale 2014; Holland 2008).

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings for the main comparison Summary of findings for the main comparisons

    The Data and analyses tables summarise the results of the meta‐analyses for the comparisons analysed in this Cochrane review. Both included studies provided sufficient data for pooling in a meta‐analysis (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). For exercise capacity, health‐related quality of life and levels of physical activity, positive values indicate improvement and for MMRC dyspnoea, negative values indicate improvement (i.e. a reduction in dyspnoea values). We found that the evidence for all outcomes was very low quality due to imprecise results, indirectness of evidence and a high risk of bias or inconsistent results, or both.

    Exercise training versus no exercise training

    Dust‐related respiratory diseases (combined dust‐related ILDs and ARPD)
    Functional exercise capacity

    Both studies measured functional exercise capacity (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Sufficient raw data were available to conduct a meta‐analysis and determine the common effect size (mean difference (MD)) for change in distance walked after exercise training. Exercise training resulted in improvements in 6MWD immediately after training with a MD of 53.81 m (95% CI 34.36 to 73.26 m) in favour of the exercise training group in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases (both dust‐related ILDs and ARPD) (Analysis 1.1).

    Both studies reported 6MWD results six months following the intervention period. In participants who participated in exercise training, improvements in 6MWD were maintained from baseline to six months following the intervention period with a MD of 52.68 m (95% CI 27.43 to 77.93 m; Analysis 1.2). Tests of heterogeneity for the analyses of functional exercise capacity for all participants with dust‐related respiratory diseases were not significant. We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Maximal exercise capacity

    Both studies measured maximal exercise capacity (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Sufficient raw data were available to conduct a meta‐analysis and determine the effect size for change in peak work capacity after exercise training. Results demonstrated that exercise training did not result in immediate improvements in peak work capacity (MD 9.54 watts, 95% CI ‐0.38 to 19.47 watts; Analysis 1.3). The results of this analysis were moderately heterogeneous (I² statistic = 46%). We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Neither study reported data on maximal exercise capacity six months following the intervention or control periods.

    Endurance time measured by a constant work rate test

    One study, Dale 2014, investigated the effect of exercise training on endurance cycle time. A meta‐analysis was not conducted. Exercise training improved endurance cycle time (MD 222 seconds (95% CI 84 to 360 seconds) immediately following the intervention period. Endurance cycle testing was not performed in Dale 2014 at six months following the intervention period so the long‐term effect is unknown. We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Health‐related quality of life

    Both studies measured health‐related quality of life (Dale 2014; Holland 2008) immediately after the intervention or control period. Sufficient raw data were available for all domains of the CRQ (Dyspnoea, Fatigue, Emotional Function and Mastery) to conduct meta‐analyses and determine the effect sizes for changes in the health‐related quality of life domains. The common effect sizes for change in Dyspnoea (MD 2.58, 95% CI 0.72 to 4.44; Analysis 1.4), Fatigue (MD 1.00, 95% CI 0.11 to 1.89; Analysis 1.5), Emotional Function (MD 2.61, 95% CI 0.74 to 4.49; Analysis 1.6) and Mastery (MD 1.51, 95% CI 0.29 to 2.72; Analysis 1.7) in favour of the exercise training group demonstrated improvements in health‐related quality of life.

    Both Dale 2014 and Holland 2008 reported health‐related quality of life at six months following the intervention or control periods. The between group differences in three domains of health‐related quality of life were greater at six months than immediately following the intervention period for Dyspnoea (MD 3.03, 95% CI 1.41 to 4.66; Analysis 1.8), Emotional Function (MD 5.57, 95% CI 2.34 to 8.81; Analysis 1.10) and Mastery (MD 2.66, 95% CI 1.08 to 4.23; Analysis 1.11). Exercise training did not result in an improvement in Fatigue six months following the intervention period (MD 2.51, 95% CI ‐0.41 to 5.42; Analysis 1.9). Tests of heterogeneity for the analyses of health‐related quality of life for participants with dust‐related respiratory diseases immediately following the intervention period were not significant. Only the analysis on the domain of Fatigue six months following the intervention period showed some moderate heterogeneity (I² statistic = 31%). We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Pulmonary function

    Both studies measured lung function six months following the intervention period (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Sufficient raw data were available to conduct meta‐analyses and determine if there was an effect on lung function. Six months following the intervention period changes in forced vital capacity (FVC), (MD ‐1.57% predicted, 95% CI ‐5.37 to 2.24% predicted; Analysis 2.1) and forced expiratory volume in one second (FEV1) (MD ‐3.57% predicted, 95% CI ‐8.38 to 1.25% predicted; Analysis 2.2) were not significant. The test of heterogeneity for the analysis on FEV1 was moderate (I² statistic = 59%). We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Dyspnoea

    Both included studies measured dyspnoea with the MMRC scale (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Sufficient raw data were available to conduct a meta‐analysis. Immediately following the intervention period and six months following the intervention period, exercise training did not result in significant decreases in levels of dyspnoea (MD ‐0.29, 95% CI ‐0.81 to 0.23 and MD ‐0.56, 95% CI ‐1.43 to 0.31 respectively; Analysis 2.3; Analysis 2.4). The test of heterogeneity for the analysis on MMRC dyspnoea six months following the intervention was moderate (I² statistic = 69%). We graded the quality of evidence for this outcome as very low quality.

    Physical activity

    One study, Dale 2014, examined the effect of exercise training on measures of physical activity. Between group differences were seen in participants with dust‐related respiratory diseases following exercise training with an increase in average daily steps with a MD of 1514 steps (95% CI 15 to 3012 steps) and an increase in average daily METs with a MD of 0.08 (0.01 to 0.15). There was no difference between the exercise and control groups in average daily energy expenditure following exercise training (MD 86 calories, 95% CI ‐57 to 230 calories). Six months following the intervention period there was an increase in average daily METs compared to baseline in the exercise training group (MD 0.11, 95% CI 0.03 to 0.20) but no difference between groups in average daily steps.

    Adverse events

    Information regarding adverse events was available for both studies (Dale 2014; Holland 2008). Neither study reported any adverse events associated with the exercise training during the study period.

    Compliance

    The Dale 2014 study pre‐defined compliance with exercise training as completion of at least 80% of 24 training sessions (i.e. 19 sessions). This study reported completion of mean (SD) 20 (five) sessions. The Holland 2008 study considered participants to have completed the exercise programme if they attended 12 or more out of 16 supervised exercise sessions. However, no data were specifically available on compliance for the three participants randomised to the exercise group.

    Hospitalisations

    No information was available on the impact of the intervention on hospitalisations following exercise training from either study.

    Survival

    For people with dust‐related respiratory diseases, there were no deaths over the 26‐week period in either study.

    Exercise training versus no exercise training

    Subgroup analyses

    There was sufficient raw data available from both studies (Dale 2014; Holland 2008) to enable us to conduct meta‐analyses on the effects of exercise training on outcomes in a subgroup of people with dust‐related ILDs. Sufficient raw data were also available from the Dale 2014 study to determine the effects of exercise training on outcomes in participants with ARPD. As data from only one study were available for people with ARPD, we did not conduct meta‐analyses. The improvements following exercise training were similar in a subgroup of participants with dust‐related ILDs and in a subgroup of participants with ARPD compared to the control group, with no statistically significant differences in treatment effects between the subgroups (Analysis 1.1; Analysis 1.2; Analysis 1.3; Analysis 1.4; Analysis 1.5; Analysis 1.6; Analysis 1.7; Analysis 1.8; Analysis 1.9; Analysis 1.10; Analysis 1.11; Analysis 2.1; Analysis 2.2; Analysis 2.3; Analysis 2.4). For people with dust‐related ILDs and for people with ARPD, we graded the quality of evidence for all outcomes as very low quality.

    • Summary of main results

    In this Cochrane review we included two studies, Dale 2014 and Holland 2008, that compared exercise training to a control group of no exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases. One study used aerobic exercise training (Dale 2014) and the second study used a combination of aerobic exercise training and resistance training (Holland 2008). Exercise training resulted in an increase in functional exercise capacity (6MWD) and improvements in health‐related quality of life immediately following eight weeks of exercise training. Improvements in functional exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life were maintained six months following exercise training. However, there were no changes in dyspnoea or pulmonary function following exercise training. No data were available on any outcomes 12 months following the intervention period. The authors of included studies did not report any adverse effects of exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases.

    The effects of exercise training were similar in the subgroups of people identified in this Cochrane review: those with dust‐related ILDs and those with ARPD, with no statistically significant differences in treatment effects between the subgroups. However, in people with ARPD there were insufficient data to conduct meta‐analyses for the main comparisons as only unpublished data for people with ARPD from the Dale 2014 study were available.

    No data were available to draw conclusions on the effects of exercise training in people with early disease compared with people with advanced disease, the impact of the length of the exercise training programme, the effect of supervised versus unsupervised exercise training or the impact of exercise training on the number of hospitalisations or survival.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    This Cochrane review is important for several reasons. Combining the data from the two included studies resulted in sufficient numbers to perform subgroup analyses on the effects of exercise training in people with dust‐related ILDs and in people with ARPD. The effects of exercise training in these two groups were previously unknown. In addition, this review has distinguished dust‐related ILDs from other ILDs, as the two are often clinically very different. This should prevent any misinterpretation of the effects of exercise training. This Cochrane review has also demonstrated that exercise training results in clinically important improvements in both functional exercise capacity and health‐related quality of life.

    The improvement in functional exercise capacity (53.81 m) demonstrated in this review is larger than the effect demonstrated in similar reviews regarding COPD and ILD. For the 6MWT, McCarthy 2015 has shown an effect size of 43.93 m in COPD and Dowman 2014 has shown an effect size of 44.34 m in ILD. Therefore, using exercise training regimens established for people with COPD (Nici 2006), people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases may experience improvements of larger magnitude following exercise training. Importantly, the improvements in 6MWD in this review were maintained in the longer term (six months following the intervention period) which differs from findings in a heterogeneous cohort of ILDs (predominantly IPF) where improvements in 6MWD were not maintained six months following an exercise training programme (Holland 2008). However, caution is required when interpreting the treatment effect size due to the wide CIs for 6MWD. The treatment effect may not be as large for all people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases. There were no reported adverse events during exercise training.

    The minimal important difference (MID) is the smallest change in an outcome following an intervention which is important for the patient and helps in evaluating whether the treatment effect size is of clinical importance to the patient. The MID for improvement in functional exercise capacity has only been determined in a heterogeneous group of people with ILDs (including a small number of participants with asbestosis) (Holland 2009) and there is no specific MID for people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases. Therefore, when comparing the treatment effect size of 53.81 m in 6MWD demonstrated in this review to the established MID of 29 to 34 m in mixed ILDs, the improvement in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases following exercise training is larger.

    Important improvements were seen in several health‐related quality of life domains following exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases compared to control. However, the MID for improvement in health‐related quality of life in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases or other ILDs has not been determined. We have therefore compared the treatment effect size in this review to the established MID for health‐related quality of life in people with COPD. The magnitude of the changes demonstrated in this review did not reach the MID for people with COPD, which has been established as a change of 0.5 points in the mean score per item for the CRQ (Jaeschke 1989). This equates to a change in actual scores for each CRQ domain of 2.5 points for Dyspnoea, 2 points for Fatigue, 3.5 points for Emotional Function and 2 points for Mastery. Therefore, although we could demonstrate improvements in health‐related quality of life both in the short‐term and six months following the exercise training period, the clinical significance of these health‐related quality of life improvements remain unclear. It should also be noted that the CRQ was not developed for people with dust‐related respiratory diseases, so interpretation of findings needs to be made with caution.

    Only one study investigated the effect of exercise training on physical activity levels. Immediately following exercise training, average daily steps and average daily METs increased in a combined group of participants with ARPD and dust‐related ILDs, with a small increase in average daily METs still present six months following the intervention period. However, the MID for change in daily steps in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases or ILD has not been established. For people with COPD, it has been suggested the MID may be between 576 to 1181 daily steps (Demeyer 2014). Therefore, the immediate improvement of 1514 steps in people with dust‐related respiratory diseases following exercise training suggests this change may be clinically relevant.

    Quality of the evidence

    Using the GRADE system (Schünemann 2013), we rated all review outcomes as providing very low quality evidence. We downgraded the quality of evidence for all outcomes by two levels because of imprecision. This was due to the small number of included studies and small number of participants and only Dale 2014 including people with ARPD. Since neither of the included studies reported details of participants' disease severity, we are unsure how well they matched the PICO question of this Cochrane review. Because of this, and because the data we could include from Holland 2008 were from a subgroup of participants, we downgraded the quality of the evidence by one level because of indirectness. Furthermore, the meta‐analyses on maximal exercise capacity immediately following the intervention, CRQ Fatigue six months following the intervention, pulmonary function (FEV¹) six months following the intervention and MMRC dyspnoea six months following the intervention in all participants or in people with dust‐related ILDs displayed moderate heterogeneity. Consequently we downgraded the quality of the evidence by one more level. However, it should be noted that, using the GRADE system, it is only possible to downgrade the quality of the evidence by three levels to a minimum of very low quality (Schünemann 2013).

    Potential biases in the review process

    We conducted a broad search for suitable studies which included searching clinical trial registries. The authors of the two included studies provided unpublished data to be included in this review. In addition, four out of five of the authors of this Cochrane review conducted one of the two included studies. To minimise this conflict of interest, one review author (TT) and a person unrelated to this Cochrane review examined the study to determine its suitability for inclusion in this review.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    No previous reviews have examined the effect of exercise training in people with non‐malignant dust‐related respiratory diseases.
