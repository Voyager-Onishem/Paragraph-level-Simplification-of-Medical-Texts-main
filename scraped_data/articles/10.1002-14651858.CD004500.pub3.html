Positive end‐expiratory pressure for preterm infants requiring conventional mechanical ventilation for respiratory distress syndrome or bronchopulmonary dysplasia

    • Background

    Conventional mechanical ventilation (CMV) is a common therapy for neonatal respiratory failure. While CMV facilitates gas exchange, it may simultaneously injure the lungs. Positive end‐expiratory pressure (PEEP) has received less attention than other ventilation parameters when considering this benefit–risk balance. While an appropriate PEEP level may result in clinical benefits, both inappropriately low or high levels may cause harm. An appropriate PEEP level may also be best achieved by an individualized approach.

    Objectives

    1. To compare the effects of PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for respiratory distress syndrome (RDS). We compare both: zero end‐expiratory pressure (ZEEP) (0 cm H2O) versus any PEEP and low (< 5 cm H2O) vs high (≥ 5 cm H2O) PEEP.

    2. To compare the effects of PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD). We compare both: ZEEP (0 cm H2O) vs any PEEP and low (< 5 cm H2O) versus high (≥ 5 cm H2O) PEEP.

    3. To compare the effects of different methods for individualizing PEEP to an optimal level in preterm newborn infants requiring CMV for RDS.

    Search methods

    We used the standard search strategy of Cochrane Neonatal to search the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, MEDLINE via PubMed, Embase, and CINAHL to 14 February 2018. We also searched clinical trials databases, conference proceedings, and the reference lists of retrieved articles for randomized controlled trials and quasi‐randomized trials.

    Selection criteria

    We included all randomized or quasi‐randomized controlled trials studying preterm infants born at less than 37 weeks' gestational age, requiring CMV and undergoing randomization to either different PEEP levels (RDS or BPD); or, two or more alternative methods for individualizing PEEP levels (RDS only). We included cross‐over trials but limited outcomes to those from the first cross‐over period.

    Data collection and analysis

    We performed data collection and analysis according to the recommendations of the Cochrane Neonatal Review Group. We used the GRADE approach to assess the quality of evidence for prespecified key clinically relevant outcomes.

    Main results

    Four trials met the inclusion criteria. Two cross‐over trials with 28 participants compared different PEEP levels in infants with RDS. Meta‐analysis was limited to short‐term measures of pulmonary gas exchange and showed no differences between low and high PEEP.

    We identified no trials comparing PEEP levels in infants with BPD.

    Two trials enrolling 44 participants compared different methods for individualizing PEEP in infants with RDS. Both trials compared an oxygenation‐guided lung‐recruitment maneuver (LRM) with gradual PEEP level titrations for individualizing PEEP to routine care (control). Meta‐analysis showed no difference between LRM and control on mortality by hospital discharge (risk ratio (RR) 1.00, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.17 to 5.77); there was no statistically significant difference on BPD, with an effect estimate favoring LRM (RR 0.25, 95% CI 0.03 to 2.07); and a statistically significant difference favoring LRM for the outcome of duration of ventilatory support (mean difference –1.06 days, 95% CI –1.85 to –0.26; moderate heterogeneity, I2 = 67%). Short‐term oxygenation measures also favored LRM. We graded the quality of the evidence as low for all key outcomes due to risk of bias and imprecision of the effect estimates.

    Authors' conclusions

    There continues to be insufficient evidence to guide PEEP level selection for preterm infants on CMV for RDS or BPD. Low‐quality data suggests that selecting PEEP levels through the application of an oxygenation‐guided LRM may result in clinical benefit. Well‐conducted randomized trials, particularly to further evaluate the potential benefits of oxygenation‐guided LRMs, are needed.

    • Positive end‐expiratory pressure for preterm infants requiring conventional mechanical ventilation for respiratory distress syndrome or bronchopulmonary dysplasia

    Review question: how do different positive end‐expiratory pressure (PEEP) levels, or different approaches for selecting PEEP levels, compare on their effects in preterm infants with respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) or bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD)?

    Background: infants born preterm (before 37 weeks' gestational age) often have respiratory failure and require medical support to achieve adequate pulmonary gas exchange (removing carbon dioxide from the blood and replenishing it with oxygen). RDS and BPD are complications of premature babies that describe early (RDS) and late (BPD) respiratory failure as a result of preterm birth. Conventional mechanical ventilation (the use of a breathing machine) following endotracheal intubation (placement of a breathing tube in the windpipe) is a commonly used therapy for both complications. While this therapy can improve pulmonary gas exchange and lead to benefits, it may simultaneously injure the lungs and cause harms. Gas exchange is achieved by several ventilator settings that help cycle heated, humidified and (sometimes) oxygen‐enriched air in and out of the lungs. One of these settings is called PEEP, which is a continuous distending pressure applied throughout the respiratory cycle. It plays an important role in keeping the lungs open, enabling all areas of the lungs to undergo gas exchange. While the use of PEEP is generally accepted, it is not clear which PEEP levels maximize benefits while minimizing possible harms. An appropriate PEEP level may also be best achieved by an individualized approach in which a good PEEP level is determined on a patient‐to‐patient basis.

    Study characteristics: we organized our study to compare the effect of low versus high PEEP levels, which we defined as: less than 5 cmH2O (low) versus 5 cmH2O or greater (high). Our primary outcomes were death by hospital discharge (for RDS) or two years of age (for BPD) and neurodevelopmental impairment (problems with growth and development of the brain or central nervous system) at two years of age. We identified four trials. Two trials with 28 infants compared different PEEP levels in infants with RDS. Two trials with 44 infants compared approaches for individually selecting PEEP levels. The compared approaches were: routine, typical practice (control) versus a "lung‐recruitment maneuver." With this maneuver, investigators slowly increased and then decreased PEEP levels while monitoring for improvements in oxygenation to guide PEEP level selection. The evidence is up to date to 14 February 2018.

    Key results: two trials comparing different PEEP levels in RDS did not report on any patient important outcomes. They compared the short‐term effect of different PEEP levels on pulmonary gas exchange, and found no differences between low and high PEEP levels. The two trials comparing the lung‐recruitment maneuver to control reported survival to hospital discharge or the development of BPD and found no clear difference between groups. However, they did suggest that an infant receiving the maneuver may have required less time on conventional mechanical ventilation and had better oxygenation for a period after the maneuver. The quality of the evidence for these results was low because they were small studies and vulnerable to bias from limitations in the study methods.

    Conclusions: evidence‐based guidance for PEEP level selection in preterm infants continues to be limited. We are unable to make recommendations for clinical practice based on our findings. This review should raise awareness of the lack of high‐quality research evidence guiding the selection of PEEP levels in preterm infants and encourage further research. Additional research evaluating lung‐recruitment maneuvers as an approach for individually selecting PEEP levels would be particularly valuable, given our findings of possible but uncertain benefit with this approach.

    Implications for research

    The findings of this review should raise awareness of the lack of evidence guiding the selection of PEEP levels in preterm infants undergoing conventional mechanical ventilation for respiratory distress syndrome or bronchopulmonary dysplasia and encourage further well‐designed trials.

    The findings of this review suggest that an individualized approach to PEEP level selection that applies gradual PEEP level titrations during an oxygenation‐guided lung‐recruitment maneuver may result in improved oxygenation and a shorter duration of invasive ventilatory support. Larger studies to confirm these findings and more precisely measure the effect of this intervention on patient‐important outcomes are warranted.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) and bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) describe the early and late clinical manifestations of respiratory failure in preterm infants. They are common causes of mortality and long‐term morbidity in this population (Schmidt 2003; Patel 2015).

    Respiratory failure occurs in part because preterm infants are born with lungs that tend to collapse (Gregory 1971). This is the result of several anatomic and physiologic factors. Immature neurologic control results in periodic breathing and central apnea (Martin 1977). A compliant chest wall and the relative shape and position of the diaphragm, ribs, and chest wall contribute to inefficient respiratory mechanics, requiring additional work to inflate the lungs (Muller 1979). Both surfactant quantity and quality are decreased (Ueda 1994). These factors and others contribute to atelectasis and inadequate pulmonary gas exchange.

    Description of the intervention

    Positive end‐expiratory pressure (PEEP) is a continuous distending airway pressure (CDP) applied through an endotracheal tube and maintained throughout the respiratory cycle during conventional mechanical ventilation (CMV). CMV is a common intervention for neonatal respiratory failure. Heated, humidified gas is driven into the lungs for a set period (inspiratory time) at a given pressure (peak inspiratory pressure (PIP)), followed by an expiratory phase of set duration (expiratory time) where PEEP is maintained. This cycle is repeated several times each minute, giving a respiratory rate or frequency. "Mean airway pressure" conveniently summarizes these parameters as a single pressure level (Boros 1979). However, the clinical effects of different mean airway pressure levels may vary as a function of the specific parameter (e.g. PIP versus PEEP) changed.

    While CMV provides benefit by facilitating pulmonary gas exchange, it simultaneously carries the risk of lung damage – ventilator‐induced lung injury (VILI) (Slutsky 2013). In newborns, most attention has focused on the potentially damaging effects of PIP and associated peak volumes (Klingenberg 2017). Although acknowledged, the contribution of PEEP on this benefit–risk balance is less well studied (Monkman 2003). As with other ventilator parameters, providers choose how much PEEP (commonly measured in centimeters of water (cmH2O)) to apply. This review focuses on the impact of different PEEP levels on morbidity and mortality in infants born preterm.

    How the intervention might work

    PEEP helps offset the factors contributing to lung collapse and inadequate gas exchange in preterm infants. Both Corbridge 1990 and Froese 1993 demonstrated in animal models that the absence of PEEP, or zero end‐expiratory pressure (ZEEP), is deleterious. The use of some amount of PEEP is standard of care for preterm infants receiving CMV.

    However, it is uncertain how much PEEP should be used. If the PEEP level is too low, alveolar collapse will persist, with impaired pulmonary mechanics, gas exchange, and risk for further lung injury from atelectrauma (Froese 1993). Higher PEEP levels may increase lung volumes and improve pulmonary mechanics and gas exchange in under‐recruited lungs (Gregory 1971; Richardson 1978; Thome 1998). But, excessive distending pressure may result in overdistension and worsen respiratory mechanics and gas exchange, while causing lung injury through air leak syndromes and volutrauma (Greenough 1992; Bartholomew 1994). Overdistension may also impair venous return and cardiovascular performance (Hausdorf 1987).

    For the comparisons in this review, we considered PEEP levels of less than 5 cmH2O to comprise 'low PEEP' and 5 cmH2O or greater to comprise 'high PEEP.' We acknowledge this dichotomy is arbitrary. However, da Silva 1994 demonstrated useful increases in functional residual capacity when increasing PEEP from levels of 2 cmH2O to 5 cmH2O, and at 5 cmH2O the mean functional residual capacity values were similar to the normal values of healthy term infants. A further pragmatic consideration is that 5 cmH2O is a commonly selected initial PEEP level (Bamat 2018).

    Identification of an 'optimal' PEEP level that minimizes harm from either insufficient or excessive distension may require an individualized approach. Individualized, optimal PEEP levels that vary between infants would reflect differences in underlying disease type and severity, and likely change over time in the same infant. In fact, determination of optimal individualized levels by monitoring esophageal pressures and gas exchange was suggested in one of the earliest applications of CDP therapy in preterm infants (Bonta 1977). Other investigators have advocated monitoring pulmonary mechanics to identify best levels. For example, Mathe and colleagues proposed examining individual pressure–volume curves to identify the lower inflection point of the inspiratory limb as the point of 'appropriate PEEP' (Mathe 1987). Controlled trials in newborn animal models suggest that setting PEEP levels above this level may provide an individualized technique to minimize lung damage (Monkman 2004). This review compares not only different PEEP levels, but also methods for individualizing PEEP therapy.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Respiratory failure and VILI remains a common source of harm in preterm infants. Systematic reviews comparing the preferential use of non‐invasive respiratory support strategies such as nasal continuous positive airway pressure (nCPAP) over mechanical ventilation suggest an improvement in BPD‐free survival (Fischer 2013; Schmölzer 2013). This benefit is likely mediated, at least in part, through avoidance of VILI. However, the magnitude of the benefit is modest, with a number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome of 25 (Schmölzer 2013) or 35 (Fischer 2013). Consistent with this limited benefit are trial data that report non‐invasive support failure requiring mechanical ventilation in 46% to 83% of preterm infants randomized to preferential nCPAP (Morley 2008; SUPPORT 2010). Likewise, non‐invasive respiratory support failure is common after extubation (Danan 2008). As such, preterm infants are commonly exposed to CMV and are at risk for VILI despite the increased adoption of non‐invasive respiratory support strategies.

    This updates a review published in 2012 (Bamat 2012). At that time, we identified one study reporting short‐term pulmonary gas exchange outcomes in 12 participants (Aufricht 1995), and concluded insufficient evidence for practice recommendation. One published study identified notable practice variation in PEEP level selection between centers despite adjustment for infant characteristics in extremely low birth weight infants (Bamat 2018). This variation likely reflects the absence of existing evidence‐based guidance. In contrast, in adults with acute RDS, one systematic review of PEEP level selection included seven randomized controlled trials (RCTs) enrolling 2565 participants (Santa Cruz 2013). This evidence disparity between adults and preterm infants highlights that PEEP level selection is a relevant clinical consideration and the relative paucity of data contributing to an evidence‐base in preterm infants.

    1. To compare the effects of PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for respiratory distress syndrome (RDS). We compare both: zero end‐expiratory pressure (ZEEP) (0 cm H2O) versus any PEEP and low (< 5 cm H2O) vs high (≥ 5 cm H2O) PEEP.

    2. To compare the effects of PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD). We compare both: ZEEP (0 cm H2O) vs any PEEP and low (< 5 cm H2O) versus high (≥ 5 cm H2O) PEEP.

    3. To compare the effects of different methods for individualizing PEEP to an optimal level in preterm newborn infants requiring CMV for RDS.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    RCTs and quasi‐RCTs. We included randomized cross‐over designs.

    Types of participants

    Preterm infants born at less than 37 weeks' gestational age (GA) and requiring CMV with endotracheal intubation for RDS or BPD. We included preterm infants evaluated in the first two weeks of life when a diagnosis of RDS was not explicitly addressed.

    For the determination of the effects of PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for RDS (objective 1), we planned subgroup analyses based on GA (less than 28 weeks' GA, 28 to 31 weeks' GA, 32 weeks' GA or greater) and for presurfactant versus postsurfactant therapy.

    Types of interventions

    Randomization to different PEEP levels in infants requiring CMV for RDS or BPD. As this review focused on CMV, we excluded trials of high‐frequency oscillatory ventilation or high‐frequency jet ventilation.

    Interventions for comparison included randomization to either:

    • different PEEP levels in infants requiring CMV for RDS or BPD, including comparisons of both ZEEP versus (any) PEEP and comparisons of low versus high PEEP;

    or

    • two or more alternative methods for individualizing PEEP in infants requiring CMV for RDS.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We used the criteria and standard methods of Cochrane and Cochrane Neonatal (see the Cochrane Neonatal search strategy for specialized register).

    Electronic searches

    For this update, we conducted a comprehensive search including: Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL 2018, Issue 1) in the Cochrane Library; MEDLINE via PubMed (1966 to 14 February 2018); Embase (1980 to 14 February 2018); and CINAHL (1982 to 14 February 2018) using the following search terms: (respiratory distress syndrome OR RDS OR hyaline membrane disease OR bronchopulmonary dysplasia OR BPD OR chronic lung disease OR CLD) AND (positive pressure respiration OR positive pressure ventilation OR PEEP OR positive end expiratory pressure), plus database‐specific limiters for RCTs and neonates (see Appendix 1 for the full search strategies for each database). We applied no language restrictions.

    We searched clinical trial registries for ongoing or recently completed trials (clinicaltrials.gov; the World Health Organization's International Trials Registry and Platform www.whoint/ictrp/search/en/, and the ISRCTN Registry). The search strategy was executed centrally through the support of Cochrane Neonatal.

    The search strategy for the original review is provided in Appendix 2. In the original review, two review authors independently executed the search strategy, resolving any discrepancies by discussion and resolution mediated by a third review author.

    Searching other resources

    We searched the reference lists of any articles selected for inclusion in this review. Additionally, we relied on the review authors' existing knowledge of potentially relevant studies to identify potentially eligible trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    For this update, we exported records of the electronic database search results to Covidence to facilitate title and abstract screening (www.covidence.org). We ensured single inclusion of duplicate data from the same study identified in multiple reports. Two review authors (JF and either YW or NB) independently reviewed the search results by titles and abstract, and excluded studies clearly not meeting the inclusion criteria. We obtained the full‐text articles of potentially relevant studies. After assessing these articles, we listed all studies excluded after full‐text assessment and the reasons for exclusion in the Characteristics of excluded studies table. A third review author (NB or HK) resolved differences and uncertainties when present. We display the study selection process in a PRISMA diagram in Figure 1.

    Data extraction and management

    Extraction of relevant data eligible for analysis was guided by a standardized and previously prepared data extraction form. Original data were taken from published reports of trials. Two review authors (NB, JF) independently extracted data with identical results then confirmed. We used Review Manager 5 for data entry, and construction of comparison tables and graphs (Review Manager 2014).

    When we required additional information to determine study inclusion or analyze data in a manner consistent with our protocol, we attempted to contact the corresponding study authors directly. Any potentially recoverable or unexplained missing data triggered an author inquiry. We made a minimum of three separate attempts at correspondence. We described any studies excluded due to an inability to obtain additional information in our review results.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (JF, NB) independently assessed the risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) of all included studies using the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool for the following domains (Higgins 2011):

    • sequence generation (selection bias);

    • allocation concealment (selection bias);

    • blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias);

    • blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias);

    • incomplete outcome data (attrition bias);

    • selective reporting (reporting bias);

    • or other bias.

    Any disagreements were resolved by discussion or by a third review author (HK). See Appendix 3 for a more detailed description of risk of bias for each domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For continuous data, we presented treatment effects as the mean difference (MD) or standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for individual studies and pooled estimates, using an inverse‐variance fixed‐effect approach for meta‐analysis (see Data synthesis).

    For dichotomous categorical data, we presented treatment effect as risk ratio (RR), risk difference (RD) and, for statistically significant comparisons, and the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs for individual studies and pooled estimates. We used a Mantel‐Haenszel fixed‐effect approach for meta‐analysis.

    Unit of analysis issues

    For cross‐over studies, we restricted outcome data to the first period given concerns for bias from carry‐over. These concerns stem from uncertainty related to the duration of time required for equilibration of lung volumes following a PEEP level change and the plausibility of lung‐recruitment maneuvers (LRMs; from PEEP level increases) resulting in persistent physiologic changes despite subsequent PEEP level decrements. For studies including repeated measures over time, particularly for physiologic outcomes such as measures of gas exchange, we used the repeated measure furthest from the intervention, so long as we judged the measure plausibly remaining a reflection of the PEEP level change.

    Dealing with missing data

    Our approach to missing data was as follows. First, we contacted the study investigators to inquire if any missing data were available. For studies using imputation to account for missing data, our primary analysis would only analyze available data (ignoring the imputed, missing data) and subsequently perform a sensitivity analysis including the imputed data. We planned to discuss the likelihood that data were 'missing at random' or 'not missing at random,' assumptions of methods used by study authors to deal with missing data, and address the potential impact of missing data on bias and the findings of the review.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    See the Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity section which outlines the steps we planned to limit aggregation and meta‐analysis of data at risk of excess heterogeneity and to facilitate assessment of heterogeneity within overall results. When we pursued meta‐analysis, when applicable, we planned to perform posterior assessments of heterogeneity through:

    • identification of studies contributing 'outlying' effect estimates. We would assess these studies for unanticipated clinical and methodologic characteristics plausibly explaining the observed heterogeneity. Identification of such characteristics would not result in exclusion from the pooled estimates of the primary analysis, but would result in exclusion from a secondary sensitivity analyses (see Sensitivity analysis);

    • quantifying the degree of heterogeneity by applying the I² statistic together with a Chi² test as a measure of corresponding statistical significance, using a conservative P value of 0.10 for statistical significance. We used the following I² statistic thresholds as a guide to help interpret the degree of heterogeneity:

      • 25%: none;

      • 25% to 49%: low;

      • 50% to 74%: moderate;

      • 75% to 100%: high.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We assessed reporting bias through visual inspection of funnel plots of effect estimates using Review Manager 5, when applicable (Review Manager 2014).

    Data synthesis

    When possible, we extracted consistent units of analysis for studies that reported the same outcome measure, or transformed units of analysis to achieve consistency (e.g. converting hours to days or days to weeks). When transforming units, we applied the same transformation to the corresponding measures of variance. When combining outcome measures from studies using different measurement scales to report the same outcome, we used the SMD as a summary measure of treatment effect. This is particularly relevant to short‐term physiologic outcomes for which a variety of measures may be used to describe a similar process (e.g. oxygenation measures).

    We used the following approaches for data synthesis and meta‐analysis: for continuous data, we used an inverse‐variance fixed‐effect approach for meta‐analysis. For dichotomous categorical data, we will use a Mantel‐Haenszel fixed‐effect approach for meta‐analysis.

    Quality of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook (Schünemann 2013), to assess the quality of evidence. For objectives 1 and 3, we assessed the following (clinically relevant) outcomes listed above: mortality by hospital discharge, neurodevelopmental impairment at approximately two years' corrected age, incidence of BPD, incidence of air leak syndromes, and duration of invasive ventilatory support. For objective 2, we assessed: mortality at two years of age, neurodevelopmental impairment at approximately two years' corrected age, incidence of air leak syndromes, and duration of ventilatory support.

    Two review authors (NB and JF) independently assessed the quality of the evidence for each of the outcomes above. We considered evidence from RCTs as high quality but downgraded the evidence one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of the evidence, precision of estimates, and presence of publication bias. We used the GRADEpro GDT Guideline Development Tool to create a 'Summary of findings' table to report the quality of the evidence.

    The GRADE approach results in an assessment of the quality of a body of evidence in one of four grades.

    • High: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited: the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We adopted a cautious approach to aggregation and meta‐analysis of data at risk of excess heterogeneity, erring on the side of avoidance when there was uncertainty. Two review authors (NB and JF) independently made this decision, and resolved any disagreements through discussion or resolution by a third review author (HK). For objective 1, we planned to limited heterogeneity, in part, through the a priori application of several subgroup analyses stratified on key clinical characteristics, as follows.

    Planned subgroups analyses

    When possible, we performed planned subgroup analyses stratified based on:

    • GA (less than 28 weeks' GA, 28 to 31 weeks' GA, 32 weeks' GA or greater);

    • presurfactant versus postsurfactant therapy.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned the following sensitivity analyses a priori:

    • excluding studies assessed as 'high' for overall risk of bias;

    • excluding studies that contributed 'outlying' effect estimates that can be plausibly attributed to unanticipated study characteristics following review as described; in the Assessment of heterogeneity section;

    • including data obtained following imputation of missing data.

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    In the original review, our search strategy resulted in 888 articles for screening (Bamat 2012). We identified 10 studies for potential inclusion and excluded nine upon full review (see: Characteristics of excluded studies table). One study met the inclusion criteria (Aufricht 1995).

    Figure 1 displays the search results for this update. The updated search identified an additional 1026 records following removal of duplicate records. We identified five studies for potential inclusion and excluded two upon full review (see: Characteristics of excluded studies table). Three additional studies met inclusion criteria (Castoldi 2011; Fajardo 2014; Wu 2014).

    Included studies

    See: Characteristics of included studies for further details of included studies.

    Four studies met the inclusion criteria (Aufricht 1995; Castoldi 2011; Fajardo 2014; Wu 2014). All were single‐center studies enrolling between 13 and 24 participants.

    Aufricht 1995 and Fajardo 2014 met inclusion for objective 1, to compare the effects of different PEEP levels in preterm infants requiring CMV for RDS. Both were cross‐over trials. Isolation of outcomes following the first cross‐over period was possible due to either the provision of individual participant data in the published manuscript (Aufricht 1995), or through correspondence with the study authors (Fajardo 2014).

    Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014 met inclusion for objective 3, to compare the effects of different methods for individualizing PEEP to an optimal level in preterm newborn infants requiring CMV for RDS. Both were single‐center RCTs.

    Participants

    The included studies enrolled 73 participants. In our original review, we excluded one participant from Aufricht 1995 receiving a deliberate hyperventilation strategy as this directly biased the study outcomes eligible for inclusion in this review (measures of pulmonary gas exchange). Of the remaining 72 participants, 28 corresponded to objective 1 and 44 corresponded to objective 3.

    Most participants were preterm infants of GA less than 32 weeks, including all participants in Castoldi 2011 (GA less than 28 weeks), Fajardo 2014 (GA less than 32 weeks), and Wu 2014 (GA 28 to 30 weeks). Participants in Aufricht 1995 were of mean GA 30.8 (standard deviation (SD) 2.6) weeks, but no GA‐based inclusion criteria were specified. Fajardo 2014 (less than 2000 g) and Wu 2014 (1000 g to 1500 g) additionally specified birth weight inclusion criteria. Most participants were studied in the first week of life. In Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014, all interventions occurred within four hours of life. The age at intervention was both older and more variable in Aufricht 1995 (mean 1.5 (SD 0.6) days) and particularly in Fajardo 2014 (mean 6 (range 2 to 71) days).

    Three studies specified a diagnosis of RDS (Aufricht 1995; Castoldi 2011; Wu 2014). All studies had an inclusion criterion of use of CMV, although specific modalities and settings varied between studies. Three studies provided ventilatory parameter details (Aufricht 1995; Castoldi 2011; Wu 2014). Details are included in the Characteristics of included studies table. Aufricht 1995 did not administer surfactant to any participants, Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014 administered surfactant to all participants immediately following endotracheal intubation, and Fajardo 2014 did not discuss surfactant.

    Other notable inclusion and exclusion criteria were: the presence of a patent ductus arteriosus as an inclusion criterion for Fajardo 2014, in which the primary objective was to evaluate the impact of PEEP level changes on the degree of left to right ductal shunting; and Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014 specified major congenital anomalies and intraventricular hemorrhage greater than grade 2 as additional exclusion criteria.

    Interventions

    Aufricht 1995 and Fajardo 2014 were both three‐period cross‐over trials evaluating between‐participant changes in various short‐term physiologic outcomes following PEEP level changes.

    Aufricht 1995 evaluated participants at PEEP levels of 2 cmH2O, 4 cmH2O, and 6 cmH2O, with allocation sequence selected in random order. To align with the objectives of this review, we restricted outcomes to those measured after the first cross‐over period and combined 2 cmH2O and 4 cmH2O as low PEEP versus 6 cmH2O as high PEEP. Infants remained at each level for 30 minutes, at which time outcomes were ascertained and the participants were crossed over to the next PEEP level intervention in the allocation sequence.

    Fajardo 2014 evaluated participants at PEEP levels of baseline, +3 cmH2O relative to baseline and –3 cmH2O relative to baseline, with allocation sequence selected in random order. As baseline PEEP were 5 cmH2O for all but one participant (with baseline 4 cmH2O), all but one participant were assessed at levels of 2 cmH2O, 5 cmH2O, and 8 cmH2O. The corresponding author provided individual participant data. To align with the objectives of this review, we restricted outcomes to those measured after the first cross‐over period and combined 2 cmH2O and 4 cmH2O as low PEEP versus 5 cmH2O and 8 cmH2O as high PEEP. Infants remained at each level for 10 to 20 minutes before being crossed over to the next PEEP level intervention in the allocation sequence. Outcomes were ascertained beginning at 10 minutes after a PEEP level change.

    Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014 were both two‐arm parallel‐group RCTs comparing the effects of PEEP level selection through the application of an oxygen‐guided LRM versus control participants who received standard care without an LRM. The study methods were nearly identical, with Wu 2014 basing their intervention on Castoldi 2011. In short, the LRM was initiated at a PEEP of 5 cmH2O, with repeated PEEP level increments by 0.2 cmH2O every five minutes while evaluating for improvements in oxygenation by monitoring peripheral oxygen saturation (SpO2) levels and FiO2 requirements. PEEP was progressively increased if oxygenation improved. If FiO2 reached 0.25, PEEP levels were then reduced in a gradual stepwise manner until oxygenation worsened. Then, PEEP was again increased until "stable oxygenation was achieved and the FiO2 level reached levels prior to the fall in oxygenation" (during the PEEP reduction phase). The intervention differed between Castoldi 2011 (83% to 93%) and Wu 2014 (85% to 93%) only with respect to SpO2 targets.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Evidence‐based guidance for PEEP level selection in preterm infants continues to be limited. With this update, we identified four eligible trials contributing data to two of the three objectives of this review. To date, we have identified no eligible data addressing the effects of different PEEP levels in preterm infants with BPD on CMV (objective 2). We identified no data addressing our primary outcomes of mortality by hospital discharge and neurodevelopmental impairment – or any other clinically important outcomes – following the application of different PEEP levels in preterm infants with RDS on CMV (objective 1). Data from two small trials enrolling 28 participants compared the effects of different PEEP levels on pulmonary gas exchange measures and found no statistically or clinically significant difference between low and high PEEP levels. With this update, we identified data from two small trials enrolling 44 participants for the primary outcome of mortality by hospital discharge for objective 3, comparing the effects of different methods to individualize PEEP (in this case, an oxygen‐guided LRM with gradual PEEP level titrations versus routine care) for infants with RDS on CMV. There was no difference in mortality between groups and the effect estimate was imprecise. Additional data from these two trials provided an imprecise, statistically insignificant effect estimate favoring the LRM for the outcome of BPD and effect estimates favoring the LRM on the outcomes of duration of invasive ventilatory support and oxygenation in the first 12 hours of life. We a priori judged the outcomes of mortality, BPD, and duration of invasive ventilatory support to be clinically important and included them in our summary of findings Table for the main comparison. These objective 3 findings are intriguing and suggest that an early LRM may be beneficial in preterm infants with RDS requiring CMV. We opine that larger trials to confirm these findings and more precisely measure the effect of this intervention on patient‐important outcomes are warranted. We do not believe that our current findings have implications for clinical practice.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Our overall findings continue to be insufficiently complete to meaningfully address the objectives of this systematic review or to merit clinical practice recommendations. The overall findings are imprecise due to the small number of participants eligible for inclusion and except for objective 3, fail to address clinically important outcomes.

    The data available for objective 1 have limited applicability to current practice. Aufricht 1995 was published 23 years ago and evaluated participants with a mean GA of nearly 31 completed weeks. Participants did not receive surfactant despite endotracheal intubation for RDS and 5/12 participants were sedated and paralyzed. In our experience, preterm infants with RDS undergoing CMV are now typically of a lower GA, routinely receive surfactant therapy, and are uncommonly sedated and paralyzed during CMV. Although we chose to include Fajardo 2014 in objective 1, we recognize limitations in the applicability of data from this trial for these comparisons. The objective of Fajardo 2014 was to assess the impact of PEEP level changes on right‐to‐left shunting through a patent ductus arteriosus (PDA); all participants had this complication of prematurity. The authors did not specify RDS in the inclusion criteria and enrolled some participants with a broader postnatal age than is consistent with RDS (2 to 71 days). This specific instance highlights broader concerns regarding the explicit inclusion of RDS in defining a population of interest for neonatal interventions. Although the incidence of RDS increases with decreasing GA (Stoll 2010), modern trials of early respiratory interventions in extremely preterm infants often do not list RDS among inclusion criteria. Rather, they base inclusion on GA, weight, postnatal age, respiratory support requirement, or a combination of these (Morley 2008; SUPPORT 2010). We considered that restricting inclusion to trials explicitly specifying a diagnosis of RDS could exclude many participants in the intended population: infants undergoing CMV for respiratory failure in the early postnatal period. Therefore, we amended the Types of participants eligible in this update, allowing studies that predominantly included preterm infants evaluated in the first two weeks of life when a diagnosis of RDS was not addressed. Nonetheless, it could be argued that infants requiring CMV in the setting of a PDA may differ in their response to different PEEP levels than infants on CMV for respiratory failure stemming from prematurity in the absence of a PDA. Thus, the contribution of data from Fajardo 2014 should be interpreted with these limitations with respect to applicability in mind.

    The data available for objective 3 are largely applicable to current practice. Both studies were published after 2010, enrolled participants commonly requiring CMV, and applied therapies consistent with modern practice, such as surfactant therapy and attempts to limit invasive ventilatory support. The only major discrepancy, perhaps, is the lower SpO2 targets chosen by the study investigators (83% to 93%, 85% to 93%) than are now sometimes preferred following findings from the various trials of the NeOProM (Neonatal Oxygenation Prospective Meta‐analysis) Collaboration (Askie 2018). While applying higher oxygen saturation targets could have influenced quantitative aspects of the LRM in each study (such as the FiO2 or PEEP required to meet goal SpO2 targets), we judged the overall rationale and process applied in these studies remains pertinent to modern practice.

    Quality of the evidence

    We graded the overall quality of the evidence for the three objective 3 comparisons listed in the summary of findings Table for the main comparison to be low based on the GRADE approach, consistent with a limited confidence in the effect estimate (Schünemann 2013). For each, we arrived at this conclusion by starting with a default of 'high‐quality' based on study design (RCT) and downgrading for both potential bias and imprecision. With respect to bias, we judged trials contributing data to have unclear risk with respect to performance and detection bias given no description of attempts at blinding and reporting bias given the lack of published or registered protocols (Castoldi 2011; Wu 2014). Although it is uncertain and perhaps even unlikely that these limitations influenced the outcomes reported in summary of findings Table for the main comparison, we erred on the side of caution. Imprecision is a problem as the comparisons resulted from two small trials. For mortality to discharge and BPD, comparisons were based on a small number of events, contributing to wide CIs around the effect estimates. While the effect estimates for duration of invasive ventilatory support and oxygenation measures favoring an oxygen‐guided LRM for PEEP level selection were statistically significant, they nonetheless reflect data from 44 participants and should be interpreted cautiously.

    Potential biases in the review process

    This review was limited by several potential sources of bias.

    The electronic search strategies used in the original review and for this update were created a priori with a deliberate effort to maximize the sensitivity for identifying relevant trials. However, in the original review, only six of the 10 studies ultimately considered for inclusion resulted from the original electronic search. Of the four remaining articles, three were known and provided by one of our senior authors and one was identified through a search of the reference section of an article being considered for inclusion. For this update, one of included studies was similarly missed by the search strategy (Fajardo 2014), and identified based on our existing awareness of this publication. This suggests that our search strategies potentially lacked the sensitivity to identify all relevant studies that may contribute data to the objectives of this review.

    A barrier to the inclusion of studies in this review was the restriction of data to the first period of cross‐over studies. Generally, the effects of interventions in preceding cross‐over periods may influence a measured outcome in subsequent cross‐over periods, so called 'carry‐over' or residual effects. We chose to restrict data to the first cross‐over period given uncertainty related to the duration of time required for equilibration of lung volumes following a PEEP level change and the plausibility of LRMs (from PEEP increases) causing persistent physiologic changes despite subsequent PEEP level decrements. Indeed, the findings from Castoldi 2011 and Wu 2014 support the latter concern as valid. Several of the identified studies employed cross‐over designs to assess the within‐participant effects of different PEEP levels in infants with RDS on CMV (Herman 1973; Aufricht 1995; Dimitriou 1999; Dinger 2001; Fajardo 2014). Of these studies, we were only able to restrict data to the first cross‐over period for Aufricht 1995 and Fajardo 2014. This criterion of restricting data to the first cross‐over period may have excluded valuable data from this review. Further, in the included studies, relatively small samples were randomized to three possible initial PEEP levels, making randomization imbalance in baseline characteristics plausible. For Fajardo 2014, a comparison of low and high PEEP groups as prespecified for this review resulted in a lower point estimate for FiO2 and LVO in the low‐PEEP group following the first cross‐over period. In contrast, summary data collapsed across the three cross‐over periods of the original study suggest a slight increase in FiO2 at lower PEEP levels, while LVO was lower at PEEP 8 cmH2O in comparison to 5 cmH2O (although not in comparison to 2 cmH2O). This may reflect chance randomization in the first cross‐over period allocations.

    For the purpose of this review, we created a dichotomy that defines low PEEP as less than 5 cmH2O and high PEEP as 5 cmH2O or greater. Although there are some data to suggest 5 cmH2O is an appropriate cut‐off value (da Silva 1994), and this level is commonly selected as an initial PEEP level in practice (Bamat 2018), this dichotomy is by necessity arbitrary. Both studies included in objective 1 assessed the effect of three separate PEEP levels (Aufricht 1995; Fajardo 2014), applying a cross‐over trial with random sequence allocation. As specified a priori, outcomes obtained at various PEEP levels were combined to create low PEEP (2 cmH2O and 4 cmH2O) and high PEEP (5 cmH2O, 6 cmH2O and 8 cmH2O) groups for comparison. As the pressure difference in PEEP level between infants assigned to, for example, 5 cmH2O and 8 cmH2O (both high PEEP group) is quantitatively greater than the difference between, for example, 4 cmH2O and 5 cmH2O (one low, one high PEEP group) the limitations of our arbitrary dichotomy are highlighted by the data available from these trials.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We are unaware of any others systematic reviews addressing the objectives of this review.
