Therapeutic exercises for idiopathic scoliosis in adolescents

    • Background

    Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) is a pathology that changes the three‐dimensional shape of the spine and trunk. While AIS can progress during growth and cause cosmetic issues, it is usually asymptomatic. However, a final spinal curvature above the critical threshold of 30° increases the risk of health problems and curve progression in adulthood. The use of therapeutic exercises (TEs) to reduce the progression of AIS and delay or avoid other, more invasive treatments is still controversial.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness of TE, including generic therapeutic exercises (GTE) and physiotherapeutic scoliosis‐specific exercises (PSSE) in treating AIS, compared to no treatment, other non‐surgical treatments, or between treatments.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, four other databases, and two clinical trials registers to 17 November 2022. We also screened reference lists of articles.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing TE with no treatment, other non‐surgical treatments (braces, electrical stimulation, manual therapy), and different types of exercises. In the previous version of the review, we also included observational studies. We did not include observational studies in this update since we found sufficient RCTs to address our study aims.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methodology. Our major outcomes were progression of scoliosis (measured by Cobb angle, trunk rotation, progression, bracing, surgery), cosmetic issues (measured by surface measurements and perception), and quality of life (QoL). Our minor outcomes were back pain, mental health, and adverse effects.

    Main results

    We included 13 RCTs (583 participants). The percentage of females ranged from 50% to 100%; mean age ranged from 12 to 15 years. Studies included participants with Cobb angles from low to severe. We judged 61% of the studies at low risk for random sequence generation and 46% at low risk for allocation concealment. None of the studies could blind participants and personnel. We judged the subjective outcomes at high risk of performance and detection bias, and the objective outcomes at high risk of detection bias in six studies and at low risk of bias in the other six studies. One study did not assess any objective outcomes.

    Comparing TE versus no treatment, we are very uncertain whether TE reduces the Cobb angle (mean difference (MD) −3.6°, 95% confidence interval (CI) −5.6 to −1.7; 2 studies, 52 participants). Low‐certainty evidence indicates PSSE makes little or no difference in the angle of trunk rotation (ATR) (MD −0.8°, 95% CI −3.8 to 2.1; 1 study, 45 participants), may reduce the waist asymmetry slightly (MD −0.5 cm, 95% CI −0.8 to −0.3; 1 study, 45 participants), and may result in little to no difference in the score of cosmetic issues measured by the Spinal Appearance Questionnaire (SAQ) General (MD 0.7 points, 95% CI −0.1 to 1.4; 1 study, 16 participants). PSSE may result in little to no difference in self‐image measured by the Scoliosis Research Society – 22 Patient Questionnaire (SRS‐22) (MD 0.3 points, 95% CI −0.3 to 0.9; 1 study, 16 participants) and improve QoL slightly measured by SRS‐22 Total score (MD 0.3 points, 95% CI 0.1 to 0.4; 2 studies, 61 participants). Only Cobb angle results were clinically meaningful.

    Comparing PSSE plus bracing versus bracing, low‐certainty evidence indicates PSSE plus bracing may reduce Cobb angle (−2.2°, 95% CI −3.8 to −0.7; 2 studies, 84 participants).

    Comparing GTE plus other non‐surgical interventions versus other non‐surgical interventions, low‐certainty evidence indicates GTE plus other non‐surgical interventions may reduce Cobb angle (MD −8.0°, 95% CI −11.5 to −4.5; 1 study, 80 participants).

    We are uncertain whether PSSE plus other non‐surgical interventions versus other non‐surgical interventions reduces Cobb angle (MD −7.8°, 95% CI −12.5 to −3.1; 1 study, 18 participants) and ATR (MD −8.0°, 95% CI −12.7 to −3.3; 1 study, 18 participants).

    PSSE plus bracing versus bracing alone may make little to no difference in subjective measurement of cosmetic issues as measured by SAQ General (−0.2 points, 95% CI −0.9 to 0.5; 1 study, 34 participants), self‐image score as measured by SRS‐22 Self‐Image (MD 0.1 points, 95% CI −0.3 to 0.5; 1 study, 34 participants), and QoL measured by SRS‐22 Total score (MD 0.2 points, 95% CI −0.1 to 0.5; 1 study, 34 participants). None of these results were clinically meaningful.

    Comparing TE versus bracing, we are very uncertain whether PSSE allows progression of Cobb angle (MD 2.7°, 95% CI 0.3 to 5.0; 1 study, 60 participants), changes self‐image measured by SRS‐22 Self‐Image (MD 0.1 points, 95% CI −1.0 to 1.1; 1 study, 60 participants), and QoL measured by SRS‐22 Total score (MD 3.2 points, 95% CI 2.1 to 4.2; 1 study, 60 participants). None of these results were clinically meaningful.

    Comparing PSSE with GTE, we are uncertain whether PSSE makes little or no difference in Cobb angle (MD −3.0°, 95% CI −8.2 to 2.1; 4 studies, 192 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). PSSE probably reduces ATR (clinically meaningful) (MD ‐3.0°, 95% CI −3.4 to −2.5; 2 studies, 138 participants). We are uncertain about the effect of PSSE on QoL measured by SRS‐22 Total score (MD 0.26 points, 95% CI 0.11 to 0.62; 3 studies, 168 participants) and on self‐image measured by SRS‐22 Self‐Image and Walter Reed Visual Assessment Scale (standardised mean difference (SMD) 0.77, 95% CI −0.61 to 2.14; 3 studies, 168 participants). Further, low‐certainty evidence indicates that 38/100 people receiving GTE may progress more than 5° Cobb versus 7/100 receiving PSSE (risk ratio (RR) 0.19, 95% CI ‐0.67 to 0.52; 1 study, 110 participants).

    None of the included studies assessed adverse effects.

    Authors' conclusions

    The evidence on the efficacy of TE is currently sparse due to heterogeneity, small sample size, and many different comparisons. We found only one study following participants to the end of growth showing the efficacy of PSSE over TE. This result was weakened by adding studies with short‐term results and unclear preparation of treating physiotherapists. More RCTs are needed to strengthen the current evidence and study other highly clinically relevant outcomes such as QoL, psychological and cosmetic issues, and back pain.

    • What type of therapeutic exercise is best for treating scoliosis in adolescents?

    Key messages

    – Due to a lack of robust evidence, the benefits of therapeutic exercises for idiopathic scoliosis are unclear.

    – Participants wearing a brace may experience less curve progression if they perform therapeutic exercises.

    – Compared to no treatment, therapeutic exercises may make little to no difference in scoliosis progression and cosmetic issues, while they may slightly increase quality of life.

    – Further research is needed in this area, focusing on highly clinically relevant outcomes such as quality of life, and psychological and cosmetic issues.

    What is idiopathic scoliosis?

    Scoliosis is a condition that causes an overall three‐dimensional curvature (torsion) of the spine (backbone). The most common type is 'idiopathic,' which means the exact cause is unknown. Scoliosis affects 2% to 3% of young people from 10 years of age to when a child is fully grown. When the curvature in the spine passes specific threshold levels at the end of growth, they can cause problems in adulthood, such as quality of life issues, disability, cosmetic issues, activity limitation, and possibly breathing problems.

    How is the condition treated?

    In many cases, scoliosis curves are small and do not require treatment. If treatment is judged appropriate, the main treatment options for preventing scoliosis progression include therapeutic exercises, bracing, and surgery. Due to the progressive nature of the condition, adolescents are generally treated when the curvature is diagnosed and further growth is expected meaning the risk of progression is still present. Once the curve progresses, no treatments succeed in fully straightening the spine. Children with larger curves may need to wear a brace or, if curves are severe, to have surgery to restore as normal a posture as possible. Therapeutic exercises are an option for less‐severe cases to reduce the risk of progression and avoid using a brace; also, they can be used in more severe curves in combination with using a brace. In this case, the aims are to reduce the side effects of using a brace (muscle weakness, rigidity, flat back), and improve its effectiveness.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if therapeutic exercises are effective in reducing spinal curves and postponing or avoiding invasive treatments such as bracing or surgery.

    What did we do?

    We searched medical databases for studies where people were allocated at random to one of two or more treatment groups comparing therapeutic exercises with no treatment, other non‐surgical treatments such as braces, or other exercises. We extracted and summarised data and rated our confidence in the evidence based on factors such as study methods and how accurate the results were.

    What did we find?

    We included 13 studies involving 583 participants. Most participants had scoliosis of low or medium severity. Five studies were conducted in China and one each in Brazil, Canada, Italy, Korea, Turkey, Saudi Arabia, the USA, and Egypt. The therapeutic exercises assessed in the studies were different in type and duration (between 12 and 42 months).

    We are very uncertain whether therapeutic exercises are less effective than using braces in reducing curve progression in adolescents with scoliosis of medium severity, or improve quality of life slightly.

    We do not know if different types of therapeutic exercises make any difference in scoliosis progression, quality of life, and cosmetic issues.

    None of the included studies assessed the unwanted effects.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    Participants included in the studies knew which interventions they received; therefore, they could have been influenced by how much trust they had in the intervention when reporting improvements based on their personal experience, and performing the exercises by increasing their motivation.

    All studies included only a few participants. Therefore, we have little confidence in their results, as they could be different when the interventions are delivered in clinical practice.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    This review updates our previous review from 2012. The evidence is up to date to 17 November 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The results of this review are in line with the recommendations of current clinical guidelines, where evidence is combined with the clinical expertise of the producing scientific societies: Society of Scoliosis Orthopaedic and Rehabilitation Treatment (SOSORT) (Negrini 2008a) and Scoliosis Research Society‐Society of Scoliosis Orthopaedic and Rehabilitation Treatment (SRS‐SOSORT) (Roye 2020). The evidence for the efficacy of therapeutic exercises is currently sparse due to heterogeneity, few trial participants in included trials, and many different comparisons. We found only one study following up participants until the end of growth and this found low‐certainty evidence favouring physiotherapeutic scoliosis‐specific exercises over therapeutic exercises on the reduction of participants who progressed more than 5° Cobb (Monticone 2014). This result was weakened by adding studies with short‐term results, and unclear preparation of treating physiotherapists.

    Implications for research

    More randomised controlled trials are needed to determine the true efficacy of therapeutic exercises conclusively, as well as to determine whether there is any difference between physiotherapeutic scoliosis‐specific exercises and generic therapeutic exercises. These trials should also include clinically relevant outcomes such as quality of life, psychological and cosmetic issues, and back pain, and have adequate follow‐up until the end of the growth period.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Scoliosis is a complex pathology of the spine and trunk that develops in three dimensions and results in the appearance of frontal curves, vertebral rotations, and a distortion of the sagittal physiological curves (Negrini 2008a). A curvature can develop at any level of the spine. Depending on the affected vertebrae, curves are classified as thoracic, thoracolumbar, or lumbar (Negrini 2008a). While scoliosis can be secondary to other pathologies, in 70% to 80% of cases, the causes are unknown (SRS 2006), and it is called idiopathic. The adolescent type is diagnosed between 10 years and the end of growth. Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) is the most common diagnosis.

    The magnitude of scoliotic curves in the frontal plane is measured from an X‐ray with the Cobb angle (Weinstein 1983). The Cobb angle measures the maximum curvature of the spine on the frontal plane. According to a consensus between the International Society of Scoliosis Orthopaedic and Rehabilitation Treatment (SOSORT) and the Non‐Operative Committee of the Scoliosis Research Society (SRS), experts classify curves that measure up to 20° Cobb as low degree, 21° to 35° as moderate, 36° to 40° as moderate to severe, and 41° to 50° as severe (Negrini 2015a). If scoliosis curvature surpasses the critical threshold of 30° Cobb at the end of growth, the risk of health problems in adulthood increases significantly (Negrini 2015a). Issues include a decrease in quality of life (QoL), and increase in disability, pain, cosmetic issues, functional limitations, sometimes pulmonary problems, and possible progression during adulthood (Weinstein 2003).

    The Cobb angle assessment of the spinal curvature relates only to the frontal plane. Although attempts have been made to complete a three‐dimensional evaluation (Negrini 2008a), today, the Cobb angle remains the gold standard. For this reason, at the start and end of treatment, a complete radiographic evaluation is usually made, involving the assessment of spinal misalignment in the sagittal plane (the magnitude of lumbar lordosis and thoracic kyphosis are generally smaller than the physiological values​​) (Negrini 2008a). The Perdriolle torsiometer (Omeroğlu 1996) or the Raimondi torsiometer (Weiss 1995) are used to measure vertebral rotation on the horizontal plane.

    The prevalence of AIS is 2% to 3% in the general population, almost 10% of whom require some form of treatment and up to 0.1% of whom will require surgery (Negrini 2008a; Roye 2020). AIS is more common in females (female:male ratio is around 7:1). AIS does not typically cause any health problems during growth; however, the resulting surface changes frequently have a negative impact on adolescents that can give rise to QoL issues and, in the worst cases, psychological disturbances (Anwer 2015; Diarbakerli 2019; Schwieger 2017).

    Description of the intervention

    Due to the progressive nature of the pathology, adolescents are generally treated when the curvature is diagnosed, and residual growth with a risk of progression is present. Once the curve progresses, no treatments succeed in fully correcting the spine. The main treatment options for preventing scoliosis progression include therapeutic exercises (TE), bracing, and surgery (Negrini 2008a; Roye 2020). The use of TE for the treatment of AIS used to be controversial. Whilst routinely used in several Mediterranean and continental European countries, only recently have centres in the rest of the world started to use them (Roye 2020).

    The conservative scoliosis treatment SOSORT experts identify TE as physiotherapeutic scoliosis‐specific exercises (PSSE) (Negrini 2008a). PSSE consists of individually adapted TE taught to the patients by experts on scoliosis treatment. They are based on two main elements: self‐correction and stabilisation (Weiss 2006a). They have been defined as "physiotherapeutic" without a professional implication but due to a larger perspective beyond simple exercises, including patient and family education (Negrini 2008a). The growing interest in the scientific community in TE for AIS has led to many studies on exercises that are not PSSE. Since the SOSORT support for PSSE is consensus‐based (Weiss 2006a), there is a reason to analyse PSSE and generic therapeutic exercises (GTE) separately. GTE can be easily identified since they miss the specific component of self‐correction.

    How the intervention might work

    TEs work mechanically by changing the spine's musculature and other soft tissues. They can also alter the motor control of the spine by affecting neurological changes that interact with each other (Smania 2008). In PSSE, self‐correction is considered the most critical specific component to achieve the required neurological changes and specific muscular action.

    TE can be used during growth in two main clinical scenarios: 1. stand‐alone as the primary treatment of AIS for curves up to 20° to 25°, and 2. in conjunction with braces for curves above 20° to 25°.

    When TE is used as a stand‐alone, the aim is to reduce the risk of progression and avoid using a brace. A critical Cobb angle of 25° to 30° is generally regarded as the threshold for brace prescription (Negrini 2008a; Roye 2020; Weinstein 2013). In this clinical scenario, PSSE is proposed to increase the neuromotor control of the patient and his/her ability to stabilise the spine in a more corrected posture while keeping good muscular strength and mobility (Berdishevsky 2016; Negrini 2008a; Smania 2008). GTE focuses on one or more of the previous elements without the self‐correction‐specific one.

    When TE is used in conjunction with brace treatment, the aims are to reduce the adverse effects of bracing (muscle weakness, rigidity, flat back) and improve its efficacy (Romano 2006). Moreover, due to their neuromotor training, TE can help avoid losing corrections while wearing the brace (Zaina 2009a). In this case, PSSEs are regarded as more effective by the SOSORT community due to their specific individualised corrective training through self‐correction (Negrini 2019a). Again, GTE focuses on one or more elements included in PSSE but not on the whole spectrum.

    Why it is important to do this review

    The first version of this review (Romano 2012) was based on a scoping literature search that identified three systematic reviews on the topic, none of which followed Cochrane methodology (Lenssinck 2005; Negrini 2003; Negrini 2008b). Therefore, we examined evidence published in these reviews and followed more rigorous methods to answer our clinical question, "Is scoliosis‐specific exercise therapy effective in delaying the progression of or reducing the speed at which the curve progresses?". Preventing the progression of the disease means avoiding the need for bracing, surgery, or both. We did not include studies on bracing because there is another Cochrane review where this is covered (Negrini 2015b).

    More recently, other non‐Cochrane reviews have been published on the same topic. Most of them included diverse types of studies and did not follow Cochrane methodology. They also answered different research questions: efficacy of various conservative treatments (Dunn 2018; Plaszewski 2014); exercises in general (Anwer 2015; Ceballos 2018; Gamiz‐Bermudez 2021); PSSE only (Fan 2020; Thompson 2019); or specific techniques, such as Schroth (Burger 2019), Pilates (Gou 2021), core‐based exercises (Li 2021), and myofascial release on control position exercises (Lopez‐Torres 2021).

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness of TE, including generic therapeutic exercise (GTE) and physiotherapeutic scoliosis‐specific exercises (PSSE) in treating adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS), compared to no treatment, other non‐surgical treatments, or between treatments.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    For this 2022 update, we only searched for randomised controlled trials (RCTs). In the previous version of the review (Romano 2012), we also included observational studies (cohort studies). However, there were sufficient RCTs identified to address our study aims for this update, so we did not search for observational studies. The previous version of the review included two studies, Wan 2005, an RCT, and Negrini 2008a, an observational study that we have now listed as an excluded study.

    Types of participants

    Participants diagnosed as having AIS with at least a 10° Cobb and between the ages of 10 years and the end of growth (in female adolescents, this is approximately between the ages of 15 and 17 years; in male adolescents, this usually occurs between 16 and 19 years of age). The end of growth can be determined by the Risser sign, which quantifies the ossification of the iliac crest. Stage 4 indicates total ossification of the apophysis, while Stage 5 indicates fusion of the apophysis to the iliac crest and the end of further growth. The Greulich‐Pyle atlas calculates the maturity of bones by assessing X‐rays of the left hand.

    We excluded studies in which participants presented any secondary scoliosis (congenital, neurological, metabolic, post‐traumatic, etc.) diagnosed according to the SRS criteria (SRS 2006).

    Types of interventions

    Experimental intervention

    Any exercise programme performed with a therapeutic aim for AIS divided into two groups:

    • PSSE defined as the performance of any self‐correction (Negrini 2006; Negrini 2008a) during the TE. Self‐correction is a specific and selective movement to reduce the curve(s) constituting the pathology;

    • GTE, including all the other non‐PSSE TE.

    Comparison interventions

    No intervention, other non‐surgical treatments (e.g. braces, electrical stimulation, manual therapy), or another type of exercise.

    We performed the following comparisons:

    • TE (PSSE or GTE) versus no treatment;

    • TE (PSSE or GTE) plus other non‐surgical treatments (e.g. braces, electrical stimulation, manual therapy) versus the other non‐surgical treatments alone;

    • TE (PSSE or GTE) versus other non‐surgical treatments (e.g. brace, electrical stimulation, manual therapy);

    • PSSE versus GTE.

    Types of outcome measures

    Major outcomes

    • Progression of scoliosis, as measured by:

      • Cobb angle in degrees Cobb (absolute values);

      • measurement of trunk rotation (e.g. angle of trunk rotation (ATR), measured by Bunnell degrees in degrees ATR (absolute values), or hump height in millimetres);

      • number of participants who have progressed by more than 5° Cobb;

      • number of participants with a prescription for bracing;

      • number of participants with a prescription for surgery.

    • Cosmetic issues, as measured by:

      • objective surface measurements, with validated scales (trunk appearance clinical evaluation (TRACE));

      • subjective perception was measured by validated questionnaires such as the Walter Reed Visual Assessment Scale (WRVAS), Spinal Appearance Questionnaire (SAQ), and Scoliosis Research Society – 22 Patient Questionnaire (SRS‐22) Self‐Image subscale.

    • QoL and disability, as measured by specific validated questionnaires, such as SRS‐22, 36‐item Short Form (SF‐36) (Asher 2003), Bad Sobernheim Stress Questionnaire (BSSQ) (Weiss 2006b), and Brace Questionnaire (BrQ) (Vasiliadis 2006).

    In this field, where patients do not perceive the deformity, results are clinically relevant outcomes because they influence future life. Patients have a very low probability of health issues if, 1. in adulthood, they remain below the 30° Cobb threshold (Negrini 2015a); and 2. during growth, they do not have to change treatment from exercises (less demanding) to bracing (more demanding). Consequently, we do not have minimal clinically important differences (MCIDs) for most measurements in this field. We interpreted the findings based on whether they exceeded the expected measurement error or not.

    • Cobb degrees: 5° (Negrini 2015a)

    • ATR degrees: 3° (Negrini 2008a)

    • TRACE: 2 points (Zaina 2009b)

    We searched MCIDs for the questionnaires in people with mild idiopathic scoliosis and found data only on the SRS‐22 subscores (Monticone 2017):

    • Function: 0.7 points;

    • Pain: 0.7 points;

    • Mental Health: 0.5 points;

    • Self‐Image: 0.4 points;

    • Total: 0.6 points (since the authors did not report the MCID for the total, we calculated it from their data).

    We also used the MCID defined by Powell for the 100‐mm Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) for pain in children aged 8 to 15 years (Powell 2001).

    We found the minimal detectable change (MDC) for:

    • SAQ 0.659 (Sanders 2007);

    • BSSQ 1.49 (Botens‐Helmus 2006).

    We could not find MCID, MDC, or measurement error in a mild idiopathic scoliosis population (or similar) for all the other questionnaires. Consequently, in the results section, we did not define the clinical relevance of these outcomes.

    Minor outcomes

    • Back pain, as measured by:

      • VAS or other validated measurement tools;

      • use of analgesic drugs.

    • Mental health, as measured by:

      • specific questionnaires such as mental health domains of SRS‐22, SF‐36, BSSQ, and BrQ.

    • Any adverse effects, as outlined in identified trials.

    All outcomes (major and minor) were measured in the very short‐term (any result before the end of growth), the short‐term (results at the end of growth), and the long‐term (results in adulthood).

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following electronic databases.

    • Cochrane Back and Neck (CBN) Trials Register (Cochrane Register of Studies (CRS)) (3 December 2018)

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL, searched using CRS Web) to 17 November 2022

    • MEDLINE (Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE(R) Daily and Ovid MEDLINE(R)) (OvidSP, 1946 to 17 November 2022)

    • Embase (OvidSP, 1980 to 17 November 2022)

    • CINAHL (1982 to 3 December 2018)

    • SportDiscus (1975 to 3 December 2018)

    • PsycINFO (2002 to 3 December 2018)

    • PEDro (1929 to 3 December 2018)

    • Index to Chiropractic Literature (ICL) (3 December 2018)

    Searching other resources

    We also used the following strategies:

    • screening of the reference lists of all relevant papers;

    • searching the primary electronic sources of ongoing trials (World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (www.who.int/clinical-trials-registry-platform), ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov));

    • contacting investigators and authors in this field for information on unpublished or incomplete trials.

    All searches included non‐English language literature.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (SN, MR) independently screened the search results by reading titles and abstracts, obtained potentially relevant studies in full text, and independently assessed them for inclusion. We resolved disagreements through discussion.

    Data extraction and management

    We used a standardised data extraction form for data in the included papers. Data extracted included study design, study characteristics (country, recruitment modality, study funding), participant characteristics (number of participants, age, sex, severity of scoliosis at baseline), experimental and comparison interventions description, co‐interventions, duration of follow‐up, outcomes assessed and results. Two review authors (SM and CA) independently extracted the data. We resolved any disagreements by discussion and consulted a third review author (SN) if disagreements persisted.

    One review author (CA) entered extracted data into Review Manager and a second review author (SM) checked the data for accuracy (RevMan 2023).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (SM, CA) independently assessed the risk of bias using the Cochrane RoB 1 tool and the criteria recommended by the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). The recommended approach for assessing the risk of bias is a two‐part tool, addressing the specific domains (i.e. sequence generation and allocation concealment (selection bias), blinding of participants and providers (performance bias), blinding of outcome assessor (detection bias), incomplete outcome data (attrition bias), and selective outcome reporting (reporting bias)). The first part of the tool describes what was reported to have happened in the study. The second part consists of judging the risk of bias for each domain as low, high, or unclear risk. See Appendix 1 for details.

    We addressed the domains of sequence generation, allocation concealment (avoidance of selection bias), incomplete outcome data and selective outcome reporting by a single entry for each study.

    We considered blinding of participants, personnel, and outcome assessors (avoidance of performance bias and detection bias) separately for objective outcomes (Cobb angle, ATR, and objective surface measurements) and subjective outcomes (subjective perception, QoL, disability, mental health, and pain).

    Performance bias for subjective outcomes, we judged at high risk in the unblinded studies. For objective outcomes, knowledge of the intervention provided by the therapist and knowledge of the intervention received by the participant cannot modify their behaviour in a way that can modify the progression of the curve (Cobb angle, ATR, objective measurement of waist asymmetry). The only 'co‐intervention' that could have modified the objective outcome was starting to wear a brace during the study and outside the study protocol, but this did not happen in any of the included studies; therefore, we judged performance bias at low risk in unblinded studies.

    Detection bias for subjective outcomes: we judged high risk in the unblinded studies. For objective outcomes, knowledge of the intervention provided by the evaluator cannot influence the progression of the curve as measured through objective systems such as ATR (measurement through a goniometer of the back prominence in forward bending) and instrumental measure of waist asymmetry. Therefore, we judged detection bias for subjective outcomes at low risk in unblinded studies. Contrarily, the Cobb angle, albeit objective in principle, depends on the subjective interpretation of the radiographic image. As such, it could be interpreted in a more or less favourable way, and it is subject to detection bias. Therefore, we judged detection bias at high risk for this measure in unblinded studies.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed dichotomous outcomes by calculating the risk ratio (RR) for each trial, with the uncertainty in each result being expressed by 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We analysed continuous outcomes by calculating the mean difference (MD) with 95% CI when the studies used the same instrument for assessing the outcome. We used the standardised mean difference (SMD) when the studies used different instruments.

    Unit of analysis issues

    In the case of multi‐arm studies, we combined all the relevant experimental or control groups into a single group to avoid double‐counting of participants.

    Dealing with missing data

    We included all randomised participants in the statistical analysis. In case of missing data, we contacted study authors.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We analysed statistical heterogeneity using the I² statistic and the Chi² test. We interpreted the heterogeneity as follows: 0% to 40% might not be important; 30% to 60% may represent moderate heterogeneity; 50% to 75% may represent substantial heterogeneity; and 75% to 100% represents considerable heterogeneity. We also considered a P value lower than 0.10 for the Chi² test for heterogeneity as substantial heterogeneity (Higgins 2020).

    We assessed clinical diversity by analysing comparability amongst studies in factor such as age, country/region, and severity of scoliosis at baseline.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to visually inspect funnel plots (plots of the effect estimate from each study against the effect standard error) to indicate possible publication bias if at least 10 studies were included in the meta‐analysis. We also investigated publication bias by checking the protocol registries and the relative publication.

    We assessed outcome reporting bias by checking trial protocols against published reports within the RoB 1 tool (reporting bias domain).

    Data synthesis

    We undertook meta‐analyses only where this was meaningful, that is, if the treatments, participants, and the underlying clinical question were similar enough for pooling to be meaningful using a random‐effects model because we expected a certain degree of heterogeneity between trials. We considered not pooling the data if the clinical or statistical heterogeneity between trials was too high (i.e. 75% to 100%).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned subgroup analyses to explore the effects of the following variables: age (15 years or under and greater than 15 years), bone age (Risser 0 to 2 and Risser 3 to 5), Cobb angle (severe scoliosis (Cobb angle greater than 45°), medium scoliosis (Cobb angle 20° to 44°), low scoliosis (Cobb angle less than 19°)), and type of exercise.

    Sensitivity analysis

    To incorporate our assessment of the risk of bias in the review process, we first plotted the intervention effect estimates stratified by risk of bias for allocation concealment (selection bias). If differences in the results were present amongst studies at different risks of selection bias, we planned to perform sensitivity analysis by excluding studies at high risk of selection bias from the analysis.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created four summary of findings tables reporting the following major outcomes: Cobb angle, ATR, number of participants with progression over 5° Cobb, brace or surgery prescribed, cosmetic issue: objective surface measurements, subjective evaluation, and QoL. We used the five GRADE considerations (study limitations, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the body of evidence related to the studies that contributed data to the meta‐analyses for the prespecified outcomes (GRADE 2004). We used methods and recommendations described in Chapter 14 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions using GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT; Schünemann 2022). We justified all decisions to downgrade the certainty of the evidence using footnotes and made comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review where necessary.

    We used the following criteria for downgrading: for the risk of bias domain, we considered the contribution (i.e. 'weight') of the study to the pooled estimate. If most of the weight (greater than 50%) came from studies at high risk of selection or attrition bias, we downgraded (Schünemann 2022). For the imprecision domain, dichotomous outcomes, we evaluated whether the sample size or number of events did not meet the optimal information size and whether the CIs of the pooled estimate included large benefits and large harms; for continuous outcomes, we applied the 'rules of thumb' of 400 participants as the cut‐off for downgrading (Schünemann 2022). For the indirectness domain, we considered whether the results may not be fully generalisable to all countries/settings/participants. For the inconsistency domain, we considered the statistical test for heterogeneity (I2 statistic), the overlap of CIs, and the similarity of point estimates. We considered downgrading if the I2 statistic was substantial and if the point estimates of individual studies were in different directions. For the publication bias domain, we looked at whether the funnel plot showed asymmetry, suggesting the possibility of publication bias (Schünemann 2022).

    The GRADE system uses the following criteria for assigning grades of evidence.

    • High certainty: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate certainty: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low certainty: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited: the true effect may differ substantially from the effect estimate.

    • Very low certainty: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    Participants

    The mean age ranged from 12 to 15 years, and the percentage of females in the trials ranged from 50% to 100%. We found clinical heterogeneity amongst the participants included in the studies.

    One study included only participants with severe scoliosis (Cobb angle greater than 45°, candidates for surgery) (dos Santos Alves 2014). Two studies considered medium‐degree curves (25° to 40° Cobb (Gao 2019) and 20° to 40° Cobb (Zheng 2018)); Dickson 1978 considered a medium degree of 41° Cobb; five studies considered low degrees (10° to 30° Cobb) (Monticone 2014; Kocaman 2021; Moubarak 2022; Qi 2022; Sun 2022), Schreiber 2015 considered low and medium degrees (10° to 45° Cobb), and Kuru 2016 considered all spectrum degrees (10° to 60° Cobb). Wan 2005 and Kim 2016 did not report the inclusion criteria for Cobb angle, but the mean was 25° in both studies, with a standard deviation (SD) of 2° and 13° Cobb, respectively.

    Five studies did not use the Risser score as an inclusion criterion and did not report it (Dickson 1978; dos Santos Alves 2014; Kim 2016; Qi 2022; Wan 2005). Monticone 2014 included adolescents with Risser less than 2; Gao 2019, Moubarak 2022, and Zheng 2018 Risser 0 to 2; Kuru 2016 and Kocaman 2021 Risser 0 to 3; and Schreiber 2015 and Sun 2022 Risser 0 to 5.

    One study included both participants already wearing and not wearing a brace (Schreiber 2015).

    Treatment regimens and settings

    The exercises proposed in the intervention, and sometimes in the control groups, were not the same. PSSEs included two approaches: Scientific Exercises Approach to Scoliosis (SEAS) and Schroth. Both approaches have the basis of PSSEs: self‐correction, stabilisation, training of activities of daily life, and education (Berdishevsky 2016; Weiss 2006a).

    The SEAS acronym reflects the changing nature with new evidence (Romano 2015). However, all the studies included in this review were performed in the same few years, presumably without specific methodological changes. Currently, SEAS requires active self‐correction performed without external aids. Active stabilisation and balance exercises of progressive difficulty challenge self‐correction to increase the neuromotor control of its performance and train a different postural behaviour (Smania 2008). Three studies included a SEAS exercises intervention group (Gao 2019; Monticone 2014; Zheng 2018). The classical protocol requires performing SEAS exercises 15 minutes per day with 1.5‐hour physiotherapy sessions performed once a month, including a cognitive‐behavioural approach to support the whole treatment (Romano 2015). Zheng 2018 proposed the classical protocol, while Gao 2019 and Monticone 2014 added physiotherapist supervision during group sessions once a week.

    Five studies included Schroth exercises in the intervention group (Kim 2016; Kocaman 2021; Kuru 2016; Schreiber 2015; Sun 2022). Schroth looks for the best possible self‐correction within one or two exercises, including the help of external aids (cushions, sticks) repeated throughout all sessions, usually lasting one hour daily. Isometric strengthening and posture maintenance in self‐correction trains the stabilisation function. The protocol includes intensive physiotherapy, classically for three to four weeks as an inpatient (Weiss 2011), but lately, also outpatient courses to teach the exercises (Rigo 2008). Kim 2016 did not specify the regimen, while Schreiber 2015 described an intensive outpatient individual training protocol of five one‐hour sessions over two weeks and Kuru 2016 described an intensive outpatient individual training protocol of 18 sessions of 1.5 hours over eight weeks. Participants regularly performed Schroth PSSEs autonomously at home: 30 to 45 minutes daily for Schreiber 2015; three times a week for Kim 2016, Kocaman 2021, and Kuru 2016; 60 minutes for Moubarak 2022; and 90 minutes for Qi 2022.

    Schreiber 2015 required group physiotherapist supervision once a week, Kuru 2016 compared PSSEs with and without caregivers' supervision, and Kim 2016 did not specify.

    Some intervention groups included GTEs (Dickson 1978; dos Santos Alves 2014; Qi 2022; Wan 2005). In Wan 2005, the experimental group of participants performed exercises with unilateral muscle contraction. dos Santos Alves 2014 proposed a preoperative aerobic treadmill or stationary bike (60% to 80% maximum heart rate) protocol, including 60‐minute physiotherapist‐supervised sessions, three times a week for four months. Finally, Kim 2016, Kocaman 2021, Moubarak 2022, and Monticone 2014 studied comparison groups that included GTEs. Kim 2016 compared interventions to 60‐minute Pilates exercises (spinal correction, core‐strengthening, and balance), and Monticone 2014, Moubarak 2022, and Kocaman 2021 to general spinal exercises, including mobilisation, strengthening, stretching, balance, and walking.

    Other interventions included bracing, traction, and electrostimulation. Dickson 1978 proposed Cotrel tractions (fixed component between the head and the pelvis and an auto‐elongation system connecting the foot to the occipital piece), practised autonomously by participants during the day. They used fixed traction at night and gradually increased weights from five to 12 pounds throughout the seven nights. Wan 2005 applied sequentially lateral electrostimulation, which increased gradually from 30 minutes three to eight hours per day, followed by mandibular‐pelvic traction 30 minutes twice daily. During treatment, participants were advised to maintain a straight, symmetrical posture during the day. Zheng 2018 used a rigid thoracolumbosacral orthosis (TLSO) prescribed for 23 hours and used an average of 13.5 hours per day.     The treatment times had a wide range, between 12 and 42 months. One study had a follow‐up of five weeks (Dickson 1978).     Allocation

    Random sequence generation

    We judged eight studies (61%) at low risk of bias (Gao 2019; Kocaman 2021; Kuru 2016; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022; Schreiber 2015; Sun 2022; Zheng 2018). The study authors did not report any information about methods of random sequence generation in five studies, so we judged them at unclear risk of bias.

    Allocation concealment

    We judged six studies (46%) at low risk of bias (Gao 2019; Kuru 2016; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022; Schreiber 2015; Zheng 2018). The authors did not report any information about allocation concealment methods in seven studies, so we judged them at an unclear risk of bias.

    Blinding

    Performance bias

    Subjective outcomes: we judged all studies at high risk of bias as the type of intervention assessed prevented any blinding. Five studies did not assess any subjective outcomes (Dickson 1978; Gao 2019; Kim 2016; Sun 2022; Wan 2005).

    Objective outcomes: we judged all studies at low risk of bias, though blinding was not possible, as the outcomes were unlikely to be biased by lack of blinding. One study did not assess any objective outcomes (dos Santos Alves 2014).

    Detection bias

    Subjective outcomes: we judged all studies at high risk of detection bias. Five studies did not assess any subjective outcomes (Dickson 1978; Gao 2019; Kim 2016; Sun 2022; Wan 2005).

    Objective outcomes: we judged six studies at low risk of bias (Gao 2019; Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022; Schreiber 2015; Sun 2022), and six at high risk of bias. One study did not assess any objective outcomes (dos Santos Alves 2014).

    Incomplete outcome data

    We judged 10 studies (77%) at low risk of bias and two studies at high risk (Sun 2022; Zheng 2018). One study did not provide information and was judged at unclear risk of bias (Dickson 1978).

    Selective reporting

    We judged two studies at low risk of bias because the protocol was available, and the article reported all the outcomes listed in the protocol (Schreiber 2015; Zheng 2018). All the remaining studies were judged at unclear risk because the protocol was unavailable.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We did not identify any other possible sources of bias.

    The GTE group had a mean Cobb angle of 18.2°. We are uncertain whether PSSE makes little or no difference compared to GTE in the Cobb angle (MD 3.0, 95% CI −8.2 to 2.1; 4 studies, 192 participants; very low‐certainty evidence, downgraded one level for imprecision and two levels for inconsistency; Analysis 4.1) (Kim 2016; Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022).

    Angle of trunk rotation

    The GTE group had a mean ATR of 6.5°. PSSE probably reduces ATR compared with GTE, which is clinically meaningful (MD −3.0°, 95% CI −3.4 to −2.5; 2 studies, 138 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence, downgraded one level for imprecision; Analysis 4.2) (Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014).

    Number of participants with progression more than 5° Cobb

    Thirty‐eight out of 100 participants receiving GTE progressed above the clinically meaningful threshold of 5° Cobb versus seven out of 100 participants receiving PSSE. PSSE may reduce the number progressed by more than 5° Cobb angle (RR 0.19, 95% CI −0.67 to 0.52; 1 study, 110 participants; low‐certainty evidence, downgraded two levels for imprecision; Analysis 4.3) (Monticone 2014).

    Number of participants with brace or surgery prescribed

    No studies comparing PSSE versus GTE assessed the number of participants prescribed brace or surgery.

    Cosmetic issues

    Objective surface measurements – waist asymmetry

    No studies comparing PSSE versus GTE assessed waist asymmetry.

    Subjective surface evaluation – SRS‐22 Self‐Image and WRVAS

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of PSSE compared with GTS on subjective surface evaluation (SMD 0.77, 95% CI −0.6 to 2.1; 3 studies, 168 participants; very low‐certainty evidence, downgraded two levels for inconsistency; one level for imprecision; and one level for high‐risk of performance and detection biases; Analysis 4.4) (Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022).

    Other issues

    Quality of life – SRS‐22 Total score

    The GTE group had a mean score of 3.58 on a 1 to 5 scale (where 1 is the worst, and 5 is the best). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of PSSE compared with GTS on QoL (MD 0.26 points, 95% CI −0.11 to 0.62; 3 studies, 168 participants; very low‐certainty evidence, downgraded two levels for inconsistency; one level for imprecision; and one level for high risk of performance and detection biases; Analysis 4.5) (Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022).

    Back pain – SRS‐22 Pain

    We found no difference in back pain between PSSE and GTE (MD 0.04, 95% CI −0.48 to 0.56; 2 studies, 170 participants; Analysis 4.6) (Monticone 2014; Moubarak 2022).

    Mental health – SRS‐22 Mental Health

    We found a difference favouring PSSE when compared to GTE (MD 0.6, 95% CI 0.4 to 0.7; 1 study, 110 participants; Analysis 4.7) (Monticone 2014).

    Adverse effects

    None of the included studies assessed the adverse effects.

    Discussion

    For a long time, TEs have been considered to have no role inAIS treatment (Hresko 2013; Weinstein 2008). However, in the last few years they have progressively gained in popularity, as shown by the increased number of RCTs reported in the current review and their presence in clinical guidelines (Negrini 2008a; Roye 2020). This review presents the existing evidence and highlights some areas for future research.

    Summary of main results

    We included 12 new RCTs in this review update, totalling 13 included RCTs. Four studies compared TE versus no treatment (PSSE: two studies and GTE: two studies). Four studies compared TE plus other non‐surgical treatments versus other non‐surgical treatments (PSSE plus bracing versus bracing alone: two studies, PSSE plus other non‐surgical intervention versus other non‐surgical intervention: one study, GTE plus other non‐surgical intervention (electrostimulation and traction) versus other non‐surgical intervention alone: one study). Two studies compared TE versus other non‐surgical treatments (PSSE versus bracing: one study and GTE versus other non‐surgical intervention (traction): one study). Four studies compared PSSE versus GTE.

    We found only one study that followed up participants at high risk of bracing (Risser 0 to 2, low‐to‐moderate curves) until the end of growth, in this specific case, one year after reaching Risser 3/4 (Monticone 2014). This study found a higher efficacy of PSSE compared to GTE. Pooling these data with all the other studies, we found that PSSE may reduce the number of participants who progressed compared with GTE (low‐certainty evidence).

    Participants undergoing TE may experience slightly better aesthetic perception and QoL than participants not receiving treatment (low‐certainty evidence). At the same time, we are very uncertain about the effect on curve progression. Participants undergoing PSSE plus bracing and GTE plus other non‐surgical interventions may experience less curve progression than participants receiving brace or other non‐surgical interventions alone (low‐certainty evidence). However, the results were clinically meaningful for GTE but not for PSSE.

    We are uncertain about the effect on curve progression in participants undergoing PSSE plus non‐surgical interventions compared with non‐surgical interventions alone (very low‐certainty evidence).

    We are uncertain whether TE alone is less effective than bracing in reducing curve progression in medium‐degree curves (very low‐certainty evidence).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The current state of the evidence has dramatically increased compared to the previous version of this review (Romano 2012). Nevertheless, recent studies show significant clinical variability. While it is possible to pool them for the comparisons performed, the variability weakens the overall reliability of the results.

    The most critical clinical scenarios concern avoiding progression (Cobb degrees) and eventual treatment upgrade to bracing (more demanding for patients) in low‐to‐moderate curves during growth. We investigated them by comparing TE versus no treatment and PSSE versus GTE. The first clinical scenario focuses on controlling curve progression until the end of growth. We found only one study performed in high‐risk patients with outcomes one year after the end of growth that demonstrated benefit of PSSE over GTE (very low‐certainty evidence) (Monticone 2014). The second clinical scenario focuses on delaying progression and eventual bracing. In this case, the pooled very short‐ and short‐term outcomes are also relevant, even if we still need some caution in interpretation because some studies also included participants after the end of growth (Kim 2016; Kuru 2016; Schreiber 2015). The comparison of TE versus no treatment shows a possible improvement (low‐certainty evidence), and the effect is above the 5° Cobb clinically relevant threshold if we consider the possibility of progression. The interpretation of the comparison of PSSE versus GTE is more complicated. Here, we found statistical heterogeneity determined mainly by a single study that showed results in the opposite direction than the others (Moubarak 2022). Moubarak 2022 was the only one in this group to study GTE (versus PSSE) and not PSSE (versus GTE). Rehabilitation research has already discussed some possible interpretations of these results (Arienti 2021; Negrini 2019b). Outcomes could be determined not by the type of treatment but by 1. the treating physiotherapist's competencies in the specific treatment or 2. the treatment researcher preferences (i.e. the studied treatment). The first could be applied to all the studies included in comparing PSSE versus GTE. The latter is particularly relevant for PSSE because they require a specific competence in TE for scoliosis and a cognitive‐behavioural approach to patients (Negrini 2008a). Moubarak 2022 studied GTE and reported that a "qualified physiotherapist" applied PSSE treatment without specifying any expertise in PSSE.

    There are two other highly relevant scenarios related to scoliosis progression. The first one is: does TE improve the efficacy of bracing (i.e. the other effective treatment in the field)? The answer to this question comes from comparing TE added to other treatments versus other treatments. The answer is possibly (low‐certainty evidence); however, the difference may not reach the clinically meaningful threshold in the short term (the only outcome retrieved). The second scenario is: could TE be a substitute for bracing? From the short‐term result of the comparison of TE versus other treatments, the answer is no for moderate‐degree curves (very low‐certainty evidence). Both these scenarios require further studies with adequate follow‐up although the comparison of TE versus bracing suggests that TE could delay the requirement for bracing.

    Another set of clinically relevant outcomes are the impact of therapy on cosmetic issues, QoL, and back pain. We found some results for better aesthetic perception and less back pain in the comparison of TE versus no treatment but between‐group differences for other comparisons and these outcomes were not clinically important.

    While TE may play a role in AIS treatment, studies show significant differences for populations (Cobb degrees and Risser stages) and types of exercise interventions studied (various PSSE and GTE). Moreover, several areas remain to be explored, and the number of studies and participants involved were too few for firm conclusions.

    In this review, we applied a principle typical of treating deformities in adolescents and younger people. In adulthood, scoliosis progression is a secondary outcome, while back pain, QoL, etc. are the primary outcomes. In contrast, scoliosis progression is the most important outcome during the period of growth and treatments for idiopathic scoliosis (and other bone deformities) are proposed to avoid health issues (the classical primary outcomes) in adulthood (between 10 and 60 years later).

    The spine should be straight. We know from prognostic studies that there are consistent and important health issues in adulthood above certain thresholds of curvature (Negrini 2015a). The scoliotic curve should not exist, and the containment and reduction of the Cobb angle is the main aim of treatment. Moreover, Cobb degrees are the main indicators for brace prescription, and their containment allows them to avoid impairing children's life even more by moving from exercises to bracing.

    Another limitation of these results concerns what constitutes a minimally clinically important between‐group difference. Because the main outcomes concern predictors of health issues in adulthood, identifying an appropriate MCID for Cobb degrees, ATR, and TRACE is problematic. MCIDs in this population are not well established.

    Quality of the evidence

    As in the previous review, almost all the outcomes in the included studies were intermediate. However, we did not downgrade for indirectness of outcomes. AIS does not typically cause any health problems during growth. However, if the curvature surpasses 30° Cobb at the end of growth, the risk of health problems in adulthood increases significantly (Negrini 2015a; Negrini 2008a). Issues include a decreased QoL, and increased disability, pain, cosmetic issues, functional limitations, sometimes pulmonary problems, and possible further progression during adulthood (Weinstein 2003). Consequently, treatments are applied to achieve intermediate outcomes that reduce the risk of final outcomes in adulthood.

    Regarding the risk of bias, the major flaw of the included studies was the lack of participant and study personnel blinding. We judged all studies at high risk of performance and detection bias for subjective outcomes such as cosmetic issues, QoL, back pain, psychological problems, and measurement of Cobb angle due to this issue. On the contrary, objective outcomes, such as the progression of scoliosis measured by ATR, are unlikely to be biased by a lack of blinding. We downgraded certainty of the evidence for imprecision for all the outcomes as we included few studies with very small sample sizes in the different comparisons.

    We found important clinical heterogeneity amongst the studies, with some being particularly relevant. One study differed from all the others for the population and outcome considered: dos Santos Alves 2014 had the aim of preparing patients for surgery, including older patients with severe curves only, and reported results on mental health and pain. The most important heterogeneity concerned the comparison of PSSE to GTE. Only one study reported chronological and not bone age, but the mean age of 15.5 years suggests they were after the end of growth (Kim 2016). Monticone 2014, Kocaman 2021, and Moubarak 2022 were comparable for bone age (Risser score 0 to 2) and Cobb degrees (low‐to‐moderate curves) but had other important differences. Kocaman 2021 and Moubarak 2022 presented very short‐term outcomes, while Monticone 2014 followed up participants one year after the end of growth. Kocaman 2021 and Moubarak 2022 considered core‐stability exercises, while Monticone 2014 included any GTE. Two studies determined whether PSSE was superior to GTE (Kocaman 2021; Monticone 2014), while Moubarak 2022 determined whether GTE was superior to PSSE. Indeed, Moubarak 2022 contrasted the two essential components of PSSE: active self‐correction and stability exercises. They are usually combined in the treatment, and while active‐self‐correction is considered mandatory to have PSSE, in some PSSE programmes, stability exercises are based on principles different from the core stability exercises. For this reason, we classified the latter group of Moubarak 2022 as GTE and the active self‐correction as PSSE. Nevertheless, this study seems to suggest that core stability exercises can work better than active self‐correction when performed as a stand‐alone exercise. This could open new perspectives in considering PSSEs, but further studies are needed. Of note, in Moubarak 2022, the treating physiotherapist was not reported to have a specific competence in PSSE. These between‐study differences indicate caution is needed in concluding that there is no overall difference in effectiveness between PSSE and GTE.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The strength of the review is the extensive and comprehensive searches conducted, including many different sources in many languages. However, we cannot rule out the risk of publication bias as funnel plots were not possible because we had too few studies included in each meta‐analysis.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We found 13 systematic reviews. Twelve concluded in favour of TE (Burger 2019; Dunn 2018; Fan 2020; Fusco 2011; Gou 2021; Lenssinck 2005; Li 2021; Lopez‐Torres 2021; Negrini 2003; Negrini 2008b; Plaszewski 2014; Thompson 2019), with one exception (Mordecai 2012). Mordecai 2012 did not include Wan 2005 and identified only non‐randomised trials favouring TE. They proposed that the authors of the papers were experts on TE and consequently biassed. As a result, they considered their systematic review unbiased and concluded that TE was ineffective for AIS.

    In our updated systematic review, we studied all the relevant primary RCTs included in the other systematic reviews. Apart from the reviews focusing only on one specific technique of PSSE (Burger 2019) or GTE (Gou 2021; Li 2021; Lopez‐Torres 2021), the most significant difference between the other systematic reviews was judging some RCTs for inclusion/exclusion criteria.

    Contrary to Gamiz‐Bermudez 2021 and Thompson 2019, we excluded Toledo 2011 because the authors studied functional and not idiopathic scoliosis. Idiopathic scoliosis is a structural pathology causing a morphological change of vertebrae; functional scoliosis is not pathological, and there are specific tests to rule it out (Negrini 2008a). Including healthy children with functional scoliosis could significantly alter the results, favouring TE efficacy. We also noted another significant difference with Gamiz‐Bermudez 2021. The authors included one study (Dickson 1978), which we did not include in our meta‐analysis. While proposed exercises were performed to control scoliosis evolution in all the other studies, Dickson 1978 aimed to determine if flexibility increased through exercises, allowing them to achieve the best possible correction during the subsequent cast treatment. Moreover, the population was different (very high‐degree curves), as well as the methods used (bending and in‐cast radiographs to evaluate the curve flexibility, instead of standing radiographs to evaluate the deformity). Hence, including this trial in our pooled analysis may have had an impact on the results.

    Compared to Gamiz‐Bermudez 2021 and Thompson 2019, we also excluded Gür 2017 because the authors compared two GTE groups. Gamiz‐Bermudez 2021 included Gür 2017 because it classified the comparison group called "rehabilitation" as "any other treatment"; nevertheless, it included exercises, so we classified it as GTE. Thompson 2019 included Gür 2017 using our previous definition of PSSE as "specific movements performed with a therapeutic aim of reducing the deformity" (Romano 2012). Since we needed to distinguish between PSSE and GTE in the current systematic review, we improved the definition (see Types of interventions). Consequently, Gür 2017 and Diab 2012 were classified as studies comparing two types of GTE and Kumar 2017 as a study comparing two types of PSSE. For this reason, all these studies were excluded from the current systematic review.
