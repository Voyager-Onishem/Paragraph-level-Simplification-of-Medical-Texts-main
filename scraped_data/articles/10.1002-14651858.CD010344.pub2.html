Pharmacological interventions for the acute management of hyperkalaemia in adults

  • Josh Batterink
  • Tara A Cessford

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Hyperkalaemia is a potentially life‐threatening electrolyte disturbance which may lead to cardiac arrhythmias and death. Renal replacement therapy is known to be effective in treating hyperkalaemia, but safe and effective pharmacological interventions are needed to prevent dialysis or avoid the complications of hyperkalaemia until dialysis is performed.

    Objectives

    This review looked at the benefits and harms of pharmacological treatments used in the acute management of hyperkalaemia in adults. This review evaluated the therapies that reduce serum potassium as well as those that prevent complications of hyperkalaemia.

    Search methods

    We searched Cochrane Kidney and Transplant's Specialised Register to 18 August 2015 through contact with the Trials' Search Co‐ordinator using search terms relevant to this review.

    Selection criteria

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs looking at any pharmacological intervention for the acute management of hyperkalaemia in adults were included in this review. Non‐standard study designs such as cross‐over studies were also included. Eligible studies enrolled adults (aged 18 years and over) with hyperkalaemia, defined as serum potassium concentration ≥ 4.9 mmol/L, to receive pharmacological therapy to reduce serum potassium or to prevent arrhythmias. Patients with artificially induced hyperkalaemia were excluded from this review.

    Data collection and analysis

    All three authors screened titles and abstracts, and data extraction and risk of bias assessment was performed independently by at least two authors. Studies reported in non‐English language journals were translated before assessment. Authors were contacted when information about results or study methodology was missing from the original publication. Although we planned to group all studies of a particular pharmacological therapy regardless of administration route or dose for analysis, we were unable to conduct meta‐analyses because of the small numbers of studies evaluating any given treatment. For continuous data we reported mean difference (MD) and 95% confidence intervals (CI).

    Main results

    We included seven studies (241 participants) in this review. Meta‐analysis of these seven included studies was not possible due to heterogeneity of the treatments and because many of the studies did not provide sufficient statistical information with their results. Allocation and blinding methodology was poorly described in most studies.

    No study evaluated the efficacy of pharmacological interventions for preventing clinically relevant outcomes such as mortality and cardiac arrhythmias; however there is evidence that several commonly used therapies effectively reduce serum potassium levels. Salbutamol administered via either nebulizer or metered‐dose inhaler (MDI) significantly reduced serum potassium compared with placebo. The peak effect of 10 mg nebulised salbutamol was seen at 120 minutes (MD ‐1.29 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐1.64 to ‐0.94) and at 90 minutes for 20 mg nebulised salbutamol (1 study: MD ‐1.18 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐1.54 to ‐0.82). One study reported 1.2 mg salbutamol via MDI 1.2 mg produced a significant decrease in serum potassium beginning at 10 minutes (MD ‐0.20 mmol/L, P < 0.05) and a maximal decrease at 60 minutes (MD ‐0.34 mmol/L, P < 0.0001). Intravenous (IV) and nebulised salbutamol produced comparable effects (2 studies). When compared to other interventions, salbutamol had similar effect to insulin‐dextrose (2 studies) but was more effective than bicarbonate at 60 minutes (MD ‐0.46 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐0.82 to ‐0.10; 1 study). Insulin‐dextrose was more effective than IV bicarbonate (1 study) and aminophylline (1 study). Insulin‐dextrose, bicarbonate and aminophylline were not studied in any placebo‐controlled studies. None of the included studies evaluated the effect of IV calcium or potassium binding resins in the treatment of hyperkalaemia.

    Authors' conclusions

    Evidence for the acute pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia is limited, with no clinical studies demonstrating a reduction in adverse patient outcomes. Of the studied agents, salbutamol via any route and IV insulin‐dextrose appear to be most effective at reducing serum potassium. There is limited evidence to support the use of other interventions, such as IV sodium bicarbonate or aminophylline. The effectiveness of potassium binding resins and IV calcium salts has not been tested in RCTs and requires further study before firm recommendations for clinical practice can be made.

    • Pharmacological interventions for the acute management of hyperkalaemia in adults

    Hyperkalaemia is a serious medical problem that may cause heart problems or death. There are several treatments that can be used to treat hyperkalaemia but the risks and benefits of each intervention are poorly understood. We performed this review to determine whether treatments for hyperkalaemia are effective and safe

    We searched for all studies that tested whether therapies were effective and safe at treating high potassium published up to 18 August 2015. We found seven studies that investigated drug therapies for treating hyperkalaemia in adults which together included results from 241 participants. Most studies tested the therapies in male and female adults with kidney problems who were medically stable. We did not find any studies that looked at the serious medical complications of high potassium such as death.

    We found that salbutamol and insulin‐dextrose were effective in reducing potassium levels in the blood. We found that salbutamol was effective whether it was given intravenously or by nebuliser. Those treatments appeared to be more effective than other treatments such as sodium bicarbonate and aminophylline. None of the studies found any serious adverse reactions to the medications.

    Overall, the quality of evidence was assessed as poor because the studies were small in size and the methods for how the studies were performed were not well described. None of the studies looked at the serious problems caused by hyperkalaemia and this limited the strength of the evidence.

    • Implications for practice

    Salbutamol given via nebuliser, MDI or IV produces a statistically significant reduction in serum potassium, but there are no clinical studies demonstrating a reduction in adverse patient outcomes. IV insulin‐dextrose appears to be as effective as salbutamol at reducing serum potassium. Despite a lack of more robust evidence of clinical benefit from highly powered RCTs, the reduction in serum potassium achieved with salbutamol or IV insulin‐dextrose is of conceivable clinical relevance, thus we support the continued use of these agents as first line therapy in the acute management of hyperkalaemia. This is in accordance with current clinical practice guidelines and reviews (Alfonzo 2014; Hollander‐Rodriguez 2006; McVeigh 2014).

    IV sodium bicarbonate appears less effective than either salbutamol or insulin‐dextrose. In the absence of severe acidosis, we feel there is insufficient evidence to justify the routine use of bicarbonate in the acute management of hyperkalaemia. Aminophylline appears less effective than insulin‐dextrose.

    The combination of IV insulin‐dextrose with salbutamol is more effective than either therapy alone and may be useful in the setting of severe hyperkalaemia. Triple therapy with insulin‐dextrose, salbutamol and bicarbonate was not significantly better than dual therapy.

    There were no RCTs found evaluating IV calcium for the treatment of hyperkalaemia; however, withholding this therapy in patients with severe hyperkalaemia or ECG changes or both given the potentially life‐threatening consequences is not advised.

    There are no RCTs evaluating potassium‐binding resins (e.g. sodium polystyrene sulphonate) therefore we cannot comment on their use at this time. Published clinical practice guidelines and reviews (Alfonzo 2014; Hollander‐Rodriguez 2006; McVeigh 2014) suggest using resins for mild to moderate hyperkalaemia. Only one clinical practice guideline (Alfonzo 2014) comments on the lack of evidence for using resins.

    Implications for research

    Due to ethical concerns, we may never demonstrate in a RCT that acute management of hyperkalaemia reduces patient morbidity, mortality or both.

    Clinical studies should seek to identify novel agents with greater efficacy than our current treatment options, in terms of magnitude and rate of serum potassium reduction. Alternatively, studies might demonstrate that novel agents have a complimentary, adjunctive role in the tiered management of hyperkalaemia. Studies should ensure that novel agents produce non‐inferior or superior clinical outcomes such as arrhythmia or death when compared to standard treatment.

    Further studies are needed to confirm the efficacy of potassium binding resins in order to clarify their role in the tiered approach to therapy.

    Background

    available in
  • English
  • Español

    • Description of the condition

    Hyperkalaemia is defined as an excess concentration of potassium ions in the extracellular fluid compartment. Hyperkalaemia occurs in both outpatients and hospitalised patients, often due to kidney failure, insulin deficiency, direct tissue trauma, or drugs and disorders that inhibit the renin‐angiotensin‐aldosterone axis. Patients with hyperkalaemia may experience muscle weakness, fatigue, distal paraesthesia and respiratory depression (Freeman 1993). The most feared consequence of hyperkalaemia is cardiac arrhythmias. Electrocardiogram (ECG) changes include peaked T waves, QRS widening and diminished P waves. Severe hyperkalaemia may lead to a sine wave ECG pattern, ventricular tachycardia, ventricular fibrillation and death (Browning 1981). The urgency of treatment varies with the cause, presence or absence of associated signs and symptoms, and the rate of rise in serum potassium.

    Description of the intervention

    The pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia is conventionally divided into three distinct tiers of therapy, each with a different mechanism of action (Alfonzo 2014). Patients with severe hyperkalaemia, a serum potassium level ≥ 6.5 mmol/L with or without ECG changes (Alfonzo 2014), first receive intravenous (IV) calcium salts to stabilize the cardiac membrane and prevent fatal arrhythmias even in the absence of any effect on serum potassium levels (Greenberg 1998; Schwartz 1978). This affords the physician time to initiate second tier therapy, pharmacological agents that shift potassium from the extracellular to the intracellular space. IV bicarbonate lowers the serum hydrogen ion concentration, thereby promoting an intracellular shift of potassium ions in exchange for hydrogen. Beta‐2 agonists and insulin enhance the activity of the Na‐K‐ATPase pump in skeletal muscle and drive potassium intracellularly (Williams 1985; Zierler 1987). Finally, third tier therapy definitively treats the problem by eliminating potassium from the body. Diuretics may promote excretion of potassium into the urine and cation exchange resins are thought to bind potassium in the intestinal tract and remove it from the body via faecal excretion (Berlyne 1966; Johnson 1976).

    Dialysis effectively treats hyperkalaemia in patients with kidney impairment, but the evidence for renal replacement therapy (RRT) will not be discussed in detail in this review. The decision to initiate dialysis depends on the severity and aetiology of the hyperkalaemia, the safety and effectiveness of alternate therapies, as well as availability of dialysis (Evans 2005; Putcha 2007). Pharmacological interventions are used to prevent the need for dialysis or to prevent complications until dialysis can be performed. In this review, the need for dialysis will be treated as an outcome rather than a treatment (Carvalhana 2006).

    Although the evidence for RRT is outside the scope of this review, it is worth noting that dialysis is generally considered the definitive treatment for hyperkalaemia. Conventional haemodialysis causes the most rapid decrease in serum potassium levels. Other forms of dialysis (peritoneal dialysis, continuous venovenous haemofiltration or continuous venovenous haemodialysis) are also effective. The amount of potassium removed during dialysis depends on dialysis duration, dialysate potassium concentration, dialyser membrane, and patient characteristics such as baseline serum potassium (Evans 2005).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Hyperkalaemia is a commonly encountered, potentially life‐threatening clinical problem that requires prompt and effective treatment to prevent serious complications. This review summarises and synthesises the literature published on the acute pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia to help clinicians best treat their patients. This review is a derivative of a Cochrane systematic literature review first published in 2005 (Clase 2005), but has narrowed the focus to the effectiveness and safety of the acute pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia in adults.

    Methods

    available in

    • Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (RCTs in which allocation to treatment was obtained by alternation, use of alternate medical records, date of birth or other predictable methods) looking at any pharmacological intervention for the acute management of hyperkalaemia in adults were included. Non‐standard study designs such as cross‐over studies were also included in the review.

    Types of participants

    Inclusion criteria

    The study population of this review was adults (aged 18 years and over) with hyperkalaemia receiving pharmacological therapy to reduce serum potassium or to prevent arrhythmias. Hyperkalaemia was defined as serum potassium concentration ≥ 4.9 mmol/L. Studies were included when it was clear that the majority of study participants met this definition. Patients with a variety of causes of hyperkalaemia, including both acute and chronic kidney failure, were included in the study.

    Exclusion criteria

    Patients with artificially induced hyperkalaemia were excluded from this review.

    Types of interventions

    All pharmacological therapies used in the short‐term management of hyperkalaemia were considered, including interventions used to reduce serum potassium as well as therapies used to prevent arrhythmias.

    Studies where interventions were compared with placebo or another pharmacological therapy were included. Studies comparing a pharmacological intervention with dialysis were not included. Studies were included regardless of the route of administration or dose of the pharmacological therapy. For the initial analysis, all studies of a particular pharmacological therapy, regardless of dose or route of administration, were analysed together.

    The source of a blood sample for potassium measurement (venous or arterial) can introduce additional variation (Johnston 2005). Ideally, all studies would report potassium measurements from the same source, but if this was not the case, potassium levels were preferentially used from whichever source was more common.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched Cochrane Kidney and Transplant's Specialised Register up to 18 August 2015 through contact with the Trials' Search Co‐ordinator using search terms relevant to this review. The Specialised Register contains studies identified from the following sources.

    1. Quarterly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials CENTRAL

    2. Weekly searches of MEDLINE OVID SP

    3. Handsearching of kidney‐related journals and the proceedings of major kidney conferences

    4. Searching of the current year of EMBASE OVID SP

    5. Weekly current awareness alerts for selected kidney journals

    6. Searches of the International Clinical Trials Register (ICTRP) Search Portal and ClinicalTrials.gov.

    Studies contained in the Specialised Register were identified through search strategies for CENTRAL, MEDLINE, and EMBASE based on the scope of Cochrane Kidney and Transplant. Details of these strategies as well as a list of handsearched journals, conference proceedings and current awareness alerts are available in the Specialised Register section of information about the Cochrane Kidney and Transplant.

    See Appendix 1 for search terms used in strategies for this review.

    Searching other resources

    1. Reference lists of review articles, relevant studies and clinical practice guidelines.

    2. Letters to investigators seeking information about unpublished or incomplete studies known to be involved in previous studies.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    The search methods described were used to obtain titles and abstracts of studies that may be relevant to the review. The titles and abstracts were screened independently by three authors who discarded studies that were not applicable. Studies and reviews that might have included relevant data or information were retained for consideration. Three authors independently assessed the retrieved abstracts and full texts, if necessary, to determine which studies satisfied the inclusion criteria.

    Data extraction and management

    Three authors were involved in the data extraction process. Data extraction was carried out independently by at least two authors using a standard data extraction form. Studies reported in non‐English language journals were translated before assessment. Where more than one publication of a study existed, reports were grouped together and the report with the most complete data was used in the analyses. Where relevant outcomes were only published in less complete versions, these data were used. Any discrepancy between published reports was highlighted. If information about results or study methodology was missing from the original publication, authors were contacted for additional information.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    The following items were independently assessed by at least two authors using the risk of bias assessment tool (Higgins 2011) (see Appendix 2).

    • Was there adequate sequence generation (selection bias)?

    • Was allocation adequately concealed (selection bias)?

    • Was knowledge of the allocated interventions adequately prevented during the study?

      • Participants and personnel (performance bias)

      • Outcome assessors (detection bias)

    • Were incomplete outcome data adequately addressed (attrition bias)?

    • Are reports of the study free of suggestion of selective outcome reporting (reporting bias)?

    • Was the study apparently free of other problems that could put it at a risk of bias?

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous outcomes (death, arrhythmia, ECG changes), results were to be expressed as risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI). Where continuous scales of measurement were used to assess the effects of treatment (serum potassium), the mean difference (MD) was used, or the standardised mean difference (SMD) if different scales had been used. Change scores were to be used if results were not available as another type and these were combined with other results using the MD. If studies did not report the within study variance for continuous outcomes we had planned to impute the standard deviation using the average standard deviation from other studies. Because of the small number of studies with complete statistical information and comparing similar interventions imputation was not possible.

    All studies of a particular pharmacological therapy were to be analysed together regardless of route of administration or dose. We reported doses and routes of administration separately because there were too few studies to perform a meta‐analysis. We intended to perform subgroup analyses to identify any effect of route or dose, but there were not enough studies with sufficient statistical data looking at the same intervention.

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cross‐over studies were available for inclusion in this review and we had planned to include the paired results in the analysis using the generic inverse‐variance method or if studies did not report the necessary data for paired analysis to impute the missing standard deviations. If that was not possible we planned to include the results in the analysis as unpaired data and a sensitivity analysis was to be performed to assess the effect of including data in this manner. Because we were unable to perform a meta‐analysis, and because paired data were not available we included the first phase of cross‐over studies as unpaired data, but sensitivity analysis was not performed.

    For studies with multiple interventions comparing a single pharmacological therapy given by a different route or dose to a control group, we had planned to combine all relevant groups into a single group. Because there were insufficient data to perform a meta‐analysis we included the groups separately.

    Dealing with missing data

    Any further information required from the original author was requested by written correspondence, and any relevant information obtained in this manner was to be included in the review. The number of screened and randomised patients, as well as intention‐to‐treat, as‐treated and per‐protocol populations were carefully evaluated. Attrition rates, including drop‐outs, losses to follow‐up and withdrawals, were investigated. We considered the impact of missing data on study results and critically appraised imputation methods employed by the authors.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to identify heterogeneity using a Chi² test with N‐1 degrees of freedom and an alpha of 0.10 and if present, quantify the degree of heterogeneity using the I² index with values of 25%, 50% and 75% corresponding to low, medium and high levels of heterogeneity (Higgins 2003). Because we were unable to combine study data we did not assess for heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Funnel plots were to be prepared to assess for the potential existence of small study bias (Higgins 2011). Because we were not able to combine study data we could not perform funnel plots.

    Data synthesis

    Data were to be pooled using the random‐effects model. The fixed‐effect model was also to be used to ensure robustness of the chosen model and susceptibility to outliers. No data synthesis was performed.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Subgroup analysis was to be used to explore possible sources of heterogeneity (e.g. participants, intervention, study quality). Heterogeneity among studies was anticipated to be related to patient age, comorbidities, kidney function, and co‐administration of medications. Heterogeneity among participants was anticipated in relation to patient age, kidney function, aetiology of hyperkalaemia, duration of hyperkalaemia, comorbidities, and co‐administration of medications. Heterogeneity in treatments was thought to potentially apply to prior therapeutic agents used, time to administration and the agent dose, route of administration and duration of therapy. Adverse effects were to be tabulated and assessed using descriptive techniques, because they were thought likely to vary among the different agents. Where possible, the risk difference was to be calculated with a 95% CI for each adverse effect, compared to no treatment or to another agent.

    Subgroup analysis was not possible due to the limited number of included studies.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to perform sensitivity analyses to explore the influence of the following factors on effect size.

    • Repeating the analysis excluding unpublished studies

    • Repeating the analysis taking account of risk of bias

    • Repeating the analysis excluding any very long or large studies to establish how much they influence the results

    • Repeating the analysis excluding studies using the following filters: diagnostic criteria, language of publication, source of funding (industry versus other), and country

    No synthesis was performed so sensitivity analyses were not possible.

    Included studies

    Refer to Characteristics of included studies

    Seven studies (241 participants) were included in this review; five were randomised, parallel group studies (Balanzario 2003; Liou 1994; Mahajan 2001; Mandelberg 1999; Ngugi 1997) and two were cross‐over studies (Allon 1989; Allon 1990). The included studies evaluated many different interventions, but only two studies included a placebo group (Allon 1989; Mandelberg 1999). None of the studies included in this review evaluated the efficacy of pharmacological interventions at preventing clinical outcomes such as mortality and cardiac arrhythmias. All included studies focused on the effect of pharmacological interventions on serum potassium levels.

    None of the included studies evaluated IV calcium or potassium binding resins in the treatment of hyperkalaemia.

    • Allon 1989 compared different doses of nebulised salbutamol with placebo.

    • Allon 1990 studied salbutamol, insulin‐dextrose and a combination of both agents. Results were reported graphically and numerically but the study did not provide sufficient statistical information for meta‐analysis. After being contacted for further information, the study authors kindly provided the serum potassium results (a continuous variable) as final values rather than as the change scores that were reported in the original article.

    • Balanzario 2003 compared salbutamol given via IV or nebuliser and reported their results graphically and numerically. Some of the graphically reported data included statistical measures of variance. The authors were contacted for comprehensive numerical data but no response was received.

    • Liou 1994 compared IV and nebulised salbutamol. The authors excluded all patients who did not respond to therapy (decrease in potassium of at least 0.5 mEq/L). The authors were contacted for the full study results, including data from those patients who were excluded, but no response was received.

    • Mahajan 2001 compared IV aminophylline and IV insulin‐dextrose and reported primarily on the change in plasma potassium. The study reported sufficient information for the results to be included in a meta‐analysis; however this was not performed because no other study looked at aminophylline.

    • Mandelberg 1999 compared salbutamol given by metered‐dose inhaler (MDI) with placebo. The results were not reported with sufficient statistical information for inclusion in a meta‐analysis. This limited our ability to combine the results with Allon 1989. The authors were contacted for additional information but no response was received.

    • Ngugi 1997 compared seven different treatment regimens. Some of the results were missing standard deviations. The authors were contacted for comprehensive results but they were unable to provide us with the data requested.

    The studies included in this review evaluated many different pharmacological interventions and serum potassium was monitored at several time points. We were unable to meta‐analyse the results due to heterogeneity among interventions and timing of outcomes. Furthermore, the study results were typically not reported using standard statistical information necessary for meta‐analysis. For example, standard deviations were not reported for all results in three studies (Balanzario 2003; Mandelberg 1999; Ngugi 1997). We were unable to impute standard deviations for the missing data because there were too few studies. All authors were contacted for additional information on study methodology and results. Additional data was obtained for Allon 1989 and Allon 1990 only. For most studies the results were extracted as serum potassium change scores as that provided the most complete data.

    Excluded studies

    See: Characteristics of excluded studies

    Six studies were excluded for studying normokalaemic patients (Allon 1993; Allon 1995; Allon 1996; De Wolf 1993; Gruy‐Kapral 1998; Wang 1976) and seven because they were not randomised (Blumberg 1998; Kim 1996;Lens 1987; Lens 1989; Mocan 1993; Mustaq 2006; Pancu 2003). Two studies were excluded because they studied chronic rather than acute management of hyperkalaemia (Buysse 2012; Kim 2007a) and one was excluded because it was performed in children (McClure 1994).

    The majority of included studies were assessed as having an unclear risk of bias due to poor methodological reporting. Most studies did not describe allocation methodology or indicate if the study was blinded. Overall, studies mostly reported all specified outcome data; only Liou 1994 demonstrated selective reporting. Mandelberg 1999 was assessed as having a low risk of bias.

    Allocation

    Allocation methodology was poorly reported in most studies. Allon 1989 and Allon 1990 did not report randomisation or allocation concealment methodology but the studies were confirmed to be randomised after discussion with the study authors. Balanzario 2003, Liou 1994 and Mahajan 2001 reported that they randomised the patients into two groups but did not provide any information on how the randomised sequence was generated or how allocation concealment was maintained. Ngugi 1997 applied a restricted randomisation method with permuted blocks. Mandelberg 1999 reported that the randomisation code was held by the study biostatistician.

    Blinding

    Blinding methodology was poorly described in most studies. Four studies (Allon 1990; Balanzario 2003; Liou 1994; Mahajan 2001) did not report blinding. Allon 1990, Balanzario 2003 and Liou 1994 compared nebulised and IV medications and did not report use of a double‐dummy technique, so it was unlikely that the studies were blinded. Allon 1989 was reported to be double‐blind with nurses and patients blinded but not physicians. Mandelberg 1999 was double‐blind with both investigators and patients blinded and placebo MDI were used to maintain blinding. Ngugi 1997 was reported to be single‐blind but no additional information was provided. For those studies that did implement blinding, there was no assessment of the maintenance of blinding at the end of the treatment period. Despite the lack of blinding, the effects are likely to be minor due to the objective nature of the outcome.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Four studies (Balanzario 2003; Liou 1994; Mahajan 2001; Mandelberg 1999) did not report any patients withdrawing from the study. Allon 1989 and Allon 1990 both had two patients with missing data due to early termination and neither study described how the missing data was handled. In Ngugi 1997, a parallel group randomised study, 10% of patients were urgently dialysed for treatment of hyperkalaemia and thus withdrew from the study. The authors provided information on when and from which groups these patients left the studies. No patient left the study until after 120 minutes of data had been collected. Two patients from the IV salbutamol group had been started on dialysis prior to the 240 minute data collection. Five more patients were started on dialysis prior to the final 480 minute data collection. One patient withdrew from each from the following groups: insulin‐dextrose, IV salbutamol, bicarbonate‐IV salbutamol, and insulin‐dextrose‐IV salbutamol groups. The authors did not describe how they handled the missing data.

    Selective reporting

    Study protocols were not available for any of the included studies. None of the study reports clearly defined their primary and secondary outcomes, thereby making it difficult to assess for selective reporting in most studies.

    Liou 1994 excluded patients from their final analysis who did not have a maximal decrease in potassium of at least 0.5 mmol/L. This resulted in 6/18 patients in the IV salbutamol group and 4/16 patients in the nebulised salbutamol group being excluded. These exclusions result in a biased estimate of treatment effect and are a serious flaw in the study design. The authors of the study were contacted but no response was received. Three studies (Balanzario 2003; Mandelberg 1999; Ngugi 1997) did not report the standard deviations for several of the potassium outcomes making them impossible to meta‐analyse.

    Other potential sources of bias

    No other sources of bias were identified.

    Effects of interventions

    Salbutamol versus placebo

    Salbutamol was compared to placebo in two studies (Allon 1989; Mandelberg 1999) but could not be meta‐analysed because standard deviations for the placebo group were not available for Mandelberg 1999.

    Allon 1989 reported significantly greater reduction in serum potassium with 10 mg and 20 mg nebulised salbutamol compared with placebo. The peak effect of 10 mg nebulised salbutamol was seen at 120 minutes (Analysis 1.1.4: MD ‐1.29 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐1.64 to ‐0.94) and at 60 and 90 minutes for 20 mg nebulised salbutamol (Analysis 2.1.2; Analysis 2.1.3) (60 minutes: MD ‐‐1.16 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐1.53 to ‐0.79; 90 minutes: MD ‐1.18 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐1.54 to ‐0.82). Mandelberg 1999 reported a significant increase in serum potassium with 1.2 mg salbutamol via MDI at 1 minute (MD 0.12 mmol/L, P < 0.05) but subsequent significant decreases in serum potassium beginning at 10 minutes (MD ‐0.20 mmol/L, P < 0.05) and a maximal decrease at 60 minutes (MD ‐0.34 mmol/L, P < 0.0001).

    Both Allon 1989 and Mandelberg 1999 found that inhaled salbutamol increased heart rate in exposed patients. In Allon 1989 only minimal side effects were reported. Mandelberg 1999 mentioned they would record known side effects of salbutamol but no side effects were mentioned (see Table 1).

    Allon 1990 reported 20 mg nebulised salbutamol plus IV insulin dextrose significantly reduced serum potassium compared to 20 mg nebulised salbutamol alone at all time points. Two patients in the combination group had hypoglycaemia and the combination group had a significant increase in heart rate. These adverse effects did not occur in the salbutamol group.

    Ngugi 1997 did not report standard deviations for IV salbutamol alone versus IV salbutamol plus IV bicarbonate and therefore we were unable to perform meta‐analyses.

    Ngugi 1997 reported no significant difference in serum potassium reduction between IV salbutamol versus IV insulin‐dextrose plus IV sodium bicarbonate at 120 minutes (Analysis 9.1). Standard deviations were not available for the remaining time points.

    A combination of salbutamol IV, insulin‐dextrose IV and bicarbonate IV produced a significantly greater reduction in serum potassium than IV salbutamol alone at 120 minutes (Analysis 10.1: MD 0.79 mmol/L, 0.31 to 1.27). Standard deviations were not available for any other time points (Ngugi 1997).

    Insulin‐dextrose versus placebo

    No studies compared insulin‐dextrose with placebo.

    Insulin‐dextrose versus sodium bicarbonate

    Ngugi 1997 reported IV insulin‐dextrose significantly reduced serum potassium compared to IV sodium bicarbonate IV at 30 minutes (Analysis 11.1: ‐0.38 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐0.73 to ‐0.03). Standard deviations for results beyond 30 minutes were not available. Adverse effects were not reported (see Table 1).

    Insulin‐dextrose versus aminophylline

    Mahajan 2001 reported IV insulin‐dextrose significantly reduced serum potassium compared to IV aminophylline at 60 minutes (Analysis 12.1.1: MD 0.32 mmol/L, 95% CI 0.12 to 0.52), 180 minutes (Analysis 12.1.2: MD ‐0.28 mmol/L, 95 % CI ‐0.52 to ‐0.04) and 360 minutes (Analysis 12.1.3: MD ‐0.32 mmol/L, 95 % CI ‐0.56 to ‐0.08). There was one episode of hypoglycaemia in the insulin‐dextrose group.

    Insulin‐dextrose versus combination therapies

    Allon 1990 reported IV insulin‐dextrose IV plus 20 mg nebulised salbutamol significantly reduced serum potassium compared to IV insulin‐dextrose alone at 30 minutes (Analysis 13.1.2: 0.27 mmol/L, 95% CI 0.02 to 0.52), 45 minutes (Analysis 13.1.3: 0.39 mmol/L, 95% CI 0.06 to 0.72), and 60 minutes (Analysis 13.1.4: 0.66 mol/L, 95% CI 0.14 to 1.18), but not at 15 minutes. Hypoglycaemia occurred in 9/12 patients with insulin‐dextrose alone and 2/10 with the combination of insulin‐dextrose and salbutamol (P = 0.01). The combination group had a significant increase in heart rate while the salbutamol alone group did not.

    Ngugi 1997 reported no significant differences in serum potassium reduction with IV insulin‐dextrose alone compared to its combination with IV salbutamol IV at 30, 60 or 120 minutes (Analysis 14.1). Standard deviations were not reported for 240 or 480 minutes.

    No significant difference in serum potassium reduction was reported between IV insulin‐dextrose alone compared to its combination with IV sodium bicarbonate at 120 minutes (Analysis 15.1). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points (Ngugi 1997).

    No significant difference in serum potassium levels was reported between IV insulin‐dextrose and IV salbutamol plus IV sodium bicarbonate at 30 minutes (Analysis 16.1). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points (Ngugi 1997).

    Ngugi 1997 reported the combination of IV insulin‐dextrose, IV salbutamol and IV sodium bicarbonate significantly reduced serum potassium compared to IV insulin‐dextrose alone at 120 minutes (Analysis 17.1: MD ‐0.79 mmol/L 95% CI ‐0.35 to ‐1.23). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points. Adverse effects were not reported.

    Sodium bicarbonate versus placebo

    No studies compared sodium bicarbonate with placebo.

    Sodium bicarbonate versus combination therapies

    Ngugi 1997 reported no significant difference in reduction in serum potassium between IV sodium bicarbonate compared to IV sodium bicarbonate plus IV salbutamol at 30 minutes (Analysis 18.1). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points

    Ngugi 1997 reported IV salbutamol IV plus insulin‐dextrose IV significantly reduced serum potassium compared to IV sodium bicarbonate alone at 30 minutes (Analysis 19.1: MD ‐0.62 mmol/L, 95% CI ‐0.21 to ‐1.03). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points.

    Ngugi 1997 not provide sufficient information to allow us to compare IV sodium bicarbonate alone and combined with IV insulin‐dextrose plus IV salbutamol. Adverse effects were not reported.

    Combination therapies

    Ngugi 1997 compared a number of different Combinations of IV insulin‐dextrose, salbutamol and sodium bicarbonate.

    At 120 minutes there was no significant difference in the reduction of serum potassium between IV insulin‐dextrose plus sodium bicarbonate compared to IV insulin‐dextrose plus IV salbutamol (Analysis 20.1).

    At 120 minutes IV insulin‐dextrose plus IV sodium bicarbonate plus IV salbutamol significantly decreased serum potassium compared to IV insulin‐dextrose plus IV sodium bicarbonate (Analysis 21.1: MD 0.50 mmol/L, 95% CI 0.04 to 0.96). Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points.

Standard deviations were not reported for any other time points.

    • Summary of main results

    Meta‐analysis of the evidence for the acute pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia was not possible because there were a limited number of studies for each intervention and measures of variance were frequently not reported. Consequently, this review presents a narrative summary rather than a meta‐analysis of the available evidence.

    The studies included in this review provide evidence that several commonly used pharmacological interventions effectively reduce serum potassium and are generally well‐tolerated and safe. There is insufficient evidence to conclude whether these interventions reduce mortality, morbidity or serious adverse events, however we recognize the ethical dilemma of enrolling patients with a potentially life‐threatening condition to a placebo‐controlled study.

    Salbutamol administered via either nebuliser (Allon 1989) or MDI (Mandelberg 1999) decreased serum potassium significantly compared with placebo. IV salbutamol was compared to nebulised salbutamol and the two treatments had comparable effects (Balanzario 2003; Liou 1994) suggesting that salbutamol by any route is effective at lowering serum potassium. The results of Liou 1994 suggest that IV salbutamol may result in more rapid lowering of serum potassium than other modes of administration. Both Balanzario 2003 and Liou 1994 reported numerically more minor side effects with salbutamol IV. The maximal decrease in serum potassium ranged from 0.40 mmol/L in Mandelberg 1999 and 1.22 mmol/L in Allon 1989 with the peak effect occurring approximately 60 to 90 minutes after administration. Allon 1989 found that a 20 mg dose of nebulised salbutamol was more effective than 10 mg of nebulised salbutamol without significant differences in side effects. There was no evidence regarding dose response with salbutamol administered via another route. Mandelberg 1999 raised safety concerns with salbutamol when they found a small increase in serum potassium one minute after administration of 1.2 mg of salbutamol via MDI. No other study had such early time points and all other studies administered salbutamol over 15 minutes (either as infusion or nebulisation), thus it is unclear whether this early increase in serum potassium is a spurious result, or if a true result, whether or not it is associated with adverse patient outcomes.

    When compared to other regimens, salbutamol had a similar effect to insulin‐dextrose but was more effective than sodium bicarbonate. Allon 1990 found that insulin‐dextrose may have a more rapid onset than salbutamol. The combination of salbutamol and insulin‐dextrose was more effective than either regimen alone in Allon 1990 but not in Ngugi 1997. In Ngugi 1997 the combination of salbutamol and sodium bicarbonate was not more effective than salbutamol alone and the study reports a non‐significant trend to lower potassium in the salbutamol monotherapy group at all time points.

    Insulin‐dextrose was not studied in any placebo controlled studies, but was found to have a similar effect size to salbutamol in Allon 1990 and Ngugi 1997. The decrease in serum potassium compared with baseline values ranged from 0.55 mmol/L to 1.14 mmol/L. Insulin‐dextrose was more effective than bicarbonate (Ngugi 1997) and aminophylline (Mahajan 2001). Insulin‐dextrose in combination with salbutamol may be more effective than salbutamol alone (Allon 1990) but this was not found in all studies (Ngugi 1997).

    IV sodium bicarbonate was not studied in any placebo controlled studies. Ngugi 1997 compared IV sodium bicarbonate with salbutamol and found that bicarbonate was less effective at some time points. Allon 1996 (not included in this review as it included patients with normal potassium values) found that IV sodium bicarbonate did not provide any additional benefit when combined with insulin‐dextrose or salbutamol.

    There are no published RCTs evaluating sodium polystyrene or other potassium‐binding resins in hyperkalaemic patients, so we cannot comment on the efficacy of this intervention. Gruy‐Kapral 1998 (excluded from this review because the patients were not hyperkalaemic) failed to demonstrate any significant effect on serum potassium in a randomised study of sodium polystyrene versus placebo in normokalaemic patients.

    Evaluation of the evidence for RRT is beyond the scope of this review, but it is worth noting that dialysis is generally considered the definitive treatment for hyperkalaemia. Conventional haemodialysis produces a rapid decrease in serum potassium levels, as do other forms of dialysis such as peritoneal dialysis, continuous venovenous haemofiltration or continuous venovenous haemodialysis. However, dialysis does not work instantaneously and can be challenging to coordinate, so there remains a role for fast‐acting pharmacological treatment options. The magnitude of potassium reduction depends on dialysis duration, dialysate potassium concentration, type of dialyser membrane and patient characteristics such as baseline serum potassium (Evans 2005).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The evidence for acute pharmacological management of hyperkalaemia is strikingly incomplete. For example, sodium polystyrene sulphonate and other potassium‐binding resins are routinely used to decrease serum potassium despite a lack of RCTs evaluating these medications in hyperkalaemic patients. Similarly, IV calcium is commonly administered to patients with hyperkalaemia for membrane stabilization despite the absence of any clinical studies to support its use. This shortage of prospective, clinical outcome data likely relates to ethical concerns associated with using endpoints such as cardiac arrhythmia or death. Clinicians cannot justify placebo‐controlled studies when available observational data suggests that patient outcomes are poor when hyperkalaemia is left untreated.

    Quality of the evidence

    The quality of the evidence evaluating pharmacological therapies for reducing serum potassium is generally poor. There was insufficient reporting of allocation procedures, blinding and statistical techniques in nearly all studies. However, the outcome evaluated by most studies (serum potassium) is objective so these methodological short‐comings may not have biased the results to any great extent. Liou 1994 excluded patients who did not respond to therapy, thus biasing the results significantly. The majority of studies were very small (10, 12, 68, 34, 30, 17, and 70 for a total of only 241 patients) which may limit the accuracy of the results. Most of the studies did not report standard deviations for all of their results and this greatly limited our ability to compare treatments.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The articles were reviewed by three independent authors, none of whom have any conflict of interest to declare.

    We attempted to contact all of the authors of the studies included in the review for additional information. We received additional information on the study methodology and results for the studies Allon 1989 and Allon 1990. Unfortunately the author was not able to provide individual patient data or additional results of paired analysis statistical tests which would have allowed us to include paired statistical data in the review. The lack of paired data limits the power of the analyses. We did receive a response from the authors of Ngugi 1997 but they were unable to provide us with any further statistical information regarding the study results. We did not receive responses concerning Balanzario 2003, Liou 1994, Mahajan 2001 or Mandelberg 1999. Because we included all RCTs regardless of methodology, we included Liou 1994 despite highly biased results due to the authors excluding treatment non‐responders. Despite applying a search strategy that was designed to identify both published and unpublished studies, we were unable to include any unpublished studies.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    This review is a revision of the previous Cochrane systematic review. The results, where overlap exists, are consistent between the reviews (Clase 2005).
