Erythromycin prior to endoscopy for acute upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage

    • Background

    Upper endoscopy is the definitive treatment for upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage (UGIH). However, up to 13% of people who undergo upper endoscopy will have incomplete visualisation of the gastric mucosa at presentation. Erythromycin acts as a motilin receptor agonist in the upper gastrointestinal (GI) tract and increases gastric emptying, which may lead to better quality of visualisation and improved treatment effectiveness. However, there is uncertainty about the benefits and harms of erythromycin in UGIH.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of erythromycin before endoscopy in adults with acute upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage, compared with any other treatment or no treatment/placebo.

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. The latest search date was 15 October 2021.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that investigated erythromycin before endoscopy compared to any other treatment or no treatment/placebo before endoscopy in adults with acute UGIH.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methods. Our primary outcomes were 1. UGIH‐related mortality and 2. serious adverse events. Our secondary outcomes were 1. all‐cause mortality, 2. visualisation of gastric mucosa, 3. non‐serious adverse events, 4. rebleeding, 5. blood transfusion, and 5. rescue invasive intervention. We used GRADE criteria to assess the certainty of the evidence for each outcome. 

    Main results

    We included 11 RCTs with 878 participants. The mean age ranged from 53.13 years to 64.5 years, and most participants were men (72.3%). One RCT included only non‐variceal haemorrhage, one included only variceal haemorrhage, and eight included both aetiologies. We defined short‐term outcomes as those occurring within one week of initial endoscopy.

    Erythromycin versus placebo

    Three RCTs (255 participants) compared erythromycin with placebo. There were no UGIH‐related deaths. The evidence is very uncertain about the short‐term effects of erythromycin compared with placebo on serious adverse events (risk difference (RD) −0.01, 95% confidence interval (CI) −0.04 to 0.02; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low certainty), all‐cause mortality (RD 0.00, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.03; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low certainty), non‐serious adverse events (RD 0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.05; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low certainty), and rebleeding (risk ratio (RR) 0.63, 95% CI 0.13 to 2.90; 2 studies, 195 participants; very low certainty). Erythromycin may improve gastric mucosa visualisation (mean difference (MD) 3.63 points on 16‐point ordinal scale, 95% CI 2.20 to 5.05; higher MD means better visualisation; 2 studies, 195 participants; low certainty). Erythromycin may also result in a slight reduction in blood transfusion (MD −0.44 standard units of blood, 95% CI −0.86 to −0.01; 3 studies, 255 participants; low certainty).

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage

    Six RCTs (408 participants) compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage. There were no UGIH‐related deaths and no serious adverse events. The evidence is very uncertain about the short‐term effects of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on all‐cause mortality (RD −0.02, 95% CI −0.08 to 0.03; 3 studies, 238 participants; very low certainty), visualisation of the gastric mucosa (standardised mean difference (SMD) 0.48 points on 10‐point ordinal scale, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.85; higher SMD means better visualisation; 3 studies, 170 participants; very low certainty), non‐serious adverse events (RD 0.00, 95% CI −0.05 to 0.05; 6 studies, 408 participants; very low certainty), rebleeding (RR 1.13, 95% CI 0.63 to 2.02; 1 study, 169 participants; very low certainty), and blood transfusion (MD −1.85 standard units of blood, 95% CI −4.34 to 0.64; 3 studies, 180 participants; very low certainty).

    Erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage

    Four RCTs (287 participants) compared erythromycin with nasogastric tube lavage. There were no UGIH‐related deaths and no serious adverse events. The evidence is very uncertain about the short‐term effects of erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage on all‐cause mortality (RD 0.02, 95% CI −0.05 to 0.08; 3 studies, 213 participants; very low certainty), visualisation of the gastric mucosa (RR 1.19, 95% CI 0.79 to 1.79; 2 studies, 198 participants; very low certainty), non‐serious adverse events (RD −0.10, 95% CI −0.34 to 0.13; 3 studies, 213 participants; very low certainty), rebleeding (RR 0.77, 95% CI 0.40 to 1.49; 1 study, 169 participants; very low certainty), and blood transfusion (median 2 standard units of blood, interquartile range 0 to 4 in both groups; 1 study, 169 participants; very low certainty).

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage

    One RCT (30 participants) compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage. The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage on all the reported outcomes (serious adverse events, visualisation of gastric mucosa, non‐serious adverse events, and blood transfusion).

    Authors' conclusions

    We are unsure if erythromycin before endoscopy in people with UGIH has any clinical benefits or harms. However, erythromycin compared with placebo may improve gastric mucosa visualisation and result in a slight reduction in blood transfusion.

    • What are the benefits and risks of erythromycin given before endoscopy for people with intestinal bleeding?

    Key messages

    • Erythromycin may improve the quality of visualisation of the stomach and may also slightly reduce the need for blood transfusion.

    • Larger, well‐designed studies are needed to give better estimates of the benefits and potential harms of different doses of erythromycin in people with upper gastrointestinal bleeding.

    Introduction to the review topic

    The upper gastrointestinal tract includes the oesophagus (food pipe), stomach, and duodenum (first part of the small intestine). People with bleeding in the upper gastrointestinal tract require endoscopy (where the doctor inserts a long, flexible tube with a light and video camera down the throat and through the oesophagus to the stomach and duodenum) to diagnose and treat the cause of the bleeding. However, it may be difficult to identify the cause of bleeding if there is food or blood in the stomach. Erythromycin is a medicine that increases contractions in the oesophagus and stomach, making the organs empty their contents faster. However, we do not know whether erythromycin, when given before upper endoscopy, is safe and effective for emptying food or blood from the stomach and improving the results of endoscopy.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to know if erythromycin was better than placebo (any dummy treatment) or other medicines to improve the following outcomes.

    • Death caused by upper gastrointestinal bleeding
    • Serious unwanted events
    • Death by any cause
    • Mild unwanted events
    • Ease of viewing the stomach during endoscopy
    • Rebleeding
    • Need for blood transfusion
    • Need for rescue procedure (such as surgery)

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that investigated the use of erythromycin compared with no treatment, placebo, or other medicines in adults with upper gastrointestinal bleeding who underwent upper endoscopy. We compared and summarised their results and rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and sizes.

    What did we find?

    We included findings from 11 studies involving 878 adults with upper gastrointestinal bleeding. Most participants were men, and the average ages ranged from 53 years to 64 years. The studies were conducted in several different countries. They compared erythromycin with placebo, lavage of the stomach with a nasogastric tube (injecting and sucking out water or saline through a tube inserted through the nose and with the tip placed inside the stomach), and metoclopramide (a medicine that speeds up the emptying of the stomach).

    Main results

    Erythromycin compared with placebo

    Three studies (255 people) compared erythromycin with placebo. There were no deaths related to bleeding. We do not know if erythromycin has an effect on serious unwanted events, death by any cause, mild unwanted effects, or rebleeding. Erythromycin may make it easier to see the stomach during endoscopy and may reduce blood transfusion slightly.

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no treatment/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage

    Six studies (408 people) compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with no treatment/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage. There were no deaths related to bleeding and no serious unwanted events. We do not know if erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared to no treatment/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage has an effect on death by any cause, ease of viewing the stomach, mild unwanted events, rebleeding, or blood transfusion.

    Erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage

    Four studies (287 people) compared erythromycin with nasogastric tube lavage. There were no deaths related to bleeding and no severe unwanted events. We do not know if erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage has any effect on death by any cause, ease of viewing the stomach, mild unwanted events, rebleeding, or blood transfusion.

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage

    One study (30 people) compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage. We do not know if erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage has an effect on any of the reported outcomes. 

    Our conclusions

    Due to a lack of solid evidence, the benefits and harms of erythromycin before endoscopy for upper gastrointestinal bleeding remain unclear. However, erythromycin compared with placebo may make it easier to see the stomach during endoscopy and may reduce blood transfusion slightly. Larger, well‐designed studies are needed to give better estimates of the benefits and potential harms of erythromycin in people with upper gastrointestinal bleeding.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    We have very little confidence in the evidence because the results varied widely across studies, and the studies involved only small numbers of people. Some reports did not clearly describe how the study was conducted, or whether the people taking part knew who had received which medicine, which could have affected the results. Further research is likely to change our results.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up to date to October 2021.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Based on the findings of this review, there is currently a lack of high‐certainty evidence to indicate any clinical benefits or harms of erythromycin before endoscopy in people with upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage (UGIH). Compared with placebo, erythromycin may improve the quality of gastric mucosa visualisation and may also result in a slight reduction in blood transfusion. However, visualisation of the gastric mucosa is a surrogate secondary outcome assessed by non‐standardised endoscopy scoring systems, so we cannot conclude that the numerical improvement in gastric mucosa visualisation is associated with better endoscopic performance, or can provide any real benefit to people with UGIH. For all other comparisons and outcomes, the evidence is very uncertain about the benefits and harms of erythromycin.

    Our conclusions are generic because differences between studies precluded subgroup analysis. These differences were related to bleeding aetiologies (non‐variceal and variceal), settings (emergency departments and intensive care units), doses of erythromycin (in some studies together with nasogastric tube lavage), and endoscopic visibility scales. It would be useful for current practice if studies distinguished between people with non‐variceal and variceal bleeding, and did not combine erythromycin with nasogastric tube lavage.

    To facilitate informed decision‐making in clinical practice, there is a need for robust evidence on patient‐focused outcomes such as mortality, serious adverse events, rebleeding, blood transfusion, and rescue invasive intervention.

    Implications for research

    Further studies are required to provide higher‐certainty evidence on the effects of erythromycin administered before initial upper endoscopy. Larger trials with adequate randomisation and blinding could clarify whether this drug has any clinical benefits or harms for people with UGIH. Study investigators should separate participants with variceal and non‐variceal haemorrhage, as the pathophysiology and comorbidities differ between the two aetiologies and can influence the evaluated outcomes. Regarding comparisons, studies should focus on erythromycin versus placebo, as nasogastric tube lavage is no longer recommended. RCTs should also analyse different erythromycin doses, ideally adjusted for bodyweight, to achieve any possible benefits with the lowest possible dose, and minimise the potential risk of adverse events and bacterial selection. Investigators should objectively standardise the scale for assessing gastric mucosa visualisation. Ideally, the scale should be based on valid, objective, and reproducible data, with a gradation that correlates to clinical evaluation.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage (UGIH) is defined as any bleeding arising from the upper gastrointestinal (GI) tract, which comprises the oesophagus, stomach, and duodenum (Antunes 2017). UGIH can manifest directly as external bleeding (haematemesis, melaena, or haematochezia) or indirectly as signs and symptoms of blood loss, or haemorrhagic shock syndrome (Fallah 2000; Tarasconi 2020). Episodes of UGIH can be categorised as non‐variceal or variceal, according to the aetiology of the bleeding and the presence of portal hypertension (Pongprasobchai 2009). The most frequent aetiologies for non‐variceal UGIH are peptic ulcer, mucosal erosion, angiodysplasia, neoplasia, and Mallory‐Weiss syndrome (Enestvedt 2008; Laine 2021). In variceal UGIH (i.e. bleeding related to portal hypertension), the most common aetiologies are oesophagogastric varices and portal hypertensive gastropathy (Lyles 2014).

    The incidence of UGIH ranges from 36 to 172 per 100,000 inhabitants per year, and UGIH‐related mortality ranges from 1% to 3% (Hreinsson 2013). Variceal haemorrhage is less common than non‐variceal haemorrhage (25% versus 75%) but has a higher mortality rate (15% versus 3%; Lanas 2009; Nahon 2012).

    Upper endoscopy is the procedure of choice to control bleeding after initial management of UGIH (de Franchis 2022; Laine 2009). However, up to 13% of people who undergo endoscopy will present an incomplete visualisation of the gastric mucosa, and 41% of second‐look endoscopies will not result in a diagnosis (Stollman 1997). Waiting until physiological emptying of the stomach is not recommended: this approach can lead to worse outcomes, and delayed upper endoscopy (after more than 24 hours) is not cost‐effective (Ananthakrishnan 2009; Cooper 1998; Cooper 1999; Lau 2020; Wysocki 2012). Furthermore, failure to diagnose blood clots by initial endoscopy is associated with higher morbidity and mortality (Cheng 2002; Stollman 1997). Gastric lavage, a traditional and accessible option to clean the stomach, may lead to pulmonary aspiration, owing to the presence of the tube in the lower oesophageal sphincter (keeping it partially open) and the high intragastric pressures caused by the infusion of a large volume of fluid for lavage (Lee 2004; Vale 2004). There is a need for a safe and effective measure for optimising gastric visualisation during upper endoscopy in people with UGIH.

    Description of the intervention

    Erythromycin (Ilotycin), a macrolide antibiotic, was discovered by McGuire and colleagues in 1952 (McGuire 1952). It is obtained from the metabolic products of Saccharopolyspora erythraea (Oliynyk 2007), and is used to treat aerobic Gram‐positive bacilli and cocci infections worldwide (Jelić 2016). Erythromycin has two main presentations: oral, in the form of base and esters of erythromycin (stearate, estolate, and ethylsuccinate), and parenteral, in the form of lactobionate (Parsons 1980; Tjandramaga 1984). The serum elimination half‐life of erythromycin is around 1.5 hours, with high levels of metabolites concentration in the liver and bile excretion (Glanzer 2015). Only 5% of oral erythromycin is excreted in urine (Josefsson 1982).

    Studies have shown adverse effects of erythromycin besides bacteriostatic effects (Boyd 1991; Haefeli 1992; Ray 2004). Most of these additional adverse effects involve the GI tract (nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, and diarrhoea; Catnach 1992), and mainly occur after parenteral administration of the drug (Bowler 1992; Downey 1986; Gholson 1990; Seifert 1989). On the other hand, some known GI effects have been used favourably to improve clinical conditions such as diabetic gastroparesis (Janssens 1990; Urbain 1990), and feed intolerance in critically ill individuals (Chapman 2000; Hawkyard 2007; MacLaren 2008; Nguyen 2007). GI effects of erythromycin are observed from parenteral doses of 70 mg (Ritz 2005). 

    How the intervention might work

    Erythromycin acts as a motilin receptor agonist in the upper GI tract (Sanger 2013). Motilin is a 22‐amino acid peptide found in the M and enterochromaffin cells that amplifies phase III of the migrating motor complex of the gut (Sanger 2012). Through this mechanism, erythromycin promotes a potent contractile action over the upper GI tract, increasing lower oesophageal pressure and gastric emptying. This effect is observed from 15 to 30 minutes after parenteral drug administration, both in animals (Itoh 1984), and in humans (Mantides 1993; Tzovaras 1996).

    There is evidence to suggest that nasogastric tube lavage may help to clear the stomach in people with UGIH (Lee 2004). Due to the similar stomach‐emptying effect of erythromycin, administration of this antibiotic, alone or together with gastric lavage, could improve gastric mucosa visibility and endoscopy performance.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Our justification for this review was based on the following facts.

    • UGIH is a prevalent disease with high morbidity and mortality.

    • Erythromycin has a documented fast and potent effect on the upper GI tract, emptying the stomach.

    • Improved gastric visualisation would improve endoscopic treatment and promote earlier discharge, saving resources.

    • To date, no Cochrane Review has analysed the effects of erythromycin before initial upper endoscopy in people with UGIH.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of erythromycin before endoscopy in adults with acute upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage, compared with any other treatment or no treatment/placebo.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included only randomised controlled trials (RCTs). We considered RCTs with parallel design (cross‐over design is not plausible for this clinical situation) and with individual‐ and cluster‐level randomisation. We considered studies published as full‐text articles and as abstract only, regardless of date or language of publication, and studies with no published data.

    Types of participants

    Eligible studies enrolled people aged 16 years or older with acute UGIH (confirmed or suspected, regardless of aetiology or severity) who were referred for upper endoscopy.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies comparing erythromycin administered before upper endoscopy, with or without nasogastric lavage, at any dose, route, or scheme of administration, to any of the following controls.

    • No intervention/placebo

    • Nasogastric tube lavage only

    • Another prokinetic drug (e.g. metoclopramide)

    • Different doses, routes of administration, or schemes of erythromycin.

    Types of outcome measures

    We assessed all outcomes in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy) and in the long term (more than one week after initial endoscopy), except visualisation of gastric mucosa, which is always concomitant with the initial endoscopy.

    Where studies measured outcomes at short‐term and long‐term follow‐up, we reported both in the main text and included only the short‐term outcome data in the summary of findings tables, as short‐term data may better represent the effects of a single dose of erythromycin. If any study reported our outcomes at more than one time point within short‐term or long‐term follow‐up, we used the last time point.

    Reporting of our predefined outcomes was not an inclusion criterion for the review.

    Primary outcomes

    Our selection of primary outcomes was based on an international consensus conference (Laine 2010), identified by the COMET (Core Outcome Measures in Effectiveness Trials) initiative (COMET). 

    • UGIH‐related mortality, defined as the number of deaths related to UGIH, including hypovolaemic shock, transfusion reaction, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, and pulmonary aspiration

    • Serious adverse events, defined as the number of participants with: at least one event that increased risk of death, required or prolonged hospitalisation, or resulted in persistent or significant disability; or any important and potentially harmful clinical event

    Secondary outcomes

    • All‐cause mortality, defined as the number of deaths from any cause

    • Visualisation of gastric mucosa, assessed through any validated tool or endoscopist opinion

    • Non‐serious adverse events, defined as the number of participants with any undesirable event not considered a serious adverse event

    • Rebleeding, defined as the number of participants presenting at least one new episode of UGIH confirmed by endoscopy after resolution of the initial episode

    • Blood transfusion, defined as the number of packs of red blood cells received by each participant

    • Rescue invasive intervention, defined as the number of participants undergoing any invasive intervention to control bleeding after endoscopic treatment failure (e.g. surgery, vascular embolisation, gastro‐oesophageal balloon, transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS))

    We searched for retraction and erratum notices related to included articles on PubMed and reported the date of the notices in the review (www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (DA and CP) independently screened titles and abstracts of all the studies identified by the search and coded them as 'retrieve' (eligible or potentially eligible) or 'do not retrieve' (clearly ineligible). Two review authors (DA and CP) independently screened the full texts, identified studies for inclusion, and identified and recorded reasons for exclusion of ineligible studies. They resolved any disagreements through discussion or by consulting a third review author (RP) if necessary. We identified and excluded duplicates and collated multiple reports of the same study so that each study, rather than each report, was the unit of interest in the review. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 1) and the Characteristics of included studies table.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (DA and CP) independently extracted the following study characteristics and outcome data to a standard data collection form that we had piloted on at least one study in the review.

    • Methods: study design, total duration of study and run‐in, number of study centres and location, study setting, withdrawals, and date of study

    • Participants: number randomised, mean age, age range, sex, severity of the condition, diagnostic criteria, and inclusion and exclusion criteria

    • Interventions: intervention, comparison, concomitant medications, and excluded medications

    • Outcomes: primary and secondary outcomes specified and collected, and time points reported

    • Notes: funding for the study, and notable conflicts of interest of study authors

    We noted in the Characteristics of included studies table if outcome data were reported in an unusable way. We resolved any disagreements by consensus or by involving a third review author (AG). One review author (DA) entered the data from the data collection form into Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2020). We double‐checked that the data were entered correctly by comparing the study reports with the presentation of data in the systematic review. A second review author (CP) spot‐checked study characteristics for accuracy against the study report.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (DA and RP) independently assessed the risk of bias for each included study using the Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB 1; Higgins 2011). We resolved any disagreements by discussion or by involving a third review author (RR). We assessed risk of bias according to the following domains.

    • Random sequence generation

    • Allocation concealment

    • Blinding of participants and personnel

    • Blinding of outcome assessment

    • Incomplete outcome data

    • Selective outcome reporting

    • Other bias

    We rated each potential source of bias as high, low, or unclear, and provided a quote from the study report to justify our judgement in a risk of bias table. We summarised the risk of bias judgements across studies for each of the domains listed. For the domains 'Blinding of participants and personnel', 'Blinding of outcome assessment', and 'Incomplete outcome data', we assessed the risk of bias at outcome level. For all other domains, we assessed risk of bias at study level. Where information on risk of bias related to unpublished data or correspondence with a study author, we noted this in the risk of bias table. When considering treatment effects, we considered the risk of bias for the studies that contribute to that outcome as part of the GRADE assessment.

    Assessment of bias in conducting the systematic review

    We conducted the review according to the published protocol and reported any deviations from it (with reasons) in the Differences between protocol and review section of the systematic review (Silva 2018).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed dichotomous data as risk ratios (RRs) and continuous data as mean difference (MDs) or standardised mean difference (SMDs). We tried to ensure that higher scores had the same meaning for all continuous outcomes; where this was not possible, we explained the change of direction for readers of the review.

    We undertook meta‐analyses only where this was meaningful (i.e. where the treatments, participants, and the underlying clinical question were sufficiently similar for pooling to make sense). We used risk differences (RDs) instead of RRs for meta‐analyses that contained at least one study with no events in both arms, because the Mantel‐Haenszel method with RRs would automatically discard this type of study (Xu 2021).

    A common way for study authors to indicate they have skewed data is to report medians and interquartile ranges (IQRs). Where we encountered this, we noted that the data were skewed and considered the implication; we did not meta‐analyse studies with skewed data but provided a narrative summary instead.

    Where studies had multiple study arms, we included only the relevant arms. If any study had entered two relevant comparisons (e.g. drug A versus placebo and drug B versus placebo) into the same meta‐analysis, we would have halved the control group to avoid double counting.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the individual participant. If we had included any cluster‐RCTs, we would have calculated the design effect based on intracluster correlation as recommended in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021).

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted study authors or study sponsors to verify key study characteristics and to obtain missing numerical outcome data as necessary (e.g. for studies published as abstracts only). If we were unable to obtain the relevant information, we did not impute missing data. When studies provided medians and IQRs rather than means and standard deviations (SDs), we presented the data narratively and conducted no meta‐analysis.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    First, we visually examined the forest plots to determine whether the confidence intervals (CIs) had good overlap, which would suggest low or no statistical heterogeneity (inconsistency) between RCTs. We then assessed the presence of statistical heterogeneity using the Chi2 test (significant if P < 0.1), and quantified statistical heterogeneity using the I2 statistic, applying the following interpretations (Higgins 2003; Higgins 2021).

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity

    We explored the causes of heterogeneity through prespecified subgroup analysis (Higgins 2021).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If we include 10 or more studies in future updates, we will create and examine a funnel plot to explore possible publication bias and use Egger's test to determine the statistical significance of the reporting bias, considering a P value below 0.05 representative of statistical significance (Egger 1997).

    Data synthesis

    We used the random‐effects model by default. To test the robustness of our findings, we conducted a sensitivity analysis for primary outcomes using a fixed‐effect model (Sensitivity analysis). In case of divergence between the two models, we presented both results; otherwise, we presented only results from the random‐effects model.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned the following subgroup analyses for the primary outcomes.

    • Variceal and non‐variceal UGIH (participants with variceal haemorrhage expected to have worse outcomes)

    • Age (participants aged 60 years or older expected to have worse outcomes)

    • Presence of coronary artery disease (participants with coronary artery disease expected to have worse outcomes)

    • Different doses of erythromycin (higher doses expected to lead to better outcomes)

    We tested subgroup differences using the formal test provided in Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2020).

    Sensitivity analysis

    To assess the robustness of our conclusions, we planned the following sensitivity analyses for the primary outcomes.

    • Excluding studies judged at high or unclear risk of selection or detection bias

    • Excluding studies where the sponsor played a role in the design or conduct of the trial

    • Excluding unpublished results

    • Repeating the analyses with the fixed‐effect model (Data synthesis)

    Reaching conclusions

    We based our conclusions on the quantitative or narrative synthesis of studies included in this review. We avoided making recommendations for practice; our implications for research gave the reader a clear sense of the needed focus of future research and remaining uncertainties in the field.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created summary of findings tables for the following outcomes (summary of findings Table 1; summary of findings Table 2; summary of findings Table 3; summary of findings Table 4).

    • UGIH‐related mortality

    • Serious adverse events

    • All‐cause mortality

    • Visualisation of gastric mucosa

    • Non‐serious adverse events

    • Rebleeding

    We created one summary of findings table for each comparison. Two review authors (DA and RP) independently assessed the certainty of the body of evidence and reached a consensus view on any downgrading decisions. We resolved any disagreements by discussion or by involving a third review author (RR).

    Where studies reported an outcome at both short‐term and long‐term follow‐up, we included only the short‐term data, as they may be more representative of the effects of a single dose of erythromycin.

    For the outcome 'Visualisation of gastric mucosa', we chose to report data measured by the most objective method (e.g. between a scoring system using an objective numerical scale versus the subjective opinion of an endoscopist, we chose the numerical scale).

    We used the five GRADE considerations (study limitations, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the body of evidence derived from studies that contributed data to each short‐term outcome. We classified the certainty of the evidence as high, moderate, low, or very low, using the methods and recommendations described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021), and employing GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT). We justified all decisions to downgrade or upgrade the certainty of the evidence in the footnotes and provided comments to aid the reader's understanding where necessary. We considered whether there was additional outcome information that was not incorporated into the meta‐analyses, noted this in the comments, and stated if it supported or contradicted the information from the meta‐analyses.

    Included studies

We contacted the authors of all studies for additional information, but only one author replied to our questions (Altraif 2011).

    Design

    The studies were parallel‐group RCTs published between 1996 and 2020.

    Sample size

    The studies randomised 878 participants in total (60 in Ali Shah 2020, 102 in Altraif 2011, 40 in Ardakani 2013, 100 in Carbonell 2006, 41 in Coffin 2002, 105 in Frossard 2002, 74 in Guardiola 2009, 45 in Habashi 2007, 43 in Na 2017, 253 in Pateron 2011, and 15 in Sears 1996), and analysed 865 participants at latest follow‐up (60 in Ali Shah 2020; 90 in Altraif 2011, 40 in Ardakani 2013, 99 in Carbonell 2006, 41 in Coffin 2002, 105 in Frossard 2002, 74 in Guardiola 2009, 45 in Habashi 2007, 43 in Na 2017, 253 in Pateron 2011, and 15 in Sears 1996).

    Thirteen participants were randomised then excluded: 12 participants in Altraif 2011 did not meet the inclusion criterion 'bleeding of variceal aetiology' (participants with cirrhosis were excluded after upper endoscopy revealed bleeding of non‐variceal aetiology), and one participant in Carbonell 2006 needed palliative care for advanced liver cancer diagnosed after randomisation.

    Settings

    Four RCTs were conducted in Europe (Carbonell 2006, Coffin 2002, and Pateron 2011 in France; and Frossard 2002 in Switzerland), four were conducted in Asia (Ali Shah 2020 in Pakistan, Altraif 2011 in Saudi Arabia, Ardakani 2013 in Iran, and Na 2017 in Korea), and two were conducted in North America (Habashi 2007 and Sears 1996 in the USA). Guardiola 2009 (abstract) did not describe the study setting. All RCTs were published in English.

    Participants
    Diagnosis of UGIH

    All studies included people with UGIH. Seven RCTs diagnosed UGIH based on direct clinical signs (haematemesis, melaena, or both; Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Frossard 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011), and Coffin 2002 diagnosed UGIH based on direct or indirect clinical signs (haematemesis, melaena, acute anaemia requiring active resuscitation, or a combination of these). Carbonell 2006 used nasogastric tube lavage to identify bleeding in the stomach and anaemia. Two RCTs did not report UGIH diagnosis criteria (Guardiola 2009; Sears 1996).

    Age

    The mean age of participants ranged from 53.13 years to 64.5 years. Most participants were men (72.3%). Two RCTs did not provide this information (Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007).

    Bleeding duration

    The maximum time interval from bleeding onset to inclusion in the study was 12 hours. Five RCTs did not report this interval (Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Sears 1996).

    Aetiology of haemorrhage

    Na 2017 included only non‐variceal haemorrhage, Altraif 2011 included only variceal haemorrhage, and eight RCTs included non‐variceal and variceal haemorrhage (Ali Shah 2020; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Habashi 2007; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996), although Ardakani 2013 did not specify the culprit lesion for its three cirrhotic participants. Guardiola 2009 provided no information about aetiology. Overall, the RCTs included 204 (23.5%) participants with non‐variceal haemorrhage, 211 (24.3%) participants with variceal haemorrhage, and 450 (52.0%) participants with unknown aetiology in the final analysis.

    Interventions and comparators

    The RCTs evaluated the following comparisons.

    • Erythromycin versus placebo: three RCTs (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002)

    • Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage: six RCTs (versus no intervention in Ardakani 2013, Carbonell 2006, Na 2017, and Pateron 2011; versus placebo in Coffin 2002 and Habashi 2007)

    • Erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage: four RCTs (Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996)

    • Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage: one RCT (Habashi 2007)

    Six RCTs used a 250 mg fixed dose of parenteral erythromycin (Carbonell 2006; Frossard 2002; Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996), while Altraif 2011 used a 125 mg fixed parenteral dose. Two RCTs (Ardakani 2013; Coffin 2002) used a weight‐adjusted parenteral dose (3 mg/kg). Habashi 2007 did not report the parenteral dose. Ali Shah 2020 was the only study to use oral erythromycin (500 mg fixed dose).

    The time from intervention (drug infusion or ingestion) to upper endoscopy was 20 minutes in Frossard 2002, 30 minutes in four RCTs (Altraif 2011; Carbonell 2006; Guardiola 2009; Pateron 2011), 20 to 60 minutes in Na 2017, 30 to 60 minutes in Ardakani 2013, 60 minutes in Sears 1996, 60 to 120 minutes in Coffin 2002, and 180 minutes in Ali Shah 2020. Pateron 2011 waited 15 minutes from nasogastric tube lavage to endoscopy, and Habashi 2007 did not report the time from intervention to endoscopy, but all other RCTs with a control intervention used the same delay for erythromycin and for control.

    Of the RCTs that used a placebo control, three used normal saline (Altraif 2011; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002), and two did not describe the placebo in the report (Ali Shah 2020; Habashi 2007).

    Regarding the administration of nasogastric tube lavage, seven studies performed lavage after randomisation (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996). Coffin 2002 did not explicitly state this, but reported that lavage took place "as part of the clinical evaluation". No RCTs described standard procedures or measures (form of washing, washing fluid, or volume).

    The numbers of participants randomised to the various interventions were as follows.

    • Erythromycin only: 275 (31.3%) participants (500 mg oral: 30 participants; 250 mg parenteral: 192 participants; 125 mg parenteral: 53 participants)

    • Nasogastric tube lavage only: 168 (19.1%) participants

    • Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage: 202 (23.0%) participants (250 mg parenteral: 148 participants; 3 mg/kg parenteral: 39 participants; unknown dose: 15 participants)

    • Metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage: 15 (1.7%) participants

    • Placebo/no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage: 85 (9.6%) participants

    • Placebo only: 133 (15.1%) participants

    Outcomes

    The RCTs evaluated the following outcomes of interest.

    • UGIH‐related mortality: five RCTs (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002; Na 2017; Sears 1996).

    • Serious adverse events: 10 RCTs (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996; Altraif 2011)

    • All‐cause mortality: eight RCTs (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996)

    • Visualisation of gastric mucosa: all 11 RCTs. The studies assessed this outcome using different numerical scores (Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996), or dichotomous criteria based on endoscopist judgement (Ali Shah 2020; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Pateron 2011)

    • Non‐serious adverse events: 10 RCTs (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996)

    • Rebleeding: four RCTs (Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). No studies reported precise criteria for defining rebleeding.

    • Blood transfusion: all 11 RCTs. Only Altraif 2011 specified (by email) the volume of packed red blood cells (250 mL to 275 mL). No RCT reported the need for other blood components, such as plasma or platelets.

    • Rescue invasive intervention: four RCTs (Altraif 2011; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Na 2017). Coffin 2002 assessed the need for emergency surgery but did not describe what type of surgery was performed, or whether it was performed before or after an endoscopic mechanical attempt to control bleeding. Frossard 2002 also assessed the need for emergency surgery (bleeding ulcer), in all cases after endoscopy revealed a quantity of blood in the stomach that warranted immediate surgery, without a prior endoscopic mechanical attempt to control bleeding (endoscopic treatment failure related to impossibility to locate the source of bleeding). Na 2017 assessed angiographic embolisation as a rescue invasive intervention for a gastric ulcer with spurting bleeding after an endoscopic mechanical attempt to control bleeding (endoscopic treatment failure related to persistent bleeding after prior source control).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 13 reports for the following reasons.

    • Five letters (Aubertin 1995; Daram 2011; Das 2008; Lawrence 1994; Nion 1998)

    • Five narrative reviews (Ebell 2010; Ferguson 2006; Khamaysi 2015; Kim 2013; Sanchez‐Yague 2012)

    • Two studies interrupted early with no available data (EudraCT2010‐024309‐11; NCT02017379)

    • One duplicated translation from Coffin 2002 (Rotondano 2003)

    Allocation

    Five RCTs reported adequate methods of random sequence generation and allocation concealment so were at low risk of selection bias (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Carbonell 2006; Frossard 2002; Pateron 2011). Six RCTs did not provide sufficient information to assess random sequence generation and allocation concealment so were at unclear risk (Ardakani 2013; Coffin 2002; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Sears 1996).

    Blinding

    Four RCTs reported that participants, personnel, and outcome assessors were blinded so were at low risk of performance and detection bias (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Carbonell 2006; Frossard 2002). Ardakani 2013 did not provide sufficient information to assess blinding (unclear risk), except for assessment of the outcome 'All‐cause mortality' (low risk). Coffin 2002 and Sears 1996 reported no attempt to blind participants, personnel, or outcome assessors (high risk), except for assessment of the outcomes 'All‐cause mortality' and 'Visualisation of gastric mucosa' (low risk). Guardiola 2009 reported no attempt to blind participants, personnel, or outcome assessors for most outcomes (high risk), and did not provide enough information to assess blinding of outcome assessors for the outcome 'Blood transfusion' (unclear risk). Habashi 2007 did not provide enough information to assess blinding (unclear risk). Na 2017 reported no attempt to blind participants and personnel (high risk), did not provide sufficient information to assess blinding of outcome assessors (unclear risk), except for the outcomes 'All‐cause mortality' (low risk) and 'Visualisation of gastric mucosa' (high risk). Pateron 2011 reported no attempt to blind participants and personnel (high risk), and did not provide sufficient information to assess the blinding of outcome assessors (unclear risk), except for the outcomes 'All‐cause mortality' (low risk) and 'Visualisation of gastric mucosa' (low risk). 

    Incomplete outcome data

    Seven RCTs reported balanced numbers across intervention groups and no important losses to follow‐up so were at low risk of attrition bias (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Four RCTs did not provide sufficient information to assess incomplete outcome data so were at unclear risk (Frossard 2002; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Sears 1996).

    Selective reporting

    The methods section of Na 2017 stated that the investigators would evaluate the endpoints 'number of transfused blood units', 'instances of rebleeding', and 'mortality ratings' at seven days and 30 days; however, the results section only presented data collected at one time point, without specifying if it was seven days or 30 days (high risk). Pateron 2011 registered the RCT retrospectively, and the study authors did not report all planned outcomes (high risk). The remaining nine studies did not provide sufficient information to assess reporting bias so were at unclear risk (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Sears 1996).

    Other potential sources of bias

    Five RCTs had no other potential sources of bias so were at low risk of bias in this domain (Altraif 2011; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Four RCTs did not provide sufficient information to assess other potential sources of bias so were at unclear risk (Ali Shah 2020; Guardiola 2009; Habashi 2007; Sears 1996). Ardakani 2013 presented baseline imbalance (high risk). Frossard 2002 presented baseline imbalance, but we are uncertain to what extent this may have impacted the outcomes (unclear risk).

    Comparison 1: Erythromycin versus placebo

    Three RCTs, totaling 255 participants, compared erythromycin with placebo (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002).

    UGIH‐related mortality

    The following three RCTs (255 participants) addressed UGIH‐related mortality and reported no events in either group (very low‐certainty evidence).

    • Ali Shah 2020: 0/30 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/30 in the placebo group (follow‐up duration not defined)

    • Altraif 2011: 0/47 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/43 in the placebo group, within one week of initial endoscopy (data provided by email)

    • Frossard 2002: 0/51 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/54 in the placebo group, within 24 hours of initial endoscopy

    There were no data regarding long‐term UGIH‐related mortality (more than one week after initial endoscopy).

    We did not perform subgroup analysis because meta‐analysis was not possible for this outcome. Only Altraif 2011 presented separate data for participants with non‐variceal and variceal UGIH, and only Altraif 2011 administered a low dose of erythromycin; there were no events reported in either group (0/47 in the erythromycin group versus 0/43 in the placebo group) in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy).

    We did not perform sensitivity analysis because meta‐analysis was not possible for this outcome.

    Serious adverse events

    Three RCTs addressed serious adverse events (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). Ali Shah 2020 reported 0/30 events in both groups without specifying the follow‐up duration; we regarded the zero events as short‐term follow‐up outcome data. Altraif 2011 reported 0/47 events in the erythromycin group versus 2/43 events (aspiration pneumonia) in the placebo group, within one week of initial endoscopy. Frossard 2002 reported no events in either group (0/51 in the erythromycin group versus 0/54 in the placebo group), during or just after initial endoscopy. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared to placebo on serious adverse events (erythromycin 0%, placebo 1.57%; RD −0.01, 95% CI −0.04 to 0.02; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1).

    We did not perform subgroup analysis because only one RCT contributed to the number of events. Only Altraif 2011 provided separate data for participants with non‐variceal and variceal UGIH, and only Altraif 2011 administered a low dose of erythromycin; there were 0/47 events in the erythromycin group and 2/47 events (aspiration pneumonia) in the placebo group within one week of initial endoscopy (RR 0.18, 95% CI 0.01 to 3.71; 1 study, 90 participants).

    We did not perform sensitivity analysis because only one RCT contributed to the number of events.

    All‐cause mortality

    Three RCTs addressed all‐cause mortality (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). Ali Shah 2020 reported 0/30 events in both groups, but did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Altraif 2011 reported 4/47 events in the erythromycin group and 6/43 events in the placebo group, within one week of initial endoscopy (in‐hospital mortality). Frossard 2002 reported 0/51 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/54 events in the placebo group within 24 hours of initial endoscopy. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared to placebo on all‐cause mortality (erythromycin 3.12%, placebo 4.72%; RD 0.00, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.03; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2).

    Frossard 2002 also reported two events in the long term (more than one week after initial endoscopy) but did not specify which group these participants belonged to.

    Visualisation of gastric mucosa

    Three RCTs assessed visualisation of gastric mucosa (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002).

    Altraif 2011 and Frossard 2002 provided continuous data for this outcome. Frossard 2002 used an 8‐point scale, where a higher score indicated a clearer stomach. Altraif 2011 expanded this scoring system to a 16‐point scale. Through a simple arithmetic transformation, we were able to combine data from the two studies in a meta‐analysis. Erythromycin may improve visualisation of gastric mucosa (MD 3.63, 95% CI 2.20 to 5.05; higher MD means better visualisation; I2 = 62%; 2 studies, 195 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3).

    Ali Shah 2020, Altraif 2011, and Frossard 2002 provided dichotomous data for this outcome. Ali Shah 2020 and Altraif 2011 used cutoff points on ordinal scales to classify the stomach as empty or not empty (5 or more points on a 7‐point scale in Ali Shah 2020; 15 or more points on a 16‐point scale in Altraif 2011). Meta‐analysis of these data favoured erythromycin (erythromycin 62.33%, placebo 30.13%; RR 2.09, 95% CI 1.44 to 3.03; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 150 participants; Analysis 1.4). Frossard 2002 reported the judgement of the endoscopist, who decided whether each participant had an empty stomach (endoscopy was easy to perform and less than 25% of the gastric mucosa was covered by blood). This was the case for 42/51 participants in the erythromycin group and 18/54 participants in the placebo group (erythromycin 80.76%, placebo 33.33%; RR 2.47, 95% CI 1.66 to 3.68; 1 study, 105 participants; Analysis 1.4);

    Non‐serious adverse events

    Three RCTs assessed non‐serious adverse events (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). Ali Shah 2020 reported 0/30 events in both groups, but did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Altraif 2011 reported 0/51 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/54 in the placebo group, within one week of initial endoscopy. Frossard 2002 reported four events (three urgent bowel movements, one moderate abdominal cramps) in the erythromycin group (4/51) and one event (headache) in the placebo group (1/54), after intervention infusion. 

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with placebo on non‐serious adverse events (erythromycin 3.12%, placebo 0.78%; RD 0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.05; I2 = 23%; 3 studies, 255 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.5).

    Rebleeding

    Two RCTs assessed rebleeding within 24 hours of initial endoscopy (Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with placebo on rebleeding (erythromycin 4.08%, placebo 7.21%; RR 0.63, 95% CI 0.13 to 2.90; I2 = 23%; 2 studies, 195 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). Neither study reported this outcome beyond 24 hours after initial endoscopy.

    Blood transfusion

    Three RCTs assessed the need for blood transfusion (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). The unit of measurement was a standard unit of blood, as defined by the study authors. Only Altraif 2011 stated the volume of each blood pack (250 mL to 275 mL). Ali Shah 2020 did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Altraif 2011 and Frossard 2002 did not report this outcome beyond 24 hours after the initial endoscopy. Erythromycin may result in a slight reduction in blood transfusion (MD −0.44 standard units of blood, 95% CI −0.86 to −0.01; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 255 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.7).

    Rescue invasive intervention

    Two RCTs assessed rescue invasive intervention (Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). Altraif 2011 reported 0/51 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/54 in the placebo group. Frossard 2002 reported 3/51 events in the erythromycin group versus 1/54 in the placebo group; in both groups, the outcome was immediate surgery after failed initial endoscopy because of a bleeding ulcer. Frossard 2002 did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Meta‐analysis of these data showed no difference between erythromycin and placebo (erythromycin 3.06%, placebo 1.03%; RD 0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.06; I2 = 20%; 2 studies, 195 participants; Analysis 1.8).

    Comparison 2: Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage

    Six RCTs, totaling 408 participants, compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011).

    UGIH‐related mortality

    Na 2017 addressed UGIH‐related mortality and reported 0/14 events in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/15 in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group (very low‐certainty evidence) within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy.

    It was not possible to perform sensitivity or subgroup analysis for this outcome. Na 2017 included only participants with non‐variceal UGIH.

    Serious adverse events

    The following six RCTs (408 participants) addressed serious adverse events and reported no events in either group (very low‐certainty evidence).

    • Ardakani 2013: 0/20 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/20 in the placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    • Carbonell 2006: 0/49 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/50 in the placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    • Coffin 2002: 0/19 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/22 in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    • Habashi 2007: 0/15 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/15 in the placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    • Na 2017: 0/14 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/15 in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    • Pateron 2011: 0/84 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 0/85 in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group

    Two RTCs assessed serious adverse events within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy (Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Four RCTs did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007).

    We did not perform sensitivity or subgroup analysis because meta‐analysis was not possible for this outcome. Only Na 2017 defined the aetiology of the haemorrhage, and included only participants with non‐variceal UGIH.

    All‐cause mortality

    Four RCTs addressed all‐cause mortality (Ardakani 2013; Coffin 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011).

    Three RCTs addressed this outcome in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy); short‐time follow‐up duration was 48 hours in Ardakani 2013 and one week in Na 2017 and Pateron 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on all‐cause mortality in the short term (erythromycin 2.54%, control 5.0%; RD −0.02, 95% CI −0.08 to 0.03; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 238 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.1);

    Two RCTs addressed this outcome in the long term (within 30 days of initial endoscopy; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage on all‐cause mortality in the long term (erythromycin 3.06%, no intervention 9.0%; RD −0.05, 95% CI −0.12 to 0.01; I2 = 0%; 2 studies,198 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.1).

    Coffin 2002 reported 0/19 events in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 2/22 events in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group. As the study authors had not specified the follow‐up duration for this outcome, we analysed the study as 'unknown follow‐up' (erythromycin 0%, no intervention 9.09%; RR 0.23, 95% CI 0.01 to 4.51; RD −0.09, 95% CI −0.24 to 0.05; 1 study, 41 participants; Analysis 2.1).

    Visualisation of gastric mucosa

    Six RCTs addressed visualisation of gastric mucosa (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). All used continuous and dichotomous measures.

    For continuous measurement, Ardakani 2013, Coffin 2002, and Habashi 2007 used a scale of 0 to 3 points; Carbonell 2006 used a scale of 0 to 10 points; and Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 used a scale of 0 to 8 points. In all studies, a higher score indicated a clearer stomach. Ardakani 2013 presented inconsistent continuous data that could not be used in the meta‐analysis of this outcome. Na 2017 reported a median of 8 (IQR not reported) in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 7 (IQR 5 to 8) in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group. Pateron 2011 reported a median of 8 (IQR 7 to 8) in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 8 (IQR 7 to 8) in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group. Given the skewed data (medians and IQRs), we were only able to meta‐analyse continuous data from Carbonell 2006, Coffin 2002, and Habashi 2007 (Measures of treatment effect). 

    • The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on visualisation of gastric mucosa (SMD 0.48, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.85; I2 = 25%; 3 studies, 170 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.2).

    For dichotomous measurement, four RCTs used the judgement of the endoscopist, with different definitions of a clear stomach ("good endoscopic status" in Ardakani 2013, "gastric mucosa entirely visualised" in Carbonell 2006 and Habashi 2007, and "empty stomach" in Pateron 2011). Meta‐analysis of these data showed no clear difference between erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage (erythromycin 79.76%, control 61.76%; RR 1.50, 95% CI 0.96 to 2.34; I2 = 84%; 4 studies, 338 participants; Analysis 2.3). Three RCTs used cutoff points on ordinal scales to classify the stomach as empty or not empty (2 or more on a 3‐point scale in Coffin 2002, and 6 or more on an 8‐point scale in Na 2017 and Pateron 2011). Meta‐analysis of these data did not clearly favour erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage over no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage (erythromycin 88.88%, no intervention 74.59%; RR 1.33, 95% CI 0.95 to 1.85; I2 = 69%; 3 studies, 239 participants; Analysis 2.3).

    Non‐serious adverse events

    Six RCTs addressed non‐serious adverse events (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Ardakani 2013, Habashi 2007, and Na 2017 reported no events in both groups. Carbonell 2006 reported gastric contractions that reduced the quality of initial endoscopy (10/49 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 7/50 in the placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage group). Coffin 2002 reported contractile activity that interfered with the quality of initial endoscopy or a haemostatic procedure (2/19 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 3/22 in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group). Pateron 2011 reported severe pain after nasogastric tube placement (20/84 in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 24/85 in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group). Two RTCs assessed non‐serious adverse events within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy (Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Four RCTs did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007).

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on non‐serious adverse events (erythromycin 15.92%, control 16.42%; RD 0.00, 95% CI −0.05 to 0.05; I2 = 0%; 6 studies, 408 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.4).

    Rebleeding

    Five RCTs addressed rebleeding (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Pateron 2011 addressed this outcome in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy) and reported 19/84 events in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 17/85 events in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on rebleeding in the short term (erythromycin 22.61%, no intervention 20.0%; RR 1.13, 95% CI 0.63 to 2.02; 1 study, 169 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.5).

    Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 addressed this outcome within 30 days of initial endoscopy. Meta‐analysis of these long‐term data showed no clear difference between the erythromycin and no intervention (erythromycin 22.4%, no intervention 19.0%; RR 1.07, 95% CI 0.61 to 1.87; I2 = 0%; 198 participants; Analysis 2.5).

    Three RCTs addressed this outcome indirectly by reporting the proportion of participants who had a second‐look endoscopy (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002). However, no studies specified how many of these procedures were indicated for rebleeding.

    Blood transfusion

    Six RCTs addressed blood transfusion (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011).

    Four RCTs addressed this outcome in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Pateron 2011). The unit of measurement was a standard unit of blood, as defined by the study authors (no studies reported the volume of each blood pack). Ardakani 2013 and Coffin 2002 assessed blood transfusion within 48 hours of initial endoscopy, Carbonell 2006 within 24 hours of initial endoscopy, and Pateron 2011 within one week of initial endoscopy. We were able to meta‐analyse data from three RCTs (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage compared with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage on blood transfusion (MD −1.85 standard units of blood, 95% CI −4.34 to 0.64; I2 = 93%; 3 studies, 180 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.6). Pateron 2011 reported a median of 2.5 (IQR 0 to 4.5) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group.

    Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 addressed blood transfusion within 30 days of initial endoscopy. We did not meta‐analyse these long‐term data as the RCTs provided only medians and IQRs, and we regarded these as skewed data (Measures of treatment effect). Na 2017 reported a median of 2 (IQR 0 to 3) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group versus 2 (IQR 0 to 3) units of blood transfusion in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group. Pateron 2011 reported a median of 3 (IQR 0 to 5) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group.

    Habashi 2007 did not specify the follow‐up duration for assessment of blood transfusion. The study reported a mean of 2.26 (SD not reported) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 2.86 (SD not reported) in the placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage group (95% CI not computed due to missing SDs; very low‐certainty evidence).

    Rescue invasive intervention

    Two RCTs addressed rescue invasive intervention (Coffin 2002; Na 2017). Coffin 2002 reported 1/19 events (emergency surgery) in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 3/22 events (emergency surgery) in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group; however, the study authors did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Na 2017 reported 0/14 events in the erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage group and 1/15 events (angiographic embolisation) in the no intervention plus nasogastric tube lavage group, within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy. Meta‐analysis of the data from these two RCTs showed no clear difference between erythromycin and no intervention (erythromycin 3.03%, control 10.81%; RR 0.38, 95% CI 0.06 to 2.24; I2 = 0%; 70 participants; Analysis 2.7).

    Comparison 3: Erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage

    Four RCTs, totaling 287 participants, compared erythromycin with nasogastric tube lavage (Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996).

    UGIH‐related mortality

    The following two RCTs (44 participants) addressed UGIH‐related mortality and reported no events in either group (very low‐certainty evidence).

    • Na 2017: 0/14 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/15 in the nasogastric tube lavage, within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy

    • Sears 1996: 0/6 events in the erythromycin group versus 0/9 in the nasogastric tube lavage; however, the study authors did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome.

    We did not perform subgroup or sensitivity analysis because meta‐analysis was not possible for this outcome. Only Na 2017 defined the aetiology of the haemorrhage, and included only participants with non‐variceal UGIH.

    Serious adverse events

    The following three RCTs (213 participants) addressed serious adverse events and reported no events in either group (very low‐certainty evidence).

    • Na 2017: 0/14 in the erythromycin group versus 0/15 in the nasogastric tube lavage group, within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy

    • Pateron 2011: 0/84 in the erythromycin group versus 0/85 in the nasogastric tube lavage group, within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy

    • Sears 1996: 0/6 in the erythromycin group versus 0/9 in the nasogastric tube lavage (follow‐up duration not defined)

    We did not perform sensitivity or subgroup analysis because meta‐analysis was not possible for this outcome. Only Na 2017 specified the aetiology of haemorrhage, and included only participants with non‐variceal UGIH.

    All‐cause mortality

    Three RCTs addressed all‐cause mortality (Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996). Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 reported this outcome at one week and 30 days. Sears 1996 did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome; however, the study authors reported no events in either group (0/6 in the erythromycin group versus 0/9 in the nasogastric tube lavage). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage on all‐cause mortality in the short term (erythromycin 6.73%, nasogastric tube 4.58%; RD 0.02, 95% CI −0.05 to 0.08; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 213 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.1). Meta‐analysis showed no clear difference between erythromycin and nasogastric tube lavage in the long term (erythromycin 9.18%, nasogastric tube 9.0%; RD 0.00, 95% CI −0.07 to 0.08; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 198 participants; Analysis 3.1).

    Visualisation of gastric mucosa

    Four RCTs addressed visualisation of gastric mucosa (Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996).

    All four RCTs measured this outcome on a continuous scale, but we could not perform a meta‐analysis because Guardiola 2009 did not define the measure of variability; Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 provided only medians and IQRs, which we regarded as skewed data (Measures of treatment effect); and Sears 1996 did not report the SD. The results of the individual RCTs are narratively described as follows.

    • Guardiola 2009 reported values of "8 ± 2.5" (not clear if SD or SE) in the erythromycin group and "7.8 ± 2.2" in the nasogastric tube lavage group for the outcome "subjective scale" (0 to 10 points, higher means cleaner); and values of "8.89 ± 2.5" in the erythromycin group and "8.64 ± 1.98" in the nasogastric tube lavage group for the outcome "objective scale" (0 to 10, higher means cleaner).

    • Na 2017 used a scale ranging from 0 to 8 points (higher means cleaner) and reported a median of 8 (IQR 6 to 8) in the erythromycin group and 7 (IQR 5 to 8) in the nasogastric tube lavage group.

    • Pateron 2011 used a scale ranging from 0 to 8 points (higher means cleaner) and reported a median of 8 (IQR not reported) in the erythromycin group and 8 (IQR 7 to 8) in the nasogastric tube lavage group.

    • Sears 1996 used a scale ranging from 0 to 3 pointes (higher means cleaner) and reported a mean of 0.67 (SD not reported) in the erythromycin group and 1.89 (SD not reported) in the nasogastric tube lavage group.

    Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 reported visualisation of gastric mucosa with dichotomous measures. Pateron 2011 reported the judgement of the endoscopist, who decided whether participants had an "empty stomach"; this was the case in 69/84 participants in the erythromycin group and 68/85 participants in the nasogastric tube lavage group (erythromycin 82.14%, nasogastric tube 80.0%; RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.89 to 1.19; 1 study, 169 participants; Analysis 3.2). Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 used a cutoff point on an ordinal scale (6 or more points on an 8‐point scale) to classify the stomach as empty or not empty. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage on visualisation of the gastric mucosa (erythromycin 84.69%, nasogastric tube 79.0%; RR 1.19, 95% CI 0.79 to 1.79; I2 = 70%; 2 studies, 198 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.2).

    Three RCTs addressed non‐serious adverse events (Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996). Na 2017 reported no events in either group (0/14 in the erythromycin group versus 0/15 in the nasogastric tube lavage group), within one week and 30 days from the initial endoscopy. Pateron 2011 reported 0/84 events in the erythromycin group and 24/85 events (severe pain) in the nasogastric tube lavage group, within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy. Sears 1996 reported no events in either group (0/6 in the erythromycin group versus 0/9 in the nasogastric tube lavage group) but did not specify the follow‐up duration for this outcome. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage on non‐serious adverse events (erythromycin 0%, nasogastric tube 22.01%; RD −0.10, 95% CI −0.34 to 0.13; I2 = 89%; 3 studies, 213 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.3).

    Rebleeding

    Two RCTs addressed rebleeding (Na 2017; Pateron 2011).

    Pateron 2011 assessed this outcome in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy), and reported 13/84 events in the erythromycin group and 17/85 events in the nasogastric tube lavage group. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of erythromycin compared with nasogastric tube lavage on rebleeding in the short term (erythromycin 15.47%, nasogastric tube 20.0%; RR 0.77, 95% CI 0.40 to 1.49; 1 study, 169 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.4)

    Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 assessed rebleeding within 30 days of initial endoscopy. Meta‐analysis of these long‐term data showed no clear difference between erythromycin and nasogastric tube lavage (erythromycin 20.40%, nasogastric tube 19.0%; RR 1.07, 95% CI 0.61 to 1.87; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 198 participants; Analysis 3.4).

    Blood transfusion

    Four RCTs addressed blood transfusion (Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996). The unit of measurement was a standard unit of blood, as defined by the study authors (no studies reported the volume of each blood pack).

    Pateron 2011 (169 participants) addressed this outcome in the short term (within one week of initial endoscopy) and reported a median of 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin group and 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in the nasogastric tube lavage group (very low‐certainty evidence).

    Na 2017 and Pateron 2011 assessed blood transfusion in the long term (within 30 days of initial endoscopy). We did not meta‐analyse their results because both studies provided only medians and IQRs, which we regarded as skewed data (Measures of treatment effect). Na 2017 reported a median of 1 (IQR 0 to 2) unit of blood transfusion in the erythromycin group and 2 (IQR 0 to 3) units of blood transfusion in the nasogastric tube lavage group. Pateron 2011 reported a median of 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in the erythromycin group and 2 (IQR 0 to 4) units of blood transfusion in the nasogastric tube lavage group.

    Guardiola 2009 and Sears 1996 assessed blood transfusion but provided no data and no definition of the follow‐up duration. Both RCTs stated that there was no difference in transfusion requirements between the groups.

    Rescue invasive intervention

    Two RCTs addressed rescue invasive intervention (Na 2017; Sears 1996). Na 2017 reported 0/14 events in the erythromycin group and 1/15 events (angiographic embolisation) in the nasogastric tube lavage group within one week and 30 days of initial endoscopy. Sears 1996 reported 0/6 events in the erythromycin group and 2/9 events (surgery) in the nasogastric tube lavage group, but did not report the follow‐up duration for this outcome. Meta‐analysis of these data showed no clear difference between erythromycin and nasogastric tube lavage in the short term (erythromycin 0%, nasogastric tube 12.5%; RR 0.32, 95% CI 0.04 to 2.62; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 44 participants; Analysis 3.5) or in the long term (erythromycin 0%, nasogastric tube 6.66%; RR 0.36, 95% CI 0.02 to 8.07; 1 study, 29 participants; Analysis 3.5).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This review included 11 RCTs with a parallel‐group design, published between 1996 and 2020, with a total of 878 participants. In all studies, a group of participants were randomised to erythromycin before initial upper endoscopy for UGIH. We identified four comparisons for this review.

    Erythromycin versus no intervention/placebo

    Three RCTs compared erythromycin with placebo (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002). There was low‐certainty evidence, based on continuous data, that erythromycin may improve visualisation of the gastric mucosa; however, the clinical relevance of this finding is unknown. One RCT showed that erythromycin may result in a slight reduction in the number of red blood cells packs transfused, with low‐certainty evidence. For all other outcomes, the evidence is very uncertain about the effects of erythromycin in the short or long term.

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage

    Six RCTs compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage on all outcomes in the short or long term.

    Erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage

    Four RCTs compared erythromycin with nasogastric tube lavage (Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of erythromycin on all outcomes in the short or long term.

    Erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage

    One RCT compared erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage with metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage (Habashi 2007). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage on all reported outcomes (serious adverse events, visualisation of gastric mucosa, non‐serious adverse events, and blood transfusion).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    This review aimed to assess whether erythromycin administered to people with UGIH was effective and safe for clearing the stomach before initial endoscopy. There was scarce evidence addressing this issue, and only 11 studies met our inclusion criteria.

    Participants

    Only two RCTs differentiated non‐variceal from variceal aetiology, and each included only one aetiology in the sample (Altraif 2011; Na 2017). The remaining nine RCTs did not distinguish between participants with non‐variceal or variceal haemorrhage, only between those who did or did not have cirrhosis. However, many participants with cirrhosis may have bleeding of a non‐variceal aetiology, such as a peptic ulcer. No studies correlated the specific source of bleeding (e.g. peptic ulcer, oesophageal varices, stomach varices, or Mallory‐Weiss) with any outcome.

    Interventions

    We identified two different erythromycin interventions: erythromycin as monotherapy (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002; Guardiola 2009; Na 2017; Pateron 2011; Sears 1996) and erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage (Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). As nasogastric tube lavage is no longer recommended, our review findings may have limited applicability in clinical practice. In addition, the included RCTs administered different doses of erythromycin, ranging from 125 mg to 500 mg (parenteral and oral); this may have introduced heterogeneity to the magnitude of the effect and adverse events.

    We identified three comparators, all of which were highly valid for determining the real effectiveness of erythromycin in clinical practice: placebo/no intervention, nasogastric tube lavage, and another prokinetic drug (metoclopramide). However, the small samples with few events reduced the certainty of the evidence according to GRADE criteria, limiting our conclusions about the effectiveness and safety of erythromycin versus these comparators.

    Outcomes

    Only five RCTs reported both of the primary outcomes planned in this review (UGIH‐related mortality and serious adverse events; Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Frossard 2002; Na 2017; Sears 1996). There were no UGIH‐related deaths and only two serious adverse events (aspiration pneumonia), which limits the certainty of the evidence in GRADE and our conclusions about the effectiveness and safety of the intervention.

    We found heterogeneity between studies in the method of assessing gastric mucosa visibility. Some studies used a discrete numerical scale, some determined a cutoff point on a scale to dichotomise the outcome, and some used the opinion of the endoscopist (e.g. clean stomach, yes or no). We were unable to determine the clinical relevance of improvement or worsening by one or more points on the gastric mucosa visibility scales when we meta‐analysed the results. The only way to overcome this limitation would be to standardise gastric mucosa visualisation scales and define the real clinical benefit or harm of variation by one or more points.

    Only two RCTS directly assessed rebleeding after endoscopy (Na 2017; Pateron 2011). The remaining studies evaluated rebleeding rate indirectly through the proportion of participants who had a second‐look endoscopy. However, rebleeding is not the only motive for second‐look endoscopies, as participants may present haemodynamic changes for other reasons. Therefore, we chose not to combine second‐look results with rebleed results.

    Nine RCTs assessed the need for blood transfusion (Ali Shah 2020; Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Habashi 2007; Na 2017; Pateron 2011); however, only Altraif 2011 provided information (via email) on the type of infused blood component (red blood cells) and the volume of each pack (250 mL to 275 mL). For an accurate comparison, study authors should report exactly the type of transfused blood product (red blood cells, plasma, or platelets) and the precise volume administered to each participant during hospitalisation. Regarding follow‐up time, two studies reported units transfused at seven days (short term) and at 30 days (long term; Na 2017; Pateron 2011). Five studies limited follow‐up of blood transfusion to 24 hours or 48 hours after initial endoscopy (Altraif 2011; Ardakani 2013; Carbonell 2006; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002). This seems insufficient, as many participants may have required a blood transfusion more than 48 hours after the initial endoscopy.

    Five RCTs reported the outcome of rescue invasive intervention (Altraif 2011; Coffin 2002; Frossard 2002; Na 2017; Sears 1996). However, the small number of events (12 in total) limited the certainty of the evidence in GRADE and our conclusions about the effectiveness and safety of the intervention.

    Regarding subgroup analysis, no studies stratified results by participant age or comorbidities.

    Clinical guidelines published in 2021 on the use of erythromycin in non‐variceal UGIH recommend "an infusion of erythromycin before endoscopy in patients with UGIB (conditional recommendation, very‐low‐quality evidence)" (Laine 2021). The same guidelines state that "although evidence was lacking for benefit of erythromycin in reducing further bleeding and mortality, erythromycin provided meaningful reductions in repeat endoscopies and length of hospitalizations: e.g., the upper bound of the 95% CI suggested at least a 1‐day decrease in hospitalization. The economic benefit of reduced procedures and hospital stay, as well as the presumed desire of patients to avoid additional procedures and hospital days, relatively low cost, and ease of administration, led the panel to a conditional recommendation for use of pre‐endoscopic erythromycin". Similarly, a guideline on variceal UGIH published in 2022 suggests that "in the absence of contraindications (QT prolongation), pre‐endoscopy infusion of erythromycin (250 mg IV 30‐120 minutes before endoscopy) should be considered (B.1)" (de Franchis 2022). We did not find sufficient indirect evidence to reach any conclusion on the need for further endoscopy (through the rebleeding outcome), and we did not assess length of hospital stay or costs. We found low‐certainty evidence indicating improved stomach visualisation and a slight reduction in the need for transfusion when comparing erythromycin to placebo. As visualisation of the gastric mucosa is a surrogate secondary outcome, and the possible reduction in blood transfusion is slight, we could not reach any firm conclusions about the benefits and harms of erythromycin.

    Summary

    The studies included in this review were few and small, with few reported events. In addition, data were heterogeneous in terms of populations (mixture of non‐variceal and variceal aetiology), comparisons (different routes and doses of erythromycin and different comparators), and outcomes (inaccurate measurements and non‐standard tools, scales without precise clinical correspondence). The literature has not investigated the most relevant types of participants, interventions and outcomes.

    Quality of the evidence

    The evidence on all outcomes assessed in this review was of low or very low certainty.

    In the comparison of erythromycin versus placebo, the global certainty of the evidence was low and very low (summary of findings Table 1). The main reasons for downgrading the evidence were risk of bias (incomplete outcome data, baseline imbalance, and selective reporting) and imprecision (small sample size, low number of events, and wide 95% CI).

    In the comparison of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus no intervention/placebo plus nasogastric tube lavage, the global certainty of the evidence was very low for all outcomes (summary of findings Table 2). The reasons for downgrading the evidence were risk of bias (blinding of participants and personnel, blinding of outcome assessment, selective reporting, and baseline imbalance), inconsistency (high heterogeneity), and imprecision (small sample size, low number of events, and wide 95% CI).

    In the comparison of erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage, the global certainty of the evidence was very low for all outcomes (summary of findings Table 3). The reasons for downgrading the evidence were risk of bias (blinding of participants and personnel, blinding of outcome assessment, and selective reporting), inconsistency (high heterogeneity), and imprecision (small sample size, low number of events, and wide 95% CI).

    In the comparison of erythromycin plus nasogastric tube lavage versus metoclopramide plus nasogastric tube lavage, the certainty of the evidence was very low for all outcomes (summary of findings Table 4). The reasons for downgrading the evidence were risk of bias (insufficient information for the judgement of all domains) and imprecision (small sample size, low number of events, wide 95% CI, and missing SDs).

    Potential biases in the review process

    We conducted this review with the highest rigour to minimise the risk of bias. We performed a comprehensive and sensitive literature search, without limitations on year of publication, language, or publication status. Two review authors independently assessed each study for inclusion, extracted data, and assessed study quality. We intended to include all eligible studies; however, one potentially eligible study was in Polish (Rudzki 2006) and, even with the rigorous translation, we may have missed some relevant content. It was unclear whether the study was an RCT, and as we received no response from the study authors after several attempts to contact them, we decided to exclude the study.

    Several attempts to obtain missing data were unsuccessful. We tried to contact the authors of all included studies by email and telephone. However, only the authors of Altraif 2011 replied. Therefore, there were missing data related to risk of bias, interventions, comparators, outcomes, and follow‐up analysis, and we could not perform the planned subgroup analyses.

    Regarding the synthesis of data, one RCT did not report SDs, so we could not compute CIs (Habashi 2007). Two RCTs reported central tendency and dispersion data as medians and IQRs, and we chose not meta‐analyse these data as it would require conversion to mean and CI (Na 2017; Pateron 2011; see Differences between protocol and review).

    • UGIH‐related mortality: no UGIH‐related deaths in either group

    • SAEs: evidence is very uncertain (RD −0.01, 95% CI −0.04 to 0.02).

    • All‐cause mortality: evidence is very uncertain (RD −0.00, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.03).

    • Visualisation of gastric mucosa: erythromycin may increase visualisation of gastric mucosa (MD 3.63, 95% CI 2.2 to 5.05).

    • Blood transfusion: erythromycin may result in a slight reduction in blood transfusion (MD −0.44, 95% CI −0.86 to −0.01).

    • Rescue invasive intervention: evidence is very uncertain (RD 0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.06).

     

    CI: confidence interval; MD: mean difference; OR: odds ratio; RD: risk difference; RR: risk ratio; SAE: serious adverse event; UGIH: upper gastrointestinal haemorrhage; WMD: weighted mean difference.

    We identified six reviews that attempted to answer similar questions to those addressed in this systematic review (Bai 2011; Barkun 2010; Guixiang 2016; Rahman 2016; Szary 2011; Theivanayagam 2013). The most recent review is from 2016 and the oldest is from 2010.

    Four reviews did not perform a sensitive literature search, restricting the search strategy to some free texts terms (Bai 2011; Guixiang 2016; Szary 2011; Theivanayagam 2013). Three reviews performed the search in only a few databases (e.g. CINAHL not included; Bai 2011; Barkun 2010; Guixiang 2016). Four reviews restricted the search by language of publication (Barkun 2010 included only English and French RCTs, Guixiang 2016 included only English RCTs, and Rahman 2016 and Szary 2011 included only English and Polish RCTs). Bai 2011 limited the search results to fully reported RCTs with detailed information.

    Barkun 2010 and Rahman 2016 included only RCTs that compared erythromycin versus placebo or no intervention. Theivanayagam 2013 did not include RCTs that compared erythromycin versus nasogastric tube lavage (only studies with nasogastric tube lavage in both treatment arms). As a result, these reviews missed some relevant RCTs.

    All reviews included a smaller number of RCTs with an erythromycin group than our systematic review (one in Guixiang 2016; four Bai 2011, Barkun 2010, and Szary 2011; seven in Theivanayagam 2013; and eight in Rahman 2016). Rahman 2016, Szary 2011, and Theivanayagam 2013 included a study with an unclear design (Rudzki 2006).

    Four reviews analysed risk of bias using less accurate tools (Bai 2011 used "empirical evidence"; and Barkun 2010, Szary 2011, and Theivanayagam 2013 used the Jadad scoring system). Only Guixiang 2016 and Rahman 2016 used RoB 1.

    All previous reviews combined studies with different comparisons in the same analysis for all reported outcomes, and all reviews included primary outcomes that were not focused on participants, such as visualisation of gastric mucosa and need for second‐look endoscopy.

    Regarding the most frequently reported outcome (visualisation of gastric mucosa), all previous reviews reported a better visualisation of the gastric mucosa in the erythromycin group. However, they combined different comparators, and did not specify which visualisation scales were addressed, nor how they were combined.

    No previous reviews distinguished between participants with non‐variceal haemorrhage and those with variceal haemorrhage. Guixiang 2016 included only RCTs with cirrhotic participants.
