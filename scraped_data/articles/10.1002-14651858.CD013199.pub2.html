Halo sign on temporal artery ultrasound versus temporal artery biopsy for giant cell arteritis

    • Background

    Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is a systemic, inflammatory vasculitis primarily affecting people over the age of 50 years. GCA is treated as a medical emergency due to the potential for sudden, irreversible visual loss. Temporal artery biopsy (TAB) is one of the five criteria of the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) 1990 classification, which is used to aid the diagnosis of GCA. TAB is an invasive test, and it can be slow to obtain a result due to delays in performing the procedure and the time taken for histopathologic assessment. Temporal artery ultrasonography (US) has been demonstrated to show findings in people with GCA such as the halo sign (a hypoechoic circumferential wall thickening due to oedema), stenosis or occlusion that can help to confirm a diagnosis more swiftly and less invasively, but requiring more subjective interpretation. This review will help to determine the role of these investigations in clinical practice.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the sensitivity and specificity of the halo sign on temporal artery US, using the ACR 1990 classification as a reference standard, to investigate whether US could be used as triage for TAB.

    To compare the accuracy of US with TAB in the subset of paired studies that have obtained both tests on the same patients, to investigate whether it could replace TAB as one of the criteria in the ACR 1990 classification.

    Search methods

    We used standard Cochrane search methods for diagnostic accuracy. The date of the search was 13 September 2022.

    Selection criteria

    We included all participants with clinically suspected GCA who were investigated for the presence of the halo sign on temporal artery US, using the ACR 1990 criteria as a reference standard. We included studies with participants with a prior diagnosis of polymyalgia rheumatica. We excluded studies if participants had had two or more weeks of steroid treatment prior to the investigations.

    We also included any comparative test accuracy studies of the halo sign on temporal artery US versus TAB, with use of the 1990 ACR diagnostic criteria as a reference standard. Although we have chosen to use this classification for the purpose of the meta‐analysis, we accept that it incorporates unavoidable incorporation bias, as TAB is itself one of the five criteria. This increases the specificity of TAB, making it difficult to compare with US.

    We excluded case‐control studies, as they overestimate accuracy, as well as case series in which all participants had a prior diagnosis of GCA, as they can only address sensitivity and not specificity.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed the studies for inclusion in the review. They extracted data using a standardised data collection form and employed the QUADAS‐2 tool to assess methodological quality. As not enough studies reported data at our prespecified halo threshold of 0.3 mm, we fitted hierarchical summary receiver operating characteristic (ROC) models to estimate US sensitivity and also to compare US with TAB. We graded the certainty of the evidence using the GRADE approach.

    Main results

    Temporal artery ultrasound was investigated in 15 studies (617 participants with GCA out of 1479, 41.7%), with sample sizes ranging from 20 to 381 participants (median 69). There was wide variation in sensitivity with a median value of 0.78 (interquartile range (IQR) 0.45 to 0.83; range 0.03 to 1.00), while specificity was fair to good in most studies with a median value of 0.91 (IQR 0.78 to 1.00; range 0.40 to 1.00) and four studies with a specificity of 1.00. The hierarchical summary receiver operating characteristic (HSROC) estimate of sensitivity (95% confidence interval (CI)) at the high specificity of 0.95 was 0.51 (0.21 to 0.81), and 0.84 (0.58 to 0.95) at 0.80 specificity. We considered the evidence on sensitivity and specificity as of very low certainty due to risk of bias (−1), imprecision (−1), and inconsistency (−1).

    Only four studies reported data at a halo cut‐off > 0.3 mm, finding the following sensitivities and specificities (95% CI): 0.80 (0.56 to 0.94) and 0.94 (0.81 to 0.99) in 55 participants; 0.10 (0.00 to 0.45) and 1.00 (0.84 to 1.00) in 31 participants; 0.73 (0.54 to 0.88) and 1.00 (0.93 to 1.00) in 82 participants; 0.83 (0.63 to 0.95) and 0.72 (0.64 to 0.79) in 182 participants.

    Data on a direct comparison of temporal artery US with biopsy were obtained from 11 studies (808 participants; 460 with GCA, 56.9%). The sensitivity of US ranged between 0.03 and 1.00 with a median of 0.75, while that of TAB ranged between 0.33 and 0.92 with a median of 0.73. The specificity was 1.00 in four studies for US and in seven for TAB. At high specificity (0.95), the sensitivity of US and TAB were 0.50 (95% CI 0.24 to 0.76) versus 0.80 (95% CI 0.57 to 0.93), respectively, and at low specificity (0.80) they were 0.73 (95% CI 0.49 to 0.88) versus 0.92 (95% CI 0.69 to 0.98). We considered the comparative evidence on the sensitivity of US versus TAB to be of very low certainty because specificity was overestimated for TAB since it is one of the criteria used in the reference standard (−1), together with downgrade due to risk of bias (−1), imprecision (−1), and inconsistency (−1) for both sensitivity and specificity.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is limited published evidence on the accuracy of temporal artery US for detecting GCA. Ultrasound seems to be moderately sensitive when the specificity is good, but data were heterogeneous across studies and either did not use the same halo thickness threshold or did not report it. We can draw no conclusions from accuracy studies on whether US can replace TAB for diagnosing GCA given the very low certainty of the evidence. Future research could consider using the 2016 revision of the ACR criteria as a reference standard, which will limit incorporation bias of TAB into the reference standard.

    • How useful is ultrasound for diagnosing giant cell arteritis?

    Key messages

    • Whether ultrasound (US) can replace biopsy for the diagnosis of giant cell arteritis (GCA) is uncertain.

    • We suggest that future studies use a different set of criteria to determine whether patients have GCA to allow US and biopsy to be compared more reliably.

    What is giant cell arteritis?

    Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is an inflammatory disease that affects blood vessels. It causes swelling and blockage of the arteries, particularly around the eye and at the side of the head, which can lead to sudden and permanent loss of vision.

    Why is it important to diagnose giant cell arteritis accurately?

    It is important to diagnose GCA accurately and quickly. Vision loss caused by the disease cannot be reversed, and it can affect both eyes within a few days. This may be prevented by starting one of a group of medications called steroids, which are used to reduce inflammation. It is important to have an accurate diagnosis, as a long course of treatment is required, and steroids can have a number of unpleasant side effects.

    What tests can be used to diagnose giant cell arteritis?

    The American College of Rheumatology (ACR) has written a list of five clinical and laboratory criteria that can be used to diagnose GCA. One of these is the results of a biopsy of an artery on the temple (side of the head). With biopsy, a small sample of the artery is taken and examined under a microscope for signs of inflammation. A biopsy is a small operation, which can take time to be arranged, performed, and interpreted, and it can be quite a while before the results are known.

    Why is the new test potentially better than the reference standard?

    Ultrasound (US) is a painless and relatively quick procedure that can be used to look at the arteries in both temples and assist with the diagnosis.

    Who will benefit, why and how?

    If US is accurate, people who have symptoms and blood results suggestive of GCA will find out if they have the condition more quickly, without needing to undergo an operation.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out how accurate temporal artery US is at detecting features of GCA and whether it can potentially replace the biopsy altogether as one of the ACR's five criteria used for diagnosing GCA.

    What did we do?

    We searched for published studies that measured the accuracy of US for diagnosing GCA and studies that also compared the accuracy of US and biopsy.

    What did we find?

    We looked in depth at 16 studies with a total of 1479 participants, about 40% of whom were diagnosed as having GCA. In a group of 100 people with symptoms suggestive of GCA, we would expect 40 to actually have the condition.

    If we set a threshold for US where 3 of the 60 people without the disease get a false‐positive result, US will correctly identify 20 people that do have GCA, but give a false‐negative result to the other 20. If a threshold is set where 12 of the 60 people without the disease get a false‐positive result, US will correctly identify 34 people with GCA, but miss the remaining 6.

    There were 11 studies with 808 participants in whom both US and TAB were performed and compared. In a group of 100 people where 40 have GCA and 60 do not, if a threshold is set where 3 out of the 60 people without the condition are wrongly given a positive diagnosis of GCA, US will identify 20 out of the 40 people with GCA, whereas TAB will identify 32. If a threshold is set whereby 12 of the 60 people are wrongly diagnosed as having GCA, US will identify 29 out of the 40 people with true GCA, and TAB will identify 7.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    These results are not reliable since most studies had flaws in the way they were designed, and their accuracy for detecting people with GCA was variable and uncertain. Additionally, as biopsy is currently part of the criteria used to diagnose GCA, it was always likely to compare favourably to US in the included studies.

    How up‐to‐date is this evidence?

    The evidence is current to September 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is insufficient reliable evidence on the accuracy of temporal artery ultrasound (US) for detecting giant cell arteritis (GCA). Ultrasound seems to be only moderately sensitive when specificity is good, but data were heterogeneous across studies, and either did not use the same halo thickness threshold or did not report it. Although this analysis provides some evidence that US and temporal artery biopsy (TAB) have different diagnostic performances, evidence for this direct comparison is not reliable. In addition, the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) criteria include unavoidable incorporation bias, as TAB is itself one of the five criteria, which may have increased the specificity of TAB, making it difficult to compare with US.

    While this review does not provide a rationale for replacement of TAB with US, a high specificity of temporal artery US was recorded in most studies. This could suggest a potential role for US as a triage test in future studies, with patients who have a negative US going on to have TAB.

    Implications for research

    Further research is needed to establish the diagnostic accuracy of various imaging modalities utilised to investigate for GCA. Studies investigating the accuracy of temporal artery US for diagnosing GCA should report sensitivity and specificity at several halo cut‐offs, particularly at 0.3 mm. The accuracy of TAB as a single diagnostic criterion should also be reported to facilitate direct comparisons with US. In addition, in 2016 a revision of the ACR criteria was published (Salehi Abari 2016), with 10 components weighted according to their significance. The sensitivity and specificity of these criteria have been found to be similar or better when compared to the original 1990 classification (Armagan 2019; Sait 2017). However, the relative importance of TAB is reduced by the inclusion of the additional criteria. Future studies comparing TAB to US could consider using the revised criteria as a more objective measure for their reference diagnosis, either instead of or alongside clinical judgement. Finally, studies should make raw data available or report subgroup data on how the index test was implemented (e.g. unilateral versus bilateral testing, different halo thresholds, examiner training).

    Background

    Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is a systemic, inflammatory vasculitis, primarily affecting people over the age of 50 years. GCA is treated as a medical emergency, due to the potential for sudden, irreversible visual loss. This occurs in approximately 15% to 20% of people with GCA, and can be either unilateral or bilateral (Gonzalez‐Gay 2000; Liozon 2013). While early treatment with high‐dose corticosteroids is currently recommended by the British Society of Rheumatology to reduce the risk of visual loss (Dasgupta 2010), it has been associated with adverse events in over 80% of patients, including cataracts, fractures, infections, hypertension, and diabetes mellitus (Proven 2003; Van der Goes 2014). A prompt and accurate diagnosis is therefore vital, to ensure that vision is preserved while avoiding unnecessarily exposing patients to a potentially toxic treatment.

    The currently recognised gold standard for the diagnosis of GCA is the American College of Rheumatology (ACR) 1990 classification (Hunder 1990), which, on comparison of 214 GCA patients with 593 patients with other forms of vasculitis, has been shown to have a sensitivity of 93.5% and a specificity of 91.2%. The ACR classification states that a person can be diagnosed as having GCA if they meet three of the following five criteria:

    • aged 50 years or more at disease onset;

    • new onset of localised headache;

    • temporal artery tenderness or decreased temporal artery pulse;

    • elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (Westergren) 50 mm/hour or above; or

    • abnormal temporal artery biopsy showing vasculitis with mononuclear cell or granulomatous inflammation, usually with giant cell infiltrates.

    Since the publication of the ACR classification, it has been shown that ultrasonography could also play an important role in the diagnosis of GCA, with the most specific finding being the 'halo sign', a dark halo around the vessel wall, most likely due to oedema (Schmidt 1997b).

    Previous meta‐analyses have suggested variable results. An analysis conducted by Arida and colleagues (encompassing eight prospective studies with 575 participants, compared against 1990 ACR criteria) found 68% sensitivity and 91% specificity for the unilateral halo sign, and 43% sensitivity and 100% specificity for the bilateral sign (Arida 2010). An analysis by Ball and colleagues (encompassing 17 studies and 998 participants, compared against either temporal artery biopsy or the 1990 ACR criteria), suggested a sensitivity of 75% and specificity of 83%. Although testing accuracy is partially dependent on ultrasonographer training, ultrasonography presents a cheap, non‐invasive, and quick diagnostic procedure (Ball 2010).

    Alternative imaging modalities include high‐resolution magnetic resonance imaging (1‐5‐3T), which has value in its ability to map cranial vessel involvement. However, testing is limited by poor visualisation of smaller vessels and the high cost of the procedure. For whole‐body imaging of larger vessels, positron emission tomography can be used, showing uptake of 18‐fluoro‐2 deoxy‐D‐glucose (F18‐FDG) by inflammatory cells in affected vasculature. However, this incurs 10 to 17 mSv of radiation exposure, allows poor visualisation of smaller vessels, and is also costly (Ness 2013; Pipitone 2008).

    More evaluation is clearly needed, both as to the role of ultrasound as a diagnostic test in isolation as well as in comparison with temporal artery biopsy. Indeed, it must be noted that temporal artery biopsy is an expensive and invasive procedure, with a complication rate of 0.5%, and can lead to formation of a haematoma, facial nerve injury, or occasionally stroke (Ball 2010). Given that ultrasound is cheaper, less invasive, and more readily available than temporal artery biopsy, it has been suggested that it be done prior to biopsy, and that in some cases it may obviate the need for biopsy altogether (Arida 2010).

    For the reasons presented above, a systematic review of the accuracy of the halo sign, measured with ultrasonography, to detect GCA is warranted.

    Target condition being diagnosed

    GCA is a systemic vasculitis of unknown aetiology, characterised by segmental, granulomatous inflammation of medium and large arteries, particularly affecting branches of the carotid artery (Dasgupta 2010). It has an incidence of 2.2 per 10,000 in the UK (Smeeth 2006), but has been shown to vary worldwide, with the highest incidence rates reported in Scandinavian populations, and lower rates in Japan and Turkey (Petri 2015). The most common comorbidity reported is polymyalgia rheumatica, which has a cited relative risk of 14.9 (Petri 2015). Associations have also been drawn between low socioeconomic status and severity of ischaemic manifestations (Mackie 2011).

    GCA has the potential to cause significant morbidity, such as transient or permanent visual loss, aortic aneurysm, and stroke. Glucocorticosteroids have been the main treatment for GCA since the 1950s. Treatment is effective for the prevention of visual loss; however, in cases where visual loss has already occurred, recovery of vision is uncommon (Danesh‐Meyer 2005). The multiple adverse side effects of glucocorticoid steroid treatment are widely recognised and include fractures, diabetes mellitus, gastrointestinal bleeding, infection, and cataracts. A higher dose and a longer duration of treatment are associated with an increased frequency of adverse side effects (Van der Goes 2014). Newer biologic therapies, such as tocilizumab, an anti‐interleukin‐6 receptor monoclonal antibody, may present a steroid‐sparing alternative treatment. In the phase two trial setting, tocilizumab, when used as an adjunct to corticosteroid, was demonstrated to increase the likelihood of achieving and maintaining remission (Villiger 2016). Availability of more accurate diagnostic testing would not only prevent unnecessary steroid treatment, but could also be used in order to validate the use of expensive newer therapies in the clinical setting.

    Index test(s)

    This review evaluated the sensitivity and specificity of the halo sign on temporal artery ultrasound, using the ACR 1990 classification as a reference standard.

    In the presence of arteritis, temporal artery ultrasonography has been demonstrated to show findings such as the halo sign (a hypoechoic circumferential wall thickening due to oedema), stenoses, and occlusions. Ultrasonography must be performed within two to four weeks of steroid treatment, as arterial oedema has been noted to resolve following this time (Karahaliou 2006; Schmidt 1997b). To ensure accurate results, temporal artery ultrasonography should be undertaken by a trained ultrasonographer. In accordance with the Vascular Technology Professional Performance Guidelines (VTPPG), a duplex Doppler ultrasound machine with a linear transducer and imaging frequencies of 12 MHz and greater should be used, frequencies of at least 4 MHz should be available with colour capability. Using B‐mode, an image of the artery and assessment of the halo sign, a dark hypoechoic area around the vessel lumen can be observed. The halo should be circumferential, present in at least two planes, and should be more than 0.3 mm thick (VTPPG 2015). A halo thickness of 0.7 mm or greater is thought to be predictive of a positive biopsy result. In addition, axillary arteries may also be imaged, which may increase sensitivity, and spectral Doppler used (with colour) to assess flow and stenoses (VTPPG 2015). Of these findings, a controlled study by Karahaliou and colleagues demonstrated that only the halo sign was of diagnostic value, as blood flow alterations (occlusions or stenoses) are also common in macrovascular disease secondary to atherosclerotic changes, and therefore are neither sensitive nor specific for GCA (Karahaliou 2006). As resolution of the halo sign occurs with treatment, repeat ultrasound scanning also has value in monitoring response to treatment (Karahaliou 2006; Pipitone 2008).

    This review also evaluated the sensitivity and specificity of temporal artery biopsy, compared with the halo sign on temporal artery ultrasound, using the ACR 1990 classification as a reference standard. The temporal artery is the smaller of two terminal branches of the external carotid and can be safely ligated due to anastomoses with the supraorbital artery. It is crossed by the facial nerve but can be dissected above this point in the temporoparietal fascia, where it lies posteriorly to the superficial temporal vein and anteriorly to the auriculotemporal nerve. Histological analysis of the haematoxylin‐ and eosin‐stained superficial temporal artery biopsy specimen is then conducted. Hallmark features include mononuclear inflammatory cell infiltrate and necrosis, and giant cell formation, intimal thickening, and luminal stenosis. Complications include pain, bleeding, scarring, nerve damage, haematoma formation, infection, and, importantly, a non‐diagnostic pathology result, due to inadequate sampling, skip lesions, or prior steroid therapy (Pieri 2013). A negative biopsy does not exclude GCA, and specificity and sensitivity reduces with duration of steroid therapy. Biopsy is advised within the first week, but in some cases may also yield a positive result at six weeks following steroid therapy (Ray‐Chaudhuri 2002).

    Clinical pathway

    People with GCA can present with symptoms such as an acute onset headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, and general malaise. Presentation of sudden vision loss or diplopia is less common, occurring in around 20% of people (Gonzalez‐Gay 2000; Liozon 2013). On examination, patients may be found to have a tender or enlarged temporal artery, reduced visual acuity, or a visual field defect (Dasgupta 2010; Gonzalez‐Gay 2000).

    Current diagnosis of GCA, with use of the ACR 1990 criteria, can involve clinical, biochemical, and histological evaluation (Hunder 1990). As the clinical manifestation is relatively non‐specific, to reinforce the diagnosis, the patient will often receive a biopsy of a temporal artery. However, this is an expensive and invasive procedure, with risk of complications (Ball 2010). Biopsy is frequently delayed, and to reduce the risk of visual loss, steroid therapy is started prior to a definitive diagnosis. As steroid therapy reduces the diagnostic accuracy of histological confirmation, a negative biopsy result, which can occur in up to 44% of cases with prior steroid treatment, is often unable to rule out GCA, limiting the value of biopsy in the clinical setting (Davies 2011).

    Rationale

    Three previous meta‐analyses looking at the use of ultrasound in the diagnosis of GCA have been published (Arida 2010; Ball 2010; Karassa 2005), the most recent of which was published in 2010. Since then, a number of prospective studies have been published, providing evidence both for and against the use of ultrasound in place of temporal artery biopsy. A 2018 review of large vessel vasculitis contained a meta‐analysis of the diagnostic performance of the halo sign on ultrasound, but only included eight studies (Duftner 2018). An updated meta‐analysis would therefore be a useful tool for clinicians involved in diagnosing GCA.

    People presenting with a clinical picture that is suspicious for GCA could have temporal artery ultrasound performed as a first‐line investigation. The ultrasound could be used to identify which people require a temporal artery biopsy for diagnosis, or, when used in conjunction with clinical and biochemical findings, it could replace temporal artery biopsy altogether (Croft 2015).

    The intended test role of ultrasound is triage before temporal artery biopsy, if temporal artery biopsy is considered: a sensitivity of at least 95% with a minimum specificity of 50% is required to rule out GCA and avoid biopsy in patients who test positive; a specificity of 95% and a sensitivity of 50% is required to rule in GCA and refer for biopsy those patients who test negative.

    Objectives

    To determine the diagnostic accuracy of the 'halo sign' on temporal artery ultrasound in people with suspected GCA, using the ACR 1990 classification as a reference standard. We considered two test roles, as follows.

    • Triage test before temporal artery biopsy, if temporal artery biopsy is considered: a sensitivity of at least 95% with a minimum specificity of 50% is required to rule out GCA and avoid biopsy in patients who test positive; a specificity of 95% and a sensitivity of 50% is required to rule in GCA and refer for biopsy patients who test negative.

    • Replacement of temporal artery biopsy as one of the ACR 1990 criteria: this is achieved if ultrasound shows sensitivity and specificity similar to biopsy.

    Secondary objectives

    To investigate sources of heterogeneity: use of the unilateral versus bilateral halo sign; concurrent use versus no use of steroids; average age of participants; training of ultrasonographers; additional use of axillary artery ultrasound in conjunction with temporal artery evaluation; and different thresholds for the halo sign (0.3 mm (primary cut‐off ), 1.5 mm or 6 mm). However, data were insufficient to conduct such analyses in this version of the review.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included single‐test accuracy studies of the 'halo sign' on temporal artery ultrasound, with use of the 1990 ACR diagnostic criteria as a reference standard. We also included comparative test accuracy studies of the halo sign on temporal artery ultrasound versus temporal artery biopsy, with use of the 1990 ACR diagnostic criteria as a reference standard. It has been noted that following two to four weeks of corticosteroid treatment, resolution of the halo sign occurs in all patients (Karahaliou 2006). We therefore excluded all studies in which ultrasound was performed more than two weeks following formal diagnosis, to exclude patients with two or more weeks of steroid treatment.

    We included prospective and retrospective cross‐sectional series conducted in any setting, as well as randomised controlled trials. We excluded case series in which all participants had a prior diagnosis of GCA, as they can only address sensitivity and not specificity. We excluded case‐control studies, since the sample would not be a series of patients in whom GCA was suspected as required.

    We included studies irrespective of the date of publication, language, or country of origin. For studies examining ultrasonography, the diagnostic value of the halo sign must be examined independently of flow abnormalities, such as occlusions or stenosis, which have not been shown to have diagnostic value in the investigation of GCA (Karahaliou 2006). We accepted papers examining axillary artery ultrasound in addition to temporal artery ultrasound.

    Participants

    We included all participants with clinically suspected GCA, who were investigated for the presence of the halo sign on temporal artery ultrasonography, using the ACR 1990 criteria as a reference standard. We also included studies with participants with a prior diagnosis of polymyalgia rheumatica.

    Index tests

    We assessed the following index tests.

    • Temporal artery ultrasonography, with use of the halo sign, which is a circumferential hypoechoic thickening around the lumen, indicative of arterial wall oedema. The primary halo cut‐off was 0.3 mm, but we accepted any cut‐off (or unspecified cut‐off), since we found few studies that explicitly reported the use of the 0.3 mm threshold.

    • Temporal artery biopsy: only if results were reported in direct comparative studies also including temporal artery ultrasound.

    Target conditions

    The target condition that the index tests were intended to identify was GCA.

    Reference standards

    The clinical reference standard that we used to establish the presence or absence of GCA in the general population was the ACR 1990 diagnostic criteria, which, on comparison of 214 GCA patients against 593 patients with other forms of vasculitis, have been shown to have a sensitivity of 93.5% and a specificity of 91.2% (Hunder 1990). This was verified in a retrospective analysis of 191 patients in Armona 1995.

    A positive diagnosis is defined as the presence of three or more of the following five criteria:

    • aged 50 years or more at disease onset;

    • new onset of localised headache;

    • temporal artery tenderness or decreased temporal artery pulse;

    • elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate (Westergren) 50 mm/hour or more; or

    • abnomal temporal artery biopsy (which leads to incorporation bias when biopsy is used as an index test).

    Because no reference standard is perfect and the diagnosis of GCA may be difficult, we followed Lord 2006 and Lord 2009 and reported on disagreements between ultrasound and ACR criteria to see if they were informative.

    Of note, the ACR criteria contain unavoidable incorporation bias, as the temporal artery biopsy is itself one of the five criteria. This increases the specificity of temporal artery biopsy, making comparison with ultrasound more difficult.

    The search results were downloaded into EndNote and duplicates removed (EndNote).     Assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We assessed the certainty of the evidence using the GRADE approach, which evaluates five domains: risk of bias, indirectness, inconsistency, imprecision, and publication bias. We followed Chapter 12: Drawing conclusions of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Diagnostic Test Accuracy (Leeflang 2023). We explained our decisions to downgrade the certainty of evidence in footnotes in the summary of findings tables and provided comments to aid the reader’s understanding.

    Statistical analysis and data synthesis

    We conducted statistical analysis and data synthesis in accordance with Chapter 10 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Diagnostic Test Accuracy (Macaskill 2010).

    Initially, we presented data in a 2 x 2 table, showing cross classification of the index test result versus the reference standard outcome. For each index test, in all studies, the sensitivity and specificity were calculated with 95% confidence interval. In order to visually evaluate the variation in calculations of sensitivity and specificity, we used RevMan Web to generate coupled forest plots and ROC (receiver operating characteristic) plots (RevMan Web 2023). We created linked ROC plots for papers with direct comparison between ultrasonographic findings and temporal artery biopsy.

    We planned for the primary analysis model to be a bivariate meta‐analysis (Reitsma 2005), at the following thresholds: 0.3 mm halo (primary threshold), 0.6 mm, and 1.5 mm. Since a single cut‐off of halo thickness was adopted in only four heterogeneous studies, and the number of included studies was relatively low, we used a hierarchical summary ROC (HSROC) model (Macaskill 2010), estimating average sensitivity at fixed specificity values of 95% and 80%, rather than according to cut‐offs for tertiles of specificity as originally planned in the protocol.

    For comparison between the index tests (halo sign on ultrasonography versus temporal artery biopsy), if sufficient data were available, we conducted two analyses: first, analyses including only studies with direct comparison between ultrasonographic findings of the halo sign and temporal artery biopsy; second, analyses of mixed comparisons also including studies on halo sign on ultrasound that did not report the accuracy of biopsy alone. These analyses were conducted with a test covariate in the bivariate or HSROC model. Since an HSROC model was appropriate, but few studies provided data for comparisons between tests, the sensitivities of ultrasonography versus temporal artery biopsy were compared using a simplified HSROC model, assuming parallel underlying summary ROC (SROC) curves.

    We conducted analyses using the 'metadas' user‐written command in SAS software (SAS software), and made predictions at fixed specificities using the NLMIXED procedure post‐estimation commands.

    Sensitivity analyses

    In the protocol for this review (Pouncey 2018), we planned to prepare a summary table to report a prespecified sensitivity analysis; however, this was precluded by insufficient data.

    We conducted the following post hoc sensitivity analysis, as it was more relevant to our review question: published data versus unpublished data from the largest study (TABUL 2016).

    Assessment of reporting bias

    As per Cochrane guidance, we assessed publication bias using the recommended method (Deeks 2005; Macaskill 2010). We maximised data collection through comprehensive search methods and by contacting study authors to request further information or data required to determine study eligibility.

    Heterogeneity investigation

    Heterogeneity investigations, as outlined in our protocol (Pouncey 2018), were not possible due to limitations and paucity of data. The mean age of participants ranged from 68 to 75 years. All ultrasonographers were reported to be either “experienced” or “trained”, with additional use of axillary artery ultrasound in conjunction with temporal artery evaluation reported by one study (Diamantopulos 2011). We wished to examine different thresholds for the halo sign: 0.3 mm (primary cut‐off), 1.5 mm, or 6 mm. However, as we anticipated, studies either did not report the threshold (10 studies) or adopted 0.3 mm as the cut‐off (4 studies: Estrada Alarcòn 2019, Maldini 2010, Schmidt 1997b, Stammler 2009), with one study also reporting for > 1 mm cut‐off (Salvarani 2002). Comparison of use of a unilateral versus bilateral halo sign was reported by one study (Nesher 2002), which found a slight increase in specificity for bilateral findings. All participants in the included studies had a duration of steroid use of less than two weeks, ranging from no steroid use (6 studies), < 3 days steroid use (6 studies), < 7 days (2 studies), and < 10 days (1 study), which was not noted to have a detrimental effect on reported sensitivity and specificity.

    Discussion

    There is interest in replacing TAB with US as one of the ACR 1990 diagnostic criteria for diagnosing GCA (Hunder 1990). Ultrasound has a number of advantages, including the fact that it is non‐invasive and is usually more readily obtainable than TAB, meaning it can be performed prior to commencement of systemic steroids and often on the same day of first suspicion of GCA. If proved accurate, a positive halo sign may avoid the need for a biopsy, which is invasive and often difficult to arrange expeditiously. Having a test that can be performed swiftly is particularly important in GCA, where prolonged treatment with steroids, which are often initiated before a definitive diagnosis is made, may increase the false‐negative rate. The authors of the largest included study, TABUL 2016, also noted that performing US scans in all patients with suspected GCA potentially reduced the need for biopsies by 43% and was more cost‐effective; however, this conclusion was based on published accuracy data, using a reference standard combining ACR criteria with the clinician's diagnosis, whereas our primary analysis was based on unpublished data in which the use of ACR criteria alone was the reference standard, in accordance with our inclusion criteria. Of interest, the sensitivity, and, particularly, the specificity of US were better with the combined reference standard. Future studies could consider using the 2016 revision of the ACR criteria (Salehi Abari 2016), with 10 components weighted according to their significance, with reduced reliance on TAB.

    For inconclusive US, the procedure can still be used to map the artery prior to surgery, which is likely to increase TAB precision and diagnostic accuracy (TABUL 2016).

    Summary of main results

    Interpretation of our findings is difficult because studies did not use the same halo cut‐off, and estimates of sensitivity varied considerably. Overall, the evidence was of very low certainty for sensitivity. Specificity was consistently high in most studies, but again with very low‐certainty evidence. Although the point estimates of sensitivity at 0.95 specificity are on the borderline of our prespecified threshold for US to rule in GCA, estimates were very imprecise, with very low‐certainty evidence, and were based on modelling rather than drawn at a definite halo thickness cut‐off of 0.3 mm, as was planned in our protocol. No inferences can thus be made.

    In a smaller set of studies that directly compared US and TAB, we also found variable sensitivity for both, especially for US. Assuming identical specificity, it was suggested that TAB is more sensitive than US, but the evidence for this comparison was also of very low certainty, partly because TAB is one of the criterion used for the reference diagnosis, and its accuracy is probably overestimated.

    Strengths and weaknesses of the review

    We conducted this review according to standard Cochrane methods and minimised the risk of missing relevant studies by cross‐referencing with the citations of other published reviews. Nevertheless, we were able to include only 16 small‐ to moderate‐sized studies with heterogeneous results, particularly regarding sensitivity. This makes the clinical use of our findings difficult.

    Insufficient reporting prevented analyses related to our secondary objectives, as detailed in our protocol (Pouncey 2018), including unilateral versus bilateral halo sign, different thresholds for the halo sign, concurrent use versus no use of steroids, average age of participants, training of ultrasonographers, or additional use of axillary artery ultrasound in conjunction with temporal artery evaluation.

    In addition, our analysis considers the halo sign in isolation, as this is considered to be the most specific US finding and reduces heterogeneity. However, in clinical practice, ultrasonographers may utilise combinations of signs or US diagnostic criteria (e.g. using Doppler flow and the halo sign), which may increase sensitivity.

    The studies included in the analysis either used the 1990 ACR criteria for the reference diagnosis (Hunder 1990), or gathered sufficient data for the criteria to be applied retrospectively. Although we have chosen to use this classification for the purpose of the meta‐analysis, we accept that it incorporates unavoidable incorporation bias, as TAB is itself one of the five criteria. This increases the specificity of TAB, making it difficult to compare with US.

    Of interest, in the largest included study (TABUL 2016), published data suggested better sensitivity, and, above all, specificity with US when using a reference standard that combined the ACR 1990 criteria with the patient's clinical diagnosis and additional signs and symptoms such as jaw claudication and optic neuropathy.

    Applicability of findings to the review question

    Studies included people with symptoms of suspected GCA, thus there are no concerns regarding the generalisability of our results.

    Comparison with other reviews

    Dua 2021 conducted a systematic review of interventions for treating GCA and also assessed the accuracy of TAB as verified with clinical diagnosis guided by ACR criteria. They found very low‐certainty evidence from six cohort and case‐control studies of 856 patients that the pooled sensitivity of TAB in patients with suspected GCA was 61% (95% CI 38% to 79%), and pooled specificity was 98% (95% CI 95% to 99%). Sensitivity was lower than the median sensitivity of 73% in our review, but specificity was similarly high.

    Rubenstein 2020 estimated the overall pooled proportion of TAB‐positive GCA cases across 32 studies and found it to be 77.3% (95% CI 71.8 to 81.9%), with high heterogeneity. They observed that the sensitivity of TAB for the diagnosis of GCA is not lower than that of temporal artery imaging, in particular temporal artery US, and concluded that decision‐making must also consider local practices in access to either test, and balance the advantage of the non‐invasiveness of US. They observed that previous meta‐analyses for US showed discordances between imaging and histopathological findings of temporal artery inflammation, therefore TAB and US may not be interchangeable. Rubenstein 2020 made reference to reviews, among which only Duftner 2018 was recently conducted; the others were published in 2010 or earlier (Arida 2010; Ball 2010; Karassa 2005).

    Duftner 2018 investigated the accuracy of the halo sign with US in 8 studies (605 patients) and yielded pooled sensitivities of 77% (95% CI 62% to 87%) and specificities of 96% (95% CI 85% to 99%). They also assessed the accuracy of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) of cranial arteries (6 studies, 509 patients) and found corresponding values of 73% (95% CI 57% to 85%), 96% (95% CI 85% to 99%), and 88% (95% CI 81% to 92%). The reference standard was a clinical diagnosis. The sensitivity found by Duftner 2018 was similar to the median sensitivity in our review (77% and 78%, respectively), although the specificity was higher in Duftner 2018 (96% and 91%, respectively).

    The review conducted by van der Geest 2020 assessed the diagnostic accuracy of several symptoms, physical signs, and laboratory tests for diagnosing GCA, but did not consider imaging tests.
