Cooling for cerebral protection during brain surgery

  • Imelda M Galvin

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی
  • Português

    • Background

    Patients undergoing neurosurgery are at risk of cerebral ischaemia with resultant cerebral hypoxia and neuronal cell death. This can increase both the risk of mortality and long term neurological disability. Induced hypothermia has been shown to reduce the risk of cerebral ischaemic damage in both animal studies and in humans who have been resuscitated following cardiac arrest. This had lead to an increasing interest in its neuroprotective potential in neurosurgical patients. This review was originally published in 2011 and did not find any evidence of either effectiveness or harm in these patients. This updated review was designed to capture current evidence to readdress these issues.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness and safety profile of induced hypothermia versus normothermia for neuroprotection in patients undergoing brain surgery. Effectiveness was to be measured in terms of short and long term mortality and functional neurological outcomes. Safety was to be assessed in terms of the rate of the adverse events infection, myocardial infarction, ischaemic stroke, congestive cardiac failure and any other adverse events reported by the authors of the included studies.

    Search methods

    For the original review, the authors searched the databases Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), MEDLINE (OvidSP), EMBASE (OvidSP) and LILACS to November 2010. For the updated review all these databases were re‐searched from November 2010 to May 2014.

    For both the original and updated versions, grey literature was sought by searching reference lists of identified studies and relevant review articles, and conference proceedings. No language restrictions were applied.

    Selection criteria

    As in the original review, we included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of induced hypothermia versus normothermia for neuroprotection in patients of any age and gender undergoing brain surgery, which addressed mortality, neurological morbidity or adverse event outcomes.

    Data collection and analysis

    Three review authors independently extracted data and two independently assessed the risk of bias of the included studies. Any discrepancies were resolved by discussion between authors.

    Main results

    In this updated review, one new ongoing study was found but no new eligible completed studies were identified. This update was therefore conducted using the same four studies included in the original review. These studies included a total of 1219 participants, mean age 40 to 54 years. All included studies were reported as RCTs. Two were multicentred, together including a total of 1114 patients who underwent cerebral aneurysm clipping, and were judged to have an overall low risk of bias. The other two studies were single centred. One included 80 patients who had a craniotomy following severe traumatic brain injury and was judged to have an unclear or low risk of bias. The other study included 25 patients who underwent hemicranicectomy to relieve oedema following cerebral infarction and was judged to have an unclear or high risk of bias. All studies assessed hypothermia versus normothermia. Overall 608 participants received hypothermia with target temperatures ranging from 32.5 °C to 35 °C, and 611 were assigned to normothermia with the actual temperatures recorded in this group ranging form 36.5 °C to 38 °C. For those who were cooled, 556 had cooling commenced immediately after induction of anaesthesia that was continued until the surgical objective of aneurysm clipping was achieved, and 52 had cooling commenced immediately after surgery and continued for 48 to 96 hours.

    Pooled estimates of effect were calculated for the outcomes mortality during treatment or follow‐up (ranging from in‐hospital to one year); neurological outcome measured in terms of the Glasgow Outcome Score (GOS) of 3 or less; and adverse events of infections, myocardial infarction, ischaemic stroke and congestive cardiac failure. With regards to mortality, the risk of dying if allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia was not statistically significantly different (risk ratio (RR) 0.87, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.59 to 1.27, P = 0.47). There was no indication that the time at which cooling was started affected the risk of dying (RR with intraoperative cooling 0.95, 95% CI 0.60 to 1.51, P = 0.83; RR for cooling postoperatively 0.67, 95% CI 0.34 to 1.35, P = 0.26). For the neurological outcome, the risk of having a poor outcome with a GOS of 3 or less was not statistically different in those who received hypothermia versus normothermia (RR 0.80, 95% CI 0.61 to 1.04, P = 0.09). Again there was no indication that the time at which cooling was started affected this result. Regarding adverse events, there was no statistically significant difference in the incidence in those allocated to hypothermia versus normothermia for risk of surgical infection (RR 1.20, 95% CI 0.73 to 1.97, P = 0.48), myocardial infarction (RR 1.86, 95% CI 0.69 to 4.98, P = 0.22), ischaemic stroke (RR 0.93, 95% CI 0.82 to 1.05, P = 0.24) or congestive heart failure (RR 0.85, 95% CI 0.60 to 1.21, P = 0.38). In contrast to other outcomes, where time of application of cooling did not change the statistical significance of the effect estimates, there was a weak statistically significant increased risk of infection in those who were cooled postoperatively versus those who were not cooled (RR 1.77, 95% CI 1.05 to 2.98, P = 0.03). Overall, as in the original review, no evidence was found that the use of induced hypothermia was either beneficial or harmful in patients undergoing neurosurgery.

    Authors' conclusions

    We found no evidence that the use of induced hypothermia was associated with a significant reduction in mortality or severe neurological disability, or an increase in harm in patients undergoing neurosurgery.

    • No evidence that cooling patients undergoing brain surgery helps to either reduce death and severe disability or increase harm

    Brain surgery is often required in patients who have abnormal brain blood vessels, had a stroke, sustained a traumatic injury or have suffered spontaneous bleeding into the brain. While surgery can be lifesaving it can also cause further damage due to an associated interruption and reduction of blood flow to the brain cells. Without an adequate supply of blood the brain cells are deprived of oxygen and they may die as a result. If brain cells are lost in large numbers or in important areas of the brain, the patient may die or be severely disabled. This has led to a search for ways to protect the brain cells during brain operations. Studies in animals have shown that lowering the body temperature can help to protect the brain cells in circumstances when the blood supply to the cells is compromised. Similarly, studies in humans who have been resuscitated after their heart stopped beating have shown that lowering their body temperature helps to reduce brain damage. The purpose of this updated version of a previous review of the same title was to determine whether cooling patients who were having brain surgery reduced death and serious disability, or was associated with increased risk of harm. A detailed search of the available literature up until May 2014 identified four eligible studies that included a total of 1219 participants. Their results were combined to answer these questions. No evidence was found that cooling patients who are having brain operations reduced the risk of death or severe disability, or produced significant harm.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    At present there is no evidence to support the use of hypothermia in patients undergoing craniotomy, although further high quality studies are required to determine its true efficacy and safety across different neurosurgical populations.

    Implications for research

    More clinical trials are needed to establish the effectiveness of hypothermia for cerebral protection during brain surgery before drawing any firm conclusions with regards to its potential in these patients. There is a need for more well designed and conducted clinical trials to detect clinically important differences between hypothermia and normothermia. More information is also needed on the minimum effective target temperature, the time period during which cooling is appropriate, the best rewarming strategies and potential complications.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    When cerebral blood flow is disrupted due to a traumatic, haemorrhagic or ischaemic insult, anoxic cell membrane depolarization occurs. Neuronal depolarization opens calcium and sodium channels to allow these cations into the cells (Rothman 1995). The resultant osmotic gradient pulls chloride and water into the cells causing cellular swelling and breakdown. The hypoxic cerebral cells also release glutamate, leading to an increase in intracellular calcium levels and further cell damage (Choi 1990). As part of a generalized and localized inflammatory response oxygen free radicals are produced, which cause mitochondrial dysfunction and further neuronal cell necrosis and apoptosis. When the cerebral blood flow is restored further damage can occur due to a secondary superoxide surge, increased production of nitric oxide and disruption of the blood brain barrier (Wei 1999). This phase is termed reperfusion injury and can lead to even more severe damage than the initial hypoxic insult.

    Patients who have sustained traumatic brain injury, intracerebral haemorrhage or ischaemia often require surgical intervention, as do those who require repair of vascular malformations. Surgery carries the potential for further cerebral ischaemia due to interruption of cerebral perfusion, vasospasm, impaired autoregulation and hyperaemic reperfusion injury, further compromising clinical outcomes (Leslie 2003). This has lead to a search for neuroprotective strategies to limit the extent of surgical induced damaged (El Beheiry 2012).

    Description of the intervention

    The use of hypothermia in neurosurgery was first described in the 1950s, with profound hypothermia being postulated as having neuroprotective effects (Hendrick 1959; Lougheed 1955). However it was largely abandoned due to excessive complications and poor outcomes (Kwak 1972; Michenfelder 1968; Steen 1980). There was a resurgence of interest in this area in the 1990s with studies showing that mild hypothermia improved outcomes in animal models of ischaemic and traumatic brain injury (Clifton 1991; Goto 1993; Karibe 1994). Clinical studies in post‐cardiac arrest populations showed improved outcomes, triggering further interest in its potential neuroprotective effects in ischaemic stroke, traumatic brain injury, cerebral aneurysm surgery etc (Els 2006; Holzer 2002; Qiu 2007; Todd 2005). Common to all of these conditions is the disruption of cerebral blood flow with resultant ischaemic cell death.

    How the intervention might work

    Hypothermia protects the brain during hypoxic injury by a reduction in cerebral metabolism by approximately 5% for every degree reduction in temperature, attenuating or delaying anoxic depolarization, and reducing glutamate release (Laptook 1995). It also reduces the production of excitatory amino acids and suppresses nitric oxide and superoxide production (Drury 2014). In vitro studies have shown that mild hypothermia reduces neuronal apoptosis by reducing the production and activation of hypoxia associated proteins that mediate cell death (Xu 2002). Animal studies have shown that hypothermia also attenuates the mechanisms involved in reperfusion injury (Thoresen 1997).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Recently there has been increasing focus on the role of hypothermia in those people undergoing neurosurgical procedures including aneurysm repair, haematoma evacuation, and decompressive craniectomy (Els 2006; Hindman 1999; Qiu 2007). The original review (Milani 2011) of the value of hypothermia in neurosurgical populations was carried out at the beginning of this emerging focus of research. It aimed to provide an insight into the overall benefits among and across neurosurgical populations, provide evidence based guidance on the optimal temperature targets and the timing and duration of treatment as well as its safety profile. That review incorporated existing evidence up until November 2010. This updated review was conducted to determine what the current state of evidence is in this regard.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness and safety profile of induced hypothermia versus normothermia for neuroprotection in patients undergoing brain surgery. Effectiveness was to be measured in terms of short and long term mortality and functional neurological outcomes. Safety was to be assessed in terms of the rate of the adverse events infection, myocardial infarction, ischaemic stroke, congestive cardiac failure and any other adverse events reported by the authors of the included studies.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Similar to the original review on this topic (Milani 2011), we intended to include both randomized and quasi‐randomized controlled trials.

    Quasi‐randomized trials were defined as trials using a method of allocation of participants to different forms of care that was not truly random (for example allocation by date of birth, day of the week, medical record number, month of the year, or the order in which participants were included in the study).

    Types of participants

    Any patient undergoing brain surgery (vascular, tumour, brain injury, intracranial arteriovenous malformations, etc), without age or gender restrictions, who were participants in a trial of cooling at any time in the perioperative period, that is either preoperatively, intraoperatively or postoperatively.

    Types of interventions

    Neurosurgical procedures where a deliberate strategy to cool the brain in the perioperative period was compared to no specific attempt to manipulate the brain temperature. While there is no generally accepted classification for levels of hypothermia, we defined the target levels of hypothermia as follows (where temperatures refer to brain or core values).

    • Mild: down to 32 ºC.

    • Moderate: from 32 ºC to 28 ºC.

    • Deep: under 28 ºC.

    Types of outcome measures

    Studies were eligible for inclusion if they reported any of the following outcome measures.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Mortality rate during treatment or the follow‐up period.

    2. Glasgow Outcome Score (GOS) of 3 or below. This cut off point was chosen as it marks the transition point between recovery to independent or dependent living and worse outcomes requiring high level care. Those with a GOS of 4 or more recover normal or near normal independence with employment possible with either no or some special provisions, while a GOS of 3 or less signifies severe disability, partial or total loss of independence or even death (Jennett 1975) (Appendix 1).

    Secondary outcomes

    The following adverse events were reported on.

    1. Any surgical infective complications including cerebral infection, septicaemia, pulmonary infection etc.

    2. Myocardial infarction, defined as a raised cardiac troponin level accompanied by at least one of the following: ischaemic symptoms, development of pathological Q waves on the electrocardiogram (ECG), ECG changes indicating ischaemia (ST segment elevation or depression) (JESC/ACC Committee 2000).

    3. Ischaemic stroke, defined as a persistent focal neurological deficit of presumed ischaemic origin lasting more than 24 hours and confirmed by computed tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) (Goldstein 1994).

    4. Congestive heart failure, defined as clinical signs of myocardial failure and requiring medical treatment.

    5. Intracranial haemorrhage, defined as bleeding into the brain caused by the rupture of cerebral blood vessels (including extradural or subdural haemorrhage) confirmed by CT or MRI.

    6. Any other adverse events which were reported by the authors of the primary studies.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    For the original review (Milani 2011), the authors searched the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) in The Cochrane Library (2010, Issue 10); MEDLINE (OvidSP) (1950 to November 2010); EMBASE (OvidSP) (1980 to November 2010); LILACS (1982 to November 2010) and CINAHL (1982 to November 2010).

    For the updated review we searched all of the above databases from November 2010 to May 2014. The same search terms used in the original review were used in the updated version (CENTRAL, Appendix 2; MEDLINE, Appendix 3; EMBASE, Appendix 4; LILACS, Appendix 5; CINAHL, Appendix 6).

    Searching other resources

    For both the original and updated versions, grey literature was sought by searching reference lists of identified studies and relevant review articles, and conference proceedings. No language restrictions were applied.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    For this updated version, three authors (IMG, RL, JGB) independently reviewed the articles retrieved by the updated search from November 2010 to May 2014 to identify studies that may have been eligible for inclusion. One review author (IMG) was responsible for contacting primary study authors should additional details have been required for determination of study eligibility and the quality. For the original version, two of the original authors scanned records retrieved by the initial search to identify potentially eligible studies and three of the original authors independently retrieved and reviewed all full‐text articles (Milani 2011).

    Data extraction and management

    For this updated version a new data extraction form was designed by one author (IMG) (Appendix 7). The form was designed to extract data from any new studies which may be identified as well as to re‐extract data from the studies included in the original review. This approach was taken to provide the reader with more comprehensive information about study methods, allow a more detailed assessment of risk of bias and ensure consistency of data extraction for all eligible studies. Three authors (IMG, RL, JGB) independently extracted the data using this form. All items recommended in Section 7 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011) were addressed in the data extraction process. Extracted data were entered initially onto the data extraction form and then into the corresponding fields in the Review Manager software (RevMan 5.2).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    The risk of bias of the included studies was assessed independently by two review authors (IMG, RL) using the Cochrane Collaboration tool for assessing risk of bias, as described in Section 8 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). Any discrepancies were resolved by discussion with a third review author (JGB). For each included primary study, bias was assessed in seven domains:

    1. random sequence generation,

    2. allocation concealment,

    3. performance bias,

    4. detection bias,

    5. attrition bias,

    6. reporting bias, and

    7. other bias.

    For each domain, we determined the risk of bias as 'low’, 'unclear' or 'high' according to the methods used in the study to ensure the minimization of each form of bias. In general these ratings were applied as follows.

    • The low risk rating was given where information was available that clearly demonstrated that efforts were made to ensure minimal bias in that domain and the described methods were robust enough to have a high likelihood of being effective.

    • The unclear risk rating was given when the information that was available was insufficient to be confident that the method used to minimize bias was robust enough to be effective.

    • The high risk rating was given when the study did not report any method to minimize bias in that domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    Categorical outcomes were presented as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI). No continuous or time to event outcome measures were addressed as part of this review.

    Unit of analysis issues

    Data were analysed using participants who were individually randomized to each intervention group for each outcome addressed.

    Dealing with missing data

    For each included trial (where such information was available) the proportion of participants who were missing from the final analysis was recorded. An intention‐to‐treat (ITT) analysis was used, where possible.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    Clinical heterogeneity was assessed by comparing study participants, settings, inventions and ancillary treatments. Methodologic heterogeneity was assessed by comparing the risk of bias in the included studies. Where included studies were deemed to be adequately homogeneous in these respects, statistical heterogeneity was assessed using the Chi2 test and the I2 statistic. A P value less than 0.10 and I2 value in excess of 50% were taken as indicative of important heterogeneity (Higgins 2003).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    To determine the presence or absence of reporting bias, we planned to examine funnel plots for each meta‐analysis that included 10 or more studies to determine if they were symmetrical. As only four studies were eligible for inclusion in this updated review an accurate assessment of reporting bias was not possible on this occasion but is planned for future updates if adequate numbers (10 or more) of eligible studies become available.

    Data synthesis

    Meta‐analysis was conducted by the authors of the original review. RevMan (RevMan 5.2) was used to perform the analyses for each outcome in the present review. For each outcome, all eligible studies that reported the outcome were included and participants who were treated with hypothermia were compared with those who were in the normothermia group. A fixed‐effect model was used in the absence of important heterogeneity and a random‐effects model if heterogeneity was deemed likely (DerSimonian 1986).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Should adequate numbers of eligible studies (at least two for each subgroup) have been identified, we planned to conduct the following subgroup analyses.

    • Outcomes by different types of participants, such as ischaemic stoke; haemorrhagic stoke; aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage; epilepsy etc.

    • Outcomes by different age groups, i.e. children versus adults.

    • Outcomes by different cooling periods, i.e. preoperatively, intraoperatively and postoperatively.

    Due to the small number of eligible studies retrieved, only one subgroup analysis was performed comparing outcomes in terms of mortality, GOS, surgical infection and myocardial infarction for those cooled during surgery versus those cooled after surgery. The remaining subgroup analyses are still planned for future updates of this review, depending on availability of adequate numbers (at least two for each subgroup) of eligible studies.

    Sensitivity analysis

    Two sensitivity analyses were performed as follows.

    • Analysis excluding those studies deemed to have a low methodologic quality as evidenced by a high risk of bias.

    • Analysis assuming two opposite scenarios for missing data. In the 'best case' scenario all missing patients in the hypothermia group were assumed to have a good outcome while all those in the normothermic group were assumed to have bad recovery. In the 'worst case' scenario the reverse assumption was made.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    A total of 7272 citations were identified (5870 by the original search (Milani 2011) and 1402 by the updated search). Of these 7272 citations, a total of 7014 were excluded on initial screening as it was obvious from the titles or abstracts that they were ineligible. A total of 258 abstracts were examined further for eligibility. Of these, one new ongoing study was identified (Nuegebauer 2013), 253 were excluded, and four were included in this review (Figure 1).

    Articles excluded at the title or abstract stage included animal studies, studies of interventions other then hypothermia, studies in non‐surgical populations and in non‐neurosurgical populations, review articles or commentaries. Articles excluded at the full‐text examination stage included case series, studies evaluating non‐clinical outcomes, and studies in which some participants did not undergo neurosurgery (Characteristics of excluded studies).

    Included studies

    Four eligible studies were included in the review.

    • Els (Els 2006) enrolled patients undergoing hemicraniectomy to treat oedema after malignant supratentorial infarction of more than two thirds of one cerebral hemisphere.

    • Hindman and Todd (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) enrolled patients undergoing cerebral aneurysm clipping.

    • Qiu (Qiu 2007) enrolled patients undergoing unilateral craniotomy for operative evacuation of intracranial haematoma or massive cerebral contusion, or both, in patients with non‐penetrating traumatic brain injury.

    Design

    All the included studies were reported to be randomized controlled trials. All studies with the exception of Els (Els 2006) reported the randomization process used.

    Setting

    Two studies (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) were multicentre studies. Hindman (Hindman 1999) was performed in five academic medical centres and Todd (Todd 2005) enrolled participants from 30 centres. The other two studies (Els 2006; Qiu 2007) were conducted at single university centres.

    Participants

    These trials included a total of 1219 participants with the following characteristics.

    Hindman (Hindman 1999) included 114 participants (9.4% of the total), 52 participants with acute subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) and 62 without SAH. The mean age was 51 years in the hypothermic group and 54 years in the normothermic group; 38 participants were men and 76 were women.

    Todd (Todd 2005) included 1000 patients (82% of the total) who had SAH no more than 14 days before planned surgical aneurysm clipping. The median age was 52 years in the hypothermic group and 53 years in the normothermic group; 344 participants were men and 655 were women.

    All of the participants in the studies by Hindman and Todd (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) had a preoperative World Federation of Neurological Surgeons (WFNS) score (Teasdale 1988) (Appendix 8) of I, II or III. This clinical grading system, proposed by the WFNS, is based on the Glasgow Coma Score (Teasdale 1974) and the finding of a motor deficit in patients with SAH. Patients classified as grade I have the best prognosis; those with grade II have a fair prognosis; and patients with grade III tend to have poor recovery. Patients with grade IV have poor prognosis and those with grade V are classified as moribund patients.

    Els (Els 2006) included 25 severely ill participants (2.1% of the total) operated on following a supratentorial ischaemic event with infarction of cerebral tissue. The mean age was 48.9 years; 15 participants were men and 10 were women.

    Qiu (Qiu 2007) assessed 80 participants (6.6% of the total) who had non‐penetrating severe traumatic brain injury (TBI). The mean age was 41.3 years in the hypothermic group and 40.2 years in the normothermic group; 52 participants were men and 28 were women.

    Interventions

    All four included studies (Els 2006; Hindman 1999; Qiu 2007;Todd 2005) assessed hypothermia versus normothermia for brain surgery.

    Hindman (Hindman 1999) induced hypothermia after the start of anaesthesia and the positioning of the patient, with a target temperature of 33.5 ºC. The initial operating room temperature was 20 ºC to 22.0 ºC. Participants in the hypothermic group were cooled by a forced‐air cooling and heating blanket set to deliver unwarmed air (room temperature); a cooling and heating mattress connected to a heating‐cooling unit set to 20.0 °C; and intravenous fluids and anaesthetic gases that were not warmed until the clip was applied. Participants in the normothermic group were maintained at a target oesophageal temperature of 36.5 ºC by a cooling and heating mattress set to 38.0 ºC; a forced‐air cooling and heating blanket set to 36.0 ºC to 38.0 ºC; and intravenous fluids and inspired anaesthetic gases warmed to 37.0 ºC. At the time of clip application, patients assigned to the hypothermia group were significantly colder than those assigned to the normothermia group, with a median temperature of 33.7 ºC versus 36.6 °C, respectively (P < 0.0001).

    Todd (Todd 2005) used a forced‐air blanket connected to a heating‐cooling unit to control the temperature for all patients. Hypothermia was induced after the start of anaesthesia and positioning of the patient, with a target temperature of 32.5 °C to 33.5 ºC. The time from induction of anaesthesia to first clip placement was 217 ± 101 minutes and the temperature was 33.3 ± 0.8 ºC. Rewarming of patients began after the last aneurysm clip had been secured and the temperature two hours after surgery was 36.4 ± 1.0 ºC. The temperature of the normothermic group was kept between 36.0 ºC and 37 °C and the time from induction of anaesthesia to the first clip placement was 214 ± 82 minutes. Both groups, normothermic and hypothermic, otherwise received similar treatment.

    Els (Els 2006) induced hypothermia immediately after the operation with a target temperature of 35.0 ºC. In the hypothermic group 10 participants were cooled by an intravenous cooling device; two participants were cooled by an external cooling device in combination with cool ventilator air fanning the body surface. All participants were cooled for 48 hours and active rewarming at a rate of 1 ºC per 24 hours was applied. The normothermic group received the same treatment as the hypothermic group, although details were not provided regarding how the equality of ancillary treatment was ensured. The authors did not report how the temperature was maintained in the normothermic group.

    Qiu (Qiu 2007) induced hypothermia for four days immediately after the operation, at a temperature of 33.0 ºC to 35.0 ºC, with the use of a water‐circulating cooling blanket and, if necessary, a cooling cap around the head with circulating water at a temperature about 4 ºC. The target temperature was generally achieved within 2.5 hours. For those who failed to achieve the target temperature within two hours refrigerated ice bags were placed around the neck. Patients were passively rewarmed over 10 to 24 hours. The normothermic group received the same treatment as the hypothermic group in all other ways, although again details were lacking as to how this was ensured. The authors did not report how the temperature was maintained in the normothermic group.

    Ongoing studies

    One new ongoing study (Nuegebauer 2013) was identified by the updated search (Characteristics of ongoing studies). No new completed eligible studies were identified.

    Excluded studies

    A total of 29 publications were excluded after the review of the full reports. Three were non‐randomized comparative trials (Bacher 1997; Beaufort 1995; Sato 2000); 13 studies presented an inappropriate comparator intervention (Baker 1994; Chouhan 2006; Gal 2002; Harris 2009; Hashiguchi 2003; Iwata 2006; Jian 2003; Karibe 2000; Leslie 2000; Li 2009; Liu 2006; Mrlian 2006; Shiozaki 2001); 12 were case series (Bernard 1999; Clifton 1992; Gal 2008; Gasser 2003; Iatrou 2002; Jian 2003; Kimme 2004; Maekawa 1994; Murthy 2005; Schwab 1998; Schwab 1998b; Shiozaki 2003) and one was a case control study (Himmelseher 2004). See Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Allocation

    Three of the included studies (Hindman 1999; Qiu 2007; Todd 2005) provided details of the randomization process used. Methods used varied in rigour from a simple randomization table to permuted block randomization but, since a reasonable method was used in all three studies, they were each judged to have a low risk of selection bias due to inadequate randomization. One study (Els 2006) stated that the participants were randomly assigned to the interventions but provided no details of the method used and was therefore judged to be at unclear risk of bias in this domain.

    Regarding allocation concealment, two studies described robust methods (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005), the third (Qiu 2007) mentioned that allocation concealment was used but did not describe a method. and the fourth (Els 2006) did not mention its use. The risk of bias in this domain was therefore judged to be low in the studies by Hindman and Todd (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005), unclear in the study by Qiu (Qiu 2007), and high in the study by Els (Els 2006).

    Blinding

    Two studies (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) provided clear details regarding blinding of study personnel and outcome assessors and were assigned a low risk of bias in these domains. In the study by Qiu (Qiu 2007) the study personnel were unaware of treatment assignments but it was not clear if the outcome assessors were blinded. This may have had implications for more subjective outcomes including neurologic recovery. However, as the GOS clearly defined the scoring criteria it was unlikely that the lack of blinding of outcome assessors resulted in significant bias, therefore the risk was judged to be low.

    The study by Els (Els 2006) did not describe any method of blinding either study personnel or outcome assessors. For the reasons detailed above, it was assigned a low risk of bias due to not blinding the outcome assessors. However, as lack of blinding of study personnel left the potential for intentional or unintentional differences in ancillary treatment it was assigned a high risk of bias in this domain.

    Incomplete outcome data

    In the studies by Hindman and Todd (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) the numbers lost to follow‐up were relatively small, three and one respectively. These studies were therefore judged to be at a low risk of attrition bias. Information was not available regarding completeness of follow‐up in the studies by Els and Qiu (Els 2006; Qiu 2007) and therefore they were both judged to be at unclear risk of attrition bias.

    Selective reporting

    Two studies reported outcomes as pre‐specified (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) while the other two studies (Els 2006; Qiu 2007) did not pre‐specify their outcome measures. Risk of reporting bias was therefore judged to be unclear in the latter two studies (Els 2006; Qiu 2007).

    Other potential sources of bias

    In the study by Qiu (Qiu 2007), the normothermic group had brain temperatures between 36.5 oC and 37.5 oC with rectal temperature even higher at 37.5 oC to 38 oC. Some of the normothermic group were therefore hyperthermic, which may have worsened their outcome making the intervention (hypothermia) seem more effective than it really was.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Mortality during treatment or the follow‐up period (Comparison 1, Outcome 1.1)

    See Analysis 1.1.

    The mortality analysis involved 1216 of 1219 participants with three patients assigned to the hypothermic group being lost to final mortality follow‐up and they were therefore excluded from the analysis for this outcome. Of these 1216 participants, 605 (49%) were allocated to hypothermia and 611 (50%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 93/1216 (7.6%) died during the treatment or the follow‐up period, 43 (7.1%) of the hypothermic group and 50 (8.2%) of the normothermic group. This small difference was not statistically significant. The RR of dying if allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia was 0.87 (95% CI 0.59 to 1.27, P = 0.47, I2 = 0%). There was no indication that the time at which cooling was started affected the risk of dying (RR with intraoperative cooling 0.95, 95% CI 0.60 to 1.51, P = 0.83, I2 = 0%; RR for cooling postoperatively 0.67, 95% CI 0.34 to 1.35, P = 0.26, I2 = 0%) (Figure 4).

    Secondary outcomes

    3. Intracranial haemorrhage (Comparison 3)

    Only Todd (Todd 2005) reported this outcome; 1000 participants (82% of the total in this review) were assessed. From these patients, 499 (49.9%) were allocated to hypothermia and 501 (50.1%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 85 (8.5%) developed an intracranial haemorrhage: 40 participants (8.0%) in the hypothermic group and 45 participants (9%) in the normothermic group. There was no statistically significant difference between these groups (RR 0.89, 95% CI 0.59 to 1.34, P = 0.58).

    4. Ischaemic stroke (Comparison 4, Outcome 4.1)

    See Analysis 4.1.

    Hindman 1999 and Todd 2005 reported outcomes involving 1114 participants (91.3% of the total in this review); 556 (49.9%) were allocated to hypothermia and 558 (50%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 524 (47%) developed an ischaemic stroke during the treatment or the follow‐up period, 252 (45.3%) of the hypothermic group and 272 (48.7%) of the normothermic group. This difference was not statistically significant and there was no significant statistical heterogeneity across these studies. The decrease of ischaemic stroke in patients allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia (RR) was 0.93, 95% CI 0.82 to 1.05, P = 0.24, I2 = 18%.

    Surgical infection complications (Comparison 5, Outcome 5.1)

    See Analysis 5.1.

    Three trials (Hindman 1999 and Todd 2005, included in the subgroup 'cooled during surgery', and Qiu 2007 included in the subgroup 'cooled after surgery') reported this outcome. They involved 1194 participants (97.9% of the total in this review): 596 (49.9%) were allocated to hypothermia and 598 (50%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 385 (32.2%) developed surgical infection complications during the treatment or the follow‐up period, 199 (33.4%) of the hypothermic group and 186 (31.1%) of the normothermic group. This difference was not statistically significant but there was significant statistical heterogeneity across these studies. The RR of surgical infection in participants allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia was 1.20 (95% CI 0.73 to 1.97, P = 0.48, I2 = 58%).

    There was some indication that the time at which cooling was started may have affected the risk of infection. For the intraoperative cooling group there was no statistical significance (RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.87 to 1.22, P = 0.76, I2 = 0%) but for those cooled postoperatively there was a statistically significant increase in the risk of infection in the hypothermic group (RR 1.77, 95% CI 1.05 to 2.98, P = 0.03).

    6. Myocardial infarction (Comparison 6, Outcome 6.1)

    See Analysis 6.1.

    Three trials (Els 2006; Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) reported this outcome. They involved 1139 participants (93.4% of the total in this review): 568 (49.8%) were allocated to hypothermia and 571 (50.1%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 17 (1.5%) had a myocardial infarction during the treatment or the follow‐up period, 11 (1.9%) of the hypothermic group and 6 (1%) of the normothermic group. This difference was not statistically significant and there was no indication of significant statistical heterogeneity across these studies. The RR of myocardial infarction in participants allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia was 1.86 (95% CI 0.69 to 4.98, P = 0.22, I2 = 0%). There was no indication that the time at which cooling was started affected the risk of myocardial infarction (RR with intraoperative cooling 2.01, 95% CI 0.69 to 5.83, P = 0.20; RR for cooling postoperatively 1.08, 95% CI 0.08 to 15.46, P = 0.95).

    7. Congestive heart failure (Comparison 7, Outcome 7.1)

    See Analysis 7.1.

    Two trials (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) reported this outcome. They involved 1114 participants (91.3% of the total in this review): 556 (49.9%) were allocated to hypothermia and 558 (50%) were allocated to normothermia. Overall, 111 (9.9%) had congestive heart failure during the treatment or the follow‐up period, 51 (9.1%) of the hypothermic group and 60 (10.7%) of the normothermic group. This difference was not statistically significant and there was no indication of statistical heterogeneity across these studies. The RR of congestive heart failure for patients allocated to hypothermia compared to normothermia was 0.85 (95% CI 0.60 to 1.21, P = 0.38, I2 = 0%).

    8. Any other adverse events reported

    None of the trials in this review reported any adverse effects directly related to the therapy other than those reported above.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    The effect estimates for the main outcomes are detailed in the summary of findings table (summary of findings Table for the main comparison). Among patients undergoing craniotomy for various neurosurgical indications including aneurysm clipping, traumatic brain injury and Ischaemic stroke:

    • there is no evidence that intraoperative or postoperative hypothermia significantly reduces or increases mortality;

    • there is no evidence that intraoperative or postoperative hypothermia significant alters the risk of severe neurologic disability;

    • there is no evidence that intraoperative hypothermia significantly alters the risk of intracranial haemorrhage, ischaemic stroke or congestive cardiac failure;

    • there is no evidence that intraoperative or postoperative hypothermia significantly alters the risk of myocardial infarction;

    • there is some weak evidence that postoperative hypothermia may increase the risk of infective complications.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The findings of this review need to be interpreted in light of the following limitations.

    Firstly, there is an overall lack of eligible high quality randomized controlled trials. The original search and this updated search identified only four eligible studies and of these only two (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) were judged to have an overall low risk of bias. The lack of adequate evidence limited the accuracy and reliability of the results. For the same reason, the scope of the review was limited and it was not unable to fulfil its objectives in terms of analysing the effectiveness and safely of hypothermia by age group or underlying neurosurgical condition.

    Secondly, there was potentially important clinical heterogeneity in terms of the underlying neurosurgical condition in the four eligible studies. While all patients in both the Hindman and Todd studies (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005) had surgery for aneurysm clipping, the study by Hindman (Hindman 1999) included both those who had and had not had a subarachnoid haemorrhage while the study by Todd (Todd 2005) was confined to those who had a confirmed subarachnoid haemorrhage. However, participants were homogenous in terms of premorbid neurologic disability, all being required to have a premorbid Modified Rankin score of 0 or 1 (Appendix 9), and patients in both trials who had had a subarachnoid haemorrhage were required to have a World Federation of Neurological Surgeons score of I, II or III (Appendix 8). The participants in the other two studies were more diverse as Els (Els 2006) studied those patients having decompressive craniectomy for cerebral oedema after acute ischaemic stoke and Qiu (Qiu 2007) studied those who had undergone craniotomy following traumatic brain injury. Among these two studies (Els 2006; Qiu 2007) there were differences in both the duration of cooling and ancillary treatment, and either of these may have contributed to the diversity and heterogeneity. Pooled estimates of effect that included those studies (Els 2006; Qiu 2007) in particular should therefore be interpreted with this limitation in mind.

    Thirdly, over 90% of the participants underwent surgery for aneurysm clipping (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005), limiting the applicability of these results to other neurosurgical populations. The results of this review should be viewed at best as a general indication of the effectiveness and safety of intraoperative and postoperative hypothermia in patients undergoing craniotomy, with the important caveat that no robust or reliable conclusions can be drawn from it regarding the effects of hypothermia in specific neurosurgical populations aside perhaps from those undergoing cerebral aneurysm clipping.

    Quality of the evidence

    The quality of the evidence contributing to the main outcomes is detailed in the summary of findings table (summary of findings Table for the main comparison).

    The overall quality of the evidence contributing to the effect estimates for mortality and the Glasgow Outcome Score was deemed to be moderate, with data coming from two studies with an overall low risk of bias, a third with a low or unclear risk of bias (Qiu 2007) and the fourth having a high or unclear risk of bias (Els 2006).

    For myocardial infarction, the quality of the evidence was rated as moderate as one of the studies had a high or unclear risk of bias (Els 2006).

    The quality of the evidence contributing to effect estimates for ischaemic stoke and congestive heart failure was deemed to be high as both studies reporting these outcomes had a low risk of bias (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005).

    For the estimate of infective complications, the quality of the evidence was judged to be low as there was significant statistical heterogeneity between studies and we lacked information on how infective complications were defined and diagnosed. The numbers analysed by individual studies for each infective complication were also small and the confidence intervals were not reported.

    Potential biases in the review process

    An unrestricted comprehensive literature search was conducted for both the original review and the updated review with the aim of identifying all studies eligible for inclusion, and so to minimize reporting and selection bias. The small number of eligible studies that were identified, however, means that the effect estimates should not be taken as an accurate indication of the true effectiveness and safely of induced hypothermia across neurosurgical populations.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Two other recent systematic reviews addressing the effectiveness and safety of perioperative hypothermia in patients undergoing surgery for intracranial aneurysms found similar results (Li 2012; Zhao 2012). A Cochrane review (Li 2012) of mild intraoperative hypothermia in patients undergoing surgery for both ruptured and unruptured intracranial aneurysms did not show any statistically significant difference in outcomes for those treated with hypothermia versus normothermia. A second systematic review (Zhao 2012) addressing the use of intraoperative hypothermia in patients undergoing intracranial aneurysm surgery similarly did not show any significant differences in mortality, neurologic outcome or adverse events between the groups. Like this review, both were limited by the lack of availability of eligible studies and both concluded that currently there was insufficient evidence to support the routine use of hypothermia in this population.

    We found only one eligible study (Qiu 2007) addressing the role of hypothermia in patients undergoing surgery following traumatic brain injury, and it is important to note that. That study had a number of methodological problems limiting the ability of this review to determine the role of hypothermia in this population. A post hoc analysis (Clifton 2012) of two randomized controlled trials of hypothermia for severe traumatic brain injury looked at the effect of hypothermia in those patients who had haematomas surgically evacuated within 24 hours of injury. They found that induction of hypothermia to 35 °C before or soon after craniotomy and maintenance at 33 °C for 48 hours postoperatively was associated with an improved neurologic outcome at six months. They postulated that early induction of hypothermia may be beneficial in those requiring surgical evacuation of post‐traumatic intracranial haematoma and emphasize the need for future trials in this area that target similar temperature goals.

    There is a paucity of trials addressing the value of hypothermia in all neurosurgical populations, especially in those undergoing surgery for epilepsy, non‐aneurysmal vascular deformities and ischaemic stoke. Further evidence is required in all these groups before the true effects of hypothermia can be determined in these populations.
