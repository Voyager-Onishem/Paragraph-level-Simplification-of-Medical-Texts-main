    • Background

    Abdominal aortic graft infections are a major complication following abdominal aortic aneurysm surgery, with high morbidity and mortality rates. They can be treated surgically or conservatively using medical management. The two most common surgical techniques are in situ replacement of the graft and extra‐anatomical bypass. Medical management most commonly consists of a course of long‐term antibiotics. There is currently no consensus on which intervention (extra‐anatomical bypass, in situ replacement, or medical) is the most effective in managing abdominal aortic graft infections. Whilst in emergency or complex situations such as graft rupture surgical management is the only option, in non‐emergency situations it is often personal preference that influences the clinician's decision‐making.

    Objectives

    To assess and compare the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections.

    Search methods

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist searched the Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and CINAHL databases and WHO ICTRP and ClinicalTrials.gov trials registers to 2 December 2019. We also reviewed the bibliographies of the studies identified by the search and contacted specialists in the field and study authors to request information on any possible unpublished data.

    Selection criteria

    We aimed to include all randomised controlled trials that used surgical or medical interventions to treat abdominal aortic graft infections. The definitions of abdominal aortic graft infections were accepted as presented in the individual studies, and included secondary infection due to aortoenteric fistula. We excluded studies presenting data on prosthetic graft infections in general, unless data specific to abdominal aortic graft infections could be isolated.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed all studies identified by the search. We planned to independently assess risk of bias of the included trials and to evaluate the quality of the evidence using the GRADE approach. Our main outcomes were overall mortality, amputation, graft re‐infection, overall graft‐related complications, graft‐related mortality, acute limb ischaemia, and re‐intervention.

    Main results

    We identified no randomised controlled trials to conduct meta‐analysis.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is currently insufficient evidence to draw conclusions to support any treatment over the other. Multicentre clinical trials are required to compare different treatments for the condition.

    • Surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections

    Background

    Abdominal aortic graft infections are a serious complication following aortic aneurysm surgery. They can be managed in two ways: either with surgery or medical management. Surgery is currently the mainstay of treatment and is usually performed by two methods, one of which involves removing and replacing the infected graft, and the other bypassing the infected graft. Medical management commonly involves a long‐term course of antibiotics. Medical management avoids the risk of potential complications of surgery, but requires use of antibiotics for several months, or even lifelong. There is currently no agreement on which intervention (surgical or medical) is the most effective in managing abdominal aortic graft infections. In emergency or complex situations such as graft rupture, surgical management is the only option, whilst for those unfit for surgery, medical management is the preferred option. However, in non‐emergency situations, it is often personal preference that influences the healthcare provider's decision‐making. This review aimed to assess and compare the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections.

    Study characteristics and key results

    We searched for randomised controlled trials (a type of study in which participants are assigned to one of two or more treatment groups using a random method) to December 2019. We found no trials that met our inclusion criteria. Hence there is currently insufficient evidence to draw conclusions relating to the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections.

    Conclusion

    Multicentre randomised controlled trials are required to identify the most effective method of treating aortic graft infection. By doing this, 'gold standard' guidelines can be formulated so that all patients can receive the same level of treatment without regard for where they are being treated.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is currently insufficient evidence to draw conclusions regarding the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections. It is essential that randomised controlled trials be performed comparing the various forms of interventions, that is surgical (in situ replacement of the graft and extra‐anatomical bypass) or medical management. This will allow for the formation of a 'gold standard' guidance so that practice carried out in different hospitals is homogenous and based on evidence rather than personal preference.

    Implications for research

    Aortic graft infection is a serious complication that needs to be managed effectively. This is a high‐value research topic that could help change practice for the benefit of patients across the world.

    It is important to conduct multicentre clinical trials to identify the most effective method of treating aortic graft infections. The importance of research in this particular topic should be discussed and brought to light at national and international vascular conferences to involve as many centres as possible and establish a standard protocol to conduct further research.

    Numerous studies could be carried out, such as 'in situ replacement versus extra‐anatomical bypass' or 'in situ replacement with allograft versus silver‐impregnated graft'. The studies should be conducted as collaborative research with similar study design, selection criteria of participants, and duration of follow‐up to ensure uniformity.

    However, there are obstacles to arranging such trials. A randomised controlled trial examining this clinical problem will be complex to conduct. The numbers of patients presenting with graft infection is low, and when they do present, they may be too ill for surgery or too fit to consider conservative therapy. Clinical equipoise may be hard to achieve, hence recruitment will be more difficult. Establishing a centralised registry of data may help guide plans for future studies.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    An abdominal aortic aneurysm is a dilatation of the aorta to over 1.5 times its normal size (Dehlin 2005; Johnston 1991). It is a potentially fatal condition due to rupture of the aneurysm, with an overall mortality rate of approximately 65% (Leonard 2008). Abdominal aortic aneurysms can be treated by either open or endovascular surgery (Paravastu 2014). For open surgical repair, the surgeon performs a transperitoneal laparotomy and replaces the aneurysmal segment with a graft. In endovascular repair (EVAR), the surgeon uses a transfemoral approach with contrast fluoroscopy to insert a stent that extends past the aneurysmal segment (Piffaretti 2013; Uehara 2017). Elective aortic aneurysm surgery has become more frequent over the last few decades due to the increased number of treatable aneurysms detected incidentally, or by screening, and the increased availability of EVAR (Lilja 2017). According to the The Getting It Right First Time (GIRFT) programme report, approximately 4000 aneurysm repairs are carried out annually in the UK, of which 75% are EVAR repairs and 25% are open repairs (Horrocks 2018). Both treatment methods have complications. Open repairs can cause wound infection, bowel ischaemia, damage to viscera at laparotomy, and renal failure (Kieffer 2012). EVAR complications include stent migration, pseudoaneurysm, bowel ischaemia, spinal ischaemia, and renal artery occlusion (Maleux 2009).

    Aortic graft infection is one of the most severe complications observed following abdominal aortic aneurysm surgery, with an incidence of between 0.5% to 2% (Seeger 2000). Whilst a relatively rare complication, it is associated with high morbidity and mortality: up to 33% perioperative mortality and 46% morbidity have been reported (Batt 2008; Garot 2014). Aortic graft infections involve infection of the primary prosthesis used. This includes the graft used in open surgery or the endovascular stent used in EVAR. Aortic graft infections can be diagnosed intraoperatively (when presenting as an emergency), radiologically, or by microbiology samples, and diagnosis is based on predefined major criteria, such as isolated micro‐organisms from perigraft samples, and minor criteria, such as persistent fever (Lyons 2016). Infections most commonly occur within a year of surgery; the risk of developing a graft infection is increased in patients with a nosocomial (hospital‐acquired) infection (Vogel 2008). There are no accepted standard criteria for a definition of aortic graft infections, hence we included studies that fulfilled any of the criteria mentioned in individual studies.

    Description of the intervention

    There are multiple surgical and medical treatments for aortic graft infections, which can be used in conjunction with one another. The 'gold standard' of treating aortic graft infections is surgical management; however, there is debate as to which surgical option is the most appropriate. The two main surgical methods currently used in practice include in situ replacement, also known as anatomical replacement, and extra‐anatomical bypass (Hallet 1997).

    The goal of extra‐anatomical bypass is revascularisation of the lower limbs, by anastomosing an artery above the infection to an inferior one, usually the axillary to the femoral artery. In the context of this review, extra‐anatomical bypass only refers to an intervention used to treat graft infections, best suited to patients with a 'hostile abdomen', those who are high risk, or who are not candidates for aortic cross clamping due to infection (Appleton 2010).

    In situ replacement is a procedure in which the source of infection is removed, and the infected graft is replaced with a new one. These grafts can be biological (homografts, from the host species, or xenografts, from another species) or prosthetic. Veins, such as the superficial femoral vein, can be used for reconstruction (Bisdas 2011; Han 2018; Umminger 2016; Vogt 1998).

    Although extra‐anatomical bypass is widely used as an intervention, recent studies have suggested that in situ replacement may be the more effective surgical technique (Batt 2017; O'Connor 2006).

    In a subgroup of patients, endovascular bridging can be used as an interim measure prior to the patient undergoing the above procedures (Wilson 2016).

    Medical management most commonly consists of a long‐term course of antibiotics, which can range from three months to life, and which are sometimes used in conjunction with percutaneous drainage in complicated, high‐risk patients. In patients who are not fit for surgical intervention, the treatment of aortic graft infections is lifelong antibiotics (Pistolese 1997).

    How the intervention might work

    Both in situ replacement and extra‐anatomical bypass removes the infected graft from being used as a conduit for the flow of blood. During extra‐anatomical bypass, the new graft bypasses the previously infected portion (e.g. by axillo‐femoral bypass) (Seeger 2000); during in situ replacement, the infected graft is replaced with a new one (Batt 2008).

    Medical management aims to manage the infection without surgery, and is used primarily in high‐risk patients, as it avoids the risk and complications associated with surgery (Paravastu 2014; Pistolese 1997).

    Why it is important to do this review

    There is a high mortality associated with aortic graft infections, with inpatient mortality reported as high as 48% (Fatima 2013). As with any procedure, it is essential to understand how best to deal with any potential complications. There is currently no consensus on which intervention (extra‐anatomical bypass, in situ replacement, or medical) is the most effective in managing abdominal aortic graft infections. Whilst in emergency or complex situations such as graft rupture, surgical management is the only option, in non‐emergency situations it is often personal preference that influences the clinician's decision‐making. This review aimed to identify the available evidence on the safety and effectiveness of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections in order to help clinicians manage this debilitating complication, and to guide decision‐making with evidence, rather than just past experience.

    Objectives

    To assess and compare the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We aimed to include all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that used surgical or medical interventions to treat abdominal aortic graft infections. We excluded any studies that presented data on prosthetic graft infections in general, unless data specific to abdominal aortic graft infections could be isolated. We also excluded all studies in which the conduit material or prosthetic graft used was impregnated with antimicrobials.

    Types of participants

    We aimed to include studies in which all participants were diagnosed with an abdominal aortic graft infection. The definitions of abdominal aortic graft infections were accepted as presented in the individual studies. This included secondary infection due to aortoenteric fistula (Lyons 2016).

    Types of interventions

    We aimed to include all surgical and medical interventions for managing abdominal aortic graft infections. Surgical interventions may be in situ replacement or extra‐anatomical bypass. In situ replacement includes replacement with a new graft, such as allografts, silver‐impregnated, or antibiotic‐impregnated grafts, etc. Extra‐anatomical bypass involves bypass procedures, such as axillo‐femoral bypass. Medical interventions include management with long‐term antibiotic use.

    Comparisons

    • Surgical: in situ replacement (anatomical) versus extra‐anatomical bypass

    • Medical: type of antibiotics used, short versus long term, oral versus intravenous, etc.

    • Surgical versus medical management

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Overall mortality: 30 days, 3 months, 1 year

    • Amputation

    • Graft re‐infection: diagnosis of new infection rather than persistence of existing infection, diagnosed according to the Management of Aortic Graft Infection Collaboration (MAGIC) criteria (Lyons 2016)

    Secondary outcomes

    • Overall graft‐related complications: directly linked to graft, such as thrombosis, occlusion, endoleak

    • Graft‐related mortality

    • Acute limb ischaemia

    • Re‐intervention

    • Time taken for cessation of antimicrobial treatment

    We aimed to report the time points presented by the included studies. Clinically relevant time points are 30 days, 3 months, and 1 year.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist conducted systematic searches of the following databases for RCTs and controlled clinical trials with no language, publication year, or publication status restrictions:

    • the Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register via the Cochrane Register of Studies (CRS‐Web) (searched 2 December 2019);

    • the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2019, Issue 11) via the Cochrane Register of Studies Online (CRSO);

    • MEDLINE Ovid (MEDLINE Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐indexed Citations, MEDLINE Daily, and MEDLINE; 1946 onwards) (searched 2 December 2019);

    • Embase Ovid (1974 onwards) (searched 2 December 2019);

    • CINAHL EBSCO (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature) (1982 onwards) (searched 2 December 2019).

    The Information Specialist modelled search strategies for other databases on the search strategy designed for MEDLINE. Where appropriate, they were combined with adaptations of the Highly Sensitive Search Strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials, as described in Chapter 6 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Lefebvre 2011). Search strategies for major databases are provided in Appendix 1.

    The Information Specialist searched the following trials registries on 2 December 2019:

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) (who.int/trialsearch);

    • US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov).

    Searching other resources

    We reviewed the bibliographies of the studies identified by the search for further relevant references. We contacted specialists in the field and the study authors to request information on any possible unpublished data. We also consulted our records from work carried out for a previously published systematic review on this topic by the author team (Niaz 2020).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (ON, AR) independently assessed all studies that were identified by the search. We identified and removed duplicate publications. We screened all abstracts and ordered full‐text papers for all relevant abstracts. We assessed the full‐text papers to ascertain if they met the inclusion criteria. There were no disagreements over the selection of the studies between the review authors. Had there been disagreement about the inclusion of a study, another review author (PS) would have been consulted for arbitration (Higgins 2011). We listed excluded studies that initially appeared to meet the inclusion criteria in a Characteristics of excluded studies table, with their reasons for exclusion (Higgins 2011). The study selection process is shown in a PRISMA diagram (Figure 1) (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    We identified no studies that fulfilled the inclusion criteria for the review. Had there been any studies for inclusion, two review authors (ON, AR) would have independently extracted data from each included study. The data would have been extracted onto a data collection form, as suggested in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of interventions (Higgins 2011). If there are studies to be included in future updates of the review, we will extract data from each report individually where there are multiple reports from one study. We will manage the data in a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet (Microsoft Excel 2018), and conduct analysis in Review Manager 5, as per Cochrane guidelines (Higgins 2011; Review Manager 2014). For each study, we will seek study details including: definition of abdominal aortic graft infection, type of original operation (endovascular repair, open surgical repair), type of management, type of graft used in surgical intervention, follow‐up period, length of hospital stay, length of stay in intensive treatment unit, any other complications, other surgical interventions, amputation, lower limb ischaemia, stroke, myocardial infarction, renal failure, overall mortality, 30‐day mortality, overall graft complication rate, graft re‐infection rate, graft leak rate, and graft occlusion.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Since no studies were included in the review, we were not able to assess risk of bias. If there are studies to be included in future updates of the review, two review authors (ON, AR) will independently assess risk of bias of the included trials using Cochrane's 'Risk of bias' tool, as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of interventions (Higgins 2011). We will create a 'Risk of bias' table to report bias associated with the included studies. We will assess the studies for selection bias (sequence generation and allocation sequence concealment), performance bias, detection bias, attrition bias, reporting bias, and other potential sources of bias (Higgins 2011). We will evaluate each item as low, high, or unclear risk, with unclear risk indicating either lack of information, or uncertainty over the potential for bias. We will also contact the study author(s) to seek clarification in cases of uncertainty over methodology or data.

    Measures of treatment effect

    IIf there are studies to be included in future updates of the review, we will present the measures of treatment effect for dichotomous data (such as overall mortality, amputation, graft complications) using risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI).

    Unit of analysis issues

    We planned for the unit of analysis to be the individual participant randomised to each group.

    Dealing with missing data

    If there are studies to be included in future updates of the review, we will record any missing data on our data collection form. We will initially contact the study authors to request the missing data. If we do not receive a response, we will evaluate the data missing due to participants’ dropout using intention‐to‐treat analysis. In the case of missing statistics, and if appropriate, we will use other data analysis and data management software, such as SPSS or Microsoft Excel (Microsoft Excel 2018; SPSS 2016), to convert reported statistics into the one required for analysis. If important information is missing, such as number of participants, means, or standard deviations, but standard error, 95% CI or P values are reported, we will calculate an effect estimate, when appropriate, using the generic inverse‐variance method (Higgins 2011).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    No studies met the inclusion criteria of the review. In the future, if appropriate studies are included, we will assess heterogeneity amongst the studies using the Chi² test from the forest plot. Heterogeneity may be indicated if there is a statistically significant result (P < 0.10). However, if the studies included in the review have small sample sizes, then careful interpretation of the Chi² test is needed. In this situation, we will use the I² statistic, which measures the percentage of the variability in effect estimates that is due to heterogeneity rather than sampling error (chance). The inconsistency amongst the studies will be quantified as follows:

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important;

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity;

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity;

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity (Higgins 2011).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    In order to reduce publication and other reporting bias, we used a robust search strategy. In the future, we will also include unpublished data from clinical trials if this information is available. If more than 10 studies are included in a meta‐analysis, we will assess the presence of publication bias and other reporting bias using funnel plots, with asymmetry indicating reporting bias (Higgins 2011).

    Data synthesis

    No studies were eligible for inclusion in the review, hence no data synthesis was carried out. If studies are included in future versions of the review, we will carry out data synthesis using Review Manager 5 software (Review Manager 2014). We will examine the included studies for sample size and heterogeneity. If the sample sizes in the studies are different, which is most likely to be the case, or the I² statistic is greater than 50%, then we will use the inverse‐variance random‐effects model. If the studies have similar sample sizes and intervention estimates, we will use a fixed‐effect model (Higgins 2011). If we are unable to or it is not appropriate to perform meta‐analysis, we will report the results narratively in the text. We will seek assistance from the statistician based at our academic institute to review the statistics of the review before interpreting the results.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We plan to carry out the following subgroup analysis when studies are identified for inclusion.

    • Abdominal aortic graft infections secondary to open procedure versus abdominal aortic graft infections secondary to endovascular procedure.

    • Comparison of different types of graft used in anatomical replacement:

      • prosthetic versus autologous or allograft versus cryopreserved;

      • surgical management: aortoenteric fistula conditions versus non‐aortoenteric fistula conditions.

    • Medical management:

      • types of micro‐organisms detected;

      • presence of aortoenteric fistula.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We were not able to conduct sensitivity analysis as there were no studies included in the review. In future versions of the review, we will conduct sensitivity analysis by excluding studies with a high risk of bias from the meta‐analysis to assess this effect on the effect estimate. We will define studies as at high risk if we assess two or more domains at high risk of bias, or if we assess a study as at high risk of selection bias (Higgins 2011).

    We will perform sensitivity analysis by excluding studies that have only reported short‐term outcomes, to check if studies reporting only short‐term outcomes affect the overall outcome of the comparison.

    Lastly, we will perform sensitivity analysis by excluding studies that do not accurately report cases of re‐infection (new infection or persistence of existing infection), to assess this effect on the effect estimate.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    No studies were eligible for inclusion in the review. If in future updates studies are eligible for inclusion, we will prepare 'Summary of findings' tables to present the key information for surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections, based on the methods described in Chapter 11 and 12 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011), employing GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT). We will justify any departures from the standard methods (Atkins 2004; Higgins 2011). The participant population will be those with confirmed abdominal aortic graft infections. In future updates, when studies are identified, we will create one table each for: surgical versus medical interventions; surgical intervention versus surgical intervention; and medical intervention versus medical intervention.

    We will include the following outcomes in each table.

    • Overall mortality

    • Amputation

    • Graft re‐infection

    • Overall graft‐related complications

    • Graft‐related mortality

    • Acute limb ischaemia

    • Re‐intervention

    Two review authors (ON, AR) will assess the certainty of evidence using GRADEpro GDT for each outcome listed above (Atkins 2004; GRADEpro GDT), assigning one of four ratings: high, moderate, low, or very low, based on five criteria: risk of bias, inconsistency, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias (Atkins 2004).     Results

    Description of studies

    We identified no studies that met our inclusion criteria, therefore no studies were included in the review.

    Results of the search

    Our database searches identified a total of 441 studies. Another 23 studies were identified from work carried out for a previously published systematic review on this topic by the same authors (Niaz 2020). See Figure 1.

    Included studies

    Unfortunately, no studies met our inclusion criteria as none of them were RCTs.

    Excluded studies

    As mentioned above, no RCTs were identified. We analysed 22 notable studies in detail; however, as these studies were not RCTs they had to be excluded (Ahmed 2018; Ali 2009; Batt 2003; Batt 2008; Belair 1998; Bisdas 2011; Davila 2015; DiMuzio 1996; Dirven 2015; Gabriel 2004; Hannon 1996; Harlander‐Locke 2014; Hayes 1999; Legout 2012; Lyons 2013; Mirzaie 2007; Oderich 2006; Pupka 2011; Quinones‐Baldrich 1991; Seeger 2000; Takano 2014; Vogt 1998).

    See also Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Because none of the studies reviewed met the inclusion criteria, we were not able to assess risk of bias of included studies. In future updates of this review where studies are included, we will assess the risk of bias associated with each study.

    Effects of interventions

    We identified no studies eligible for inclusion in the review. There is currently insufficient evidence to draw conclusions to support any one treatment over another.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We identified no RCTs assessing the effects of surgical and medical interventions for abdominal aortic graft infections. We were unable to perform any analysis, which therefore limits any conclusions that can be drawn.

    Multicentred RCTs need to be performed so that national guidelines can be formulated. As this is a concerning complication with high levels of morbidity and mortality, it is essential that there is a unified approach across all centres. This will help provide a high level of care, which will most importantly be consistent, so that patients are able to access adequate care wherever they are located.

    By having nationally accepted guidelines, clinical practice can be audited and high standards maintained. This is essential in providing healthcare services. The European Society for Vascular Surgery (ESVS) has published guidelines on the management of graft infections that are based on the current expert opinion on this topic (Chakfé 2020). These are helpful guidelines that clinicians can refer to until larger‐scale studies are carried out.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    As no RCTs were included in the review, there were no conclusions to apply to practice.

    The excluded studies showed that the mainstay of treatment was surgery, with poorer outcomes being seen when patients were managed conservatively. In situ replacement was the most commonly used intervention, followed by extra‐anatomical bypass. However, there was a lack of uniformity and variability in the studies.

    Quality of the evidence

    We did not assess the certainty of the evidence as no studies were eligible for inclusion in the review.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We followed standard Cochrane procedures during the review process. We used a robust search strategy, and two review authors independently screened the titles and abstracts of references identified by the searches. Any disagreements were settled through discussion.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Niaz 2020 performed a systematic review including non‐randomised studies. The studies in the review by Niaz 2020 were excluded in this Cochrane Review as they were not RCTs. Niaz 2020 was based on cohort and observational studies, with some non‐randomised comparative studies, that mainly compared in situ replacement and extra‐anatomical bypass for surgical management of the condition. A few case series have also been reported which have shown the use of surgical intervention after failure of medical management of the condition. Based on Niaz 2020 and the excluded studies in this review, it is evident that the mainstay of treatment is surgery, with poorer outcomes being seen when patients were managed conservatively. In situ replacement was the most commonly used intervention, followed by extra‐anatomical bypass. No graft type was seen to be superior; however, there were limitations in studies in the literature: there was a lack of uniformity and variability in the data which limits the accuracy of any data analysis (Niaz 2020).

    The findings of another recent systematic review conducted by Post and colleagues concurred with those of Niaz 2020, and concluded that surgery was the mainstay of treatment, with in situ replacement having the best one‐year survival (Post 2019).
