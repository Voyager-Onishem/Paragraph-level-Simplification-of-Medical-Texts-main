Higher versus lower sodium intake for preterm infants

    • Background

    Infants born preterm are at increased risk of early hypernatraemia (above‐normal blood sodium levels) and late hyponatraemia (below‐normal blood sodium levels). There are concerns that imbalances of sodium intake may impact neonatal morbidities, growth and developmental outcomes.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects of higher versus lower sodium supplementation in preterm infants.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL in February 2023; and MEDLINE, Embase and trials registries in March and April 2022. We checked reference lists of included studies and systematic reviews where subject matter related to the intervention or population examined in this review. We compared early (< 7 days following birth), late (≥ 7 days following birth), and early and late sodium supplementation, separately.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised, quasi‐randomised or cluster‐randomised controlled trials that compared nutritional supplementation that included higher versus lower sodium supplementation in parenteral or enteral intake, or both. Eligible participants were preterm infants born before 37 weeks' gestational age or with a birth weight less than 2500 grams, or both. We excluded studies that had prespecified differential water intakes between groups.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed eligibility and risk of bias, and extracted data. We used the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of evidence.

    Main results

    We included nine studies in total. However, we were unable to extract data from one study (20 infants); some studies contributed to more than one comparison. Eight studies (241 infants) were available for quantitative meta‐analysis. Four studies (103 infants) compared early higher versus lower sodium intake, and four studies (138 infants) compared late higher versus lower sodium intake. Two studies (103 infants) compared intermediate sodium supplementation (≥ 3 mmol/kg/day to < 5 mmol/kg/day) versus no supplementation, and two studies (52 infants) compared higher sodium supplementation (≥ 5 mmol/kg/day) versus no supplementation. We assessed only two studies (63 infants) as low risk of bias.

    Early (less than seven days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake

    Early higher versus lower sodium intake may not affect mortality (risk ratio (RR) 1.02, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.38 to 2.72; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 83 infants; low‐certainty evidence). Neurodevelopmental follow‐up was not reported. Early higher versus lower sodium intake may lead to a similar incidence of hyponatraemia < 130 mmol/L (RR 0.68, 95% CI 0.40 to 1.13; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 83 infants; low‐certainty evidence) but an increased incidence of hypernatraemia ≥ 150 mmol/L (RR 1.62, 95% CI 1.00 to 2.65; I2 = 0%; 4 studies, 103 infants; risk difference (RD) 0.17, 95% CI 0.01 to 0.34; number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome 6, 95% CI 3 to 100; low‐certainty evidence). Postnatal growth failure was not reported. The evidence is uncertain for an effect on necrotising enterocolitis (RR 4.60, 95% CI 0.23 to 90.84; 1 study, 46 infants; very low‐certainty evidence). Chronic lung disease at 36 weeks was not reported.

    Late (seven days or more following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake

    Late higher versus lower sodium intake may not affect mortality (RR 0.13, 95% CI 0.01 to 2.20; 1 study, 49 infants; very low‐certainty evidence). Neurodevelopmental follow‐up was not reported. Late higher versus lower sodium intake may reduce the incidence of hyponatraemia < 130 mmol/L (RR 0.13, 95% CI 0.03 to 0.50; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 69 infants; RD −0.42, 95% CI −0.59 to −0.24; number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome 2, 95% CI 2 to 4; low‐certainty evidence). The evidence is uncertain for an effect on hypernatraemia ≥ 150 mmol/L (RR 7.88, 95% CI 0.43 to 144.81; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 69 infants; very low‐certainty evidence). A single small study reported that later higher versus lower sodium intake may reduce the incidence of postnatal growth failure (RR 0.25, 95% CI 0.09 to 0.69; 1 study; 29 infants; low‐certainty evidence). The evidence is uncertain for an effect on necrotising enterocolitis (RR 0.07, 95% CI 0.00 to 1.25; 1 study, 49 infants; very low‐certainty evidence) and chronic lung disease (RR 2.03, 95% CI 0.80 to 5.20; 1 study, 49 infants; very low‐certainty evidence).

    Early and late (day 1 to 28 after birth) higher versus lower sodium intake for preterm infants

    Early and late higher versus lower sodium intake may not have an effect on hypernatraemia ≥ 150 mmol/L (RR 2.50, 95% CI 0.63 to 10.00; 1 study, 20 infants; very low‐certainty evidence). No other outcomes were reported.

    Authors' conclusions

    Early (< 7 days following birth) higher sodium supplementation may result in an increased incidence of hypernatraemia and may result in a similar incidence of hyponatraemia compared to lower supplementation. We are uncertain if there are any effects on mortality or neonatal morbidity. Growth and longer‐term development outcomes were largely unreported in trials of early sodium supplementation.

    Late (≥ 7 days following birth) higher sodium supplementation may reduce the incidence of hyponatraemia. We are uncertain if late higher intake affects the incidence of hypernatraemia compared to lower supplementation. Late higher sodium intake may reduce postnatal growth failure. We are uncertain if late higher sodium intake affects mortality, other neonatal morbidities or longer‐term development.

    We are uncertain if early and late higher versus lower sodium supplementation affects outcomes.

    • Are higher or lower salt (sodium) supplements better for premature babies to prevent illness and improve growth and development?

    Key messages

    • During the first days after birth, premature babies need lower salt (sodium) intakes to prevent high blood sodium levels. However, we are uncertain if there are any effects on infant death or newborn health whilst in hospital. Growth and longer‐term development were largely unreported in studies of early sodium supplementation.

    • After the first days following birth, premature babies need higher sodium intakes to prevent low blood sodium levels. Higher sodium intake may also reduce postnatal growth failure of premature babies. However, we are uncertain if there are any effects on death, newborn health whilst in hospital, or longer‐term development.

    • Due to insufficient evidence, we don't know if giving higher or lower sodium in the first days after birth and also about a week after birth affects blood sodium, growth, death, outcomes whilst in hospital (including chronic lung problems and severe gut injury ‐ necrotising enterocolitis) and long‐term development.

    What is high sodium?

    Babies born very preterm (before 32 weeks' gestation) are at increased risk of high blood sodium (salt) levels (150 mmol/L or higher) in the first days (early hypernatraemia). Much of this is due to high water losses through the skin.

    What is low sodium?

    Babies born very preterm are also at increased risk of low blood sodium levels (less than 130 mmol/L) after the first days (late hyponatraemia). Much of this is due to high sodium losses in the urine and infant growth needs.

    What did we want to find out?

    Too much or too little sodium intake may affect newborn outcomes in hospital, growth and developmental outcomes. We aimed to determine the effects on sodium levels, newborn outcomes in the nursery (including death, gut and lung problems), growth and developmental outcomes of higher versus lower sodium intake in preterm infants.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that investigated higher versus lower intake of sodium in both intravenous (with a drip into a vein) or enteral (via a tube through the mouth or nose into the gut) feeding given to premature babies. We conducted separate comparisons of studies that assessed early (less than 7 days after birth), late (7 or more days after birth), and early and late sodium supplementation.

    What did we find?

    We found 4 studies with 103 babies that compared early (less than 7 days after birth), 4 studies with 138 babies that compared late (7 or more days after birth), and one study with 20 babies that reported early and late higher versus lower sodium intakes.

    Main results

    Early (before 7 days after birth) higher sodium intake may result in more infants with high sodium levels but similar numbers of infants with low sodium levels in preterm infants. However, we are uncertain if there are any effects on infant death or other effects on infant health whilst in hospital. Few studies reported growth and longer‐term infant development after discharge from hospital.

    Late (7 or more days after birth) higher sodium intake may reduce the incidence of low sodium levels. We are uncertain if late higher intake affects the incidence of high sodium levels. Late higher sodium intake may reduce postnatal growth failure. We are uncertain if late higher sodium intake affects infant death or has other effects on health whilst in hospital, or longer‐term infant development after discharge from hospital.

    We don't know if early and late higher versus lower sodium intake affects blood sodium, growth, infant death, newborn health in hospital, and long‐term outcomes.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    The studies in this review are all small, and many outcomes were incompletely or not reported. As a result, we are very uncertain about the effects of higher versus lower sodium intakes for premature babies on infant health whilst in hospital, including infant death, growth and longer‐term infant development after discharge from hospital. More studies are needed, particularly to determine the best sodium intake in the first day after birth, the effect of higher versus lower sodium intake during the first week on hospital outcomes, growth and long‐term outcomes, and the effect of higher versus intermediate sodium intake after the first week on growth, hospital outcomes and longer‐term infant development after discharge from hospital.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up‐to‐date as of August 2022.

    Implications for practice

    Early (< 7 days) higher versus lower sodium supplementation may result in an increased incidence of hypernatraemia and may result in a similar incidence of hyponatraemia. We are uncertain if there are any effects on mortality or neonatal morbidity. Growth and longer‐term development outcomes were largely unreported in trials of early sodium supplementation.

    Late (≥ 7 days) higher versus lower sodium supplementation may reduce the incidence of hyponatraemia. We are uncertain if late higher intake affects the incidence of hypernatraemia. Late higher sodium intake may reduce postnatal growth failure. We are uncertain if late higher sodium intake affects mortality, other neonatal morbidities or longer‐term development.

    We are uncertain if early and late higher versus lower sodium supplementation affects outcomes.

    Implications for research

    More trials are needed, particularly to determine optimal sodium intake in the first day, the effect of higher versus lower sodium intake during the first week on morbidity and long‐term outcome, and the effect of higher versus intermediate sodium intake after the first week on growth, morbidity and long‐term development. Alternative approaches including targeting higher versus lower thresholds of serum sodium for providing sodium supplementation need evaluation in clinical trials.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Sodium imbalances may impact on neonatal outcomes including intraventricular haemorrhage, patent ductus arteriosus, respiratory distress, sensorineural hearing impairment and motor development (Moritz 2009; Oh 2012). Preterm infants are particularly at risk of early hypernatraemia (Gawlowski 2006; Jones 1976; Rees 1984; Wilkins 1992b), and, later, hyponatraemia (Al‐Dahhan 1983; Day 1976; Mannan 2012; Rees 1984). Hypernatraemia has been variably defined as a serum sodium more than 145 mmol/L or higher (Balasubramanian 2012), or 150 mmol/L or higher (Baraton 2009; Harkavy 1983; Takahashi 1994); and hyponatraemia as a serum sodium less than 135 mmol/L (Kloiber 1996), or less than 130 mmol/L (Baraton 2009; Day 1976; Takahashi 1994).

    Serum sodium levels reflect water and sodium balances which change over time after birth. Initially, after birth, the extracellular fluid space contracts in association with a net negative sodium and water balance with accompanying weight loss (Bauer 1989; Bauer 1991; Bauer 1993; Lorenz 1995; Shaffer 1989). A substantial portion of the loss is transepidermal water loss, particularly in extremely preterm infants (Agren 1998; Lorenz 1995; Maurer 1984). Excess water loss is associated with early hypernatraemia (Jones 1976; Rees 1984; Wilkins 1992b). Higher renal fractional excretion of sodium may ameliorate the tendency to hypernatraemia (Ross 1977), although this is limited by the relatively low glomerular filtration rate (GFR) in the first few days in extremely preterm infants (Al‐Dahhan 1983; Aperia 1981; Mannan 2012; Wilkins 1992a). Clinicians typically modify water intakes of infants in response to changes in serum sodium and other indicators of hydration, particularly in the first week.

    Hyponatraemia by the end of the first week is common in very preterm infants if strategies for its prevention are not applied. A negative sodium balance was reported in 100% of infants born at less than 30 weeks' gestation, 70% at 30 to 32 weeks, 46% at 33 to 35 weeks, and 0% after 36 weeks (Al‐Dahhan 1983). Reported incidences of hyponatraemia less than 130 mmol/L in very low birth weight infants increases from 25% in the first week to 65% thereafter (Baraton 2009; Day 1976; Kloiber 1996; Takahashi 1994). Various mechanisms are reported to contribute to the development of hyponatraemia in preterm infants including renal losses and needs for growth (Agostoni 2010). Preterm infants have high fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) secondary to impaired tubular sodium reabsorption (Al‐Dahhan 1983; Aperia 1981; Mannan 2012; Wilkins 1992b). Despite a relatively low GFR, it is thought the increase in GFR over time frequently exceeds the limited tubular sodium reabsorption capacity in very preterm infants (Al‐Dahhan 1983; Aperia 1981). Reported risk factors for hyponatraemia include increasing prematurity (Mannan 2012), birth weight of less than 1000 grams, feedings of fortified human milk and occurrence of an intraventricular haemorrhage (Kloiber 1996). Additional treatments including diuretics and indomethacin are also associated with hyponatraemia (Kim 2015).

    Description of the intervention

    Prevention of sodium imbalance in preterm infants requires specific approaches to both water and sodium intake. The effect of restricted versus liberal water intake in preterm infants is assessed in a separate Cochrane Review (Bell 2014). Reflecting variations in clinical practice, water intakes were variable in the five included trials but typically commenced between 50 mL/kg/day and 80 mL/kg/day, increasing to 140 mL/kg/day to 200 mL/kg/day, with liberal groups receiving 20 mL/kg/day to 30 mL/kg/day higher than restricted groups. The review found that restricted water intake significantly increases postnatal weight loss and reduces the risk of patent ductus arteriosus and necrotising enterocolitis, but with a trend towards increased dehydration, although this was not statistically significant.

    Sodium intake in preterm infants is from a combination of parenteral and enteral sources. The European Society for Paediatric Gastroenterology, Hepatology and Nutrition (ESPGHAN) recommended parenteral sodium intakes specific for the phase of adaptation (Jochum 2018; Koletzko 2005). During the initial transition phase (hours to days), ESPGHAN recommends allowing for a negative net sodium balance of 2 mmol/kg to 5 mmol/kg; during the second intermediate phase to replete the body for electrolyte losses, and replace actual water and electrolytes; and during the third stable growth phase to provide 3.0 mmol/kg/day to 5.0 mmol/kg/day (69 mg/kg/day to 113 mg/kg/day), or higher if required, to replace losses and provide for building new tissue at intrauterine growth rates. A recent Australasian group consensus for standardised parenteral nutrition recommended minimal sodium intake of approximately 1 mmol/kg/day on day 1 using a starter parenteral nutrition formulation, with an increase to a maximum 4.5 mmol/kg/d in preterm and 3.5 mmol/kg/day in term infants (Bolisetty 2014). ESPGHAN recommendations for enteral intakes are 3 to 5 mmol/kg/day (Agostoni 2010). For the purposes of this Cochrane Review, early higher sodium supplementation is defined as commencement parenteral or enteral intakes of 2 mmol/kg/day or higher; and later higher sodium supplementation is defined as parenteral or enteral intakes of 5 mmol/kg/day or higher, and lower sodium supplementation as less than 3 mmol/kg/day.

    How the intervention might work

    Although early hypernatraemia more than 145 mmol/L is common in very preterm infants and may not be associated with increased morbidity (Gawlowski 2006), severe hypernatraemia has been associated with intraventricular haemorrhage in preterm infants (Lee 2015; Li 2007; Lim 2011). Severe hypernatraemia and rapid sodium correction is associated with increased mortality and convulsions in newborn infants (Bolat 2013). Early sodium restriction, particularly in parenteral fluids, may reduce the incidence and severity of hypernatraemia and its associated morbidities. Given early hypernatraemia is associated with substantial extracellular water loss, sodium requirements may be substantially influenced by water intake.

    In addition, later hyponatraemia may not be a benign condition (Moritz 2009). Preterm neonates receiving restricted sodium have an increased incidence of hyponatraemia, impaired neonatal growth (Al‐Dahhan 1983; Day 1976), and worse neurodevelopment at 10 to 13 years of age compared to sodium‐supplemented infants (Al‐Dahhan 1983). Hyponatraemia has also been reported to be a risk factor for sensorineural hearing loss (Ertl 2001), and cerebral palsy (Murphy 1997). After the initial period of adaptation, providing sufficient sodium intake to replace losses and provide for growth may reduce postnatal growth failure and influence development.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Sodium is an integral part of fluid and electrolyte therapy in newborn infants. Preterm infants have a significant burden of mortality and morbidity (Bolisetty 2015). There are concerns that imbalances of sodium intake may impact on neonatal outcomes including intraventricular haemorrhage, patent ductus arteriosus, respiratory distress, sensorineural hearing impairment and motor development (Moritz 2009; Oh 2012). In addition, extremely preterm infants continue to have high rates of postnatal growth failure (Ofek Shlomai 2014; Stoltz Sjostrom 2013). Optimising sodium intake has the potential to reduce morbidity and improve growth and long‐term outcomes of infants born preterm. There are no current systematic reviews assessing the benefits and harms of higher versus lower sodium intakes in preterm infants.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects of higher versus lower sodium supplementation in preterm infants, given:

    1. early (< 7 days following birth);

    2. late (≥ 7 days following birth);

    3. early and late.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs), quasi‐RCTs or cluster‐RCTs.

    Types of participants

    We included preterm infants born before 37 weeks of gestational age and low birth weight infants (< 2500 grams), or both.

    Types of interventions

    Nutritional supplementation that includes higher versus lower sodium supplementation in parenteral and enteral intake, or both. We excluded studies that had prespecified differential water intakes between groups.

    We performed separate comparisons of studies that compared:

    1. early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intakes;

    2. late (≥ 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intakes; and

    3. early and late higher versus lower sodium intake.

    Definitions of sodium intake for early comparisons were:

    1. higher sodium intake: commencing at 2 mmol/kg/day or higher; and

    2. lower sodium intake: commencing at less than 2 mmol/kg/day.

    Definitions of sodium intake for late comparisons were:

    1. higher sodium intake: 5 mmol/kg/day or higher;

    2. intermediate sodium intake: 3 mmol/kg/day or higher to less than 5 mmol/kg/day (as per ESPGHAN recommendations: Agostoni 2010);

    3. lower sodium intake: less than 3 mmol/kg/day.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Mortality (latest time reported up to hospital discharge)

    2. Neurodevelopmental disability at at least 18 months of postnatal age (defined as neurological abnormality including cerebral palsy on clinical examination, developmental delay more than two standard deviations below population mean on a standardised test of development, blindness (visual acuity less than 6/60), or deafness (any hearing impairment requiring amplification) at any time after term corrected)

    Secondary outcomes

    At latest time reported up to hospital discharge unless otherwise specified. For each comparison, we will report only outcomes that are chronologically appropriate.

    1. Hyponatraemia (defined as < 130 mmol/L); early (< 7 days); late (≥ 7 days)

    2. Hypernatraemia (defined as ≥ 150 mmol/L); early (< 7 days); late (≥ 7 days)

    3. Postnatal growth failure (weight < 10th percentile) at 28 days; near term‐corrected age (36 to 40 weeks) or at discharge; and follow‐up (at least 1 year of age)

    4. Late onset sepsis (≥ 1 episode of positive bacterial culture in cerebrospinal fluid, sterile urine or blood after 48 hours of age)

    5. Necrotising enterocolitis (Bell's grade ≥ 2; Neu 2011)

    6. Patent ductus arteriosus (either haemodynamically significant (Skelton 1994), or treated with a cyclo‐oxygenase inhibitor or ligation)

    7. Chronic lung disease (respiratory support or oxygen requirement at or beyond 36 weeks' postmenstrual age; Shennan 1988)

    8. Bronchopulmonary dysplasia (Jobe 2001):

      1. mild bronchopulmonary dysplasia, defined as a need for supplemental oxygen for 28 days or more but not at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age or discharge

      2. moderate bronchopulmonary dysplasia defined as oxygen for 28 days or more plus treatment with less than 30% oxygen at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age

      3. severe bronchopulmonary dysplasia defined as oxygen for 28 days or more plus 30% oxygen or higher, or positive pressure, or both, at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age

    9. Intraventricular haemorrhage (any intraventricular haemorrhage or severe intraventricular haemorrhage (grade 3 or 4); Papile 1978)

    10. Periventricular leukomalacia (cystic)

    11. Term magnetic resonance imaging brain abnormalities graded as normal, mild, moderate or severe (e.g. Inder 2003)

    12. Retinopathy of prematurity (any or severe retinopathy of prematurity (stage 3 or 4); International Committee 2005).

    13. Individual components of neurodevelopment at at least 18 months' postnatal age:

      1. cerebral palsy on clinical examination

      2. developmental delay more than two standard deviations below population mean on a standardised test of development

      3. blindness (visual acuity less than 6/60)

      4. deafness (any hearing impairment requiring amplification) at any time after term‐corrected age

    14. Growth of infant:

      1. days to regain birth weight

      2. maximal weight loss: gram; per cent

      3. weight gain: up to 1 month of age (g/kg/day); at latest time measured (g/kg/day) (defined as from 1 month to time of discharge); to follow‐up beyond 12 months (kg/year)

      4. linear growth: up to age 1 month (cm/week); at latest time measured (cm/week); to follow‐up beyond 12 months (cm/year)

      5. head circumference: up to age 1 month (cm/week); at latest time measured (cm/week); to follow‐up beyond 12 months (cm/year)

      6. change in weight z‐score: up to age 1 month; at latest time measured; to follow‐up beyond 12 months

      7. change in length z‐score: up to age 1 month; at latest time measured; to follow‐up beyond 12 months

      8. change in head circumference z‐score: up to age 1 month; at latest time measured; to follow‐up beyond 12 months

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (DO and ND) assessed titles and abstracts retrieved from the search in Covidence to determine eligibility. We managed disagreements through discussion. We entered search results into EndNote 2013 and assessed them for eligibility in either Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020), or Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2023), or both.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (DO and ND) independently extracted data. One review author (ND) extracted data to a data extraction form. The other author extracted data in Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020), or RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2023), or both. Both authors cross‐checked data entry in Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020), or RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2023), or both. We resolved any discrepancies by discussion.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (DO and ND) independently assessed the risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) of all included studies using the Cochrane risk of bias tool, RoB 1 (Higgins 2017), for the following domains.

    1. Sequence generation (selection bias)

    2. Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    4. Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    5. Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    6. Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    7. Any other bias

    We managed any disagreements through discussion. We presented our assessment of risk of bias for the included studies in the risk of bias summary tables and graphs as generated through input into Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020), or RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2023), or both. See Appendix 2 for a more detailed description of risk of bias for each domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    1. We present dichotomous (binary) data as a measure of relative risk by using a risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Where analyses of RR were significant, we calculated the risk difference (RD) and calculated the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) for comparison against other treatments or non‐treatment (Higgins 2022a).

    2. We present continuous data as a mean difference (MD) when studies used the same scale. Alternatively, if they used different scales, we planned to calculate a standardised mean differences (SMD) and combine in meta‐analysis (Higgins 2022a).

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis in our review is the participant. Nonetheless, if the unit of analysis was not the same as the unit of randomisation, such as in cluster‐randomised trials, we planned to adjust for clustering by using the guidance given in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2022b). If there are multiple measurements for the same participant (e.g. multiple reported instances of hyponatraemia or hypernatraemia or sepsis in a single infant) this will be counted as an event for each outcome for the participant.

    Dealing with missing data

    We intended to contact the original study authors if the information provided in the published article did not provide sufficient data or results to address the outcomes of our review. However, given the historical nature of the majority of the eligible studies in this review, we did not.

    We performed analyses based on intention‐to‐treat (ITT) principles. The potential for bias from missing data was assessed independently and studies were downgraded accordingly. Sensitivity analysis was performed that excluded studies with >10% loss of data.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed the included studies for heterogeneity through three successive steps to determine if they should be pooled with the rest of the included studies or considered separately.

    1. Two review authors (DO and ND) independently analysed the included studies for their similarity of participants, interventions and outcomes, and for methodological diversity (study design and risk of bias; Deeks 2022). We carried out subgroup analyses for gestational age and birth weight, study fluid intakes, and sick or stable infants to explore possible reasons for heterogeneity.

    2. We then assessed the included studies for statistical heterogeneity using the Chi² test with a P value of less than 0.10 being statistically significant (Deeks 2022).

    3. We then calculated the I² statistic (Higgins 2003), as instructed in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Deeks 2022); where less than 25% is likely to indicate no heterogeneity, 25% to 49% may represent minimal heterogeneity, 50% to 74% may represent moderate heterogeneity, and greater than 75% may represent substantial or high heterogeneity. The importance of the observed value of the I² statistic does depend on the magnitude and direction of the treatment effects, and strength of evidence for heterogeneity (that is, the P value from the Chi² test or the confidence interval for I² statistic).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If we had found 10 or more included studies, we intended to construct a funnel plot. We would have visually inspected the funnel plot for asymmetry and, if detected, we would have attempted to explain it using exploratory analyses (e.g. Rücker's arcsine test for dichotomous data and Egger's linear regression test for continuous data) to further investigate funnel plot asymmetry. A P value of less than 0.1 would have been considered as the level of statistical significance (Deeks 2022).

    Data synthesis

    We synthesised the data in accordance with the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Deeks 2022), using one of the following methods.

    1. We used the fixed‐effect model in the absence of statistical heterogeneity according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions where the analysis produces an estimate of the true effect (Deeks 2022).

    2. If cluster‐RCTs were included, we planned to use the generic inverse variance method (Higgins 2022b).

    We did not pool clinically heterogeneous studies in a meta‐analysis.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Where sufficient data were available or heterogeneity existed, we explored further the treatment effect in specific subgroups (restricted to the summary of findings outcome measures), including the following.

    • Gestational age of infant: extremely preterm (born < 28 weeks' gestational age); very preterm (< 32 weeks); preterm (< 37 weeks).

    • birth weight: extremely low birth weight (≤ 1000 grams); very low birth weight (≤ 1500 grams); low birth weight (≤ 2500 grams); appropriate for gestational age (birth weight percentile > 10th centile); small for gestational age (birth weight percentile ≤ 10th centile).

    • Fluid intake: restricted water intake (≤ 60 mL/kg day 1 and ≤ 150 mL/kg day 7) versus liberal water intake (≥ 80 mL/kg day 1 and > 150 mL/kg after day 7; Tammela 1992)

    • Stable (no significant system‐related comorbidity) versus sick infants (included infants with comorbidity)

    • Sodium intake varied in parenteral intake only versus varied in enteral intake only versus varied in parenteral and enteral intake

    Sensitivity analysis

    We explored methodological heterogeneity through sensitivity analyses.

    We included only studies at low risk of bias, with adequate allocation concealment, randomisation or blinding of treatment, and less than 10% loss to follow‐up. Where significant heterogeneity was found, in order to determine the impact of risk of bias on the overall effect estimate, we planned to compare studies at high risk of bias to studies at low risk of bias and test for subgroup differences using the Chi² and I² statistics (Deeks 2022).

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook (Schünemann 2013), to assess the certainty of evidence for the following (clinically relevant) outcomes.

    • Mortality

    • Neurodevelopmental disability

    • Hyponatraemia (< 130 mmol/L)

    • Hypernatraemia (≥ 150 mmol/L)

    • Postnatal growth failure

    • Necrotising enterocolitis

    • Chronic neonatal lung disease

    Two review authors (DO and ND) independently assessed the certainty of the evidence for each of the outcomes above. We considered evidence from RCTs as high certainty but downgraded the evidence one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of the evidence, precision of estimates and presence of publication bias. We used GRADEpro GDT to create three summary of findings tables to report the certainty of the evidence for the following comparisons.

    • Early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake for preterm infants (summary of findings Table 1)

    • Late (≥ 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake for preterm infants (summary of findings Table 2)

    • Early and late (day 1 to 28 after birth) higher versus lower sodium intake for preterm infants (summary of findings Table 3)

    The GRADE approach results in an assessment of the certainty of a body of evidence as one of four grades.

    • High certainty: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate certainty: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low certainty: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited; the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low certainty: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

Ertl 1982 reported plasma prostaglandin and prostaglandin F2 values. We did not include any outcomes from this study in the quantitative meta‐analysis.

    Goldman 1962 enroled well, premature infants 1000 g to 1800 g on human milk feeds with higher fluid intake (approximately 200 mL/kg/day) and compared pooled human milk with 13 mEq/L of NaCl added from 24 hours to 72 hours up to day 28 versus no supplementation.     Isemann 2016 enroled infants born at less than 32 weeks’ gestational age on enteral feeds. They did not report fluid intakes. They compared higher oral sodium intake 4 mEq/kg/day from 7 to 35 days' age versus placebo (sterile water). The primary outcome was weight gain during the first six weeks of age. Hyponatraemia and hypernatraemia were defined as serum sodium concentration less than 135 mmol/L and more than 145 mmol/L. Isemann 2016 also reported weekly weight gain velocities (g/kg/d), anthropometry at birth and at six weeks of age, and late‐onset sepsis, necrotising enterocolitis, chronic lung disease at 36 weeks’ corrected gestational age, patent ductus arteriosus after two weeks, death, chronic diuretic therapy, systemic hypertension measured at 36 weeks and duration of hospital stay.

    Shaffer 1989 enroled infants with birth weight 700 g to 1500 g, appropriate weight for gestational age, and postnatal age less than 12 hours with absence of severe medical or surgical problems other than prematurity, on intravenous or enteral feeds. The infants received lower fluid intakes, which started at 75 mL/kg/day from day 1, increased by 10 mL/kg/day until day 5. The higher group received sodium 3 mmol/kg/day versus the lower group, who received sodium 1 mmol/kg/day from day 1 to 30. Shaffer 1989 reported fluid intake, sodium intake, urine output, and body weight were measured daily, serum sodium concentration daily for the first 10 postnatal days, sodium, creatinine, and atrial natruretic factor (ANF) on postnatal days 1, 5, 10, 20, and 30 with six‐hour urine collections obtained simultaneously for measurement of sodium, creatinine, aldosterone and arginine vasopressin (AVP). They calculated sodium balance, creatinine clearance, and fractional sodium excretion.

    Vanpee 1995 enroled infants with gestational age 29 to 34 weeks on enteral feeds, with lower to intermediate fluid intake (total fluid intake 143 versus 136 mL/kg/day at 1 week and 170 versus 164 mL/kg/day at 2 weeks). The higher group received oral sodium chloride supplementation 4 mmol/kg/day from day 4 to 2 weeks (total sodium intake 5.0 ± 0.9 mmol/kg/day) versus the non‐supplemented group, with total sodium intake 1.7 ± 0.6 mmol/kg/day.     Comparison 1. Early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake

    Four studies (103 infants) compared early higher versus lower sodium intake (Costarino 1992; Ekblad 1987; Hartnoll 2000; Shaffer 1989).     Primary outcomes
    Mortality

    Meta‐analysis found early higher versus lower sodium intake may not affect mortality (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.38 to 2.72; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 83 infants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1).

    Neurodevelopmental disability

    Not reported

    Secondary outcomes
    Hyponatraemia less than 130 mmol/L

    Meta‐analysis found that early higher versus lower sodium intake may lead to a similar incidence of hyponatraemia (RR 0.68, 95% CI 0.40 to 1.13; I2 = 0%; 3 studies, 83 infants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake

    Four studies reporting outcomes of 103 infants compared early higher versus lower sodium intake (Costarino 1992; Ekblad 1987; Hartnoll 2000; Shaffer 1989). Studies compared higher intake with 3 mmol/kg/day to 4 mmol/kg/day sodium supplementation after the first day to either no sodium supplementation, lower (2 mmol/kg/day) sodium supplementation, or delayed sodium supplementation when 6% of birth weight had been lost. For primary outcomes of the review, we found that early higher versus lower sodium intake may not affect mortality (low‐certainty evidence). However, no studies reported neurodevelopmental follow‐up.

    For secondary outcomes, early higher versus lower sodium intake may lead to a similar incidence of hyponatraemia less than 130 mmol/L (83 infants; 3 studies; low‐certainty evidence) but may lead to an increased incidence of hypernatraemia 150 mmol/L or higher (RD 0.17, 95% CI 0.01, 0.34; NNTH 6, 95% CI 3 to 100; 4 studies, 103 infants; low‐certainty evidence).

    No studies reported postnatal growth failure and other neonatal growth measures. A single small study (Hartnoll 2000; 46 infants), reported that early higher versus lower sodium intake may reduce the maximal weight loss percent. There were no reported differences found in neonatal morbidities including necrotising enterocolitis, patent ductus arteriosus, intraventricular haemorrhage or bronchopulmonary dysplasia (≥ 28 days of age), although the certainty of evidence is low or very low for these outcomes. No studies reported chronic lung disease at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age.

    Late (≥ 7 days or more following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake

    Three studies reporting outcomes for 106 infants compared late higher versus lower sodium intake (Ayisi 1992; Isemann 2016; Vanpee 1995). Two studies compared intermediate sodium supplementation (≥ 3 mmol/kg/day to < 5 mmol/kg/day) versus no supplementation, and one study compared higher sodium supplementation (≥ 5 mmol/kg/day) versus no supplementation. For primary outcomes of the review, we found that late higher versus lower sodium intake may not affect mortality (low‐certainty evidence). However, no studies reported neurodevelopmental follow‐up. For secondary outcomes, late higher versus lower sodium intake may reduce the incidence of hyponatraemia less than 130 mmol/L (RD −0.42, 95% CI −0.59 to −0.24; NNTB 2, 95% CI 2 to 4; low‐certainty evidence). We are uncertain if late higher versus lower sodium intake affects the incidence of hypernatraemia, but data are limited to two studies comparing intermediate sodium supplementation (≥ 3 mmol/kg/day to < 5 mmol/kg/day) to no supplementation and reporting outcomes for only 69 infants.

    A single small study reported that later higher versus lower sodium intake may reduce the incidence of postnatal growth failure (low‐certainty evidence; Isemann 2016). However, several analyses of neonatal growth parameters found increased growth associated with higher late sodium supplementation.     A single study reported that late intermediate sodium intake ≥ 3 mmol/kg/day to < 5 mmol/kg/day may increase weight gain by an average of 4.03 g/kg/day (Isemann 2016). In addition, single small studies reported that late higher sodium intake of 5 mmol/kg/day or higher may increase linear growth by an average of 0.11 cm/week (Ayisi 1992), and late higher sodium intake of 3 mmol/kg/day or higher to less than 5 mmol/kg/day may increase percent weight change from birth to two weeks (Vanpee 1995).

    Reporting of neonatal morbidities was limited, with low‐ to very low‐certainty evidence that there was no effect on necrotising enterocolitis, patent ductus arteriosus and chronic lung disease at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age.

    Early and late higher versus lower sodium intake

    Two studies reporting outcomes for 52 infants compared early and late intermediate (≥ 3 mmol/kg/day to < 5 mmol/kg/day) sodium supplementation from day 1 up to day 28 or 30 (Goldman 1962; Shaffer 1989). However, infants in one study had clinical adjustment of sodium supplementation after day 10 in both groups (Shaffer 1989). Outcomes reported are limited to early hypernatraemia and weight gain by single studies. Hypernatraemia was reported to be no different between the groups with lower versus higher intake (very low‐certainty evidence). A single study reported no difference in weight gain.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    For early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake, this review found limited evidence from four studies reporting outcomes of 103 infants. Supplementation was predominately after the first day after birth, so may not be applicable to the first 24 hours after birth. For infants after the first day, higher intake with 3 mmol/kg/day to 4 mmol/kg/day sodium supplementation compared to either no sodium supplementation, lower (2 mmol/kg/day) sodium supplementation, or delayed sodium supplementation when 6% of birth weight had been lost may be associated with an increased incidence of hypernatraemia and a similar incidence of hyponatraemia. This review was not able to adequately explore the interaction of water intake and sodium supplementation as the prespecified subgroup analyses are underpowered. In addition, there were no eligible neurodevelopmental data available limiting conclusions regarding the impact of early sodium intake on development. Reporting of growth was lacking, and neonatal morbidity outcomes were limited.

    For late (seven or more days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake, this review found limited evidence from four studies reporting outcomes of 138 infants. Late sodium supplementation of 3 mmol/kg/day or higher may reduce the incidence of hyponatraemia, but we are uncertain of an effect on hypernatraemia. Limited data on growth outcomes were reported, but where reported there was low‐ to very low‐certainty evidence that there was a reduction in incidence of postnatal growth failure, and increased postnatal weight and linear growth with higher sodium supplementation. Reporting of neonatal morbidities was incomplete and underpowered, and no neurodevelopmental data were reported.

    As the review is underpowered to conduct meaningful subgroup analyses, particularly according to gestational age and birth weight, fluid intake and sick or stable infants, we are not able to make conclusions regarding the applicability of the overall results to specific gestational age or birth weight strata, clinical care practices including fluid intake strategies, and infant wellness.

    The studies in this review largely supplemented infants to higher versus lower sodium intakes irrespective of the infant's serum sodium levels. As such, the review findings largely relate to prophylactic sodium supplementation. The review studies do not address the question of using a higher versus lower threshold of serum sodium level to guide sodium supplementation.

    Quality of the evidence

    All analyses are underpowered to provide precise estimates of effect and certainty of evidence. All outcomes reported are of low‐ or very low‐certainty evidence.

    The review was underpowered to undertake meaningful subgroup analysis including for gestational age and birth weight, fluid intake, and sick or stable infants.

    We assessed two studies that compared early higher versus lower sodium intake as low risk of bias with adequate allocation concealment, randomisation or blinding of treatment, and less than 10% loss to follow‐up (Costarino 1992; Hartnoll 2000). We considered no other studies to be at low risk of bias. The sensitivity analysis is underpowered for all outcomes and limited to the early higher versus lower sodium intake comparison.

    Potential biases in the review process

    This review followed a prespecified, prepublished protocol (Chan 2017), completed a thorough search of the literature, independently assessed eligibility, and risk of bias, and cross‐checked data extraction. Two review authors independently assessed the GRADE certainty of evidence (DO and ND). We did not contact study authors as the majority of the reports are now historical. However, there is a potential review bias from selective reporting of outcomes as a result of incomplete reporting.

    We included a study that provided supplementation from day 0 to 10 in the comparison of early (< 7 days following birth) higher versus lower sodium intake as the single reported outcome, hypernatraemia, relates to early supplementation (Shaffer 1989).

    We did not include data from a single study that reported an increased rate of late onset sepsis in the higher sodium group in analysis as the data included culture‐negative infants treated with antibiotics for five days, which did not meet the review definition of late onset sepsis (Isemann 2016).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    There are no other systematic reviews of sodium intake published in the literature. A review of sodium requirements for preterm infants included RCTs and observational studies of sodium with or without multicomponent supplementation of preterm infants (Bischoff 2016). The review concluded that sodium deficiency should be avoided by maintaining serum level between 135 mEq/L and 145 mEq/L. The review found that sodium supplementation reduced the incidence of hyponatraemia. They concluded that the data provided by the included studies show that instead of the purported sodium supplementation beneficial effect on growth, what was demonstrated is the growth impairing effects of inadequate sodium intake. They recommended that, in well‐hydrated preterm infants, sufficient sodium intake should be provided to maintain serum sodium values between 135 mEq/L and 145 mEq/L to promote adequate growth early in life. Our Cochrane Review reports the evidence from RCTs for early and late sodium supplementation with the findings relating to late sodium supplementation consistent with the Bischoff 2016 review. In addition, the Bischoff 2016 review makes gestational age‐ and postnatal age‐specific recommendations for sodium intakes with recommended intakes for preterm infants born under 28 weeks of 6 mEq/L/day to 12 mEq/kg/day on day 3, and 4 mEq/L/day to 8 mEq/kg/day on day 7; these intakes are above those assessed by studies included in our review.

    The ESPGHAN/ESPEN/ESPR guidelines on paediatric parenteral nutrition: fluid and electrolytes (Jochum 2018), recommended sodium intakes in the first week based on term or preterm gestation and birth weight over 1500 g or under 1500 g. Recommended early intakes for infants born under 1500 g started at 0 mmol/kg/day to 2 mmol/kg/day from day 1, increasing up to 2 mmol/kg/day to 5 mmol/kg/day from day 4, consistent with the studies included in this review. For intermediate and growth period intakes from day 7, the recommended range was 3 mmol/kg/day to 5 (up to 7) mmol/kg/day, again consistent with the studies included in our review. The ESPGHAN Committee on Nutrition recommendation on enteral nutrition for preterm intakes (Agostoni 2010), was for a daily intake of 3 mmol/kg/day to 5 mmol/kg/day, again consistent with the studies and findings of this review.
