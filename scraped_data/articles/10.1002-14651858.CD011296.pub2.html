Sliding scale insulin for non‐critically ill hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus

    • Background

    Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder resulting from a defect in insulin secretion, function, or both. Hyperglycaemia in non‐critically ill hospitalised people is associated with poor clinical outcomes (infections, prolonged hospital stay, poor wound healing, higher morbidity and mortality). In the hospital setting people diagnosed with diabetes receive insulin therapy as part of their treatment in order to achieve metabolic control. However, insulin therapy can be provided by different strategies (sliding scale insulin (SSI), basal‐bolus insulin, and other modalities). Sliding scale insulin is currently the most commonly used method, however there is uncertainty about which strategy provides the best patient outcomes.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of SSI for non‐critically ill hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus.

    Search methods

    We identified eligible trials by searching MEDLINE, Embase, LILACS, and the Cochrane Library. We searched the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) and ClinicalTrials.gov trial registers. The date of the last search for all databases was December 2017. We also examined reference lists of identified randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and systematic reviews, and contacted trial authors.

    Selection criteria

    We included RCTs comparing SSI with other strategies for glycaemic control in non‐critically ill hospitalised adult participants of any sex with diabetes mellitus.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently extracted data, assessed trials for risk of bias, and evaluated the overall certainty of evidence utilising the GRADE instrument. We synthesised data using a random‐effects model meta‐analysis with 95% prediction intervals, if possible, or descriptive analysis, as appropriate.

    Main results

    Of 720 records screened, we included eight trials that randomised 1048 participants with type 2 diabetes (387 SSI participants and 615 participants in comparator groups were available for final analysis). We included non‐critically ill medical and surgical adults with the diagnosis of diabetes mellitus. The mean follow‐up time was measured by the mean length of hospital stay and ranged between five and 24 days. The mean age of participants was 44.5 years to 71 years.

    Overall, we judged the risk of bias on the trial level as unclear for selection bias, high for outcome‐related performance and detection bias with regard to hypoglycaemic episodes, other adverse events, and mean glucose levels, and low for all‐cause mortality and length of hospital stay. Attrition bias was low for all outcome measures.

    Six trials compared SSI with a basal‐bolus insulin scheme, three of which investigating 64% of all participants in this category also applying an SSI approach in the bolus comparator part. One trial had a basal insulin‐only comparator arm, and the remaining trial used continuous insulin infusion as the comparator. For our main comparison of SSI versus basal‐bolus insulin, the results were as follows. Four trials reported mortality data. One out of 268 participants in the SSI group (0.3%) compared with two out of 334 participants in the basal‐bolus group (0.6%) died (low‐certainty evidence). Severe hypoglycaemic episodes, defined as blood glucose levels below 40 mg/dL (2.2 mmol/L), showed a risk ratio (RR) of 0.22, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.05 to 1.00; P = 0.05; 5 trials; 667 participants; very low‐certainty evidence. The 95% prediction interval ranged between 0.02 and 2.57. All nine severe hypoglycaemic episodes were observed among the 369 participants on basal‐bolus insulin (2.4%). The mean length of hospital stay was 0.5 days longer for the SSI group, 95% CI ‐0.5 to 1.4; P = 0.32; 6 trials; 717 participants; very low‐certainty evidence. The 95% prediction interval ranged between ‐1.7 days and 2.7 days. Adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes, such as postoperative infections, showed a RR of 1.16, 95% CI 0.25 to 5.37; P = 0.85; 3 trials; 481 participants; very low‐certainty evidence. The mean blood glucose levels ranged across basal‐bolus groups from 156 mg/dL (8.7 mmol/L) to 221 mg/dL (12.3 mmol/L). The mean blood glucose level in the SSI groups was 14.8 mg/dL (0.8 mmol/L) higher (95% CI 7.8 (0.4) to 21.8 (1.2); P < 0.001; 6 trials; 717 participants; low‐certainty evidence). The 95% prediction interval ranged between ‐3.6 mg/dL (‐0.2 mmol/L) and 33.2 mg/dL (1.8 mmol/L). No trial reported on diabetes‐related mortality or socioeconomic effects.

    Authors' conclusions

    We are uncertain which insulin strategy (SSI or basal‐bolus insulin) is best for non‐critically hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus. A basal‐bolus insulin strategy in these patients might result in better short‐term glycaemic control but could increase the risk for severe hypoglycaemic episodes. The certainty of the body of evidence comparing SSI with basal‐bolus insulin was low to very low and needs to be improved by adequately performed, well‐powered RCTs in different hospital environments with well‐educated medical staff using identical short‐acting insulins in both intervention and comparator arms to compare the rigid SSI approach with flexible insulin application strategies.

    • Sliding scale insulin for non‐critically ill hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus

    Review question

    What are the effects of sliding scale insulin compared with other insulin therapies for non‐critically ill hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus?

    Background

    There are several options to treat people with diabetes who have been admitted to the hospital. Both low glucose levels (hypoglycaemia) and high glucose levels (hyperglycaemia) might increase the risk of death and complications such as infections and longer hospital stay and therefore should be avoided. The most common method to treat hospitalised people with diabetes is sliding scale insulin therapy. The term 'sliding scale' refers to the increasing administration of the pre‐meal insulin dose based on the blood sugar level before the meal. For example, if a person has a blood glucose level between 140 mg/dL to 180 mg/dL, a short‐acting usual insulin dose could be 4 units insulin, and for a blood glucose level between 181 mg/dL and 220 mg/dL, it could be 6 units insulin. This kind of rigid insulin application usually fixes the amount of carbohydrate to be eaten at each meal but does not deliver insulin in a physiologic manner. It is therefore doubtful whether good glycaemic control (satisfactory blood glucose levels) can be achieved by sliding scale insulin in hospitalised diabetic people and whether this approach results in better outcomes in the long term. There are other insulin strategies like background (basal) insulin doses combined with flexible pre‐meal insulin (bolus insulin) according to what and how much people want to eat. Sliding scale insulin means strict adherence to a regular schedule of meals and physical activity, and people must follow a prescribed diet. Intensified insulin therapy (basal‐bolus strategy) allows flexible insulin doses according to physical activity, stress, and meal preferences, and under hospital conditions demands well‐trained medical staff. Sliding scale insulin is still widely used, and it remains unclear which insulin strategy is best to treat hospitalised diabetic people.

    Study characteristics

    We found eight randomised controlled trials (studies in which participants are assigned to one of two or more treatment groups in a random manner) where 387 people on sliding scale insulin therapy and 615 on mainly basal‐bolus insulin treatment with type 2 diabetes could be analysed. The average length of hospital stay was between five and 24 days. Six studies compared sliding scale insulin with basal‐bolus insulin; one study compared sliding scale insulin with basal insulin only; and the remaining study used continuous insulin infusion as the control group. The average age of participants was 44.5 years to 71 years.

    The evidence is up to date as of December 2017.

    Key results

    The main comparison was between sliding scale insulin and basal‐bolus insulin therapy with the following results. Of the four studies reporting deaths, one out of 268 participants in the SSI group compared with two out of 334 participants in the basal‐bolus group died. Severe hypoglycaemic episodes, defined as blood glucose levels below 40 mg/dL, occurred in 5 per 1000 people in the sliding scale insulin groups compared with 24 per 1000 people in the basal‐bolus insulin groups. These data are uncertain because further analyses showed neither a positive nor a negative effect comparing both insulin strategies. The length of hospital stay in the sliding scale insulin groups compared with the basal‐bolus insulin groups was 0.5 days longer, again with further analyses indicating that this information is uncertain. The results for adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes, such as postoperative infections, did not indicate an advantage or disadvantage of either strategy. The average blood glucose level during hospital stay in the sliding scale groups was 14.8 mg/dL higher compared with the basal‐bolus groups. We are uncertain about these data because analyses showed neither a positive nor a negative effect comparing both insulin strategies. No trial reported on deaths caused by diabetes as such or socioeconomic effects like costs of the interventions or absence from work.

    Certainty of the evidence

    Overall, our confidence in the results for all analysed outcomes was low or very low mainly due to the low number of studies and participants and because the results were not precise, that is they could change in any direction once new studies are published.

    • Implications for practice

    Based on low‐ to very low‐certainty evidence, our results for non‐critically ill hospitalised adults with diabetes mellitus in the medical and surgical in‐hospital setting showed that a basal‐bolus insulin strategy that applied higher total daily doses of insulin than the sliding scale insulin regimen might result in better short‐term glycaemic control but might also cause an increase in severe hypoglycaemic episodes. We are uncertain whether sliding scale insulin increases or decreases mortality, length of hospital stay, and adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes (such as infectious complications).

    Implications for research

    Future randomised controlled trials should adequately define the clinical conditions of hospitalised people with diabetes mellitus and the associated appropriate treatment choice. Multinational trials would improve the external validity of trial results. There is a need for adequately performed, well‐powered randomised controlled trials in different hospital environments with well‐educated medical staff. Identical short‐acting insulins in both intervention and comparator arms should be used to compare the rigid sliding scale insulin approach with flexible insulin application strategies.

    • Description of the condition

    Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a metabolic disorder resulting from a defect in insulin secretion, function, or both. The consequence of DM is chronic hyperglycaemia, with subsequent effects on carbohydrate, fat, and protein metabolism. Long‐term DM complications include retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy, and an increased risk of cardiovascular disease (e.g. myocardial infarction, cerebrovascular events). Adequate glycaemic control in hospitalised people with DM represents a clinical challenge due to the wide range of treatment strategies and the morbidity/mortality risk that these participants present (Umpierrez 2002).

    Diabetes mellitus is the most common chronic disease in low‐, middle‐, and high‐income countries. In 2000, the worldwide diabetes prevalence was 2.8% (for all age groups); in 2011, the global prevalence in the adult population was 8.3%. It is estimated that in 2030 diabetes prevalence will be 4.4% (for all age groups), affecting approximately 366 million people worldwide (Whiting 2011; Wild 2004).

    People diagnosed with DM are frequently admitted for hospital care, with an estimated admission rate by year of about 25% to 30% (Moss 1999). At hospital admission, glycaemic control becomes a priority; there is an expected increase in serum glucose because of the metabolic stress (caused by the present illness or procedures) resulting in hyperglycaemia, increased infection risk, and poor wound healing. Insulin therapy regimens are currently under discussion (Braithwaite 2008; Buchleitner 2012). Most of the data comes from people with critical care illness; however, even in these patients the best treatment regimens are unclear (Farrokhi 2012; Friedrich 2010; Griesdale 2009; Klonoff 2011).

    Description of the intervention

    Four strategies are used to achieve glycaemic control in hospitalised diabetic people: basal insulin, prandial insulin, correction‐dose insulin, and sliding scale insulin (SSI). These strategies are used to maintain optimal glucose levels (fasting and postprandial). The main difference between these strategies is the intervention purpose: basal insulin administration inhibits hepatic glucose production and is mainly responsible for controlling fasting glucose; prandial (bolus or mealtime) insulin aims to control postprandial glucose levels by promoting glucose disposal into the muscles; and correction dose insulin is administered in addition to prandial insulin using an algorithm‐based treatment to provide an adjustment to carbohydrate intake (Lien 2011).

    Sliding scale insulin (sometimes called correctional insulin) is poorly defined and is used in different ways: traditionally, the insulin prescription is administered according to the measured blood glucose levels and therefore constitutes a reactive response to hyperglycaemia; SSI is an intervention usually characterised by a subcutaneous administration (regular insulin or rapid‐acting insulin analogues), regardless of the timing of food intake, presence or absence of pre‐existing insulin administration, and sensitivity to insulin, without the acute illness consideration. The SSI scheme aims to maintain the glucose levels between 7.7 mmol/L (140 mg/dL) and 10 mmol/L (180 mg/dL) (Lien 2011; Umpierrez 2012). Some physicians even employ this approach using long‐acting insulin analogues or neutral protamine Hagedorn (NPH) insulin.

    Sliding scale insulin is regularly prescribed as part of the medical treatment in hospitalised diabetic people, despite the lack of physiological congruence and strong evidence to support it.

    Adverse effects of the intervention

    There are many considerations regarding the use of SSI in the treatment of diabetic inpatients. This strategy has been widely criticised when used to obtain glycaemic control. Previous studies have revealed longer days to achieve metabolic control, greater variability of glucose levels (hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia), higher infection rate, and increased mortality (Baldwin 2005; Childs 2003; Umpierrez 2012).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Sliding scale insulin is one of the most frequently used therapies in the metabolic control of diabetic inpatients. However, this strategy may be associated with poor glycaemic control causing more complications (higher infection rate, increased mortality, prolonged hospital stay). Several non‐systematic review articles have addressed the SSI scheme (Miller 2011; Umpierrez 2007b). Given the potential public health relevance, we have systematically collected and examined all randomised controlled trials on SSI treatments for non‐critical diabetic hospitalised people in order to establish the risk and benefits of this therapy.

    • Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled clinical trials (RCTs).

    Types of participants

    Non‐critically ill hospitalised adult (18 years or older) diabetic people. We expected that most of the trials would have type 2 diabetes participants, because people with type 1 diabetes mellitus require a basal‐bolus insulin treatment. We excluded nursing home residents and people treated in intensive care units.

    Diagnostic criteria

    In order to be consistent with changes in the classification of and diagnostic criteria for diabetes over the years, the diagnosis had to be established using the standard criteria valid at the time of the trial commencing (e.g. ADA 1999; ADA 2008; WHO 1998). Ideally, the diagnostic criteria should have been described. Where necessary, we used the trial authors' definition of diabetes mellitus and planned to subject diagnostic criteria to a subgroup analysis.

    Types of interventions

    We planned to investigate the following comparisons of intervention versus comparator.

    Intervention

    • Sliding scale insulin.

    Comparators

    • Basal insulin only.

    • Basal‐bolus insulin (delivered by multiple subcutaneous injections or an insulin pump).

    • Correction dose insulin.

    Other concomitant interventions had to be the same in the intervention and comparator groups to establish fair comparisons.

    Minimum duration of intervention and follow‐up

    We accepted any duration of intervention and follow‐up.

    We defined extended follow‐up periods (also called open‐label extension studies) as follow up of participants once the original trial, as specified in the trial protocol, has been terminated. However, such studies are frequently of an observational nature, and we only planned to evaluate them for adverse events (Buch 2011; Megan 2012).

    Summary of specific exclusion criteria

    • Nursing home residents and people treated in intensive care units.

    • Children and adolescents up to 18 years of age.

    We did not exclude trials because one or several of our primary or secondary outcome measures were not reported in the publication. If no additional data were available, we planned to provide some basic information in a supplementary table.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • All‐cause mortality.

    • Diabetes‐related mortality.

    • Hypoglycaemic episodes.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Length of hospital stay.

    • Adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes.

    • Mean blood glucose.

    • Socioeconomic effects.

    Method and timing of outcome measurement

    • All‐cause mortality: defined as the total number of deaths from any cause, measured as in‐hospital mortality and as 30‐day all‐cause mortality.

    • Diabetes‐related mortality: defined as the total number of deaths due to myocardial infarction, ischaemic heart disease, stroke or sequelae of stroke, heart failure, cardiac arrest, peripheral vascular disease, kidney disease, hyperglycaemia, and hypoglycaemia, measured as in‐hospital mortality and as 30‐day mortality.

    • Hypoglycaemic episodes: defined as the number of blood glucose values less than 70 mg/dL or according to trial authors' definitions.

    • Length of hospital stay: defined as the duration of a single episode of hospitalisation and measured by subtracting the day of admission from the day of discharge.

    • Adverse events other than hypoglycaemia, e.g. hypokalaemia defined as a serum potassium concentration less than 3.5 mEq/L or injection site reactions, measured at any time during the hospital stay.

    • Mean blood glucose: as measured during participant hospital stay.

    • Socioeconomic effects, e.g. costs of the intervention, absence from work, medication consumption.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following sources from the inception of each database to the date given, placing no restrictions on language of publication.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via the Cochrane Library (Issue 11 of 12, November 2017; searched on 22 December 2017).

    • MEDLINE Ovid SP (Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE Daily and Ovid MEDLINE; 1946 to present; searched on 22 December 2017).

    • Embase Ovid SP (1974 to 21 December 2017; searched on 22 December 2017).

    • LILACS (Latin American and Caribbean Health Science Information database; last update 8 December 2017; searched on 22 December 2017).

    • US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov) (searched on 22 December 2017).

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) (www.who.int/trialsearch/); searched on 22 December 2017).

    We applied a continuous MEDLINE (via Ovid SP) email alert service to identify newly published trials using the same search strategy as described for MEDLINE (Appendix 1).

    Searching other resources

    We attempted to identify other potentially eligible trials or ancillary publications by searching the reference lists of included trials, (systematic) reviews, meta‐analyses, and health technology assessment reports.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (LECL, NDF) independently scanned the abstract, title, or both, of every record retrieved to determine which trials should be assessed further. We evaluated the full texts of all potentially relevant articles for inclusion in the review. Any discrepancies were resolved through consensus or by recourse to a third review author (AVH). We prepared a flow diagram of the number of trials identified and excluded at each stage in accordance with the PRISMA flow diagram of trial selection (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (LECL, DGP) independently extracted key participant and intervention characteristics from the included trials. We reported data on efficacy outcomes and adverse events using standard data extraction forms from Cochrane Metabolic and Endocrine Disorders. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion or by consultation with a third review author (AVH) where necessary (for details see Table 1; Appendix 2; Appendix 3; Appendix 4; Appendix 5; Appendix 6; Appendix 7; Appendix 8; Appendix 9; Appendix 10; Appendix 11; Appendix 12).

    Dealing with duplicate and companion publications

    In the event of duplicate publications, companion documents, or multiple reports of a primary trial, we maximised the information yield by collating all available data, and used the most complete data set aggregated across all known publications. We listed duplicate publications, companion documents, multiple reports of a primary trial, and trial documents of included trials (such as trial registry information) as secondary references under the study ID of the included trial. Furthermore, we listed duplicate publications, companion documents, multiple reports of a trial, and trial documents of excluded trials (such as trials registry information) as secondary references under the study ID of the excluded trial.

    Data from clinical trials registers

    If data from included trials were available as study results in clinical trials registers such as ClinicalTrials.gov or similar sources, we made full use of this information and extracted the data. If there was also a full publication of the trial, we collated and critically appraised all available data. If an included trial was marked as a completed study in a clinical trials register but no additional information (study results, publication, or both) was available, we added this trial to the Characteristics of studies awaiting classification table.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (LECL, DGP) independently assessed the risk of bias of each included trial. Any disagreements were resolved by consensus or by consultation with a third review author (AVH) where necessary. If adequate information was unavailable from the trials, trial protocols, or both, we contacted the trial authors for missing data on 'Risk of bias' items.

    We used the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' assessment tool to assess each 'Risk of bias' domain as low, high, or unclear risk of bias as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (for details see Appendix 2) (Higgins 2011a; Higgins 2017).

    Summary assessment of risk of bias

    We presented a 'Risk of bias' graph and a 'Risk of bias' summary figure.

    We distinguished between self reported, investigator assessed, and adjudicated outcome measures.

    We considered the following self reported outcomes.

    • Adverse events other than hypoglycaemia.

    • Hypoglycaemic episodes.

    We considered the following investigator‐assessed outcomes.

    • Adverse events other than hypoglycaemia.

    • All‐cause mortality.

    • Diabetes‐related mortality.

    • Hypoglycaemic episodes.

    • Length of hospital stay.

    • Mean blood glucose.

    • Socioeconomic effects.

    Risk of bias for a trial across outcomes

    Some 'Risk of bias' domains such as selection bias (sequence generation and allocation sequence concealment) affect the risk of bias across all outcome measures in a trial. In case of high risk of selection bias, we marked all endpoints investigated in the associated trial as being at high risk. Otherwise, we did not performed a summary assessment of the risk of bias across all outcomes for a trial.

    Risk of bias for an outcome within a trial and across domains

    We assessed the risk of bias for an outcome measure by including all entries relevant to that outcome (i.e. both trial‐level entries and outcome‐specific entries). We considered a judgement of low risk of bias to denote a low risk of bias for all key domains; unclear risk of bias to denote an unclear risk of bias for one or more key domains; and high risk of bias to denote a high risk of bias for one or more key domains.

    Risk of bias for an outcome across trials and across domains

    These are the main summary assessments that we incorporated into our judgements about the certainty of evidence in the 'Summary of findings' tables. We defined outcomes as at low risk of bias when most information came from trials at low risk of bias; unclear risk of bias when most information came from trials at low or unclear risk of bias; and high risk of bias when a sufficient proportion of information came from trials at high risk of bias.

    Measures of treatment effect

    When at least two trials were available for a comparison of a given outcome, we expressed dichotomous data as risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We planned to calculate time‐to‐event data as hazard ratio (HR) with 95% CI with the generic inverse variance method. We expressed continuous data reported on the same scale as mean difference (MD) with 95% CIs. For trials addressing the same outcome but using different outcome measure scales, we planned to use standardised mean differences (SMD) with 95% CI.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We took into account the level at which randomisation occurred, such as cross‐over trials, cluster‐randomised trials, and multiple observations for the same outcome. If more than one comparison from the same trial was eligible for inclusion in the same meta‐analysis, we either combined groups to create a single, pairwise comparison, or we appropriately reduced the sample size so that the same participants did not contribute multiply times (splitting the 'shared' group into two or more groups). Although the latter approach offers some solution for adjusting the precision of the comparison, it does not account for correlation arising from inclusion of the same set of participants in multiple comparisons (Higgins 2011b).

    We planned to re‐analyse cluster‐randomised trials that did not appropriately adjust for potential clustering of participants in their analysis. Variance of the intervention effects would have been inflated by a design effect (DEFF). Calculation of a DEFF involves estimation of an intracluster correlation (ICC). We planned to obtain estimates of ICCs through contact with trial authors, or by imputing ICC values using either estimates from other included trials that reported ICCs or external estimates from empirical research (e.g. Bell 2013). We planned to examine the impact of clustering by performing sensitivity analyses.

    Dealing with missing data

    We attempted to obtain missing data from the authors of included trials. We carefully evaluated important numerical data such as screened, randomly assigned participants, as well as intention‐to‐treat and as‐treated and per‐protocol populations. We investigated attrition rates (e.g. dropouts, losses to follow‐up, withdrawals), and we critically appraised issues concerning missing data and imputation methods (e.g. last observation carried forward).

    When included trials did not report means and standard deviations for outcomes and we were unable to obtain the requested information from trial authors, we imputed these values by estimating the mean and the variance from the median, the range, and the size of the sample (Hozo 2005).

    We planned to investigate the impact of imputation on meta‐analyses by performing sensitivity analyses.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    In the event of substantial clinical or methodological heterogeneity, we planned not to report trial results as the pooled effect estimate in a meta‐analysis.

    We identified heterogeneity (inconsistency) by visually inspecting the forest plots and by using a standard Chi² test with a significance level of α = 0.1 (Deeks 2017). In view of the low power of this test, we also considered the I² statistic, which quantifies inconsistency across trials, to assess the impact of heterogeneity on the meta‐analysis (Higgins 2002; Higgins 2003).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If we included 10 or more trials investigating a particular outcome, we used funnel plots to assess small‐trial effects. Several explanations can account for funnel plot asymmetry, including true heterogeneity of effect with respect to trial size, poor methodological design (and hence bias of small trials), and publication bias (Sterne 2017). We therefore interpreted results carefully (Sterne 2011).

    Data synthesis

    We planned to undertake (or display) a meta‐analysis only if we judged the participants, interventions, comparisons, and outcomes to be sufficiently similar to ensure an answer that would be clinically meaningful. Unless good evidence showed homogeneous effects across trials of different methodological quality, we primarily summarised data judged as at low risk of bias using a random‐effects model (Wood 2008). We interpreted random‐effects meta‐analyses with due consideration for the whole distribution of effects and presented a 95% prediction interval (Borenstein 2017a; Borenstein 2017b; Higgins 2009). A prediction interval requires at least three trials to be calculated and specifies a predicted range for the true treatment effect in an individual trial (Riley 2011). For rare events such as event rates below 1%, we planned to use the Peto odds ratio (OR) method, provided there was no substantial imbalance between intervention and comparator group sizes, and intervention effects were not exceptionally large. In addition, we performed statistical analyses according to the statistical guidelines presented in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Deeks 2017).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We expected the following characteristics to introduce clinical heterogeneity and performed subgroup analyses to investigate interactions (Altman 2003).

    • Medical or surgical intervention.

    • Comorbid conditions.

    • Age, depending on data.

    • Time of follow‐up.

    • Type of insulin used.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to perform sensitivity analyses to explore the influence of the following factors (when applicable) on effect sizes by restricting the analysis to the following.

    • Published trials.

    • Taking into account risk of bias, as specified in the Assessment of risk of bias in included studies section.

    • Use of the following filters: imputation, language of publication, source of funding (industry versus other), or country.

    We planned to test the robustness of results by repeating analysis using different measures of effect size (i.e. RR, OR, etc.) and different statistical models (fixed‐effect and random‐effects models).

    Certainty of evidence

    We presented the overall certainty of the evidence for each outcome specified below according to the GRADE approach, which takes into account issues related not only to internal validity (risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, publication bias) but also to external validity, such as directness of results. Two review authors (LECL, DGP) independently rated the certainty of evidence for each outcome. Differences in assessment were solved by discussion or by consultation with a third review author (AVH).

    Appendix 14 'Checklist to aid consistency and reproducibility of GRADE assessments' helps with standardisation of the 'Summary of findings' tables (Meader 2014). Alternatively, we planned to use GRADEpro GDT software and wanted to present evidence profile tables as an appendix (GRADEpro GDT 2015). We presented results for outcomes as described in the Types of outcome measures section. If meta‐analysis was not possible, we presented the results in a narrative format in the 'Summary of findings' table. We justified all decisions to downgrade the certainty of the evidence with footnotes, and made comments to aid the reader's understanding where necessary.

This provides key information about the best estimate of the magnitude of effect, in relative terms and as absolute differences for each relevant comparison of alternative management strategies, numbers of participants and trials addressing each important outcome, and a rating of overall confidence in effect estimates for each outcome.     Included studies

    For a detailed description of the characteristics of the included trials, see Characteristics of included studies and Appendix 3; Appendix 4; Appendix 5; Appendix 6; Appendix 7; Appendix 8; Appendix 9; Appendix 10; Appendix 11; Appendix 12). The following is a succinct overview.

    Source of data

    Eight trials met our inclusion criteria. These were published as peer‐reviewed articles in English.

    Comparisons

    All included trials investigated SSI therapy against other strategies for glycaemic control. Six trials used a basal‐bolus regimen (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). Two were three‐arm trials (Said 2013; Umpierrez 2013). Umpierrez 2013 also employed a basal‐bolus correction regimen, and Said 2013 also used a mixed correction regimen (70/30 regular insulin plus neutral protamine Hagedorn (NPH) by means of SSI). One trial employed a basal insulin regimen by means of glargine plus regular insulin in 6% of cases (Datta 2007), and another trial used a continuous insulin infusion strategy (Gustafson 2002).

    Overview of trial population

    A total of 1048 participants with type 2 diabetes were randomised to SSI intervention and comparators; 387 participants in the SSI groups and 615 in the comparator groups were finally analysed. The sample size of randomised individuals per trial ranged from 50 to 375 participants.

    Trial design

    Seven trials had a parallel RCT design, and one trial was a cluster RCT (Schroeder 2012). The trials were published between 2002 and 2013. One trial was self funded (Said 2013), and five trials were partly or entirely sponsored by the pharmaceutical industry (Datta 2007; Korytkowski 2009; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013).

    Settings

    Six trials were conducted in the USA (Datta 2007; Gustafson 2002; Korytkowski 2009; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013), one trial in Israel (Schroeder 2012), and one in Egypt (Said 2013). Four trials evaluated surgical participants (Datta 2007; Gustafson 2002; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2011); three trials evaluated medical inpatients (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Umpierrez 2007a); and one trial evaluated both surgical and medical participants (Umpierrez 2013).

    Participants

    Two trials specified the ethnic background of their participants (Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011). Two trials reported the duration of diabetes mellitus (Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). The percentage of female participants per intervention/comparator group ranged between 26% and 86%. The mean age of participants ranged between 44.5 years and 71 years. The mean participants' glycated haemoglobin ranged between 7.4% and 8.9%. The mean body mass index (BMI) ranged between 26.9 kg/m² and 55 kg/m². Major exclusion criteria within the included trials were no history of diabetes, type 1 DM, corticosteroid therapy during hospitalisation, hyperglycaemic crisis, admission to an intensive care unit, severe heart failure or cardiogenic shock, clinically relevant hepatic disease, impaired renal function, gastrointestinal bleeding, pregnancy, inability to give informed consent, and any mental condition.

    Interventions

    The average daily insulin dose of SSI ranged between 8 units and 28 units. The average daily dose of basal‐bolus insulin ranged between 12 units and 44 units, and the average daily dose of basal‐plus insulin was 27 units. The mean duration of hospital stay ranged between 5 and 24 days.

    Outcomes

    Five trials explicitly stated a primary endpoint in the publication (Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). The most commonly defined primary outcomes in the publications were differences in glycaemic control, as measured by mean daily blood glucose concentration. Five trials were registered in ClinicalTrials.gov (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 29 of 45 articles/records after evaluation of the full publication/record. The main reasons for exclusion were wrong trial design, wrong population, wrong setting, and wrong comparison (for further details see Characteristics of excluded studies).

    Two trials had a low risk of selection bias (Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013), and six trials did not provide enough information to adequately judge selection bias (Datta 2007; Gustafson 2002; Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a).

    The majority of trials showed a high risk of performance and detection bias for the outcome measures hypoglycaemic episodes, adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes, and mean glucose levels.

    Incomplete outcome data

    We rated all trials as at low risk of attrition bias if all data for the associated outcome measures were reported. Umpierrez 2011 excluded 10% of randomised participants from the analysis, and Umpierrez 2013 excluded 6% of randomised participants from the analysis (for details see Table 1).

    Selective reporting

    We rated one trial as at high risk of reporting bias (Schroeder 2012).

    Other potential sources of bias

    We rated one trial as at unclear risk of other bias because it did not use adequate statistical analyses for its cluster‐randomised design (Schroeder 2012).

    Effects of interventions

    Most trials compared an SSI regimen with a basal‐bolus insulin regimen (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). Three of these trials, investigating 64% of all participants for this comparison, also applied the SSI approach in the bolus comparator part (Korytkowski 2009; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2013), thus essentially reducing the comparison to the evaluation of additional basal insulin administration. Umpierrez 2013 had a second comparator arm ("basal‐plus"), which did not differ in a clinically relevant way from the basal‐bolus arm of the trial. We used only the results of the basal‐bolus comparator part of this trial in our review.

    SSI versus basal‐bolus insulin

    Primary outcomes
    All‐cause mortality

    Four trials reported data on death (Said 2013; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). One of 268 participants in the SSI group (0.3%) compared with two of 334 participants in the basal‐bolus group (0.6%) died (Peto odds ratio 0.50, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.05 to 4.85; 4 trials; 602 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1).

    Diabetes‐related mortality

    No trial reported on diabetes‐related mortality.

    Hypoglycaemic episodes

    Non‐severe hypoglycaemia

    Non‐severe hypoglycaemia was usually defined by trial authors as a blood glucose level below 60 mg/dL (3.3 mmol/L) or 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L). Schroeder 2012 reported that "no significant difference was noted in hypoglycaemic rates (blood glucose level below 70 mg/dL) between the two groups (P = 0.6)". Korytkowski 2009 reported hypoglycaemia frequency as the percentage of patient‐days with one or more event (SSI compared with basal‐bolus insulin: 4.8 days versus 2.7 days, with P = 0.34) and percentage of all glucose values less than 3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) (SSI compared with basal‐bolus insulin: 1.1 blood glucose measures versus 1.3 blood glucose measures, P = 0.35).

    The risk ratio (RR) for non‐severe hypoglycaemic episodes comparing SSI with basal‐bolus insulin was 0.25 (95% CI 0.11 to 0.59 in favour of SSI; P = 0.001; 3 trials; 561 participants; Analysis 1.2). Overall, nine non‐severe hypoglycaemic episodes occurred in 246 participants on SSI (3.7%) compared with 49 non‐severe hypoglycaemic episodes in 315 participants on basal‐bolus insulin (15.6%). Calculation of the 95% prediction interval did not reveal a meaningful interval. Performance bias and detection bias were high in the majority of the included trials reporting this outcome.

    Comparing the subgroup of medical with surgical inpatients did not show a statistically significant interaction effect of subgroup differences. There were not enough data to analyse the other prespecified subgroups.

    Severe hypoglycaemia

    Five trials reported on severe hypoglycaemic episodes (Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). Severe hypoglycaemia was defined by trial authors as blood glucose levels below 40 mg/dL (2.2 mmol/L), Said 2013; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013, or as hypoglycaemia requiring intravenous glucose administration (Schroeder 2012). The RR for severe hypoglycaemic episodes comparing SSI with basal‐bolus insulin was 0.22 (95% CI 0.05 to 1.00; P = 0.05; 5 trials; 667 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3). All nine severe hypoglycaemic episodes were observed among the 369 participants on basal‐bolus insulin (2.4%). The 95% prediction interval ranged between 0.02 and 2.57. The fixed‐effect model showed a RR of 0.20 (95% CI 0.05 to 0.87). Elimination of the cluster RCT, Schroeder 2012, showed a statistically non‐significant effect estimate for both the random‐effects and the fixed‐effect model (RR 0.22, 95% CI 0.04 to 1.24 and RR 0.20, 95% CI 0.04 to 1.03, respectively). Performance bias and detection bias were high in the majority of the included trials reporting this outcome.

    Secondary outcomes
    Length of hospital stay

    Six trials reported data on length of hospital stay (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). The mean length of hospital stay ranged across basal‐bolus groups from 5.3 days to 24.2 days. The mean length of hospital stay in the SSI groups was 0.5 days longer (95% CI ‐0.5 to 1.4; P = 0.32; 6 trials; 717 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4). The 95% prediction interval ranged between ‐1.7 days and 2.7 days. Elimination of the cluster RCT, Schroeder 2012, showed a mean difference between intervention and comparator groups of ‐0.06 days (95% CI ‐0.91 to 0.80).

    Comparing the subgroup of medical with surgical inpatients did not show a statistically significant interaction effect of subgroup differences. There were not enough data to analyse the other prespecified subgroups.

    Adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes (such as postoperative infections)

    Four trials reported on adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes (Korytkowski 2009; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2011Umpierrez 2013).

    Schroeder 2012 reported that "there was no difference in the postoperative complications between the groups. No difference in operative‐related and unrelated complications between groups was noted".

    The RR for postoperative complications comparing SSI with basal‐bolus insulin was 1.16 (95% CI 0.25 to 5.37; P = 0.85; 3 trials; 481 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.5). Overall, 20 complications among 206 participants on SSI (9.7%) occurred compared with 17 complications among 275 participants on basal‐bolus insulin (6.2%). Calculation of the 95% prediction interval did not reveal a meaningful interval. Performance bias and detection bias were high in the majority of the included trials reporting this outcome, and selection bias was high for Schroeder 2012.

    Mean blood glucose levels

    Six trials reported on mean glucose level during hospital stay (Korytkowski 2009; Said 2013; Schroeder 2012; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011; Umpierrez 2013). The mean blood glucose level ranged across basal‐bolus groups from 156 mg/dL (8.7 mmol/L) to 221 mg/dL (12.3 mmol/L). The mean blood glucose level in the SSI groups was 14.8 mg/dL (0.8 mmol/L) higher (95% CI 7.8 (0.4) to 21.8 (1.2); P < 0.001; 6 trials; 717 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). The 95% prediction interval ranged between ‐3.6 mg/dL (‐0.2 mmol/L) and 33.2 mg/dL (1.8 mmol/L). The cluster RCT, Schroeder 2012, did not have a substantial impact on the effect size (data not shown).

    Comparing the subgroup of medical with surgical inpatients did not show a statistically significant interaction effect of subgroup differences. There were not enough data to analyse the other prespecified subgroups.

    Four trials reported on hyperglycaemia, which was variably defined (Datta 2007; Said 2013; Umpierrez 2007a; Umpierrez 2011). Datta 2007 reported that 16% of SSI‐treated participants compared with 6% of basal‐bolus insulin‐treated participants had blood glucose levels greater than 200 mg/dL. Said 2013 reported that 68% of SSI‐treated participants compared to 53% of basal‐bolus insulin‐treated participants had blood glucose levels greater than 300 mg/dL. Blood glucose levels greater than 240 mg/dL were reported in 9% of SSI‐treated participants in Umpierrez 2007a and in 12% of SSI‐treated participants in Umpierrez 2011.

    All trials reported that the mean total daily dose of insulin in the basal‐bolus group was greater than in the SSI group.

    Socioeconomic effects

    No trial reported on socioeconomic effects.

    SSI versus basal insulin

    Only one trial compared an SSI regimen with a basal insulin regimen (Datta 2007). This trial, which involved 81 participants, did not provide information on all‐cause mortality, diabetes‐related mortality, or socioeconomic effects. One of 39 participants in the SSI group (2.6%) compared with two of 42 participants in the basal insulin group (4.8%) reported a non‐severe hypoglycaemic episode (Analysis 2.1). Mean length of hospital stay was the same in both the intervention and the comparator group (3.8 days; Analysis 2.2). Adverse events other than hypoglycaemic episodes (postoperative infections) were reported in two participants in both the intervention and the comparator group (Analysis 2.3). The mean blood glucose level in the SSI group was 20 mg/dL higher than in the basal insulin group (Analysis 2.4).

    One trial compared SSI with a continuous insulin infusion during coronary artery bypass surgery in 50 participants (Gustafson 2002). The only outcome measure of relevance for this review was mean blood glucose levels, which were 110 mg/dL less in the post‐cardiopulmonary bypass period in the SSI group compared with the continuous insulin infusion group (Analysis 3.1). There was also a difference in the cardioplegic solution between the groups: the continuous insulin infusion group received a dextrose 5%‐based cardioplegic solution, and the SSI group received normal 0.9% saline, limiting the interpretation of the results of this trial.

    Subgroup analyses

    With the exception of medical versus surgical inpatients for some outcome measures, we did not perform subgroup analyses as data were insufficient to estimate effects in various subgroups.

    Sensitivity analyses

    With the exception of comparing the random‐effects model with the fixed‐effect model for some outcome measures, we did not perform sensitivity analyses due to insufficient data.

    Assessment of reporting bias

    We did not draw funnel plots due to the limited number of trials (N = 8).

    Ongoing studies

    We found one ongoing trial, NCT01143948, and two trials awaiting classification, IRCT201311118307N2; ISRCTN55845280 (for details see Characteristics of ongoing studies and Characteristics of studies awaiting classification sections).

    The ongoing trial is investigating an SSI regimen compared with a basal‐bolus insulin regimen in approximately 45 participants with type 2 diabetes and liver cirrhosis.

    Two trials awaiting classification, with a total of approximately 320 participants, compared an SSI regimen with a basal‐bolus insulin regimen in diabetic people with cardiovascular diseases and diabetic people with acute medical and surgical conditions.

    • Summary of main results

    This systematic review analysed the evidence from published RCTs of SSI therapy for the treatment of non‐critically ill adults with diabetes mellitus. We included eight trials randomising 1048 participants with type 2 diabetes. The main comparison with SSI was basal‐bolus insulin therapy. A basal‐bolus insulin strategy might result in better short‐term glycaemic control, but might also result in an increase in severe hypoglycaemic episodes. We are uncertain about the effects of SSI and basal‐bolus insulin on length of hospital stay, postoperative complications, and mean blood glucose levels. No studies reported on diabetes‐related mortality or socioeconomic effects.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Most trials analysed in our review were conducted in the USA, and younger adult diabetic participants were underrepresented in the trials. Our inclusion criteria specified surgical and medical patients, and both were represented, reflecting the general practice in the non‐critical hospital environment. However, most trials did not include people with newly diagnosed diabetes, people who were treated with insulin before hospitalisation, or people on corticosteroid therapy, who all form an important part of the hospitalised population with hyperglycaemia. The total daily dose of insulin was lower in the SSI groups compared with the basal‐bolus insulin groups, probably reflecting that the SSI regimen on admission remained unchanged throughout the hospitalisation period. It is therefore unclear whether the results of this review are associated with the SSI regimen per se or a suboptimal insulin dosing in the SSI intervention groups, which might be an attribute of the rigid SSI treatment approach.

    Quality of the evidence

    We assessed the certainty of the evidence as low to very low mainly because of trial limitations (especially performance bias and detection bias), imprecision (low number of participants and trials, wide prediction intervals), and inconsistency (non‐consistent direction of effects) for most of the outcome measures.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We attempted to contact the trial authors to obtain additional information (Appendix 13), as well as information about potential admissions of non‐critically ill participants to intensive care units, but did not receive an answer from the authors of three included trials. We therefore cannot exclude the possibility that in some trials some participants were also treated in critical care. Furthermore, we did not systematically search the grey literature.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Lee 2015 published a meta‐analysis of SSI treatment for blood glucose control. The review authors included 11 trials in their meta‐analysis but did not discriminate between critical and non‐critical care participants. Gómez Cuervo 2016 published a meta‐analysis of adverse events related to basal‐bolus insulin therapy compared with SSI. The review authors included five trials combining observational studies and RCTs in their meta‐analysis. Christensen 2017 published a meta‐analysis of basal‐bolus insulin regimens compared to other insulin regimens. The review authors included five trials using basal‐bolus insulin as the main intervention in their meta‐analysis. The overall results of these reviews were similar to our results, indicating that SSI therapy resulted in lower hypoglycaemia rates and higher mean glucose levels compared with non‐SSI therapies.
