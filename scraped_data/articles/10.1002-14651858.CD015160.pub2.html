Mesh versus non‐mesh for emergency groin hernia repair

    • Background

    A groin hernia is a collective name for inguinal and femoral hernias, which can present acutely with incarceration or strangulation of the hernia sac content, requiring emergency treatment. Timely repair of emergency groin hernias is crucial due to the risk of reduced blood supply and thus damage to the bowel, but the optimal surgical approach is unclear. While mesh repair is the standard treatment for elective hernia surgery, using mesh for emergency groin hernia repair remains controversial due to the risk of surgical site infection.

    Objectives

    To assess the benefits and harms of mesh compared with non‐mesh in emergency groin hernia repair in adult patients with an inguinal or femoral hernia.

    Search methods

    On 5 August 2022, we searched the following databases: CENTRAL, MEDLINE Ovid, and Embase Ovid, as well as two trial registers for ongoing and completed trials. Additionally, we performed forward and backward citation searches for the included trials and relevant review articles. We searched without any language or publication restrictions.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing mesh with non‐mesh repair in emergency groin hernia surgery in adults. We included any mesh and any non‐mesh repairs. All studies fulfilling the study, participant, and intervention criteria were included irrespective of reported outcomes.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methodology. We presented dichotomous data as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI). We based missing data analysis on best‐ and worst‐case scenarios. For outcomes with sufficiently low heterogeneity, we performed meta‐analyses using the random‐effects model. We analysed subgroups when feasible, including the degree of contamination. We used RoB 2 for risk of bias assessment, and summarised the certainty of evidence using GRADE.

    Main results

    We included 15 trials randomising 1241 participants undergoing emergency groin hernia surgery with either mesh (626 participants) or non‐mesh hernia repair (615 participants). The studies were conducted in China, the Middle East, and South Asia. Most patients were men, and most participants had an inguinal hernia (41 participants had femoral hernias). The mean/median age in the mesh group ranged from 35 to 70 years, and from 41 to 69 years in the non‐mesh group. All studies were performed in a hospital emergency setting (tertiary care) and lasted for 11 to 139 months, with a median study duration of 31 months. The majority of the studies only included participants with clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields.

    For all outcomes, we considered the certainty of the evidence to be very low, mainly downgraded due to high risk of bias (due to deviations from intended intervention and missing outcome data), indirectness, and imprecision.

    Mesh hernia repair may have no effect on or slightly increase the risk of 30‐day surgical site infections (RR 1.66, 95% CI 0.96 to 2.88; I² = 21%; 2 studies, 454 participants) when compared with non‐mesh hernia repair, but the evidence is very uncertain. The evidence is also very uncertain about the effect of mesh hernia repair compared with non‐mesh hernia repair on 30‐day mortality (RR 1.38, 95% CI 0.58 to 3.28; 1 study, 208 participants). In summary, the results showed 70 more (from 5 fewer to 200 more) surgical site infections and 29 more (from 32 fewer to 175 more) deaths within 30 days of mesh hernia repair per 1000 participants compared with non‐mesh hernia repair. The evidence is very uncertain about 90‐day surgical site infections after mesh versus non‐mesh hernia repair (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.15 to 6.64; 1 study, 60 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). No 30‐day recurrences were recorded, and mesh hernia repair may not reduce recurrence within one year (RR 0.19, 95% CI 0.04 to 1.03; I² = 0%; 2 studies, 104 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). Within 30 days of hernia repair, no meshes were removed from clean to clean‐contaminated fields, but 6.7% of meshes (1 study, 208 participants) were removed from contaminated to dirty surgical fields. Among the four studies reporting 90‐day mesh removal, no events occurred.

    We were not able to identify any studies reporting complications classified according to the Clavien‐Dindo Classification or reoperation for complications within 30 days of repair.

    Authors' conclusions

    Our results show that in terms of 30‐day surgical site infections, 30‐day mortality, and hernia recurrence within one year, the evidence for the use of mesh hernia repair compared with non‐mesh hernia repair in emergency groin hernia surgery is very uncertain. Unfortunately, firm conclusions cannot be drawn due to very low‐certainty evidence and meta‐analyses based on small‐sized and low‐quality studies. There is a need for future high‐quality RCTs or high‐quality registry‐based studies if RCTs are unfeasible.

    • Mesh or no mesh: what works best for groin hernias (swelling or lump in the groin area) requiring emergency surgery?

    Key messages

    • Overall, we are uncertain about the effect of placing a mesh during emergency surgery in people with groin hernias.

    • The poor quality of the studies and the low number of people involved does not allow us to draw any firm conclusions.

    What are emergency groin hernias?

    A groin hernia is a bulge that protrudes through a weakness in the abdominal wall in the groin region. It is a common surgical condition in both men and women, but is notably more widespread among males, affecting over one in four men. A rare but dangerous complication of a hernia occurs if the blood supply to the hernia content gets compromised (strangulation) as a result of the bowel or other content getting stuck in the hernia opening (incarceration). These conditions are clinically recognised by the sudden onset of a painful lump in the groin area, sometimes also causing nausea, vomiting, and severe discomfort. People with such groin hernias are in need of urgent surgical treatment to avoid permanent injury to the bowel, if manual reduction is unsuccessful.

    How are emergency groin hernias treated?

    There is no agreement on what surgery method is best for treating emergency groin hernias. Firstly, the bulge with tissue (either fatty tissue or bowel) needs to be returned to the abdominal cavity and the bowel's blood supply needs to be restored so that it does not die. Secondly, the weakness in the abdominal wall needs to be fixed. In general, this can be done by placing a mesh or using only stitches (non‐mesh). A mesh is a surgical net placed in the abdominal wall where the weakness occurred, intended to strengthen it. The different surgical methods for groin hernias are referred to as 'hernia repair'. While mesh is the gold standard for planned surgery of groin hernias, its role in emergency surgery is debated. The theory is that implanting a foreign body (mesh) could increase the risk of infection following emergency groin hernia surgery, potentially favouring a non‐mesh hernia repair, but this theory is yet to be confirmed.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if mesh hernia repair differed from non‐mesh hernia repair in terms of infections related to the surgical wound, deaths after surgery, the return of the hernia (recurrence), and complications. We also wanted to see how many meshes were later removed due to complications.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that examined mesh compared with non‐mesh hernia repairs in adult people who needed emergency surgery for their groin hernia.

    We compared and summarised the results of the studies. We rated our confidence in the evidence based on factors such as the quality of the studies and the number of participants.

    What did we find?

    We found 15 studies involving 1241 people, of which 626 received a mesh repair, and 615 received a non‐mesh hernia repair. Most studies were conducted in China (10 studies), and the remaining studies were conducted in the Middle East and South Asia. The studies lasted from 11 months to over 11 years.

    We found that mesh hernia repair might not affect the risk of wound infections or deaths within 30 days of surgery when compared with non‐mesh hernia repair.

    No hernias returned within 30 days. Some hernias returned after one year following the repair, but the analysis could not find any differences in this risk between mesh and non‐mesh hernia repairs.

    No meshes were removed within 30 days of repair in the least severe cases of emergency groin hernia but, based on one study, one out of 15 meshes were removed in the more severe cases.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    There are significant limitations of the evidence, therefore we can not draw strong conclusions. Firstly, we are not certain about the evidence since the overall quality of the included studies was very low. Also, we could not find data about everything we wanted to find out, and the few studies that reported the things we were interested in were unfortunately too small to show any potential differences in the treatment effects.

    How up‐to‐date is this evidence?

    This evidence is up‐to‐date to August 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The use of mesh in emergency groin hernia repair has been controversial due to the increased risk of surgical site infections, mortality, and other harms. Unfortunately, this review has produced results that are most likely underpowered and based on low‐quality studies; strong conclusions therefore cannot be drawn. Overall, in terms of mortality, surgical site infection, and recurrence rates, the evidence is very uncertain about the effect of mesh compared with non‐mesh for hernia repair. The current guideline for the management of emergency groin hernia repairs suggests a "tailored approach", due to insufficient evidence to support an optimal approach (HerniaSurge Group 2018). Further, the guideline suggests using mesh in clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields, but advises careful consideration if the contamination worsens, with mesh implementation not recommended in contaminated to dirty surgical fields or worse (HerniaSurge Group 2018).

    Implications for research

    Since all the clinical trials assessing mesh versus non‐mesh emergency groin hernia repairs were of very low quality, future high‐quality randomised controlled trials (RCTs) are very much needed for this important clinical topic. The need for more research is especially crucial regarding patients with contaminated to dirty surgical fields.

    There is a lack of evidence regarding emergency groin hernia repair and the existing trials are, unfortunately, of low quality. For many of the outcomes, we could not identify any difference, and additional studies would help to detect any potential difference or increase the robustness of the findings of no difference between mesh and non‐mesh repairs. However, many studies had zero events in both arms, probably due to small sample sizes and exclusion criteria that removed the most severe cases of emergency groin hernia from the study participants. From this perspective, the feasibility of an RCT might be questionable as it would probably require multicentre trials with thousands of participants to detect rare adverse events. High‐quality registry‐based studies could be a better method of research to detect adverse events on a population basis and would complement data from RCTs (Bergqvist 2007). Furthermore, it is important to record the degree of contamination in these studies to facilitate stratified outcome assessment according to disease severity, in order to accommodate the inevitable confounding by indication in observational studies.

    Assessing the degree of contamination of the surgical field is essential when investigating the use of mesh, as its use is controversial when contamination is present. None of the included studies used the wound classification system from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) (Birindelli 2017), and the classification in this review was made by translating the clinical description provided in the articles to one of the four contamination degrees. Several studies included participants with more than one degree of contamination, pooling the results and making it difficult to draw separate conclusions for each degree. Future studies should report the degree of contamination according to the CDC wound classification system (Birindelli 2017), to eliminate this barrier to analysis.

    The included studies were of low methodological quality. All studies randomised patients into treatment groups, but only a few specified the method of randomisation. This should be done better in future studies. Furthermore, the blinding of investigators and participants was poorly reported. Due to the nature of the intervention, it is not possible to blind the surgical team, creating a significant source of bias in all surgical clinical trials. However, even though double‐blinding is an unrealistic goal to achieve, future studies should focus their efforts on blinding the outcome assessors and at least blinding the participants to the intervention.

    We have been challenged by different follow‐up times as well as outcome and complication reporting, which were all sources of substantial heterogeneity between the trials. Future studies should focus on time point‐specific outcomes (e.g. reporting how many participants had a recurrence after one year instead of a mean follow‐up time) and more uniform outcome reporting, to facilitate comparability of studies.

    In this review, we have only included short‐term outcomes up to one year, but long‐term outcomes, especially long‐term recurrence rates and chronic pain conditions, should be investigated further for this patient group. Since recurrence is an important quality measure in hernia surgery, and many recurrences may not be present at 30 days or one year after repair, a longer follow‐up time is necessary and is recognised as a limitation of this review. Furthermore, laparoscopic treatment has shown benefits in elective groin hernia repair, and the World Society of Emergency Surgery state in their guidelines for complicated abdominal wall hernias that a laparoscopic approach should be considered, especially in the absence of strangulation and the need for bowel resection (Birindelli 2017). Another matched case‐control study also showed that laparoscopy is an appropriate alternative for repairing emergency groin hernias in selected patients (Olivero 2022). Future research should investigate its safety and feasibility compared with open mesh repairs (e.g. Lichtenstein) or open non‐mesh repairs (e.g. Bassini) for incarcerated or strangulated groin hernias.

    All the included studies in this review used synthetic mesh for the mesh‐based hernia repair. None reported assessing absorbable or biologic meshes; the latter especially have received awareness for potentially being favourable in emergency hernia surgery when dealing with potentially contaminated fields (Köckerling 2015). Research on the different mesh types for emergency groin hernia repair is therefore necessary. We have identified one registered trial assessing the effect of an acellular dermal matrix in emergency inguinal hernia repair (NCT02469142).

    In conclusion, the existing clinical trials on mesh versus non‐mesh for emergency groin hernia repair are of low quality, and more high‐quality studies are needed. It is essential to assess the degree of contamination of the surgical fields, particularly for patients with contaminated to dirty surgical fields. Long‐term outcomes and laparoscopic treatment should be investigated further, and registry‐based studies could complement data from RCTs. Future RCTs should focus on the blinding of outcome assessors and participants.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Groin hernia is a collective name for inguinal and femoral hernias (Brooks 2021). Around 5% of inguinal hernias and 37% of femoral hernias present acutely with incarceration or strangulation of the hernia sac content and require emergency treatment due to the risk of reduced supply of blood and thus oxygen to the bowel (bowel ischaemia) (Nilsson 2007; Reinke 2020). In incarcerated hernias, the sac content cannot be pushed back into its original compartment (Brooks 2021). Strangulation occurs when an incarcerated hernia compromises arterial supply to the content of the hernia sac, resulting in ischaemia and eventually necrosis (Brooks 2021). Emergency groin hernia repair is associated with higher morbidity and mortality compared with elective repair (Kjaergaard 2010; Malek 2004; Nilsson 2007; Sæter 2022b).

    Femoral hernias are associated with an increased risk of incarceration or strangulation (Dahlstrand 2009; Hernández‐Irizarry 2012). A previous study estimated the cumulative probability of strangulation in femoral hernias to be 22% at one month and 45% at 21 months (Gallegos 1991). In comparison, the same study estimated the cumulative probability of strangulation in inguinal hernias to be 3% at three months and 5% at 24 months. Other risk factors for incarceration and the need for emergency groin hernia repair are advanced age, female sex, hernia‐related hospitalisation in the year preceding hernia repair, and recurrent hernia (Abi‐Haidar 2011; Dahlstrand 2009; Primatesta 1996).

    Description of the intervention

    Surgical repair is the only definitive treatment for emergency groin hernias (Brooks 2021). This procedure must be performed immediately after diagnosis due to the risk of strangulation and bowel necrosis (Reinke 2020). However, since there is no evidence supporting an optimal surgical approach, current guidelines suggest a tailored approach for adult patients presenting with acutely incarcerated or strangulated groin hernias (HerniaSurge Group 2018). In general, hernia repair involves the use of mesh or the use of sutures only. In mesh repair, the surgeon covers the hernia defect with a prosthesis that is typically made from a synthetic polymer like polypropylene or polyester (Lockhart 2018). This procedure can be performed using an open surgical approach or a minimally invasive laparoscopic approach (McCormack 2003). In elective groin hernia repair in adults, open or laparoscopic mesh repair is recommended to decrease the risk of recurrence (HerniaSurge Group 2018). Incarcerated or strangulated groin hernias are usually repaired with an open approach, but a laparoscopic approach may also be feasible in experienced hands (Karatepe 2008).

    Surgical site infections are the most common kind of nosocomial infections (hospital‐acquired infections) following surgery; they lead to longer hospitalisations, higher mortality, more intensive care unit transfers, and more readmissions (Kirkland 1999). Bacteria colonise all surgical wounds, but not all contaminations lead to infection (Birindelli 2017). An expert panel of the World Society of Emergency Surgery approved the following classification to define recommendations for emergency repair of abdominal wall hernias, which is based on the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention wound classification that stratifies wounds according to the likelihood and degree of contamination (Birindelli 2017):

    • Clean surgical field: no signs of strangulation and no bowel resection

    • Clean‐contaminated surgical field: complicated hernia with strangulation, or bowel resection without gross enteric spillage

    • Contaminated surgical field: strangulation with bowel necrosis, or gross spillage during intestinal resection

    • Dirty surgical field: peritonitis from a bowel perforation

    Implantation of a foreign body, such as a mesh, may increase the risk of postoperative surgical site infection. However, several studies have reported that synthetic mesh placement is feasible in emergency groin hernia repair (Sakamoto 2022; Venara 2014), specifically in clean surgical fields (Lohsiriwat 2007), or in clean‐contaminated surgical fields, even with concurrent bowel resection (Atila 2010; Bessa 2015; Papaziogas 2005; Sakamoto 2022; Sawayama 2014; Ueda 2012). Some studies have found mesh hernia repair to be feasible in contaminated and dirty hernias (Kelly 2002; Pandey 2018), and a recent randomised controlled trial assessing mesh use for contaminated ventral hernias found no mesh‐related reoperations after six months (Rosen 2022). However, the use of mesh in emergency groin hernia repair in a contaminated surgical field is still controversial due to the potential risk of surgical site infections following mesh implantation. This risk precludes the laparoscopic approach in these circumstances as it always requires the use of mesh. To date, no comprehensive systematic review has been carried out to assess whether the use of mesh is safe or feasible in emergency groin hernia repair in all surgical fields (clean, clean‐contaminated, contaminated, and dirty).

    How the intervention might work

    Reinforcing the abdominal wall with a prosthetic mesh during groin hernia repair reduces the risk of hernia recurrence compared with non‐mesh repair (Lockhart 2018). However, mesh placement in contaminated surgical fields may be avoided to minimise the risk of infection. Furthermore, mesh infections can be more complicated to treat: patients may need another surgical procedure to have the mesh removed and the hernia repaired again, or they may need a prolonged course of antimicrobial treatment. Hence, mesh infections are potentially associated with higher costs than simple surgical site infections without mesh, which has been described for ventral hernia repairs (Plymale 2020).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Emergency groin hernia repair is a common surgical emergency, representing over one out of three femoral hernia operations (Dahlstrand 2009) and around one out of 11 inguinal hernia admissions (Primatesta 1996). While mesh repair is the standard treatment for elective hernia repair (HerniaSurge Group 2018), the optimal surgical approach to treat emergency groin hernias remains controversial (HerniaSurge Group 2018). Elective and emergency groin hernia repairs are performed under very different clinical circumstances, which might not be comparable in research. There are several published Cochrane Reviews on elective groin hernia repairs (Lockhart 2018; Scott 2001; Sun 2017), but none have examined emergency groin hernia repair. In addition, there is no consensus on the use of mesh in acutely contaminated or dirty surgical fields due to the potential risk of infection (HerniaSurge Group 2018). Even though mesh repair reduces the risk of recurrence in hernia surgery, it should not be used in an emergency setting if it increases the risk of postoperative complications. Therefore, this Cochrane Review aims to synthesise the evidence from all relevant literature to assess whether the use of mesh in emergency groin hernia repair is associated with an increased risk of surgical site infections, postoperative morbidity, and mortality.

    Objectives

    Main objective

    To assess the benefits and harms of mesh compared with non‐mesh in emergency groin hernia repair in adult patients with an inguinal or femoral hernia.

    Secondary objective

    To assess whether the effects of mesh versus non‐mesh in emergency groin hernia repair differ according to the degree of contamination.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    The following section is based on the methods presented in the published protocol for the review (Sæter 2022). Any deviations from the protocol, including methods not implemented, are outlined and justified in Differences between protocol and review.

    We included all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (e.g. randomisation based on personal registration numbers) (Differences between protocol and review) that compared the use of mesh with non‐mesh for emergency groin hernia repair. There was no limitation on the number of randomised participants in trials, and we applied no restrictions on publication status or language.

    Types of participants

    • We included all adult patients (aged 18 and over) with an inguinal or femoral hernia undergoing emergency groin hernia repair.

    • We excluded studies with a paediatric population (aged less than 18 years) if the data on the adult population could not be extracted separately.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies that compared the use of mesh with the use of sutures only (non‐mesh) in emergency groin hernia repair. We considered the procedure 'emergency surgery' when the authors defined it as such or when it was performed within 24 hours.

    As the intervention of interest was mesh use, we chose to pool all mesh‐based and non‐mesh repairs into two groups.

    Any technique for surgical repair of a groin hernia with mesh implantation was accepted. Any type of mesh could be used (e.g. absorbable, synthetic, biological). Open techniques included, but were not limited to:

    • Lichtenstein repair;

    • mesh plug repair;

    • plug and patch;

    • bilayer repair; and

    • preperitoneal repair.

    Laparoscopic techniques included:

    • transabdominal preperitoneal repair (TAPP);

    • totally extraperitoneal repair (TEP);

    • robotic‐assisted laparoscopic repair; and

    • single‐incision laparoscopic surgery (SILS).

    For open non‐mesh approaches, any type of sutures could be used (absorbable or non‐absorbable). We accepted both tension and tension‐free repair techniques, including:

    • Marcy;

    • Bassini;

    • McVay;

    • Shouldice; and

    • Desarda.

    The non‐mesh group was considered the control group for all outcomes.

    Types of outcome measures

    All studies fulfilling the study, participant, and intervention criteria were included irrespective of reported outcomes. We focused on short‐term outcomes that were relevant to an emergency surgical setting.

    If the following outcome measures were not reported in the studies, we contacted the study authors to request the missing data. If we did not receive a response, we sent at least two follow‐up emails (Differences between protocol and review). The study was included irrespective of response.

    Primary outcomes

    • Surgical site infections (as defined by the included studies) within 30 days of repair

    • Mortality (defined as all‐cause mortality) within 30 days of repair

    Secondary outcomes

    • Surgical site infections (as defined by the included studies) within 90 days of repair

    • Complications, classified by the Clavien‐Dindo Classification (Dindo 2004) (as classified by the included studies), within 30 days of repair

      • Clavien‐Dindo Classification grade 5 was excluded, as this complication was covered in the primary outcome of 30‐day mortality

    • Clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days of repair and within one year of repair

    • Reoperation due to complications (e.g. infection, seroma, bleeding) within 30 days of repair

    • Mesh removal within 30 days of repair

    • Mesh removal within 90 days of repair

    Search methods for identification of studies

    In collaboration with a professional Cochrane‐affiliated research librarian, we developed a search strategy for Ovid MEDLINE, which we then adapted to the other databases. There were no restrictions on language or publication date.

    Electronic searches

    We conducted a comprehensive literature search to identify all published and unpublished RCTs.

    The searches were last run on 5 August 2022 in the following databases:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL 2022, Issue 8) in the Cochrane Library (Appendix 1);

    • MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 5 August 2022) (Appendix 2);

    • Embase Ovid (1974 to 5 August 2022) (Appendix 3).

    Searching other resources

    On 5 August 2022, we searched the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP; apps.who.int/trialsearch) and the US National Institutes of Health ongoing trials register (clinicaltrials.gov) for completed and ongoing studies. The search terms used for searching trial registries can be found in Appendix 4.

    Other search sources included reference list searching, forward citation searching, and grey literature. To complete the search for relevant studies, we reviewed the reference lists of all included articles (Greenhalgh 2005), and the reference lists of relevant systematic reviews and guidelines (Bittner 2005; Finch 2019; Hentati 2014; HerniaSurge Group 2018; Li 2012; Lin 2020; Lockhart 2018; Reinke 2020). We also performed a forward citation search of the included articles using Google Scholar (scholar.google.com). For each additional record identified from other sources, we performed subsequent reference list and forward citation searches. The first forward citation search was done on 15 August 2022, and the last forward citation search was completed on 19 August 2022.

    If we found unpublished relevant studies in the registries, we contacted the authors to enquire about unpublished data and results.

    Data collection and analysis

    In general, when studies written in a non‐English language were identified, at least one native speaker of the language assessed eligibility for inclusion and performed the data extraction and risk of bias assessment. The other author had assistance from a native speaker if any questions arose (Differences between protocol and review).

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors independently screened the titles and abstracts of the included records using Covidence and then retrieved and assessed the full texts of all records considered potentially eligible. Any discrepancies were resolved by discussion within the review author group.

    We searched all eligible studies in the Retraction Watch Database (Differences between protocol and review).

    Data extraction and management

    At least two authors independently extracted data into piloted forms in Covidence. We checked all data for accuracy in Covidence before entering them into Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2020). We used RevMan Web 2020 to perform all data analyses. Any discrepancies were resolved by discussion within the author group.

    We collected information on study design and setting, patient characteristics (including race, smoking status, comorbidities, etc.), the authors' definition of emergency groin hernia repair, intervention characteristics (including surgical approach and contamination grade), outcomes assessed, source of funding, and any conflicts of interest stated by the study authors. We dichotomised the following subgroups prior to data extraction: age into participants ≥ 65 and < 65 years, American Society of Anaesthesiologists’ (ASA) scores into ASA score 1‐2 and ASA score 3‐4, and body mass index (BMI) into ≥ 30 and < 30 (Differences between protocol and review). Dichotimisation was done to facilitate any feasible subgroup analyses to assess heterogeneity.

    If any additional data were required, we contacted the study authors by email, following up with two further emails in case of no response. We addressed missing data with worst‐case and best‐case scenarios (see Dealing with missing data).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    At least two independent review authors conducted the risk of bias assessment for RCTs using the Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB 2; Sterne 2019). Any discrepancies were resolved by discussion within the author group.

    We applied the risk of bias tool to the following outcomes and time points.

    • Surgical site infections within 30 days of repair

    • Mortality within 30 days of repair

    • Clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days of repair and within one year of repair

    • Mesh removal within 30 days and 90 days of repair

    For the following outcomes, we performed no risk of bias assessment since we did not identify any studies reporting them:

    • Complications within 30 days of repair

    • Reoperation due to complications (e.g. infection, seroma, bleeding) within 30 days of repair

    Further, if we included studies that did not report a numerical result for an outcome of interest for this review, thus not contributing to the review, we did not perform a risk of bias assessment (Differences between protocol and review).

    We assessed the effect of allocation to the intervention through an intention‐to‐treat analysis, meaning that the effect of interest was the effect of assignment. We assessed the risk of bias arising from the following domains:

    • The randomisation process

    • Deviations from intended interventions

    • Missing outcome data

    • Measurement of outcome

    • Selection of the reported results

    Based on the answers to a series of signalling questions, we made a risk of bias judgement for each domain (low risk of bias, some concerns, or high risk of bias). We used the 22 August 2019 version of the Excel tool provided by riskofbias.info for the assessment.

    The overall risk of bias for an outcome was the highest individual risk of the five domains. This means only considering an outcome to be at low risk of bias when we judged all five domains to be at low risk. This information is presented in the review and included in the analyses and conclusion.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed data using RevMan Web 2020.

    Dichotomous outcomes are expressed as risk ratios (RRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) (Differences between protocol and review). The Clavien‐Dindo‐classified complications were the only categorical outcomes with more than two categories and we dichotomised these for analyses into grades 1‐2 and grades 3‐4.

    None of our outcomes were continuous.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the participant (not the hernia).

    Dealing with missing data

    We dealt with missing data as follows.

    • We contacted the authors to request supplementary data (e.g. dropouts, loss to follow‐up, or when a study was only published as an abstract).

      • If any additional data were obtained, we included them in the analysis.

    • If the study did not report means and standard deviations, we attempted to calculate these statistics from the available reported data. If data were not reported numerically but graphically, we extracted data from the figures.

    • If some participants had missing data, we only included the available data in the primary analysis.

    • We performed sensitivity analyses using a best‐case scenario (participants with missing data in the intervention arm developed events for desirable outcomes and participants with missing data in the control arm did not) and a worst‐case scenario (participants with missing data in the intervention arm developed events for undesirable outcomes and participants with missing data in the control arm did not).

    • We addressed the potential impact of the missing data in the discussion section.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed clinical, methodological, and statistical heterogeneity. For clinical heterogeneity, we considered differences in participant characteristics (e.g. American Society of Anesthesiology (ASA) score, age, sex), intervention, outcome, and length of follow‐up. For methodological heterogeneity, we considered study design (e.g. type of follow‐up) and risk of bias. We assessed statistical heterogeneity using the I2 statistic (Higgins 2003), interpreting the results as follows (Deeks 2022):

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If more than 10 studies were identified, we planned to assess the risk of reporting bias visually using a funnel plot. Funnel plot asymmetry may indicate substantial publication bias and overestimation of the intervention effect (Page 2021).

    Data synthesis

    We pooled dichotomous outcomes in a meta‐analysis if clinical, methodological, and statistical heterogeneity was sufficiently low.

    We adopted a random‐effects model for data synthesis as we expected some heterogeneity among the populations, study designs, and definitions of emergency groin hernia repair. All results are presented with 95% CIs. We generated forest plots with RevMan Web 2020. All eligible studies were included in the primary analyses, regardless of risk of bias. If the I2 value was above 75%, the pooled estimate is not presented in the forest plots (Burcharth 2015). If a meta‐analysis was not possible, we intended to use non‐statistical methods to synthesise findings across studies, visually displaying the results and summarising the effect estimates with the range and distribution of the observed effects (McKenzie 2021).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    If clinical heterogeneity was suspected and there was a sufficient number of participants, we performed subgroup analyses based on the following characteristics, regardless of the statistical heterogeneity.

    • Patient characteristics (sex, age, BMI, ASA score, diabetes, smoking, and race)

    • Hernia type:

      • Femoral hernia

      • Inguinal hernia

    • Contamination according to the World Society of Emergency Surgery classification (Birindelli 2017):

      • Clean surgical field: no signs of strangulation and no bowel resection

      • Clean‐contaminated surgical field: complicated hernia with strangulation, or bowel resection without gross enteric spillage

      • Contaminated surgical field: strangulation with bowel necrosis, or gross spillage during intestinal resection

      • Dirty surgical field: peritonitis from a bowel perforation

    • Mesh type:

      • Absorbable mesh, synthetic mesh, and biological mesh

    • Type of surgery

      • Bowel resection

      • No bowel resection

    • Use of antibiotics preoperatively at index surgery

    If different contamination degrees were pooled in the original data, they were also pooled in the subgroup analysis (e.g. pooling clean surgical field and clean‐contaminated surgical field) (Differences between protocol and review).

    We used the I2 statistic to examine subgroup differences (Higgins 2003).

    Sensitivity analysis

    We performed sensitivity analysis by excluding studies with low methodological quality (overall high risk of bias).

    As explained in Dealing with missing data, if some data were missing from the included studies, we performed sensitivity analyses using a best‐case scenario (participants with missing data in the intervention arm developed events for desirable outcomes and participants with missing data in the control arm did not) and a worst‐case scenario (participants with missing data in the intervention arm developed events for undesirable outcomes and participants with missing data in the control arm did not).

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used GRADEpro GDT to create a summary of findings table for the following outcomes.

    1. Surgical site infections within 30 days of repair

    2. Mortality within 30 days of repair

    3. Complications within 30 days of repair

    4. Clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days of repair and within one year of repair

    5. Reoperation due to complications (e.g. infection, seroma, bleeding) within 30 days of repair

    6. Mesh removal within 30 days of repair

    Two authors independently carried out a certainty of evidence assessment for each of the above outcomes using the GRADE approach. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion. Evidence from RCTs was considered high‐certainty at the beginning, but could be downgraded for limitations related to risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, indirectness, or publication bias, according to the GRADE recommendations (Schünemann 2021).

    We used the overall RoB 2 judgement to feed into the GRADE assessment, as risk of bias is one of the five GRADE domains. We summarised risk of bias data before downgrading the certainty of the evidence (Schünemann 2021). The three possible categories were:

    • low risk of bias (most information for the outcome is generated from studies at low risk of bias);

    • moderate risk of bias (most information is from studies at low risk of bias or studies with some concerns); and

    • high risk of bias (the proportion of information from studies at high risk of bias is sufficient to affect the interpretation of results).

    The findings and certainty of evidence are phrased according to the informative statement guidance (Santesso 2020).

    Included studies

    Study characteristics

    Fifteen trials randomising a total of 1241 participants met the inclusion criteria for this review (Chen 2016; Darwish 2018; Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Memon 2017; Panda 2012; Ren 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012; Yin 2013; Zhu 2015). Among the participants, 626 received a mesh hernia repair, and 615 received a non‐mesh repair. The number of participants in each trial ranged from 33 (Ren 2012) to 246 (Karaca 2016). Ten trials were performed in China (Chen 2016; Duan 2018; Guo 2015; Li 2010; Ren 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012; Yin 2013; Zhu 2015), and the remaining trials were conducted in Egypt (Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008), India (Panda 2012), Pakistan (Memon 2017), and Türkiye (Karaca 2016). All trials were performed in a hospital emergency setting (tertiary care), and the study duration ranged from 11 (Zhu 2015) to 139 months (Duan 2018).

    Sex

    The majority of the participants were males, with a total of 972 males. Five trials only included male participants (Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Li 2010; Memon 2017; Ren 2012), while the rest included both male and female participants. However, one study did not report the male/female ratio but stated in the methods that both males and females were included as trial participants (Panda 2012).

    Age

    The mean/median age of the population in the mesh group ranged from 35 years (Elsebae 2008) to 70 years (Sun 2010), while the mean/median age for the non‐mesh groups ranged from 41 years (Panda 2012) to 69 years (Ren 2012). Three studies did not report a mean/median age for the two groups separately (Duan 2018; Guo 2015; Memon 2017), but reported an overall mean/median age of 54 years (Duan 2018) and 61 years (Guo 2015), or solely reported an age group ranging from 20 to 60 years (Memon 2017).

    Hernia types

    Most of the trials included solely participants with inguinal hernias (Chen 2016; Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Memon 2017; Panda 2012; Ren 2012; Ye 2012; Yin 2013). The remaining trials included participants with both inguinal and femoral hernias (Duan 2018; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Zhu 2015), comprising 41 participants with a femoral hernia. The trials did not always report the type of inguinal hernia (indirect/direct). However, among the trials that did report the type of inguinal hernia, the vast majority of the inguinal hernias were indirect, ranging from 86% (Wang 2014) to 100% (Guo 2015; Ren 2012; Zhu 2015) of all inguinal hernias.

    Whether the hernia was primary or recurrent was only reported in six trials (Chen 2016; Darwish 2018; Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Memon 2017; Panda 2012), and, among these, four trials only included primary hernias (Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Memon 2017; Panda 2012). Any previous mesh implantation in patients with recurrent hernias was not reported.

    Description of intervention

    All the trials used an open mesh‐based tension‐free hernia repair for the interventional mesh repair group, either simply described as an "open tension‐free hernia repair" (Chen 2016; Guo 2015; Li 2010; Ren 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012; Zhu 2015) or a "Lichtenstein repair" (Darwish 2018; Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Karaca 2016; Memon 2017; Panda 2012; Yin 2013).

    Two trials did not report the type of mesh used in the intervention group (Wang 2014; Yin 2013). All the remaining studies used synthetic meshes, and no studies reported using absorbable or biological meshes. The most common mesh was a polypropylene synthetic mesh (Chen 2016; Darwish 2018; Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Panda 2012; Sun 2010; Zhu 2015). Other meshes included Goretex mesh (Ren 2012), polyethylene mesh (Ye 2012), and solely "synthetic mesh" without further specification (Memon 2017).

    Description of comparison

    The Bassini repair was the most common non‐mesh hernia repair (Chen 2016; Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Panda 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012; Zhu 2015). Other non‐mesh hernia repairs included "traditional hernia repair" (Ren 2012; Yin 2013), sutured repair (Duan 2018), Darn repair (Memon 2017), and McVay repair (Wang 2014). Ren 2012 further described "traditional hernia repair" as either high hernia sac ligation, Ferguson repair (on the basis of high hernia sac ligation), or Bassini repair.

    Other co‐interventions

    The reporting of the administration of antibiotics varied in the included trials. Five trials reported administering antibiotics preoperatively (Guo 2015; Sun 2010; Darwish 2018; Memon 2017; Zhu 2015), and three other trials reported administering antibiotics postoperatively (Chen 2016; Elsebae 2008; Ye 2012). Some trials reported the use of perioperative antibiotics, without specifying the time of administration (Duan 2018; Karaca 2016; Panda 2012), and for the remaining four trials, the use of antibiotics was either not reported (Li 2010; Ren 2012) or unclearly reported (Wang 2014; Yin 2013). For details, see Characteristics of included studies.

    Contamination degree

    In total, 11 studies enabled the classification of the degree of contamination (Darwish 2018; Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Panda 2012; Ren 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012). Among these, all but one study (Duan 2018) included participants with clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields. One trial only included participants with a clean surgical field (Sun 2010), six trials included participants with clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields (Darwish 2018; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Panda 2012; Wang 2014; Ye 2012), and three trials only included participants with a clean‐contaminated surgical field (Elsebae 2008; Li 2010; Ren 2012).

    Only one trial assessed mesh repair in contaminated to dirty surgical fields (Duan 2018). In this study, they used a modified three‐grade classification system to identify the degree of bowel necrosis. This study grading has been translated to the protocol grading, so grade 1 bowel necrosis corresponds to a contaminated surgical field, and grade 2 and 3 bowel necrosis corresponds to a dirty surgical field. For details, see Characteristics of included studies.

    The remaining studies either did not report perioperative findings to allow the classification of contamination degree (Chen 2016; Memon 2017; Zhu 2015) or reported the findings unclearly (Yin 2013). For the latter, the participants most likely had clean‐contaminated surgical fields due to "strangulated inguinal hernia", but since it is unclear whether bowel resection or peritonitis was present, it is uncertain whether cases with contaminated and dirty surgical fields were included.

    Outcomes reported by the included studies

    The most common outcomes reported in the included studies were the length of hospitalisation (n = 13, 87%), followed by postoperative local infection (n = 11, 73%), recurrence rate (n = 11, 73%), and operative time (n = 10, 67%). Other reported outcomes included scrotal haematoma, wound haematoma, postoperative pain (measured by analgesia rate, visual analogue scale (VAS) scores, and not reported), intraoperative blood loss, time to get out of bed, patient satisfaction, urinary retention, dysuria, pneumonia, mesh removal, hospitalisation cost, subfebrilia, vital intraoperative parameters, seroma, mortality, systemic complications (pulmonary, cardiac, and renal), and return to daily activities.

    Detailed descriptions of the outcomes reported in each trial can be found in the Characteristics of included studies.

    Most of the reported outcomes were not defined with time points for measurement, making the numerical results ineligible for inclusion in the meta‐analysis.

    Funding sources

    Funding sources were not reported in most studies (Chen 2016; Duan 2018; Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Ren 2012; Sun 2010; Wang 2014; Ye 2012; Yin 2013; Zhu 2015). The remaining studies either declared no funding sources (Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Memon 2017), or provided an ambiguous funding source statement (Panda 2012).

    Excluded studies

    Of all the studies assessed in full text, we excluded 83 records. The studies were excluded as they did not meet the inclusion criteria of being a prospective RCT or quasi‐RCT, only elective surgery was studied, the hernia type was wrong, or a non‐mesh control group was missing. Examples of excluded studies are Huang 2012, Jiang 2016, Jin 2016, and Karatepe 2008, and the reasons for exclusion of these studies can be found under Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Key excluded studies are referenced in this review. A full comprehensive list of the 83 excluded studies assessed in full text can be provided by the first author upon request.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    The overall risk of bias assessment across the included studies is presented following the methods given in the Assessment of risk of bias in included studies. The risk of bias assessment for each predefined outcome of relevance in this review for each study is presented in the interactive Risk of bias (tables). Each prespecified outcome eligible for risk of bias assessment has a table with detailed descriptions of the support for each judgement and the final score (high, low, or some concerns). Moreover, the risk of bias assessment is included in the forest plots with traffic lights in all primary analyses for both primary and secondary outcomes. The full RoB 2 Excel tool provided by riskofbias.info used for the assessment is available on reasonable request.

    We carried out 27 individual outcome‐dependent risk of bias assessments for the 12 studies presenting a numerical result for an outcome of interest for this review. In general, the risk of bias assessment was very similar for all outcomes in this review, and we judged the overall risk of bias in outcomes for all studies to be either 'some concerns' (n = 5, 18.5%) or 'high' (n = 22, 81.5%).

    Regarding the randomisation process, we assessed all the included studies to have 'some concerns' as the only information about the randomisation method was that the allocation was randomised without specifying any further details. However, in general, no baseline differences were found, suggesting no problems with the randomisation process.

    For the domain regarding deviations from intended interventions, none of the included studies had sufficiently described if or how blinding was carried out. However, since the people delivering the intervention were surgeons, the question about whether the persons delivering the intervention were aware of the assigned treatment was responded with 'probably yes' for all studies.

    No trial protocols were found for any of the included studies. Therefore, judging any deviations from the intended intervention was not possible and hence none of the included trials was assessed to have low risk of bias for this domain. Most trials were assessed to have 'high risk of bias' (78%) and the remaining trials had 'some concerns' (22%). The main difference between studies assessed as high risk of bias and some concerns was that for studies reporting that all patients were followed up, we assessed there to be no potential for a substantial impact (on the result) of the failure to analyse participants in the group since they were all followed up.

    Not all included studies reported or discussed missing outcome data, and loss to follow‐up was rarely addressed. However, some studies reported no loss to follow‐up, and thus had no missing data, achieving a low risk of bias for this particular domain (22%). The remaining studies received a judgement of 'high risk of bias' (78%).

    The reporting of outcome measurement was poor. In general, the authors did not report how outcome data were measured or collected, other than stating that the participants were followed up for a certain amount of time. For the risk of bias assessment, we considered the outcomes of all‐cause mortality and operation for mesh removal unlikely to be influenced by the knowledge of the intervention received. We therefore assessed these outcomes to have low risk of bias regarding the measurement of outcome even when the attempt at outcome assessment blinding was not described. An equal number of trials received a low (44%) and high risk (44%) of bias judgement, and the remaining trials had 'some concerns' (11%).

    The assessment of risk of bias for the domain selection of the reported results was challenged by none of the included trials having discussed pre‐specified analysis plans, and analysis intentions were not available for any of the included trials. Hence, we assessed none of the included trials at low risk of bias for this domain, and we assessed most trials as having 'some concerns' (85%) due to the lack of information. We assessed the remaining trials as having 'high risk of bias' (15%). Trials receiving a high risk for selection of the reported results were those that only reported the outcome in one treatment group, or if the reported outcomes were not in accordance with the analysis intentions described.

    Three included studies did not present outcome data of interest with a numerical result. Hence, the risk of bias assessment was not completed for these studies as they did not contribute to the review (Memon 2017; Sun 2010; Yin 2013).

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Summary of findings table ‐ mesh repair vs. non‐mesh repair for adult patients with incarcerated or strangulated groin hernia

    Mesh versus non‐mesh repair for emergency groin hernia surgery

    For all outcomes, both primary and secondary, we obtained data from published literature. We sought correspondence with the study authors, and searched trial registers and web‐based data repositories, but failed to obtain any additional data.

    Primary outcomes
    Surgical site infections (as defined by the included studies) within 30 days of repair

    Two studies randomising 454 participants reported surgical site infections within 30 days of repair (Duan 2018; Karaca 2016). In total, there were 41/229 events of 30‐day surgical site infections in the mesh group and 24/225 events in the non‐mesh group. The meta‐analysis demonstrated little to no effect on surgical site infections after mesh repair (RR 1.66, 95% CI 0.96 to 2.88; I2 = 21%; Analysis 1.1); thus, there were 70 more (from 5 fewer to 200 more) surgical site infections in the mesh group per 1000 participants. Using GRADE, we assessed the certainty of the evidence for surgical site infections within 30 days of repair as very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to high risk of bias, imprecision, and indirectness. The GRADE assessments are outlined in summary of findings Table 1.

    We performed a subgroup analysis for 30‐day surgical site infections regarding the degree of contamination for clean to clean‐contaminated, contaminated, and dirty surgical fields. Overall, only one study was identified per subgroup and the test for subgroup differences did not suggest any subgroup effect (P = 0.20) (Analysis 3.1). However, looking separately at the subgroups, little to no difference was found in a clean to clean‐contaminated field (RR 1.09, 95% CI 0.43 to 2.73; 1 study, 246 participants) and a contaminated surgical field (RR 1.37, 95% CI 0.55 to 3.40; 1 study, 145 participants), but mesh repair may increase the risk of surgical site infections in a dirty surgical field (RR 2.69, 95% CI 1.48 to 4.89; 1 study, 63 participants) (Analysis 3.1). However, the test for subgroup differences suggested no evidence of a difference between the groups. The subgroups of clean surgical field and clean‐contaminated surgical field were pooled in this subgroup analysis, as they were pooled in the original data (Differences between protocol and review). It was not possible to conduct subgroup analyses for hernia type, sex, age, BMI, ASA score, diabetes, smoking, race, mesh type, or preoperative antibiotics.

    We did not perform a sensitivity analysis excluding studies with low methodological quality as there were only two studies reporting 30‐day surgical site infection and we assessed both of these studies to have high risk of bias. Neither of the two studies reported whether there were any missing data, so a sensitivity analysis based on best‐case/worst‐case scenarios was not possible.

    In summary, mesh hernia repair may have no effect or slightly increase the risk of 30‐day surgical site infections (RR 1.66, 95% CI 0.96 to 2.88; I² = 21%; 2 studies, 454 participants; Analysis 1.1) when compared with non‐mesh hernia repair, but the evidence is very uncertain. However, mesh hernia repair may increase the risk of 30‐day surgical site infections in dirty surgical fields (RR 2.69, 95% CI 1.48 to 4.89; 1 study, 63 participants; Analysis 3.1), but the test for subgroup differences suggested no evidence of a difference between the degrees of contamination.

    Mortality (defined as all‐cause mortality) within 30 days of repair

    Four studies randomising 541 participants reported mortality within 30 days of repair as an outcome (Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Karaca 2016; Ren 2012). Three trials randomising participants with clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields had no deaths in either group (Elsebae 2008; Karaca 2016; Ren 2012). In total, there were 11/273 events of mortality within 30 days of repair in the mesh group and 8/268 events in the non‐mesh group, resulting in little to no difference in mortality (RR 1.38, 95% CI 0.58 to 3.28; 1 study; Analysis 1.2). Thus, there were 29 more (from 32 fewer to 175 more) deaths in the mesh group per 1000 participants. We assessed the certainty of the evidence for mortality within 30 days of repair as very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to most studies having a high risk of bias, imprecision, and indirectness. The details of the GRADE assessments are outlined in summary of findings Table 1.

    Two subgroup analyses could be performed. Firstly, regarding the degree of contamination, no deaths occurred in a clean to clean‐contaminated field (3 studies, 333 participants), so an effect measure was not estimable, and little to no difference was found in a contaminated to dirty surgical field (RR 1.38, 95% CI 0.58 to 3.28; 1 study, 208 participants; Analysis 3.2). Secondly, regarding bowel resection, no deaths occurred in participants not undergoing bowel resection (1 study, 54 participants), and little to no difference was found for participants undergoing bowel resection (RR 1.38, 95% CI 0.58 to 3.28; 1 study, 208 participants; Analysis 5.1). Testing for subgroup differences was not possible since both subgroups had one group with zero events. It was not possible to conduct subgroup analyses for hernia type, sex, age, BMI, ASA score, diabetes, smoking, race, mesh type, or preoperative antibiotics.

    We did not perform sensitivity analysis excluding studies with low methodological quality, since only one study reported 30‐day mortality with events. Two of the studies reported no missing data (Elsebae 2008; Ren 2012), and the remaining two studies did not report whether there were any missing data. Hence, sensitivity analyses based on best‐case and worst‐case scenarios were not possible.

    In summary, the evidence is very uncertain about the effect of mesh hernia repair compared with non‐mesh hernia repair on the risk of all‐cause mortality within 30 days of repair (RR 1.38, 95% CI 0.58 to 3.28; 1 study, 208 participants; Analysis 1.2).

    Secondary outcomes
    Surgical site infections (as defined by the included studies) within 90 days of repair

    One study with 60 participants reported surgical site infections within 90 days of repair (Darwish 2018), reporting 2/30 infections (6.67%) both for mesh and non‐mesh repair (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.15 to 6.64; Analysis 1.3). Surgical site infections within 90 days were not discussed in the remaining 14 studies.

    As only one study reported 90‐day surgical site infections, we performed no subgroup or sensitivity analyses. Neither loss to follow‐up nor missing data were reported, so sensitivity analysis based on best‐case/worst‐case scenarios was not possible.

    Using GRADE, we assessed the certainty of the evidence for 90‐day surgical site infections as very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to high risk of bias, imprecision, and indirectness, as the trial only included male participants with incarcerated inguinal hernias, excluding the most severe cases of emergency groin hernia (e.g. female and femoral hernias)

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of mesh on surgical site infections within 90 days of repair.

    Complications, classified by the Clavien‐Dindo Classification within 30 days of repair

    None of the included studies reported complications classified by the Clavien‐Dindo Classification within 30 days of repair.

    Clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days of repair

    Five studies with a total of 254 participants reported 30‐day recurrence as an outcome (Darwish 2018; Elsebae 2008; Panda 2012; Wang 2014; Zhu 2015). Three trials did not report any recurrences within 30 days (Darwish 2018; Panda 2012; Zhu 2015) (Analysis 1.4). However, Elsebae 2008 and Wang 2014 reported recurrences but did not report any time point for recurrence other than that the participants were followed up for 22 ± 6 and 30 ± 6 months, respectively. Both studies observed zero recurrences in the mesh group and 3/27 (11%) and 6/42 (14%) recurrences in the non‐mesh group, respectively. Since a meta‐analysis was not performed, we also did not perform a sensitivity analysis.

    Following GRADE assessment, we rated the certainty of the evidence for 30‐day clinical recurrence as very low. We downgraded the evidence due to an overall high risk of bias, imprecision due to small studies and zero events, and indirectness, since none of the studies included severe cases of emergency groin hernia. The GRADE assessment is outlined in summary of findings Table 1.

    Testing for subgroup differences in the following subgroup analyses for clinical recurrence within 30 days could not be done as there were zero events in both groups: inguinal (3 studies, 180 participants; Analysis 2.1) and femoral hernias (1 study, 20 participants; Analysis 2.1), as well as male (2 studies, 125 participants; Analysis 4.1) and female sex (1 study, 35 participants; Analysis 4.1). It was not possible to conduct the remaining planned subgroup analyses.

    Clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within one year of repair

    Six studies reported recurrence within one year of repair as an outcome (Chen 2016; Elsebae 2008; Panda 2012; Ren 2012; Wang 2014; Ye 2012). Three of these studies did not report the specific time point for measuring recurrence (Chen 2016; Elsebae 2008; Wang 2014), excluding these studies from the meta‐analysis. However, in these studies, there were no recurrences in the mesh group, but the recurrence rate in the non‐mesh group was reported to be > 15% (Chen 2016; raw data not reported), 3/27 (11%) (Elsebae 2008), and 6/42 (14%) (Wang 2014). The remaining three RCTs, with a total of 144 participants, reported the recurrence rate in both arms, yielding 1/73 events of recurrence in the mesh group and 7/71 events in the non‐mesh group. However, Panda 2012 did not report any events, so only two RCTs randomising 104 participants were included in the meta‐analysis (Ren 2012; Ye 2012). The statistical heterogeneity was low I² = 0%, and the meta‐analysis demonstrated a very small, but uncertain decrease in the risk of recurrence in the mesh group within one year of repair (RR 0.19, 95% CI 0.04 to 1.03; I2 = 0%; Analysis 1.5). In absolute terms, mesh repair resulted in 11 fewer recurrences (ranging from 132 fewer to 4 more) per 1000 participants within one year of repair. The GRADE assessment for clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within one year of repair resulted in a very low certainty of evidence. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to high risk of bias, imprecision, and indirectness. The results are outlined in summary of findings Table 1.

    It was not possible to conduct any of the planned subgroup analyses.

    Regarding sensitivity analysis, one study reported losing five patients to follow‐up after one year (Ye 2012), with 36/38 participants in the mesh group and 35/38 participants in the non‐mesh group available for follow‐up at one year. We performed a sensitivity analysis based on a best‐case/worst‐case scenario where the best‐case scenario supported the result of the primary analysis, and the worst‐case scenario also demonstrated no difference in the two treatment groups regarding recurrence (RR 0.45, 95% CI 0.09 to 2.34; Analysis 6.1). Furthermore, we assessed all studies included in the meta‐analysis to have a high risk of bias when assessing the outcome of clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within one year of repair. Hence, we did not perform a sensitivity analysis removing studies of low methodological quality.

    In summary, the evidence suggests that mesh hernia repair may reduce clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within one year of repair compared with non‐mesh hernia repair, but the evidence is very uncertain.

    Reoperation due to complications (e.g. infection, seroma, bleeding) within 30 days of repair

    None of the included studies reported reoperation rates within 30 days of repair.

    Mesh removal within 30 days of repair

    Five studies included 30‐day mesh removal as one of their reported outcomes (Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Guo 2015; Li 2010; Panda 2012), comprising a total of 402 participants. Only one study reported events of mesh removal within 30 days of repair (Duan 2018), removing 7/104 meshes (6.7%), and included only participants with a contaminated or dirty surgical field. The remaining four studies reported zero mesh removals and only included participants with a clean or clean‐contaminated surgical field. We assessed the certainty of the evidence as very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to risk of bias (assessed as 'some concerns' and 'high'), imprecision, and indirectness. The GRADE assessment can be found in summary of findings Table 1.

    Due to zero events in one or more subgroups, no test of subgroup differences for mesh removal could be estimated for clean to clean‐contaminated (4 studies, 194 participants; Analysis 3.3) and contaminated to dirty surgical fields (1 study, 208 participants; Analysis 3.3), or for bowel resection (1 study, 208 participants; Analysis 5.2) and no bowel resection (2 studies, 94 participants; Analysis 5.2). Regarding the subgroup bowel resection, 7/104 meshes (6.7%) were removed (Analysis 5.2), corresponding to the subgroup of contaminated to dirty surgical field (Analysis 3.3). No mesh removals were recorded for patients not undergoing bowel resection (Analysis 5.2).

    Since only one study reported 30‐day mesh removal with events, we did not perform sensitivity analyses. The article did not report any loss to follow‐up or missing data, so a sensitivity analysis based on best‐case/worst‐case scenarios was not possible.

    We are very uncertain about the effect of mesh hernia repair on the outcome of mesh removal within 30 days of repair.

    Mesh removal within 90 days of repair

    Four studies with 194 participants reported mesh removal within 90 days of repair (Guo 2015; Karaca 2016; Li 2010; Panda 2012). All studies only included participants with clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields, and no meshes were removed (Analysis 1.7). Since no events occurred, we did not perform a meta‐analysis and, consequently, sensitivity analyses were not possible. Furthermore, we also did not perform a GRADE assessment.

    Since no events were reported in either arm regarding mesh removal within 90 days of repair, we performed no subgroup analyses, but we reported the outcome of 90‐day mesh removal separately for the following subgroups: inguinal hernia (4 studies, 194 participants), a clean‐contaminated surgical field (2 studies, 104 participants), male sex (2 studies, 104 participants), ASA 1‐2 (3 studies, 154 participants), diabetes (2 studies, 33 participants), no bowel resection (2 studies, 94 participants), and preoperative antibiotics (1 study, 50 participants).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included 15 trials randomising 1241 participants in this review. The trials were conducted in China, the Middle East, and South Asia.

    The effect of mesh on 30‐day surgical site infection remains uncertain and no firm conclusions can be drawn. Two studies with 454 participants suggest that mesh repair may have no effect on or slightly increase the risk of surgical site infections.

    The effect of mesh hernia repair on 30‐day all‐cause mortality is also uncertain. Most studies did not report any mortality events, but one study with 208 participants showed a potential increase in deaths in the mesh group, suggesting that mesh repair may have no effect on or slightly increase the risk of mortality.

    Limited evidence is available for 90‐day surgical site infections, and no events were reported for clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days. However, two studies with 104 participants suggest that mesh hernia repair may reduce recurrence within one year, but the confidence intervals failed to exclude the possibility of no difference, and the evidence is very uncertain.

    Mesh removal within 30 and 90 days was reported in a small number of cases. Data on complications classified by the Clavien‐Dindo Classification and reoperation for complications within 30 days of repair were not available.

    Overall, the evidence for all outcomes is very uncertain. The findings of this review are concisely summarised in summary of findings Table 1.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The benefits and harms of mesh compared with non‐mesh in emergency groin hernia repair have been assessed. Overall, the evidence regarding the effects of mesh hernia repair is uncertain for all outcomes. The secondary objective of this review was to assess whether the effects of mesh compared with non‐mesh hernia repair differ according to the degree of contamination. Regarding 30‐day surgical site infections, pooling all degrees of contamination suggested little to no effect on surgical site infections. However, looking separately at the different degrees of contamination, mesh hernia repair may increase the risk of surgical site infections compared with non‐mesh hernia repair in a dirty surgical field, but testing for subgroup differences based on the degree of contamination suggested no subgroup effect, which is probably due to underpowered analyses. In summary, no firm conclusions can be made about the effect of mesh hernia repair on surgical site infections because the evidence is very uncertain, but it appears that mesh use is safe in clean to clean‐contaminated surgical fields and we can conclude that the overall 30‐day surgical site infection rate in this subgroup of participants is low.

    Four studies reported 30‐day mortality (Duan 2018; Elsebae 2008; Karaca 2016; Ren 2012), but only one study had events of death (Duan 2018), making it difficult to reach any conclusion regarding the effect of mesh on postoperative mortality. The single study reporting events of death was also the only study conducted on participants with higher degrees of contamination (contaminated to dirty surgical fields) (Duan 2018). On the other hand, since three out of the four RCTs reporting mortality did not have any deaths within the first 30 days, the overall risk of mortality following emergency groin hernia repair might be lower than the numbers presented. However, it is important to note that patients with higher degrees of contamination were excluded from these studies. Furthermore, a total of seven meshes were removed within 30 days of repair, and all of them were removed from participants with contaminated or dirty surgical fields (Duan 2018). The single study with contaminated to dirty surgical fields was also the study contributing the most adverse events in this review.

    The majority of the patients were male, middle‐aged, and with an inguinal hernia. Furthermore, most studies excluded patients with preoperative peritonitis, local contamination, or the need for bowel resection, and we were only able to identify one study randomising participants with contaminated to dirty surgical fields (Duan 2018). Therefore, the results presented in this review may be regarded as indirect as they are mainly based on the least severe cases of emergency groin hernia, hence they are not generalisable to all patients presenting with an emergency groin hernia (e.g. higher degrees of contamination, elderly patients, female sex, and femoral hernias). Furthermore, the studies were conducted in China, Egypt, India, Pakistan, and Türkiye, which might make it problematic to extrapolate the findings to more Western healthcare systems in high‐income countries.

    To our knowledge, this review is the first to consider the degree of contamination of the surgical fields when assessing the use of mesh versus non‐mesh in emergency groin hernia repair. Furthermore, it is also the first review regarding emergency groin hernia repair to perform a comprehensive literature search in several databases (Appendix 1; Appendix 2; Appendix 3; Appendix 4), which we supplemented with searches in trial registries and forward and backward citation searching. Citation searching was not only restricted to the included trials but also done for review articles, books, and international guidelines on the topic for relevant studies. In addition, we imposed no language or publication restrictions, enabling the identification of several trials not previously cited in the emergency groin hernia literature.

    However, we are not able to draw any strong conclusions from our findings. This is because the analyses in this review are limited to few events or rare outcomes in small study populations, resulting in imprecision and, for some of the outcomes, only a single study formed the basis of the analysis. For other outcomes, there were either no data reported or no events reported as the outcome was rare or the follow‐up time was not long enough for the event to occur, preventing meta‐analysis. As a result of this, we have likely failed to identify any potential differences between mesh and non‐mesh hernia repair, as several of the estimated confidence intervals could not exclude the possibility of no difference.

    The certainty of the evidence for each outcome was very low, primarily due to studies of low methodological quality with a high risk of bias, and imprecision due to small sample sizes and few events. Therefore, future high‐quality RCTs would most likely have a substantial impact on the findings in this review.

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, the methodological quality of the included studies was poor. Despite identifying 15 trials with 1241 participants, only a few of these could be included in the meta‐analyses due to time points for outcomes not being specified or reported and studies with zero events in both arms. The risk of bias was mostly assessed as 'no information' as the studies did not specify or clearly state what methods were used, and none of them addressed any potential biases in their studies.

    Using GRADE, we assessed the certainty of the evidence as very low for all outcomes. The reasons for downgrading the certainty of evidence were, in general, similar for all outcomes, resulting in downgrading due to risk of bias, indirectness, and imprecision.

    Due to an overall high risk of bias, we downgraded the certainty of the evidence for 30‐day mortality by one level due to serious risk of bias and for 30‐ and 90‐day surgical site infections, one‐year clinical recurrence, and 30‐day mesh removal by two levels due to very serious risk of bias. Moreover, inclusion and exclusion criteria varied between the included studies, so we regarded the results as indirect. Some trials included only the most severe cases (e.g. irreversible bowel necrosis) and other trials excluded participants with a higher risk of adverse events (e.g. females, femoral hernias, elderly, higher degree of contamination). Therefore, we downgraded the certainty of the evidence for all outcomes by one level for indirectness. Finally, we downgraded the certainty of the evidence for all outcomes due to imprecision, because the results were all based on small‐sized studies and few events were recorded, potentially failing to identify any benefits or harms related to the use of mesh for emergency groin hernia repair.

    We did not perform a GRADE assessment of the certainty of the evidence for the outcomes of reoperation due to complications (e.g. infection, seroma, bleeding) within 30 days of repair or complications (classified by the Clavien‐Dindo Classification) within 30 days of repair as none of the included studies reported these outcomes. Furthermore, we did not perform GRADE assessment for the outcomes of clinical recurrence or reoperation for recurrence within 30 days of repair and mesh removal within 90 days of repair as none of the included studies reported any events for these outcomes, preventing an effect measure from being estimated.

    Our GRADE assessment of the certainty of the evidence is outlined in summary of findings Table 1.

    Potential biases in the review process

    To reduce the risk of potential bias, at least two authors undertook several stages of the review independently, including literature screening, data extraction, risk of bias assessment, and GRADE assessment. Due to the identification of several clinical trials written in Chinese, we included a native Chinese‐speaking author (SQL) to assist with the assessment of eligibility for inclusion, data extraction, and risk of bias assessment for the articles written in Chinese. The main author (AHS) extracted data and assessed the risk of bias in the Chinese articles in collaboration with a native Chinese speaker (YH) to ensure the robustness of these review stages, and all conflicts were resolved by oral discussion. Furthermore, we used a random‐effects model for meta‐analyses to take heterogeneity into account, and when zero events were encountered in both arms, preventing meta‐analyses, we also presented these results in forest plots to ensure transparency.

    However, this Cochrane Review has some limitations, which are discussed as follows.

    Overall, we mainly assessed the risk of bias for the outcomes investigated in this review as 'high risk of bias' and none of the included studies received a 'low risk of bias' judgement for any of the outcomes investigated. In addition, the reported outcomes in the included studies were often missing time points for measurements, which is why we could not include them in the analyses. We contacted all study authors three times to ask them to specify the measured outcomes, but unfortunately none responded.

    Despite including 15 trials randomising 1241 participants, the outcomes reported were scarce, and most of our analyses were therefore underpowered. Due to limited data, several of the preplanned subgroup analyses could not be performed, and the analyses conducted mostly had zero events in both arms. Furthermore, since few studies were included in the meta‐analysis for each outcome, sensitivity analyses were not always possible to achieve, and it was not possible to create funnel plots to assess publication bias as fewer than 10 studies were included in the analyses for each outcome (Differences between protocol and review).

    Moreover, most studies measured outcomes that were not reported in a usable format to be incorporated into the meta‐analyses. These were mainly complication rates without specific time points and recurrence rates without specification of the length of follow‐up. We attempted correspondence with all study authors, hoping to retrieve time point‐specific outcomes, but unfortunately without any response. Therefore, these outcomes are not reported in this review, which may introduce bias as adverse outcomes following emergency groin hernia repair are most likely under‐reported. Furthermore, trials reporting zero events for a given outcome over a longer follow‐up time could be extracted to fit an outcome with a shorter follow‐up time relevant to this review (e.g. 30‐day, 90‐day, or one‐year follow‐up). In contrast, as an example, we could not use articles reporting six recurrences with a mean follow‐up of 48 months. Therefore, our review methods, which prespecified time points for outcomes, did not allow the detection of all adverse outcomes and this is a limitation of this review.

    Lastly, we have expressed dichotomous outcomes as risk ratios (RRs) using the Mantel‐Haenszel method with a random‐effects model according to the methods defined in our protocol (Sæter 2022). However, since most outcome events were rare, a Peto odds ratio could have been considered (Deeks 2022).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We found one systematic review and meta‐analysis assessing mesh versus non‐mesh repair for strangulated inguinal hernias (Hentati 2014). This review included both randomised and non‐randomised studies and retained nine studies for analysis, comprising two RCTs (Elsebae 2008; Karatepe 2008), three non‐randomised prospective studies, and four retrospective studies. Our review included 15 RCTs with 1241 participants, which is mainly due to the identification of Chinese literature and the inclusion of non‐English reports. Regarding surgical site infections, Hentati 2014 found no difference between mesh and non‐mesh groups when pooling all studies (OR 0.46, 95% CI 0.20 to 1.07; 5 studies, 413 participants), but when outliers were excluded, a difference favouring mesh repair was found (OR 0.25, 95% CI 0.08 to 0.72; 4 studies, 282 participants). Furthermore, in contrast to our review, Hentati 2014 found fewer recurrences in the mesh repair group (OR 0.20, 95% CI 0.05 to 0.78; 3 studies, 260 participants). However, the time point for outcome measurement of recurrence was not reported or discussed, and Hentati 2014 did not stratify the results according to degree of contamination.

    We identified another systematic review and meta‐analysis assessing the effectiveness and safety of mesh repair for incarcerated or strangulated hernias (Lin 2020). This study included different types of hernias, including groin, ventral, paraumbilical, umbilical, incisional, epigastric, and Spigelian hernias, and presented results based on two RCTs (one of which included inguinal hernias; Elsebae 2008) and six prospective studies where participants with groin hernias comprised 63% of the total study population. The results indicated that mesh repair lowered the recurrence rate. If bowel resection was needed, the surgical site infection rate was slightly increased when mesh was used, but these were successfully treated with antibiotics and no mesh removal was needed, concluding that mesh repair should not be contraindicated in emergency hernia repair even if concomitant bowel resection is required. This review did not stratify its results according to degree of contamination.

    Surgical site infections (as defined by the included studies) within 30 and 90 days of repair

    In our review, the included studies reported 41/229 events of 30‐day surgical site infections (18%) in the mesh group and 24/225 events (11%) in the non‐mesh group. These numbers are comparable to previously reported rates, with Ndong 2020 finding a 17% surgical site infection rate following non‐mesh repairs and Ueda 2012 finding the rates to be 20% and 18% in the mesh and non‐mesh groups, respectively. Furthermore, we could not identify any overall differences between the mesh and non‐mesh group, which is in line with the results of Hentati 2014, finding no increase in the risk of surgical site infections when using mesh. However, it should be noted that the analysis might be underpowered, disabling it from excluding the possibility of no difference.

    Mortality (defined as all‐cause mortality) within 30 days of repair

    Among all four studies with 30‐day mortality as an outcome, only one reported deaths within 30 days, resulting in an overall mortality rate of 3.5%. Previously, the 30‐day mortality following emergency groin hernia repair has been reported to range from 0% to 12% (Sæter 2022b). The results of this review are, therefore, in line with the estimate, but possibly on the lower end of what has previously been reported, reflecting that the most severe cases of emergency groin hernia repair were excluded from the clinical trials, which limits the external validity of the results from this review. Furthermore, the one study included in this review that reported deaths only included participants undergoing bowel resection, which is in accordance with previous findings supporting that bowel resection increases the risk of mortality substantially (Köckerling 2021; Nilsson 2007; Sæter 2022b). These studies also identified female gender, femoral hernias, and increased age as risk factors for mortality for patients undergoing emergency groin hernia repair, but our planned subgroup analyses to investigate these factors were too limited in data to be performed.

    Clinical recurrence

    Regarding clinical recurrence, we found no events of recurrence within 30 days and no difference between the two groups within one year of repair. Previous non‐randomised studies on emergency groin hernia repair have reported a higher recurrence rate in the non‐mesh group compared with the mesh group. For instance, Chen 2020 reported, after 20.5 months of follow‐up, a recurrence rate of 21.4% in the non‐mesh group compared with 4.3% in the biological mesh group and 0.0% in the synthetic mesh group. A large nationwide register‐based study found, in a multivariate Cox regression analysis, that mesh reduced the cumulative reoperation rates for recurrence when compared with non‐mesh repairs (Sæter 2022c). Lastly, both the systematic reviews and meta‐analysis previously mentioned found that mesh reduced the risk of recurrence following emergency groin hernia repair (Hentati 2014; Lin 2020). The discrepancy in the results between previous systematic reviews and this Cochrane Review can be explained by longer follow‐up times, the nature of non‐randomised trials, and an underpowered meta‐analysis in our review failing to exclude the possibility of no difference. On the other hand, in the literature on the topic of elective mesh versus non‐mesh repairs of groin hernias, international hernia management guidelines provide a strong recommendation to use a mesh‐based technique in inguinal hernia repair (HerniaSurge Group 2018), due to the lower recurrence rate following mesh‐based techniques and a comparable risk of postoperative pain. From that perspective, mesh hernia repair would probably also reduce recurrence following emergency repair in selected patients where mesh placement is feasible.

    Mesh removal

    The results from the included studies in this review showed that no meshes were removed from 98 participants not receiving bowel resection, while 7% of meshes in 104 participants undergoing bowel resection were removed (Duan 2018). Mesh removal rates are comparable to those previously reported, with no meshes removed (Chen 2020; Ueda 2012). However, there are, in general, very few studies conducted on participants with contaminated to dirty surgical fields, so it is difficult to compare the findings from Duan 2018 with previous literature. Lastly, Bessa 2015 conducted a 10‐year study, including 234 patients with acutely incarcerated and/or strangulated groin hernias. During the follow‐up period, only one patient (0.5%) developed a mesh infection, occurring after six months of repair, which necessitated mesh removal. Therefore, even though the event of mesh removal seems rare, short‐term follow‐up, such as 30 and 90 days, might not be long enough to identify all mesh removals. A longer follow‐up time is therefore necessary.
