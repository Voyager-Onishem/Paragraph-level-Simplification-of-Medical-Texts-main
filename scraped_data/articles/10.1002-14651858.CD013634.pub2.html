Surgery for chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia

    • Background

    Chronic arthropathy is a potentially debilitating complication for people with haemophilia — a genetic, X‐linked, recessive bleeding disorder, characterised by the absence or deficiency of a clotting factor protein. Staging classifications, such as the Arnold‐Hilgartner classification for haemophilic arthropathy of the knee, radiologically reflect the extent of knee joint destruction with underlying chronic synovitis. Management of this highly morbid disease process involves intensive prophylactic measures, and chemical or radioisotope synovectomy in its early stages. However, failure of non‐surgical therapy in people with progression of chronic arthropathy often prompts surgical management, including synovectomy, joint debridement, arthrodesis, and arthroplasty, depending on the type of joint and extent of the damage. To date, management of people with mild to moderate chronic arthropathy from haemophilia remains controversial; there is no agreed standard treatment. Thus, the benefits and disadvantages of non‐surgical and surgical management of mild to moderate chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia needs to be systematically reviewed. 

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of surgery for mild to moderate chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia A or B.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Coagulopathies Trials Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, and two trial registers to August 2022. We also handsearched relevant journals and conference abstract books.

    Selection criteria

    Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs comparing surgery and non‐surgical interventions, for any joint with chronic arthropathy, in people with haemophilia, who were at least 12 years old.

    Data collection and analysis

    The review authors did not identify any trials to include in this review.

    Main results

    The review authors did not identify any trials to include in this review.

    Authors' conclusions

    The review authors did not identify any trials to include in this review.

    Due to a lack of research in this particular area, we plan to update the literature search every two years, and will update review if any new evidence is reported. There is a need for a well‐designed RCT that assesses the safety and efficacy of surgical versus non‐surgical interventions for chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia.

    • Surgery for long‐term joint disease in people with haemophilia

    How does surgery compare to non‐surgical treatment in terms of safety and efficacy for people with chronic (long‐term) joint disease from a rare condition that affects the ability of the blood to form clots (haemophilia)?

    Key messages

    We did not find any relevant studies.

    More research is needed in this field. Future studies should compare surgical and non‐surgical treatments for chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia.

    Background

    Haemophilia is a disease that affects the ability of blood to clot. People with severe haemophilia often suffer from bleeding into their joints (haemarthrosis). Repeated bleeding over a long period will eventually lead to chronic joint disease (arthropathy), which may take the form of arthritis, joint pain or joint stiffness. This can lead to worsening quality of life and loss of limb function.

    Chronic joint disease from prolonged joint bleeding can be treated non‐surgically or surgically. But neither of these treatment options are perfect; both have their own risks and benefits. Non‐surgical treatments may not work for everyone. if they do not work, surgery may be recommended. Physicians are still uncertain about the safest and most effective way to treat chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia.

    What did we do?

    We searched medical databases and journal up to August 2022, for reports of randomised controlled studies (RCT) that compared surgical and non‐surgical treatments, in people who were at least 12 years old, suffered from haemophilia and were developing chronic joint disease. RCTs are those in which participants are assigned randomly to two or more treatment groups. This is the best way to ensure that groups of participants are similar, and that investigators and participants do not know who is in which group.

    What did we find?

    We did not find any studies that met our criteria.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The review authors did not identify any trials to include in this review, therefore, we are unable to conclude the effects and safety profile for any of the procedures. Thus, when considering this treatment, clinicians and people with haemophilia should weigh the potential efficacy of interventions against the risks of potential side effects and complications described in sources of lower‐certainty evidence.

    Due to a lack of research in this particular area, we plan to update the literature search every two years, and will update the review if any new evidence is reported.

    Implications for research

    Findings from this review indicate that there is a need for a well‐designed randomised controlled trial that assesses the safety and efficacy of surgical versus non‐surgical interventions for chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia. Primary outcomes should evaluate pain relief, with validated pain score scales, and annual bleed rate of the affected joint. Secondary outcomes should evaluate relief of joint symptoms, improvement in range of motion, functional outcomes, quality of life, amount of clotting factor concentrates used, and complications arising from both surgical and non‐surgical interventions. 

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Haemophilia is a genetic, X‐linked, recessive bleeding disorder, characterised by the absence or deficiency of a clotting factor protein, hindering the clotting of blood. It affects males with a defective X chromosome; females are carriers who potentially may experience an increased risk of bleeding. One third of new cases present sporadically, with no family history. The two most common types of haemophilia are haemophilia A (factor VIII deficiency), and haemophilia B (factor IX deficiency or Christmas disease), the former of which represents 80% to 85% of all cases. There is an estimated incidence of one in 10,000 births, with approximately 400,000 people living with the condition worldwide (Aznar 2009; WFH 2012).

    Pathophysiologically, bleeding occurs due to the failure of secondary haemostatic mechanisms, during which stabilisation of the platelet plug formed in primary haemostasis is impaired as a result of insufficient generation of thrombin. Factors VIII and IX are known to be key drivers of thrombin generation (Bolton‐Maggs 2003). 

    A clotting factor level of less than 0.01 international units (IU) per mL (1%) is classified as severe, and is often clinically associated with bleeding into the joints and muscles, which may occur spontaneously, without preceding trauma. People with factor levels between 1% and 5% are classified as having moderate haemophilia; they usually experience fewer joint bleeds relative to those with severe haemophilia, and rarely develop the sequelae of chronic arthropathy. Those with factor levels from 5% to 40% are classified as having mild haemophilia, and only experience severe bleeding after surgery or trauma. Approximately 70% to 80% of all bleeding episodes occur in the joint (haemarthrosis); whilst 10% to 20% occur in the muscle. Hinge joints are the most commonly affected, including the elbows, knees, and ankles. The majority of children with severe haemophilia experience their first episode of haemarthrosis by the age of four, but most have already bled from other sites at a younger age (WFH 2012).

    Bleeding arises from haemorrhaging in the small vessels in the synovial membrane, causing distension of the joint capsule over time. Blood effusion‐derived haemosiderin deposits potentiate an acute inflammatory response within the synovium, followed by the removal of blood by synoviocytes. Erosion and destruction of the articular cartilage ensues, due to damage by the inflammatory enzymes and the blood itself. Other key players orchestrating the inflammatory process include a high molecular weight complex and iron, both of which regulate the secretion of pro‐inflammatory cytokines. Angiogenesis also plays a role in the pathogenesis of chronic arthropathy, as there is evidence of elevated proangiogenic growth factors and macrophages in the synovium and peripheral blood of people with haemophilia (Abshire 2011; Melchiorre 2017).

    People with haemophilia often experience clinical symptoms of pain, muscle spasm, reduction in range of motion, loss of joint proprioception, and a reduced quality of life (Rodriguez‐Merchan 2010; Roosendaal 2008). Unfortunately, most people experience repeated episodes of haemarthroses, sometimes starting before the previous episode has resolved. Repeated episodes lead to the development of what is termed as a 'target joint', which results in chronic arthropathy (Solimeno 2017). This is characterised by chronic inflammation of the synovium, accompanied by synovial hypervascularity and hypertrophy; epiphyseal changes; joint space narrowing and cartilage erosion; osteophytic changes; subchondral cysts; and joint surface irregularity. Although people with severe haemophilia B may experience less severe chronic arthropathy compared to those with severe haemophilia A, this debilitating manifestation affects 90% of all people with severe haemophilia, and chiefly contributes to the greatest morbidity and cost in this population (Manco‐Johnson 2004; Melchiorre 2017).

    Currently, no cure exists for either haemophilia A or B, but allogeneic liver transplant and gene therapy are undergoing clinical trials (Marchesini 2021). Until the 1960s, the standard treatment for the older generation of people with haemophilia was emergency surgery for chronic arthropathy, which lead to high mortality rates, ranging between 25% and 50% (Craddock 1948). The current standard of treatment to prevent spontaneous joint bleeds in people with severe haemophilia involves the intravenous administration of the relevant missing factor, either on demand or prophylactically, which has been shown to be highly effective (Oldenburg 2015). Primary prophylaxis is widely used and advocated in Europe, especially in Sweden and the UK, where the Medical and Scientific Advisory Council of the National Hemophilia Foundation and the Pediatric Working Party of the United Kingdom Haemophilia Doctors' Organisation recommends it as the optimal therapeutic modality for people with severe haemophilia (Löfqvist 1997; Richards 2010). Several trials, from both Europe and the USA, have reported reduced morbidity and bleeding rates with primary prophylaxis in comparison to on‐demand therapy (Collins 2010; Gringeri 2011; Manco‐Johnson 2013; Valentino 2012). Primary prophylaxis should start by the age of four years or before a second episode of joint bleed (Manco‐Johnson 2004). However, the high cost of prophylaxis makes it difficult to be adopted globally. Up to 66% of those who receive prophylaxis have tried to discontinue treatment, leading to issues associated with compliance (Roosendaal 2008). Other reported major reasons for discontinuing treatment are the burden associated with the need for life‐long treatment, forgetfulness, lack of time, and a reduction of symptoms (de Moerloose 2008; Walsh 2009).

    There is also a risk of developing inhibitors, which are neutralising antibodies to factor VIII and IX concentrates (WFH 2022). This occurs in up to 30% of people with haemophilia A, and renders the factor concentrates less effective. Immune tolerance induction is used to eradicate inhibitors. Treating bleeding episodes in those with inhibitors requires the administration of bypassing agents, such as recombinant activated factor VII concentrate or activated prothrombin complex concentrate (Iorio 2010; Leissinger 2011). However, immune tolerance induction may not be successful, and is costly (Bolton‐Maggs 2003).

    Description of the intervention

    With the onset of chronic haemophilic arthropathy, therapeutic options are limited. Non‐surgical management involves the use of compressive dressings; pharmacologics, such as non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and steroidal medications; rehabilitation; use of orthoses; more intensive prophylaxis with factor concentrates; and minimally invasive procedures, such as radionuclide or chemical synovectomy. However, these may not be effective, and invasive surgical procedures may be required.

    The most common surgical procedures include synovectomy, joint debridement, arthrodesis, and joint arthroplasty, depending on the type of joint and extent of the damage; for descriptions of these procedures, please see the glossary (Appendix 1). The main indications for synovectomy and synoviorthesis are recurrent joint haemarthrosis, and synovitis recalcitrant to medical management. They can be performed by arthroscopy or open surgery, ideally, before the joint is irreversibly damaged (Luck 2004). Arthroplasty is indicated in cases of severe disabling pain, together with deformity and impaired functional range of motion. It can be performed for various joints, including the knee, elbow, ankle, hip, and the shoulder (Luck 2004). Antibiotics are administered, osteophytes may be removed, and synovectomy may be undertaken prior to the implantation of the prosthetic components during an arthroplasty (Luck 2004; Solimeno 2017). Despite the heightened interest in arthroplasty, arthrodesis is another well‐accepted treatment for end‐stage chronic haemophilic arthropathy of the ankle, which primary involves bone‐to‐bone fusion to immobilise the joint (Bluth 2013). 

    How the intervention might work

    Synovectomies are performed to reduce the frequency of synovitis (haemarthrosis), but not to improve existing joint degeneration. Both open (80%) and arthroscopic (70%) methods are highly successful in abolishing recurrent bleeding after the perioperative period. Radiosynovectomy, during which radioisotopes are injected into the intra‐articular space, shows encouraging clinical results, with an average 75% success rate. Since it is minimally invasive compared to surgical synovectomy, it preserves range of motion. However, radiosynovectomy is not as widely available across different centres as surgical synovectomy; its high cost limits access to people living in countries with a high gross national income. It also poses safety concerns related to radiation exposure in children (Rodriguez‐Merchan 2015).  

    Arthroplasty for the knee is clinically successful, with marked improvement in pain relief, flexion and extension contractures, and overall function compared to the preoperative status (Luck 2004). The major pitfall of arthroplasty is prosthetic joint infection, which should be prevented with prophylactic antibiotics (Song 2017). Similar trends have been observed in total ankle replacement surgeries, with encouraging rates of clinical success and survival (Barg 2016; Eckers 2017; Haddad 2007). However, knee arthroplasty may be complicated by fibrosis or bone deformities in the preoperative stage, which are attributed to the presence of contractures, resulting from repeated haemarthrosis and fibroblastic proliferation (Lobet 2014). Hip procedures may also be complicated by the stiff‐legged gait often associated with people with haemophilia, which places increased stresses on the hip, and consequent loosening of the prosthetic components (Luck 2004). Chronic haemophilic arthropathy of the shoulder joint is often characterised by erosion and thinning of the glenoid, which may be too weak structurally to support a prosthetic component. As such, arthrodesis may be an effective alternative to improve functional strength, coupled with a reduced risk of infection (Luck 2004).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Without successful treatment, haemophilic synovitis often leads to recurrent haemarthroses, which eventually leads to chronic arthropathy. It is crucial to start treatment with clotting factor concentrates and bypassing agents in the early stage; prophylactic factor replacement in infancy may also prevent chronic synovitis and arthropathy. However, some people progress to chronicity despite the best medical therapy, and are now limited to surgical alternatives. Surgical management is a mainstay treatment in recurrent haemarthroses recalcitrant to non‐surgical management.

    However, the aim of this Cochrane Review is to compare surgery versus non‐surgical treatment for chronic arthropathy prior to the failure of non‐surgical treatment, but at a point when the person might potentially benefit from a surgical intervention to improve their quality of life, and mitigate disease progression‐related morbidity. To date, management of people with mild to moderate chronic arthropathy from haemophilia remains controversial. This review will endeavour to assess the safety and efficacy of surgery in people with haemophilia with chronic arthropathy, in preventing bleeding episodes, restoring joint mobility, and improving overall physical function and quality of life.

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of surgery for chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia A or B.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs. Cross‐over trials were not appropriate in this setting and were not eligible.

    Types of participants

    People with haemophilia with chronic arthropathy, aged 12 years and older.

    Types of interventions

    Experimental interventions

    Surgery, which included open or arthroscopic synovectomy, arthroplasty, alignment osteotomy, or arthrodesis.

    Comparator interventions

    Non‐surgical interventions, which included compressive dressings, pharmacological options (oral analgesics, such as non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), steroidal medications), rehabilitation (i.e. physiotherapy), use of orthoses (i.e. splints and braces), and minimally invasive procedures, such as factor administration, radionuclide or chemical synovectomy.

    Comparisons

    Surgery versus non‐surgical intervention for any joint with chronic arthropathy, i.e. shoulder, elbow, knee, hip, ankle.

    We planned to analyse different types of surgery separately. We also planned to present control groups separately (e.g. surgery versus non‐surgical therapy and surgery versus physiotherapy would be analysed separately). If trials assessing different surgical modalities were available, we planned to compare surgery 'A' versus surgery 'B'.

    Types of outcome measures

    The review authors planned to assess the following outcome measures.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Pain relief, measured using validated pain score scales (Atisook 2021), e.g. Visual Analogue Scale (VAS (Jensen 2011)), 6‐point Verbal Rating Scale (VRS‐6 (Caraceni 2002)), 0 to 10 Numerical Rating Scale (NRS‐11 (Chien 2013)), and Faces Pain Scale‐Revised (FPS‐R (Bieri 1990))

    2. Annual bleed rate (ABR) of the affected joint

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Objective measure of joint health status, measured by a validated outcome measure (Gouw 2018), e.g. WFH score (Gilbert 1993), the Colorado Physical Examination score (CPE (Manco‐Johnson 2000)), the Petrini joint score (PJS (Hill 2003)), and different versions of the Hemophilia Joint Health Score (HJHS (Hilliard 2006)), radiologic joint score (Pettersson 1980)

    2. Improvement in joint range of motion, e.g. difference between range of motion of joint after surgery and before surgery

    3. Functional outcome or quality of life, assessed through self‐administered questionnaires, such as the Hemo‐QOL (Pollack 2006), HAEMO‐QoL‐A (Rentz 2008), and SF‐36 (Ware 1992))

    4. Amount of clotting factor concentrates used peri‐procedure, as reported in the trials

    5. Surgical complications

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The review authors formulated a search strategy to identify all relevant trials, regardless of language or publication status (published, unpublished, in press, and in progress). There were no language or publication restrictions; they planned to include both full articles and abstracts.

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Information Specialist conducted a search of the Group's Coagulopathies Fibrosis Trials Register for relevant trials, using the terms: haemophilia*[kw] AND arthropathy[kw].

    The Coagulopathies Trials Register is compiled from electronic searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; updated each new issue of the Cochrane Library), weekly searches of MEDLINE, and handsearching of one journal ‐ Haemophilia. Unpublished work is identified by searching the abstract books of major conferences: the European Haematology Association conference; the American Society of Hematology conference; the British Society for Haematology Annual Scientific Meeting; the Congress of the World Federation of Hemophilia; the European Association for Haemophilia and Allied Disorders; the American Society of Gene and Cell Therapy; and the International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis. For full details of all searching activities for the register, please see the relevant section of the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's website.

    The review authors also searched the following databases and trial registries; see Appendix 2 for the search strategies:

    1. Cochrane Central Register of Controlled trials (CENTRAL; 2022, Issue 8) in the Cochrane Library (searched 1 August 2022); 

    2. MEDLINE OvidSP (1946 to 1 August 2022);

    3. Embase OvidSP (1974 to 1 August 2022);

    4. CINAHL EBSCO (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature; 1982 to 1 August 2022);

    5. US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov; searched 1 August 2022);

    6. World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP; apps.who.int/trialsearch; searched 2 August 2022).

    Searching other resources

    Grey literature

    The review authors handsearched abstract books for unpublished work from the following relevant conferences:

    1. Congress of the World Federation of Hemophilia (1996 to 2019);

    2. Musculoskeletal Congresses of World Federation of Hemophilia (2005 to 2019);

    3. European Haematology Association Conference (2002 to 2019);

    4. British Society for Haematology Annual Scientific Meeting (2007 to 2019);

    5. American Society of Hematology conference (2006 to 2019).

    Manual searching

    The review authors planned to check the bibliographies of included trials and any relevant systematic reviews identified, for further references to relevant trials. When relevant, they planned to contact experts and organisations in the field to obtain additional information on relevant trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    The review authors did not identify any relevant trials that were potentially eligible for inclusion in this review. In future updates of this review, they will perform data collection and analysis as below. 

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (BC and IW) will independently assess all titles and abstracts retrieved by electronic searching (using reference management software (EndNote 2013)). They will exclude reports that clearly do not fit the inclusion criteria, and obtain full‐text copies of trials deemed relevant. Two review authors (IW and NS) will independently evaluate the eligibility of the full‐text reports, and resolve conflicts through discussion, or by appeal to a third review author (ES or PL). They will depict the two‐stage screening process using the PRISMA flow diagram (Page 2021).

    Data extraction and management

    Using a data collection form, two review authors (IW and NS) will independently extract the information from included trials. They will resolve disagreements first by discussion, or by appeal to a third review author (ES or PL). In future updates of the review, if trials are eligible for inclusion, one review author (IW) will transfer the collected data into the Cochrane Review Manager software file (RevMan Web 2022); and a second review author (NS) will ensure the accuracy of the data by comparing them to the original trial reports.

    They will extract the following types of data, where available.

    1. author, year of publication

    2. randomisation methods and type of statistical analyses

    3. baseline characteristics of participants

    4. interventions: these will be extracted separately, accordingly to the different types of surgeries

    5. results for primary and secondary outcomes

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    If the review authors are able to include trials in future versions of this review, two review authors (BC and IW) will conduct independent assessments of the risk of bias for each included trial, using Cochrane's RoB 1 tool (Higgins 2017). They will resolve conflicts by consensus or by appeal to a third review author (ES or PL). They will assess the following domains as having either a low, unclear, or high risk of bias.

    1. random sequence generation (selection bias)

    2. concealment of allocation (selection bias)

    3. blinding both participants and personnel staff (performance bias)

    4. blinding outcome assessment (detection bias)

    5. incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    6. selective outcome reporting (reporting bias)

    7. other potential forms of bias

    Measures of treatment effect

    For time‐to‐event data, the review authors will generate a hazard ratio (HR), using the inverse variance method, along with its respective 95% confidence intervals (CIs), or the log of the HR and its respective standard error (SE (Deeks 2022)).

    For dichotomous outcomes, the review authors will extract the number of participants in each treatment group for the outcome of interest, along with the total number of participants included at endpoint measurement, which will allow them to analyse the unadjusted risk ratio (RR) or odds ratio (OR). They will pool either the OR or RR, or calculate the raw values from individual studies, with 95% CIs (Deeks 2022).

    For continuous outcomes, they will extract the mean values and standard deviation (SD), as well as the total number of participants included at endpoint assessment, thus allowing them to analyse the mean difference (MD) between the experimental and comparative arms if the trials use the same scale; otherwise they will use the standardised mean difference (SMD (Deeks 2022)).

    For ordinal outcomes, the review authors will dichotomise the scale if it is short (five values or less), or regard it as a continuous outcome if it is longer (more than five values). If they perform the former, they will undertake a sensitivity analysis to determine the optimal cut‐off point (Deeks 2022).

    The review authors will extract both unadjusted and adjusted statistics, and any data relevant to an intention‐to‐treat (ITT) and per‐protocol analysis. For the primary meta‐analysis, they will complete an ITT analysis, where they include all participants in each group in the analyses, regardless of whether they received the intervention or not (Deeks 2022).

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cluster‐randomised trials

    The review authors plan to include cluster‐randomised trials along with individually randomised trials. In the event of including both, they will deem it appropriate to combine the results if there is minimal heterogeneity (if I² is 40% or lower) between the trial designs, suggesting little or no influence of the choice of the randomisation unit.

    Cross‐over studies

    We excluded cross‐over trials.

    Multi‐arm intervention

    For the primary analysis, the review authors plan to combine arms to obtain a single pairwise comparison, in order to avoid double‐counting participants in either the experimental or control arms. For instance, if a trial compares surgery versus non‐surgical regimen A versus non‐surgical regimen B, they will combine the results from non‐surgical regimens (A and B) in order to obtain a single comparison of surgery versus non‐surgical regimen A + B.

    Dealing with missing data

    The review authors will collect the numbers and reasons for dropouts in all intervention groups. They will contact the original investigators for clarification on any missing information, when necessary. 

    If necessary, they plan to impute missing data, using the methods described by Hozo 2005 for continuous outcomes, and Parmar 1998 for time‐to‐event outcomes. If trials report data as median and interquartile ranges (IQRs), the review authors will use the median to estimate the mean for trials with moderate to large sample sizes (N > 25). For trials with small sample sizes, they plan to use the methodology proposed in Hozo 2005. The review authors plan to calculate the SD with the width of the IQR equivalent to 1.35 times the SD, when necessary (Higgins 2022). If trials report a range and a median instead of an IQR, they plan to estimate the SD to be one quarter of the range (Higgins 2022).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    The review authors will assess inter‐trial heterogeneity by visually inspecting the forest plots, and using both the Chi² test and I² statistic for heterogeneity (Higgins 2002). They will consider P < 0.10 to be statistically significant (Deeks 2022). They will interpret the I² as follows:

    1. 0% to 40%: might not be important;

    2. 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity;

    3. 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity; and

    4. 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    The review authors will evaluate funnel plots for the meta‐analysis of each primary outcome that includes data from 10 trials or more, to assess for publication bias. They will visually assess the funnel plots for asymmetry, and if present, they will perform exploratory analyses to investigate further (Page 2022).

    Data synthesis

    The review authors plan to perform meta‐analyses using Review Manager software, in accordance with Cochrane guidelines (Deeks 2022; RevMan Web 2022). They plan to use a fixed‐effect model if there is no substantial heterogeneity, as defined by an I² statistic of less than 50%; otherwise, they plan to use the random‐effects model (I² statistic greater than 50%).

    They plan to analyse different types of surgery and control groups separately (e.g. surgery versus non‐surgical therapy and surgery versus physiotherapy will be analysed separately). If they include trials assessing different surgical modalities, the review authors will attempt to compare surgery 'A' versus surgery 'B'.

    If they are unable to conduct any meta‐analysis, they will perform a narrative synthesis of the results.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    The review authors plan to perform a subgroup analysis comparing the long‐term and short‐term (up to two years) impacts of surgical interventions on the recurrence of haemarthrosis.

    Sensitivity analysis

    The review authors will perform sensitivity analyses by excluding trials with either high or unclear risk of bias for allocation concealment, randomisation, blinding, or incomplete outcome data. For cluster‐randomised trials, they will conduct sensitivity analyses to assess the impact of variation in the intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC).

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    The review authors will use GRADEpro GDT software to create summary of findings tables, which involves evaluating the certainty of evidence for each important outcome, based on five domains (GRADEpro GDT; Schünemann 2022). These include:

    1. risk of bias of included studies;

    2. inconsistency or heterogeneity;

    3. indirectness (relevance to the review question);

    4. imprecision (CIs);

    5. publication bias.

    Two review authors will independently grade the certainty of evidence; they will resolve conflicts by consensus or by appeal to a third review author. The summary of finding tables present the main findings, including the effect estimate for each outcome, the total number of trials and participants included per outcome, and the overall confidence in the effect estimates. The review authors will include the following outcomes in the summary of findings tables (one table for each unique comparison).

    1. Pain relief

    2. Annual bleed rate (ABR) of the affected joint

    3. Objective measure of joint health status 

    4. Improvement in joint range of motion

    5. Functional outcome or quality of life

    6. Amount of clotting factor concentrates used peri‐procedure

    7. Surgical complications

    Discussion

    Despite the well‐established debilitating complications of chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia, there are no existing randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that compare surgical and non‐surgical methods of management.

    Current literature shows that in people with haemophilic arthropathy, synovectomy is indicated for recurrent haemarthroses that is recalcitrant to medical management. Likewise, synoviorthesis is indicated for chronic haemophilic synovitis that is recalcitrant to medical treatment, with joint arthroplasties and arthrodesis usually reserved for end‐stage arthropathy. However, while surgical treatment is often more effective in improving joint range of motion, providing pain relief, and improving functional outcomes than their medical counterparts, they are directly linked to intraoperative and postoperative complications, such as blood loss, prosthetic joint infections, and eventual implant loosening (Luck 2004). With growing evidence that surgical intervention should be performed before the joint is irreversibly damaged, the benefits, side effects, disadvantages, or complications of surgical and non‐surgical treatment of chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia need to be further evaluated (Luck 2004).

    Potential biases in the review process

    Identifiable potential biases in the review process are primarily selection bias, since relevant literature may not have been included. However, the selection criteria were carefully designed, and the decision to include trials was not affected by the review authors' perceptions regarding results, authors, or journals. 

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We did not identify any trials to include in this review. There is a paucity of comparative studies analysing surgical versus non‐surgical management of chronic arthropathy in people with haemophilia. There are limited studies comparing different surgical interventions for the treatment of advanced chronic arthropathy secondary to haemophilia (i.e. ankle arthroplasty versus arthrodesis in end‐stage haemophilic arthropathy (Ahn 2020)), or comparing the effect of different regimens of prophylaxis on haemophilic arthropathy (van den Berg 2003).
