Splenectomy for people with thalassaemia major or intermedia

  • Akshay Sharma
  • Manu Easow Mathew
  • Latika Puri
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 17 September 2019 Version history

Abstract

    • Background

    Thalassaemia is a genetic disorder of the haemoglobin protein in red blood cells. It has been historically classified into thalassaemia minor, intermedia and major, depending on the genetic defect and severity of the disease. The clinical presentation of β‐thalassaemia varies widely from a mild asymptomatic form in thalassaemia minor, to a severe disease in thalassaemia major where individuals are dependant on life‐long blood transfusions. The hallmark of thalassaemia syndromes is the production of defective red blood cells that are removed by the spleen resulting in an enlarged hyperfunctioning spleen (splenomegaly). Removal of the spleen may thus prolong red blood cell survival by reducing the amount of red blood cells removed from circulation and may ultimately result in the reduced need for blood transfusions.

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of splenectomy in people with β‐thalassaemia major or intermedia.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Review Group's Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register, compiled from searches of electronic databases and the handsearching of journals and conference abstract books. We also searched online trial registries and the reference lists of relevant articles and reviews (27 July 2018).

    Date of the most recent search of the Group's trials register: 02 August 2019.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled and quasi‐randomised controlled studies of people of any age with thalassaemia major or intermedia, evaluating splenectomy in comparison to conservative treatment (transfusion therapy and iron chelation) or other forms of splenectomy compared to each other (laparoscopic, open, radio‐frequency).

    Data collection and analysis

    Two authors independently selected and extracted data from the single included study using a customised data extraction form and assessed the risk of bias. The quality of the evidence was assessed using GRADE.

    Main results

    One study, including 28 participants was included in the review; the results were described, primarily, in a narrative manner. This study assessed the feasibility of splenectomy using a laparoscopic approach versus open surgery. Given the lack of detail regarding the study methods beyond randomisation, the overall risk of bias for this study was unclear. The study was carried out over a period of 3.5 years, with each participant followed up only until discharge (less than one week after the intervention); it did not assess the majority of the outcomes outlined in this review (including two of the three primary outcomes, frequency of transfusion and quality of life). A total of three serious post‐operative adverse events (the review's third primary outcome) were reported in the laparoscopic splenectomy group (one case of atelectasis and two cases of bleeding), compared to two events of atelectasis in the open surgery group; however, there were no significant differences between the groups for either atelectasis, risk ratio (RR) 0.50 (95% confidence interval (CI) 0.05 to 4.90) or for bleeding, RR 5.00 (95% CI 0.26 to 95.61) (very low‐quality evidence). In addition, the study also reported three serious cases of intra‐operative bleeding in the laparoscopic group which mandated conversion to open surgery, although the difference between groups was not statistically significant, RR 7.00 (95% CI 0.39 to 124.14) (very low‐quality evidence). These effect estimates are based on very small numbers and hence are unreliable and imprecise. From this small study, there appeared to be an advantage for the laparoscopic approach, in terms of post‐operative hospital stay, although the group difference was not large (median difference of 1.5 days, P = 0.03).

    Authors' conclusions

    The review was unable to find good quality evidence, in the form of randomised controlled studies, regarding the efficacy of splenectomy for treating thalassaemia major or intermedia. The single included study provided little information about the efficacy of splenectomy, and compared open surgery and laparoscopic methods. Further studies need to evaluate the long‐term effectiveness of splenectomy and the comparative advantages of surgical methods. Due to a lack of high quality evidence from randomised controlled studies, well‐conducted observational studies may be used to answer this question.

    • Removal of the spleen in people with thalassaemia major and intermedia

    Review question

    We reviewed the evidence comparing the removal of the spleen (splenectomy) to other treatments (such as blood transfusions and chelation (removal of excess iron) in people with thalassaemia major and intermedia.

    Background

    Thalassaemia is a genetic disease, caused by a defect in production of haemoglobin ‐ which carries oxygen in red blood cells to all the tissues of the body. The clinical presentation of thalassaemia varies widely from a mild asymptomatic form to being dependant on life long blood transfusions. The genetic defect in thalassaemia also results in formation of ineffective red blood cells, which are removed by the spleen which in turn becomes enlarged. Removing the spleen can lengthen the lifespan of red blood cells and reduce the need for transfusions. We wanted to evaluate whether current evidence showed that removing the spleen was safe and effective in the long term and compare different surgical methods of removing the spleen.

    Search date

    The evidence is current to: 02 August 2019.

    Study characteristics

    One study with a total of 28 participant was included in the review. This study compared two methods of splenectomy ‐ laparoscopic (keyhole) versus an open surgical approach. Study participants were recruited over a period of 3.5 years, but participants were only followed up to the end of their hospital stay (usually less than one week).

    Key results

    The study evaluated the two types of surgical methods. Only one of our three primary outcomes were reported, the number of people experiencing major adverse events (bleeding during and after the operation and complete or partial collapse of a lung). However, the amount of information available is not sufficient to draw any reliable conclusions (very‐low quality evidence). Hence, we were unable to provide recommendations regarding the use of splenectomy in people with thalassaemia. Appropriate clinical judgement, in view of the various risks and benefits described by other lower quality sources of evidence (e.g. observational studies), may be necessary when considering splenectomy in people with thalassaemia.

    Quality of the evidence

    While we are satisfied that the participants had equal chances of undergoing either type of surgery, there is not enough information on other aspects of the study to make any overall judgement on its quality.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Current evidence, based solely on observational studies, suggests that regular transfusion therapy along with goal‐directed iron chelation can help reduce complications of thalassaemia, as well as the need for splenectomy. The individualisation and intensification of iron chelation therapy appear to be feasible alternative approaches to splenectomy for the purpose of preventing iron overload and transfusion‐associated complications. Due to the lack of adequate good quality evidence from randomised controlled studies, we are unable to confidently conclude that splenectomy reduces transfusion requirements or iron toxicity. We were also unable to suggest the best option between various surgical options for splenectomy, although laparoscopic splenectomy is considered the current gold standard technique for removal of normal sized or slightly enlarged spleen based on consensus from experts.

    Implications for research

    Good quality randomised controlled studies are required to evaluate the comparative efficacy of the various splenectomy surgical methods. Since adequate blinding is difficult to achieve due to the nature of the intervention, authors should try to limit detection and performance bias, by measuring objective outcomes. Surgical expertise and sophistication of care can play a role in the outcome of surgery and thus it might be worthwhile to explore these effects. Additionally, since the final treatment choice might be dependent on the difference between the long‐term costs of the various interventions, studies should also try to incorporate a measurement of healthcare costs.

    Non‐randomised study designs may eventually contribute to the evidence base on the benefits and harms of splenectomy. Propsective cohort studies may be a feasible option as people with thalassaemia will often require routine and long‐term follow‐up.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Thalassaemias are a group of genetic disorders which are characterised by pathological defects in the production of globin chains of the haemoglobin molecule. Depending on the globin gene affected, thalassaemia is classified into α‐ or β‐thalassaemia (Harvey 2010).

    Thalassaemia is highly prevalent in historically malaria‐endemic areas, including sub‐Saharan Africa, the Mediterranean region, the Indian subcontinent, and Southeast Asia (Martin 2013;Weatherall 2012). It has been estimated that 5 percent of the world's population has at least one thalassaemia variant allele, with about 900,000 individuals with clinically significant disease expected during the early 21st century, the majority in Southern China, India, and Southeast Asia [Vichinsky 2005].

    β‐thalassaemia is the more common of the two thalassaemia disorders (Vichinsky 2005). Although it is particularly prevalent in people of Mediterranean, Middle Eastern and Southeast Asian origin, it is estimated that approximately 1.5% of the global population is a carrier for a defective β‐thalassaemia (β‐globin) gene (Colah 2010). Worldwide, β‐thalassaemia is believed to affect approximately 1 in 100,000 live births (Galanello 2010). β‐thalasseamia can be caused by one of over 200 known mutations (Cao 2010), with each genetic defect resulting in absent or reduced synthesis of β‐globin chains.

    β‐thalassaemia has a wide spectrum of clinical severity and has been previously classified into thalassaemia major, thalassaemia intermedia and thalassaemia minor. Thalassaemia major is characterised by severe anaemia presenting early in life requiring lifelong transfusions and iron chelation therapy, whereas thalassaemia minor is at other end of the clinical spectrum where individuals are asymptomatic with mild anaemia. Thalassaemia intermedia is a more diverse group with varying clinical severity from mild, moderate to severe anaemia requiring no, intermittent to chronic transfusions. Other complications in the disease group include iron overload, pulmonary hypertension, extramedullary hematopoiesis or chronic ulcers. There is emerging clinical research that suggests that thalassaemia intermedia even though is classified as a mild disease with mild anaemia and transfusion requirements, can have significant co‐morbidities that emerge later in life. Thus, the clinical classification of thalassaemias has evolved to classify the individuals with thalassaemia into either transfusion‐dependent thalassaemia (TDT) or non‐transfusion‐dependent thalassaemia (NTDT) (Cappellini 2014; Taher 2013). Differentiation of a new individual with thalassaemia as either TDT or NTDT requires a careful clinical evaluation of baseline haemoglobin and other clinical and hematological parameters (Viprakasit 2018).

    Treatment of β‐thalassaemia

    Depending on the severity of the disease several treatment options are available for people with thalassaemia (Lichtman 2005).

    Blood transfusion and iron chelation therapy

    Blood transfusions have helped to improve both the quality of life of people with thalassaemia as well as the expected life‐span (Modell 2008; Telfer 2009). Transfusions of whole blood, or preferably packed red blood cells, are used to compensate for the falling haemoglobin and red blood cell levels and help to transiently improve the state of anaemia. The frequency of transfusions depends on severity of anaemia. Those with TDT usually require routine transfusions, which are administered every two to five weeks to promote normal growth and adequately suppress the bone marrow from producing the ineffective red blood cells. People with NTDT may need occasional transfusions during times of stress, such as pregnancy, infections and surgery etc. More frequent transfusions may be needed to treat growth failure, significant anaemia with splenomegaly, thromboembolic events, pulmonary hypertension, extramedullary haematopoietic pseudotumours (Taher 2013). Routine blood transfusions lead to iron overload. Iron overload can also occur in thalassaemia due to underlying ineffective erythropoiesis (Gardenghi 2007, Ginzburg 2011). Excess iron can accumulate in various organs leading to significant organ damage. Deposition in the liver results in liver fibrosis, cirrhosis and eventually hepatocellular carcinoma. Accumulation in the myocardium can cause heart failure. Endorocrine complications like hypogonadism, growth retardation, delayed puberty, hypothyroidism and hypoparathyroidism are also seen (Cappellini 2014). Thus monitoring for iron overload and iron chelation therapy are essential in managing people with thalassaemias. Other uncommon adverse effects of multiple blood transfusions include serious infections such as hepatitis B or C and hypersensitivity reactions (Prati 2000). Increased heme turnover resulting from multiple transfusions in addition to ineffective erythropoiesis, can result in commonly encountered surgical complications such as gall bladder stones, leg ulcers, fractures and extramedullary pseudotumours (Saad 2011). Although transfusions may improve clinical symptoms and quality of life for people with thalassaemia, this intervention is not curative.

    Bone marrow transplantation and gene therapy

    Currently, the only curative treatment for β‐thalassaemia is a bone marrow transplant (BMT). Gene therapy with β‐globin gene transfer to the individual's hematopoietic stem cells followed by autologous transplantation of these cells is under clinical investigation. Other emerging therapies that deliver exogenous regulators of key modulators of erythropoiesis or iron homeostasis are also being currently explored and are in pipeline for treatment for thalassaemia (Guerra 2018).

    Splenectomy

    An alternative treatment option described in detail below (Description of the intervention).

    Description of the intervention

    Splenectomy is a surgical procedure that involves the complete removal of the spleen (Sabiston 2004). Laparoscopic splenectomy is associated with a lower rate of intra‐operative blood loss, peri‐operative morbidity and mortality, shorter hospitalisations and favourable cosmesis than open splenectomy (Canda 2009; Winslow 2003; Konstadoulakis 2006). Partial splenectomy has also been used at some centres to preserve spleen’s immune function while reducing the degree of hypersplenism (Al‐Salem 1998; de Montalembert 1990) although the long‐term success is not known (Sheikha 2007)

    How the intervention might work

    The basic defect in β‐thalassaemia is a reduced or absent production of β‐globin chains with relative excess of α‐globin chains causing a globin chain imbalance. This results in ineffective erythropoiesis with extensive premature destruction of red cell precursors in the bone marrow and extramedullary sites, primarily the spleen leading to splenomegaly (Cappellini 2014).

    The rationale for splenectomy in those with TDT is to decrease blood consumption and transfusion requirement with the ultimate goal of reducing iron overload (Rachmilewitz 2011). However, with judicious pre‐transfusion haemoglobin levels and the maintenance of adequate intervals between transfusions, the probability for people with thalassaemia having to undergo splenectomy within the first 10 years of life has decreased from 57 % in 1960s to 7% in 1990s (Piga 2011). In those with NTDT, splenectomy is mainly performed to increase the total haemoglobin level by 1 to 2 g/dL and avoid the need for blood transfusions (Olivieri 2008, Musallam 2013).

    However, splenectomy could be associated with several long‐term effects. A hypercoagulable state has been described in individuals with NTDT because of presence of activated platelets and abnormal red blood cells. These abnormalities become more prominent following splenectomy because of the absence of the beneficial role of the spleen in scavenging these procoagulant platelets and red blood cells. Thus, after splenectomy these individuals are at a higher risk of thrombotic and vascular events (Taher 2013). Observational studies confirm that splenectomised NTDT individuals have a higher risk of venous thromboembolism, pulmonary hypertension, leg ulcers, and silent cerebral infarction as compared to non‐splenectomised individuals (Vichinsky 2012; Musallam 2011; Crary 2009, Taher 2010). Spleen also acts as a reservoir for iron and possibly has a scavenging effect on free iron (Taher 2009). It has been noted that splenectomised individuals have a higher rate of iron overload related organ morbidity than their non‐splenectomised peers (Taher 2010). Additionally, splenectomy can put people with thalassaemia at a risk of severe post splenectomy infections with encapsulated organisms like Streptococcus pneumoniae , Haemophilus influenza and Nisseria meningitidis (Cadili 2008) as well as other infections like malaria (Boone 1995). Lastly there could be peri‐operative complications related to anaesthesia and the surgical procedure it self.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Splenectomy may reduce the need for transfusion in people with thalassaemia, but it may also be associated with several serious adverse outcomes that may offset any benefits. There are no clear guidelines, supported by robust evidence, detailing the relative efficacy, safety, and risks of splenectomy in people with thalassaemia compared to other available treatment options (Taher 2011). Currently, subjective decisions are made on a case‐by‐case basis. We wish to undertake this systematic review to compile evidence on the efficacy and safety profile of splenectomy in people with thalassaemia.

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of splenectomy in people with β‐thalassaemia major or intermedia.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled studies and quasi‐randomised controlled studies.

    Types of participants

    People of any age with thalassaemia major or intermedia.

    Types of interventions

    Splenectomy compared with other treatment options, such as transfusion therapy and iron chelation, HSCT and foetal haemoglobin inducers; or different forms of splenectomy compared to each other (laparoscopic, open, radio‐frequency).

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Frequency of transfusion (post splenectomy or discharge)

    2. Occurence of serious adverse events (resulting in death or life threatening complications, inpatient hospitalisation, significant or permanent disability, or one that necessitates an additional intervention to prevent further damage)

    3. Quality of life (as assessed by validated instruments or scales)

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Occurence of non‐serious adverse events (as reported in the study)

    2. Hematological indices

      1. Haemoglobin levels

      2. Red blood cell counts

      3. Reticulocyte count

      4. White blood cell (WBC) counts

      5. Platelet counts

    3. Duration of hospital stay

    4. Serum ferritin

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We searched for all relevant published and unpublished trials without restrictions on language, year or publication status.

    Electronic searches

    We identified relevant studies from the Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register using the keywords: (thalassaemia OR (haemoglobinopathies AND general)) AND splenectomy.

    The Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register is compiled from electronic searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (updated each new issue of the Cochrane Library) and weekly searches of MEDLINE. Unpublished work is identified by searching the abstract books of five major conferences: the European Haematology Association conference; the American Society of Hematology conference; the British Society for Haematology Annual Scientific Meeting; the Caribbean Public Health Agency Annual Scientific Meeting (formerly the Caribbean Health Research Council Meeting); and the National Sickle Cell Disease Program Annual Meeting. For full details of all searching activities for the register, please see the relevant section of the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's website.

    Date of the most recent search of the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register: 02 August 2019.

    In addition to the above, we searched PubMed, CENTRAL and Embase using a specialised search strategy (seeAppendix 1). Date of the most recent search: 27 July 2018.

    We also searched the following trial registries for ongoing trials:

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov/);

    • WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/);

    • ISRCTN registry (www.isrctn.com/).

    Date of the most recent search: 27 July 2018 (Appendix 1)

    Searching other resources

    Scanning for references

    We scanned the reference list of the included study for further relevant studies.

    Contacting experts

    Additionally, we attempted to identify unpublished studies by contacting the authors of the included study.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    The search results from the various databases were fed into a reference manager software and duplicates were removed. Two review authors (MEM and AS) independently screened the remaining titles and abstracts of studies identified from the sources listed above using pre‐defined inclusion criteria. We excluded studies that did not fit the criteria and clearly documented the reasons. If we had had any disagreements we would have resolved them by a consensus meeting with the third author. We did not find any additional publications of the included study.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (MEM and AS) independently extracted data from the included study using a pre‐defined data extraction form. One author (MEM) entered the data into the Review Manager software (RevMan 2014). If there had been any disagreements we would have resolved these by holding a consensus meeting with the third author.

    We attempted to extract the following data.

    1. General information: title; authors; source; country; year of publication; setting.

    2. Study characteristics: design; method of randomisation; concealment of allocation; blinding of participants, personnel and outcome assessors, potential conflicts of interest.

    3. Participants: inclusion and exclusion criteria; underlying disease; indication for splenectomy; sample size; baseline characteristics (e.g. median age, pre‐existing co‐morbidities; duration of transfusion and chelation); losses to follow‐up.

    4. Intervention and comparison data: data regarding the operative procedure; alternative treatments used as comparisons; additional treatments.

    5. Outcomes: as mentioned above (primary and secondary outcomes).

    6. Additional data: adverse events and outcomes not described above.

    If the full text version did not provide sufficient information, we intended to contact the corresponding author for further details.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MEM and AS) independently assessed the risk of bias of the included study using Cochrane's 'Risk of bias' tool (Higgins 2011a).

    1. Sequence generation: we assessed the study report for the mention of an appropriate method of random sequence generation.

    2. Allocation concealment: we assessed the study report to check whether the allocation into the different study arms were concealed by a clearly stated method.

    3. Blinding: from the study report we tried to assess whether participants, personnel (performance bias) and outcome assessors (detection bias) were adequately blinded for the various subjective and objective outcomes.

    4. Incomplete outcome data: we hoped to analyse whether there were significant missing outcome data based on the number of participants who entered the study and availability of data at the time of follow‐up, and those who completed the study. We would have undertaken a sensitivity analysis if the percentage loss to follow‐up had exceeded 10%.

    5. Selective reporting: we hoped to assess for selective outcome reporting by comparing reports of the same study from different sources (study protocol, multiple publications, direct correspondence). We also looked for non‐significant results that were not reported adequately or if common or important outcome variables were missed by the study.

    6. Other sources of bias ‐ we looked for design‐specific sources of bias such as: recruitment bias; baseline imbalance; loss of clusters; incorrect analysis; source of funding; ethics committee clearance; informed consent procedures.

    Based on the appropriateness of the method used to achieve the above, we judged the study to be of high, low or unclear risk of bias. We recorded our reasons for the judgements in the 'Risk of bias' tables in the Review Manager software (RevMan 2014).

    Measures of treatment effect

    Rate data

    There are no rate data in the included study. For future updates of the review, if more studies are included, if available, we will report two of our primary outcomes (frequency of transfusion, frequency of serious adverse events) as rates, by calculating rate ratios with 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs).

    Continuous data

    No continuous outcomes are reported for the study included in this review. For future updates of the review, if more studies are included and If outcome data are continuous, we will extract the means and their corresponding standard deviations (SD) to calculate the mean difference (MD) with corresponding 95% CIs. We expect quality of life scores, duration of hospital stay and haematological secondary outcomes to be reported as continuous data. If quality of life scales use different continuous scales, we plan to calculate the standardised MD (SMD) and its associated 95% CIs.

    Dichotomous data

    Outcomes were reported as risk ratios (RR) with their associated 95% CIs.

    Time‐to‐event data

    For future updates of the review, If outcomes are reported as time‐to‐event data we will report hazard ratios (HR) and 95% CIs.

    Unit of analysis issues

    For future updates of the review, If we find studies with multiple treatment groups, we will ensure that a participant group was not represented more than once in the same analysis. Our planned method will be to combine all experimental intervention groups of the study into a single group, and to combine all relevant control intervention groups into a single control group, so allowing a two‐group comparison in the usual way.

    Cross‐over studies were not eligible, as the intervention is irreversible. We did not expect to, and did not find any cluster randomised studies; however, if we do identify any for future updates of this review, and they report data adjusted for design effect, we plan to use this adjusted information. In cases where the effect estimates are not adjusted, we plan to extract the intra‐cluster correlation co‐efficient provided in the study report, or contact the authors for the information and adjust the effect estimate for the design effect, as described in chapter 16 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011b).

    Dealing with missing data

    We extracted data for the number of people randomised and the number analysed for each outcome. For future updates of the review, if more studies are included, if possible, we plan to calculate the percentage change and designate it as loss to follow‐up and assess the implication of the same in risk of bias due to attrition. In cases where information is missing or unclear (outcome data, summary data and missing individual data), we plan to contact the primary investigator(s).

    In the presence of missing data, we planned to perform selected sensitivity analyses to assess the influence of the missing information. For dichotomous outcomes, we would use per protocol analysis on the data. For outcomes with continuous data and missing SDs, we planned to either calculate these from other available data such as standard errors (SE), or to impute them when possible. We planned to impute SDs on the basis of SDs for the same outcome using the same scale, from within our review or from other similar studies, reviews or meta‐analyses, as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011b).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    Heterogeneity could be due either to differences in participant or study characteristics, or due to random error. For future updates of the review, if we include more studies, we plan to visually examine forest plots for overlapping CIs and use the Chi² test for homogeneity and the I² test for heterogeneity, to estimate the contribution of true heterogeneity. We consider the Chi² statistic to represent significant heterogeneity at a level of 10% significance (P value < 0.1). We will use an I² statistic value of less than 40% (may represent mild heterogeneity which can be ignored), 30% to 60% (may represent moderate heterogeneity) and 50% to 90% (may represent significant heterogeneity). We plan to explore moderate or significant heterogeneity using subgroups. We consider a I² value of 75% or greater as substantial heterogeneity, and should we identify this, we plan not to undertake meta‐analysis.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    For future updates of the review, if we include more studies, we plan to assess for publication bias using funnel plots. If funnel plots show asymmetry, we will consider publication bias as a possible reason for this (Higgins 2011c).

    Data synthesis

    For future updates of the review, if more studies are included, we plan to pool results only when there is no evidence of clinical or substantial statistical heterogeneity (I² less than 75%). If moderate heterogeneity is present, not explained by sub grouping, we plan to analyse the studies using the random‐effects model. We plan to group outcome data in a range of follow‐up periods (zero to three months, over three months to six months, over six months to two years, over over years) and perform a stratified meta‐analysis. If outcome data do not permit such grouping, we will pool end‐point data.

    We were unable to quantitatively synthesise data as there was only one included study. We have presented a descriptive, qualitative critical appraisal of the information.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    For future updates of the review, if more studies are included, we plan to undertake subgroup analyses for the following study characteristics:

    • age of participants at splenectomy (under five years, five years to 15 years and over 15 years);

    • duration of treatment with blood transfusion therapy (less than five years, five to 10 years and over 10 years);

    • mode of splenectomy (open laparotomy, laparoscopic or radio ablation);

    • blood iron levels (high (greater than 2500 μg/L), low (less than 2500 μg/L)).

    Sensitivity analysis

    For future updates of the review, if more studies are included, we plan to analyse the robustness of assumptions and decisions made during the review process by performing sensitivity analyses with regard to loss to follow‐up and for studies with missing outcome data. For studies with loss to follow‐up greater than 10%, we plan to examine the change in effect estimates by assuming worse‐case and best‐case outcomes among those lost to follow‐up. We plan to examine the effect of data imputation by removing such studies from the meta‐analysis and observing the change in the pooled estimate.

    Summary of findings tables

    As a post hoc analysis, we aimed to report on all seven outcomes in the summary of findings table. For clarity in the tables, we aimed to present haematological indices, not according to the sub‐domains as presented in Effects of interventions; but instead we aimed to insert a general statement about the summary of findings for these outcomes, with the evidence to be graded based on all of the sub‐domains combined; however, the included study did not report on any of these sub‐domains.

    We determined the quality of the evidence using the GRADE approach; and downgraded evidence in the presence of a high risk of bias in at least one study, indirectness of the evidence, unexplained heterogeneity or inconsistency, imprecision of results, high probability of publication bias. We downgraded the evidence by one level if we considered the limitation to be serious and by two levels if very serious.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    We identified 349 records, after the removal of duplicates we screened 276 for possible inclusion. We screened the records using the titles and abstracts and were able to exclude 275 records. Only one full‐text study was assessed for eligibility and this was included in the review (Konstadoulakis 2006). We were unable to find any further suitable studies for inclusion through contacting authors. The study flow diagram is represented in Figure 1.

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 1

    Study flow diagram.

    Included studies

    The Konstadoulakis study was a randomised controlled study comparing laparoscopic splenectomy to open splenectomy (Konstadoulakis 2006). The study recruited 28 participants with β‐thalassaemia major undergoing elective splenectomy over a period of 3.5 years (September 1999 to March 2003). Participants had been vaccinated against encapsulated bacteria (pneumococcus, Hemophilus influenza, and meningococcus). Cholecystectomy was routinely performed in all participants. The study measured operation time, splenectomy complications, required transfusions (post‐ and pre‐operative), and length of hospital stay. Further details of the study are provided in the Characteristics of included studies section.

    Excluded studies

    After the initial assessment of the identified references, no further studies were considered for inclusion or were relevant for the excluded studies section (Figure 1).

    Risk of bias in included studies

    The risk of bias judgements are represented in a figure (Figure 2).

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 2

    Risk of bias summary: review authors' judgements about each risk of bias item for each included study.

    Allocation

    The included study consecutively allocated participants into the laparoscopic and open surgery groups by using a random number table (Konstadoulakis 2006). While the procedure for random sequence generation is appropriate (assessed as 'low risk'), the participants were allocated consecutively (allocation concealment assessed as 'unclear risk').

    Blinding

    The included study appears to be open label; however, it is not explicitly stated (Konstadoulakis 2006). It would not have been possible to blind surgeons and participants, however, investigators and outcome assessors could have been blinded. The lack of blinding may have caused performance and detection bias in outcomes like duration of hospital stay. We have therefore assessed this domain as having an 'unclear' risk of bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Three out of 14 participants in the laparoscopic splenectomy group had intra‐operative bleeding and the procedure had to be converted to open splenectomy (Konstadoulakis 2006). This constitutes a dropout of 21.4%. However, this is likely to be an unpredictable event and was judged to be missing at random. We have assessed this as having a 'unclear' risk of bias.

    Selective reporting

    The included study did not report on the majority of the important outcomes that we sought to assess in the review and follow‐up was only to the time of discharge (Konstadoulakis 2006). We did not have access to the study protocol which precluded us from knowing whether the follow‐up of participants for a longer period was planned. We have therefore assessed this domain as having an 'unclear' risk of bias.

    Other potential sources of bias

    In the included study, it is unclear how the additional procedure of cholecystectomy was performed in the two groups (Konstadoulakis 2006). If they used different procedures in different groups it may have implication on the final outcomes. We have therefore assessed this as having an 'unclear' risk of bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings for the main comparison

    The quality of the evidence has been graded for those outcomes included in the summary of findings table. For the definitions of these gradings, please refer to the summary of findings tables (summary of findings Table for the main comparison).

    Primary outcomes

    1. Frequency of transfusion (post splenectomy or discharge)

    The included study did not report this outcome, the study ends at the point of discharge (Konstadoulakis 2006).

    2. Serious adverse events

    The included study described two types of serious adverse events, that of post‐operative bleeding and atelectasis (Konstadoulakis 2006). A total of three events were reported post‐operatively (one case of atelectasis and two cases of bleeding) in the laparoscopic splenectomy group, compared to two events of atelectasis in the open laparoscopic group; however, there were no significant differences between the groups for either atelectasis, RR 0.50 (95% CI 0.05 to 4.90) or for bleeding, RR 5.00 (95% CI 0.26 to 95.61) (very low‐quality evidence) (Analysis 1.1). In addition to these, the study also reported three serious cases of intra‐operative bleeding in the laparoscopic group which mandated conversion to open splenectomy, although the difference between groups was not statistically significant, RR 7.00 (95% CI 0.39 to 124.14) (very low‐quality evidence) (Analysis 1.2) (Konstadoulakis 2006). These effect estimates are based on very small numbers and hence are unreliable and imprecise.

    3. Quality of life

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Non‐serious adverse events

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    2. Haemoglobin indices
    a. Haemoglobin levels

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    b. Red blood cell counts

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    c. Reticulocyte count

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    d. WBC counts

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    e. Platelet counts

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    3. Duration of hospital stay

    The study only reported median post‐operative stay. For laparoscopic splenectomy it was reported as 5.0 days +/‐ 2.4 days and for open laparotomy it was 6.5 days +/‐ 1.2 days (P = 0.035, Mann‐Whitney U test) (Konstadoulakis 2006). It is not clear in the published paper whether SDs or inter‐quartile ranges are reported with the median figures.

    4. Serum ferritin

    The included study did not report on this outcome.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    In this review we hoped to evaluate the efficacy and safety of splenectomy, comparing different splenectomy techniques, and also comparing splenectomy to other treatment options (such as transfusion therapy or iron chelation). However, we were able to find only one randomised study comparing two surgical methods of splenectomy in people with thalassaemia. This study was piloting the feasibility of laparoscopy as a surgical approach to remove the spleen in comparison to open surgery. It provided some information in relation to benefits in terms of operating time and harms (bleeding and atelectasis). However, this systematic review was unable to comment on most of the primary or secondary outcomes that we sought to evaluate.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The review was unable to find good quality evidence, in the form of randomised controlled studies, regarding the efficacy of splenectomy as a method to treat β‐thalassaemia major or intermedia. The single study included in this review provided little information about the efficacy of splenectomy, and compared open surgery and laparoscopic methods (Konstadoulakis 2006). It included only a small number of participants and aimed to demonstrate the feasibility of laparoscopic splenectomy. These participants were followed up for a very short duration and for a limited number of outcomes.

    Despite the lack of evidence, splenectomy is generally considered as an acceptable option in people with TDT and NTDT. Future studies comparing the effect of splenectomy with other advanced modalities are needed. However, given that splenectomy is already a widely accepted treatment modality in people with thalassaemia, some benefits and harms of splenectomy may eventually be evaluated through appropriately designed prospective cohort studies.

    Quality of the evidence

    In relation to the only primary outcome (serious adverse events) for which we could included data for in this review, we found the evidence included to be to be very low‐quality (summary of findings Table for the main comparison).

    The included study reported more intra‐ and post‐operative bleeding in the laparoscopic splenectomy group and suggested that it could be a result of surgical error. Due to the low number of events in the group, the stability of this adverse outcome rate is low ‐ larger numbers of participants would be required to increase the precision of reported adverse‐event rates. There were other considerations (operating time, intra‐operative transfusion requirement, cardiac function) that needed to be considered while making a decision on which procedure is better for the individual. This might suggest that that laparoscopic splenectomy could be an option worth considering provided the surgeon has adequate expertise in the procedure. Currently, the rationale for performing a splenectomy, and surgical technique used must be based on clinical judgement.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We have conducted an extensive search of electronically available databases for potential studies to include. However, we have not searched for unpublished trials apart from those included in CENTRAL. Although unlikely, we may have missed including evidence from such trials and may constitute a bias in the review process.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    From this review, we conclude that there is limited evidence for performing splenectomy in people with thalassaemia from randomised controlled studies. However, several observational studies have explored the effect of splenectomy in people with thalassaemia. Splenectomy is required in people with thalassaemia when their disease is not sufficiently controlled by blood transfusions alone (Piga 2011). Studies have observed that the requirement of packed cells is reduced considerably following splenectomy (Al‐Salem 2016), and that iron overload and serum ferritin levels also reduce after splenectomy (Casale 2013). However, the benefits of splenectomy may not be long‐lasting, necessitating long‐term studies to prove the efficacy (Modell 1977). Peri‐operative compilations such as bleeding, atelectasis and subphrenic abscess and post‐operative complications such as thrombocytosis, sepsis may also outweigh any benefits of splenectomy. Splenectomy may reduce immune function on the long term and increase the risk of infection with encapsulated organisms such as Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis, gram negative bacilli such as Escherichia coli, Klebsiella and Pseudomonas aeruginosa or parasites such as malaria. This may in turn necessitate long‐term chemoprophylaxis with antibiotics and immunoprophylaxis with vaccines (Cappellini 2008).

    There is growing evidence that complications associated with thalassaemia, such as pulmonary hypertension and cardiac dysfunction, are not reversed even after splenectomy (Aessopos 2005;Morsy 2008). This strengthens the argument that regular transfusion therapy, coupled with an effective chelation regimen, can alter the disease process and prevent the development of several complications of thalassaemia which previously necessitated splenectomy. Over the years, a progressive awareness and knowledge of the adverse effects of splenectomy has resulted in a gradual decline in the tendency to perform this procedure in people with thalassaemia. Data from an Italian registry has shown that the probability of undergoing splenectomy within the first decade of life has declined significantly in the last 40 years (Piga 2011).

    Alternative approaches are partial surgical splenectomy (de Montalembert 1990) and splenectomy by embolisation (Pringle 1982). Laparoscopic splenectomy seems to be associated with more complications due to the sheer technical difficulty of the procedure. When employing laparoscopic techniques, experience of the surgeon and co‐morbidities such as cholecystitis, may affect individual outcomes (Iolascon 2017, Konstadoulakis 2006; Laopodis 1998).
