Progestin intrauterine devices versus copper intrauterine devices for emergency contraception

    • Background

    The copper intrauterine device (Cu‐IUD) is a highly effective method of contraception that can also be used for emergency contraception (EC). It is the most effective form of EC, and is more effective than other existing oral regimens also used for EC. The Cu‐IUD provides the unique benefit of providing ongoing contraception after it is inserted for EC; however, uptake of this intervention has been limited. Progestin IUDs are a popular method of long‐acting, reversible contraception. If these devices were also found to be effective for EC, they would provide a critical additional option for women. These IUDs could not only provide EC and ongoing contraception, but additional non‐contraceptive benefits, including a reduction in menstrual bleeding, cancer prevention, and pain management.

    Objectives

    To examine the safety and effectiveness of progestin‐containing IUDs for emergency contraception, compared with copper‐containing IUDs, or compared with dedicated oral hormonal methods.

    Search methods

    We considered all randomized controlled trials and non‐randomized studies of interventions that compared outcomes for individuals seeking a levonorgestrel IUD (LNG‐IUD) for EC to a Cu‐IUD or dedicated oral EC method. We considered full‐text studies, conference abstracts, and unpublished data. We considered studies irrespective of their publication status and language of publication.

    Selection criteria

    We included studies comparing progestin IUDs with copper‐containing IUDs, or oral EC methods for emergency contraception.

    Data collection and analysis

    We systematically searched nine medical databases, two trials registries, and one gray literature site. We downloaded all titles and abstracts retrieved by electronic searching to a reference management database, and removed duplicates. Three review authors independently screened titles, abstracts, and full‐text reports to determine studies eligible for inclusion. We followed standard Cochrane methodology to assess risk of bias, and analyze and interpret the data. We used GRADE methodology to assess the certainty of the evidence.

    Main results

    We included only one relevant study (711 women); a randomized, controlled, non‐inferiority trial comparing LNG‐IUDs to Cu‐IUDs for EC, with a one‐month follow‐up. With one study, the evidence was very uncertain for the difference in pregnancy rates, failed insertion rates, expulsion rates, removal rates and the difference in the acceptability of the IUDs. There was also uncertain evidence suggesting the Cu‐IUD may slightly increase rates of cramping and the LNG‐IUD may slightly increase bleeding and spotting days. 

    Authors' conclusions

    This review is limited in its ability to provide definitive evidence regarding the LNG‐IUD’s equivalence, superiority, or inferiority to the Cu‐IUD for EC. Only one study was identified in the review, which had possible risks of bias related to randomization and rare outcomes.

    Additional studies are needed to provide definitive evidence related to the effectiveness of the LNG‐IUD for EC.

    • Progestin intrauterine devices versus copper intrauterine devices for emergency contraception

    Why is this review important?

    This review is important as there are few available choices for emergency contraception (EC). Copper intrauterine devices (Cu‐IUDs) are the most effective method for EC currently available, but their use and uptake is low. We examined the literature to see if the levonorgestrel IUD (LNG‐IUD) was as effective as other methods for EC. If it were found to be as effective, it would increase the range of options available to women seeking EC.

    How did we identify and evaluate the evidence?

    We searched nine medical research databases and two trials registries for randomized controlled trials, and one website for gray literature (i.e. research produced by organizations outside the traditional commercial and academic publishing and distribution channels).

    We considered randomized and non‐randomized studies comparing progestin‐containing IUDs with copper‐containing IUDs, or oral EC methods for emergency contraception. 

    We screened all abstracts identified in our search, and the full text of potential reports. We assessed the risk of bias and used the GRADE system to evaluate the certainty of the evidence.

    What did we find?

    We identified 2828 records. After removing duplicates and applying inclusion criteria, we identified one randomized controlled study that met our criteria. It included 711 women, and compared LNG‐IUDs to Cu‐IUDs for EC. There may be little or no difference in pregnancy rates at one month between LNG‐IUD and Cu‐IUD users, but we are very uncertain about the evidence. Evidence was also very uncertain about whether the risk of a failed insertion was different for the two types of IUDs.

    The evidence is very uncertain about the comparative acceptability of the two types of IUDs for EC. Regarding outcomes related to harms, low‐certainty evidence suggests that the copper IUD may increase cramping, while the LNG‐IUD may increase the number of days with bleeding and spotting.

    What does this mean?

    The current evidence does not provide definitive evidence on the effectiveness of LNG‐IUD for EC. 

    More comparative studies are needed to see if the LNG‐IUD is as effective as the Cu‐IUD for EC.

    How up to date is this review?

    The evidence is current to July 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    We identified one RCT that explored the utility of a progestin‐containing IUD for EC. Our review is limited in its ability to provide definitive evidence regarding the LNG‐IUD's equivalence, superiority, or inferiority to the copper IUD for EC, as the primary outcome is rare (pregnancy), and only one study of moderate size exists with possible risk of bias related to randomization and rare outcomes. The practical design of the study, including a real‐world population presenting for EC where some individuals may or may not be at highest risk for pregnancy, does provide some reassurance regarding 'quick starts' for LNG IUDs. Specifically, placing LNG‐IUDs at varying points in the cycle, even after unprotected intercourse, is unlikely to lead to pregnancy, as shown in prior studies such as Boraas 2021. For individuals at very high risk of pregnancy (i.e. those having unprotected intercourse within the fertile window/multiple times per cycle without recent exposure to a contraceptive method), the evidence is insufficient to support the LNG‐IUD as an option for EC. A person could receive an LNG‐IUD in this setting if their values and preferences are such that utility of having their preferred highly effective long‐acting method outweighs the unknown risk of immediate pregnancy. For those who are more risk‐averse and more concerned with possible pregnancy, healthcare providers should offer a copper IUD, oral EC alone, or oral EC combined with LNG‐IUD. The evidence identified in this review related to comparative rates of failed insertions between the LNG‐IUD and the Cu‐IUD was also unclear.

    The limited evidence identified in this review suggests that bleeding and spotting days are slightly more common with the LNG‐IUD within one month, and cramping is slightly more common with the Cu‐IUD. These data are consistent with prior studies related to IUD side effects (Andersson 1994; Diedrich 2015; Kaneshiro 2010; Lara‐Torre 2011). Patient preferences for bleeding irregularities versus cramping and pain can be critical regarding the decision to use an IUD, and in the context of EC, these trade‐offs can be very impactful. Side effect profiles likely also influence satisfaction. The evidence in this review was uncertain, but it did not suggest a difference in user satisfaction between those using LNG‐IUDs and Cu‐IUDs, although Cu‐IUD users may be slightly more polarized at the extremes of satisfaction. Together, the different side effect profile and potential for variation in method satisfaction underscore the critical need for providing individuals with a range of options for EC. Ideally, they should be offered a highly effective and acceptable method that meets all their current and long‐term reproductive health needs.

    Implications for research

    The copper IUD works for emergency contraception by stopping the swimming mechanism of sperm and preventing sperm from reaching an egg. In addition, copper creates an inflammatory environment further reducing endometrial receptivity. It is also likely that the LNG‐IUD creates a similar inflammatory milieu (Levrier 1982; Sheppard 1987), but it is unclear what the true effect of a progestin‐containing IUD on the uterine environment, the sperm or the egg in the immediate time period after insertion might be. The ovulatory effect of progestin has also been considered a potential mechanism for EC, but doses within the first few days of insertion are likely to be too low to sufficiently suppress ovulation. Nilsson and colleagues analyzed hysterectomy samples from women who had an LNG IUD inserted before hysterectomy and found endometrium levels to be high, but these LNG‐IUDs were all inserted 39 to 46 days before the procedure (Nilsson 1982).

    Understanding a plausible mechanism is a critical step in further exploring the possibility of an LNG‐IUD for EC. In addition, future EC studies exploring this and other methods should focus on those who have unprotected intercourse multiple times in the same cycle as EC. These are often the most at‐risk individuals and understanding their pregnancy rates at three and six months after IUD insertion would provide a broader understanding of the true impact of IUDs on pregnancy risk, and the implications of using a longer‐term device and method to prevent unintended pregnancy.

    The design of this study and the rare event of the primary outcome of pregnancy highlights a fundamental question and challenge with all EC trials — how do we build a rigorous evidence base for EC with an outcome that is always going to have a low event rate (i.e. pregnancy)? Even with meta‐analysis, event rates are likely to remain low. A very large trial with thousands of people may be a solution, but it will never be practical. In addition, those seeking EC are often not truly at risk of pregnancy, so the likelihood of the event occurring will always be low.

    The best way to obtain data related to LNG‐IUD as EC may be to leverage surveillance data and assess pregnancy rates after IUD insertion. Future reviews may want to broaden the inclusion criteria and incorporate this type of data, as this may be the most helpful and practical way to assess the true utility of a versatile, long‐acting method like the LNG‐IUD for EC.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Unintended pregnancy continues to have a significant global impact on health outcomes and socioeconomic progress. The global unintended pregnancy rate has declined in the last twenty years, but remains around 6.4% (Bearak 2020). An estimated 89 million unintended pregnancies occur each year in low‐ and middle‐income countries (Larson 2020). Emergency contraception (EC) is a critical tool to prevent unintended pregnancy if an individual is not routinely using a contraceptive method, does not have access to reliable contraceptive methods, has a regular contraceptive method that fails, or is a victim of sexual violence.

    Description of the intervention

    The copper intrauterine device (Cu‐IUD) is a highly effective method of contraception that can also be used for EC when inserted within five days of expected ovulation. The Cu‐IUD has been demonstrated to be the most effective form of EC, with failure rates around 0.09% (Cleland 2012; Goldstuck 2019). Copper inhibits sperm function and motility, which prevents fertilization from occurring. Progestin‐based oral regimens are another EC option and work by providing high serum levels of progestin, which inhibit or delay ovulation. These EC methods also have relatively low failure rates; around 2% to 3% for levonorgestrel(LNG) pills. Ulipristal acetate (UPA), which blocks progesterone receptor‐dependent pathways leading to ovulation inhibition, also has low failure rates; approximately 1.4% (Goldstuck 2019). The product labels for EC pills indicate use within three days for LNG, and five days for UPA, but both pill formulations are more likely to be effective when taken as soon as possible after unprotected intercourse. UPA has a longer window of efficacy because it works closer to the time of ovulation. For women weighing more than165 pounds, LNG EC may have reduced efficacy. UPA appears to be effective for women weighing up to 196 pounds (FSRH 2017).

    The Cu‐IUD provides a unique benefit as EC, as it can be left in place to provide ongoing contraception for up to 12 years. In addition, there is no impact of the individual's weight on the Cu‐IUD’s efficacy as EC. Despite its benefits and advantages, Cu‐IUD’s uptake for EC is limited. Studies have shown that fewer than 15% of clinicians offer the Cu‐IUD as an option when individuals present for EC (Batur 2016; Harper 2012). Those seeking care may be unfamiliar with Cu‐IUDs as an EC option, so are hesitant to use them (Schwarz 2009). Other barriers to use are device costs and the need for in‐office visits (Schwarz 2009).

    Progestin IUDs (currently available only as LNG‐IUDs) are a popular method of long‐acting reversible contraception. They are highly effective and have additional benefits beyond contraception. LNG‐IUDs have been used for medical management of a broad array of conditions, including chronic pain, endometriosis, and endometrial cancer prevention. No LNG‐IUDs are approved for use as EC. These devices work to thicken cervical mucus and thin the endometrial lining, creating a hostile environment for sperm, and thereby, preventing fertilization. LNG‐IUDs containing 52 mg levonorgestrel can be used safely and effectively for up to eight years (Rowe 2016).

    How the intervention might work

    It is not known whether the levonorgestrel IUD (LNG‐IUD) is as safe or effective for EC as the Cu‐IUD. Inert IUDs appear to incite enough of an inflammatory process to prevent fertilization through phagocytosis and destruction of both oocytes and sperm (Moyer 1971; Sivin 1989). This mechanism, paired with a large burst of local progestin, may enable LNG‐IUDs to function as EC. If found effective for EC, LNG‐IUDs may also provide the benefit of providing ongoing contraception, if left in place.

    Concern has been raised that the progestin IUD may not be as effective as the Cu‐IUD. The progestin ‘burst’ with IUD placement may not be similar enough to the effect of taking an LNG‐based oral EC. The placement of a progestin‐containing IUD may also increase the risk of an ectopic pregnancy if fertilization were to occur, as this is a potential consequence of the effect of local progestin on tubal motility (Speroff 2019).

    Why it is important to do this review

    This review was important because there are currently few available choices for EC. EC pills have limited efficacy, particularly for some individuals with obesity (Glasier 2011), those who delay taking EC, or those who have several episodes of unprotected sex prior to taking EC. Cu‐IUDs represent the most effective EC method currently available, but their use and uptake is low. We reviewed the evidence to see if the LNG‐IUD was as effective as other EC methods. Had we found them to be as effective, this would increase the range of options available to individuals seeking EC. IUDs for EC also provide an ongoing method of contraception; in particular, LNG‐IUDs provide added benefits for the management of heavy bleeding, pelvic pain, and menstrual‐related pain.

    Objectives

    To examine the safety and effectiveness of progestin‐containing intrauterine devices for emergency contraception, compared with copper‐containing IUDs, or compared with dedicated oral hormonal methods.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered all randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and non‐randomized studies of interventions that compared outcomes for individuals seeking a levonorgestrel IUD (LNG‐IUD) for EC to a Cu‐IUD or dedicated oral EC method. RCTs represent the most rigorous type of study to address questions of efficacy and safety, but because the key effectiveness outcome for this topic is rare – pregnancy following an instance of unprotected intercourse – adequately powered trials are unlikely to be feasible or undertaken. For this reason, we also considered nonrandomized studies of interventions (NRSIs) for this review. We did not expect to find enough trial evidence to address the review objectives, and NRSI were likely to provide the best available data for testing comparisons between oral hormonal methods and the novel intervention evaluated in this review, i.e. progestin IUDs for EC. We considered NRSIs with study designs that compared interventions used as EC, including prospective or retrospective cohort studies that were designed with concurrent parallel, nested case‐control, geographic/site, or time (e.g. before‐after) study groups.

    We considered full‐text studies, conference abstracts, and unpublished data for the review. We included studies irrespective of their publication status and language of publication.

    Types of participants

    Studies included any individuals capable of becoming pregnant who were seeking EC after unprotected intercourse.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies comparing progestin IUDs with copper‐containing IUDs, or oral EC methods for emergency contraception.

    The specific comparisons for this review were:

    • LNG‐IUD versus Cu‐IUD

    • LNG‐IUD versus any dedicated and effective oral EC method (i.e. ulipristal acetate or levonorgestrel)

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    We identified two key domains related to primary outcomes: safety and effectiveness. 

    We planned to capture effectiveness by pregnancy rates, specifically the number of pregnancies divided by the total number of individuals receiving LNG‐IUDs when presenting for EC, compared with the same pregnancy rate in the Cu‐IUD or oral EC comparison group. 

    We measured safety by rates of failed insertions.

    Secondary outcomes

    Secondary outcomes focused on other safety dimensions and acceptability. We sought to capture outcomes related to safety with:

    • rates of ectopic pregnancy;

    • rates of infection after placement;

    • rates of placements complicated by perforation;

    • rates of expulsion.

    Acceptability outcomes included:

    • removal and continuation rates;

    • frequency of bleeding leading to removal;

    • frequency of cramping leading to removal;

    • patient satisfaction scores.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The Fertility Regulation Group Information Specialist conducted a search for all published, unpublished, and ongoing studies, without restrictions on language or publication status. Database search strategies are available in Appendix 1. The latest updated search was conducted in July 2022. We considered adverse effects described in included studies only.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases from their inception dates:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via EBM Reviews (Ovid; 2022 Issue 5);

    • MEDLINE ALL Ovid (inception to July 2022);

    • Embase.com (inception to July 2022);

    • Global Health Ovid (1973 to July 2022);

    • LILACS (Latin American and Caribbean Health Science Information database;lilacs.bvsalud.org/en/; inception to July 2022);

    • China National Knowledge Infrastructure (中国学术期刊网专题全文数据库, CNKI);

    • WANFANG DATA of China Online Journals (万方数据知识平台, WANFANG);

    • Chongqing VIP Information (维普资讯中文期刊服务平台, VIP);

    • Chinese Biomedical Literature Database (中国生物医学文献服务系统, SinoMed).

    We searched the following trials registries:

    • US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov);

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP; please note: due to the COVID‐19 pandemic, we were unable to search the WHO ICTRP while this systematic review was in progress; we used WHO ICTRP records accessible via CENTRAL instead.)

    We searched the following gray literature sites:

    • Scopus (1970 to present) (conference abstracts only)

    Searching other resources

    We checked the bibliographies of included studies and any relevant systematic reviews identified during the electronic searches for further references to relevant studies.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    We downloaded all titles and abstracts retrieved by electronic searching to a reference management database, and removed duplicates. Three review authors (YC, NG, and SR) independently screened titles and abstracts for inclusion. These authors independently screened the full‐text reports of studies deemed potentially eligible, identified studies for inclusion, and identified and recorded reasons for exclusion of ineligible studies.

    Data extraction and management

    We used a standard data collection form for study characteristics and outcome data. Two review authors (SR and NG) independently extracted study characteristics from the included studies.

    • Methods: study design, number of study centers and location, study setting, withdrawals, date of study, follow‐up

    • Participants: number, mean age, age range, gender, severity of condition, diagnostic criteria, inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, other relevant characteristics

    • Interventions: intervention components, comparison, fidelity assessment

    • Outcomes: main and other outcomes specified and collected, time points reported

    • Handling of missing data, statistical adjustments for study design features and potential confounders

    • Notes: funding for trial, notable conflicts of interest of trial authors, ethical approval

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (SR and NG) independently assessed risk of bias using the criteria outlined for the Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB 2) in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions, Section 8.2 (Higgins 2020).

    We assessed risk of bias according to the following domains.

    • Bias arising from the randomization process

    • Bias due to deviations from intended interventions

    • Bias due to missing outcome data

    • Bias in measurement of the outcome

    • Bias in selection of the reported result

    We judged each potential source of bias as low risk of bias, some concerns, or high risk of bias, along with a justification for our judgment. We did not exclude studies on the grounds of their risk of bias, but reported the risk of bias when presenting the results of the studies. When considering treatment effects, we took into account the risk of bias for the studies that contribute to that outcome. We conducted the review according to the published protocol (Cleland 2020).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We planned to estimate effects with mean differences (MDs) for continuous data, together with the 95% confidence interval (CI). We planned to explore sensitivity analysis based on rates of loss to follow‐up.

    Unit of analysis issues

    For this review, we expected the unit of analysis to be the individual. 

    If any trials had multiple arms that were compared against the same control condition that would be included in the same meta‐analysis, we planned to either combine groups to create a single pair‐wise comparison, or select one pair of interventions and exclude the others. If studies with multiple intervention arms were included, we planned to analyze the data by combining groups to create a single pair‐wise comparison. If combining the groups was not clinically reasonable, we planned to select the intervention and comparator that most closely met our inclusion criteria and exclude the others in accordance with recommendations outlined in Section 23.3 of the Cochrane Handbook of Systematic Reviews of Intervention (Higgins 2020). 

    Dealing with missing data

    We planned to carry out the analysis according to the intention‐to‐treat (ITT) principle for studies in which information related to missing data was not available. When we made any assumptions about missing data, we planned to report the potential impact in the Discussion.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to describe the included studies and their key design features, study populations, treatments, and outcomes to communicate the clinical diversity of the included studies. We planned to report the I2 and Tau2 estimates as measures of statistical heterogeneity. Our interpretation of heterogeneity was planned to be based on the effect magnitudes, relative and overall study sizes, and features of the evidence.

    The decision to conduct meta‐analyses was to be based on the clinical diversity and statistical heterogeneity of the included studies. With very few studies (e.g. fewer than 10), the estimation of statistical heterogeneity is unreliable and the decision to pool studies would be based on study comparability (e.g. study design, outcomes, populations) and the magnitude, direction, and consistency of effects. In other words, we planned not to pool results of very few studies if the effects were highly variable. If a larger number of studies was available (10 or more), the decision to pool would be more strongly informed by the statistical heterogeneity reflected in the I2 estimate. In general, we planned to present pooled estimates if statistical heterogeneity was not substantial or considerable (i.e. I2 of 60% or less). When there was considerable statistical heterogeneity (i.e. I2 of 75% to 100%), we planned not to present an overall pooled estimate but rather to provide descriptive forest plots and explore potential sources of heterogeneity in prespecified stratified analyses with subgroup comparisons. 

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to attempt to contact study authors, asking them to provide missing outcome data. When this was not possible, and the missing data were thought to introduce serious bias, we planned to explore the impact of including such studies in the overall assessment of results, with a sensitivity analysis. If we were able to pool more than 10 trials for each outcome, we planned to create and examine a funnel plot to explore possible publication biases, interpreting the results with caution (Sterne 2011).

    Data synthesis

    We planned to undertake a meta‐analysis to estimate pooled effects when comparable data were reported that could support statistical pooling. 

    The decision to conduct meta‐analyses was based on the clinical diversity and statistical heterogeneity of the included studies. With very few studies (e.g. fewer than 10), the estimation of statistical heterogeneity is unreliable and the decision to pool studies would be based on their qualitative comparability (e.g. study design, outcomes, populations) and the magnitude and direction of effects. If a larger number of studies was available (10 or more), the decision to pool would be informed by the statistical heterogeneity reflected in the I2 estimate. In general, we planned to present pooled estimates if statistical heterogeneity was not greater than moderate (I2 of 60% or less). When there was considerable statistical heterogeneity (75% to 100%) we planned not to present an overall pooled estimate but rather to present descriptive forest plots and explore potential sources of heterogeneity in prespecified subgroup comparisons. We planned to explore sources of heterogeneity when substantial heterogeneity was present (over 50%; Higgins 2020).

    We planned to use a random‐effects model, using the DerSimonian and Laird technique. However, if there was little statistical heterogeneity and clinical diversity, we planned to use a fixed‐effect model with the Mantel‐Hanzel approach, especially if there was evidence of potential variation in outcome effects by study size (i.e. small‐study effects). We planned to take additional precautions in the selection of the meta‐analytic approach and interpretation of results when analyzing rare outcomes or studies with no events in one or both study arms (Higgins 2020).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to conduct subgroup analyses for the following outcomes.

    • Pregnancy rates

    • Safety outcomes (rates of infection, perforation or expulsion)

    We planned to carry out the following subgroup analyses.

    • Days from unprotected intercourse: we planned to compare studies that had more than or equal to 50% of participants who used EC more than five days following unprotected intercourse with studies having fewer than 50% of participants using EC more than five days following unprotected intercourse.

    • BMI: we planned to compare studies that had more than or equal to 30% of participants with BMI > 30 with studies having fewer than 30% of participants with high BMI.

    We would conduct statistical tests for interactions to evaluate potential subgroup differences in outcomes, according to the factors described above.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to perform the following sensitivity analyses to assess the robustness of our conclusions and explore their impact on effect sizes, as feasible.

    • Restricting the analysis to published studies

    • Restricting the analysis to studies with a low risk of bias

    • Restricting the analysis to studies that had less than 20% loss to follow‐up

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a summary of findings table for the main intervention comparisons and planned to include all important outcomes, including pregnancy rates, safety outcomes and acceptability outcomes. The outcomes in the final summary of findings table did not include most of the safety outcomes initially planned; these differences are detailed in the Differences between protocol and review section. Two review authors (SR and NG) independently assessed the certainty of the evidence (high, moderate, low, and very low) using the five GRADE considerations (risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias [Guyatt 2008]). We used methods and recommendations described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2020), and the Cochrane Effective Practice and Organisation of Care (EPOC) worksheets(EPOC 2013). We used GRADEpro software to create our summary of findings tables (GRADEpro GDT). We planned to use plain language statements to report these findings in the review (EPOC 2013).

    Because we were unable to summarize the data obtained in our review using meta‐analysis, we conducted a narrative synthesis, summarizing the results and important strengths and limitations of the evidence. The values reported in the summary of findings table incorporate unadjusted values while the narrative synthesis also describes adjusted values.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    We identified 2828 reports, and reviewed titles and abstracts. We excluded 2827 reports as they involved the wrong intervention. We reviewed and included one full‐text study; see Figure 1.

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 1

    PRISM flow chart for study selection.

    Included studies

    We identified only one study that met our inclusion criteria. Turok 2021 was a randomized, controlled, non‐inferiority trial (N = 711) comparing a 52 mg levonorgestrel intrauterine device (IUD) to a copper T380A IUD for use as emergency contraception (EC). The study aimed to assess the one‐month pregnancy risk between the two. Secondary outcomes included incidence of IUD discontinuation, participant satisfaction metrics, and IUD‐related pain and bleeding outcomes.

    This study included 18 to 25‐year‐old women, fluent in English or Spanish, who were seen at six Utah Family Planning clinics between August 2016 and December 2019. They were eligible if they presented to the clinic requesting EC after unprotected intercourse within the previous five days; were willing to use an IUD; wanted to prevent pregnancy; had a negative urine pregnancy test, a history of regular menstrual cycles, and a known date of their last menstrual period.

    The study excluded women who: were actively breastfeeding; had vaginal bleeding from an unknown cause; were currently using highly effective methods of contraception (either permanent contraception, an IUD, or contraceptive implant); had an intrauterine infection in the prior three months; had untreated Neisseria Gonorrhea or Chlamydia Trachomatis infection in the previous 30 days; had an allergy to copper; had used oral emergency contraception during the previous five days; or had known uterine anomalies.

    The women were selected from the entire cohort of women who presented to the clinics for EC. Each woman was informed about the trial and the two types of IUDs in the trial. They were advised that those who received an IUD could have it removed at any time, and could switch to another contraceptive method, including another type of IUD. Participants had their initial visit for screening and IUD insertion, and then a one‐month follow‐up visit for repeat urine pregnancy test. A survey was administered at three and six months to assess longer‐term outcomes.

    Excluded studies

    All studies, except Turok 2021, were excluded at the reference level, as they did not involve our specified interventions.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We used the RoB 2 tool to assess the risk of bias for the primary and secondary outcomes in the one included RCT.

    Risk of bias arising from the randomization process

    Turok 2021 described the use of a valid randomization and allocation concealment processes, but risk of bias owing to unbalanced study groups was a concern. The study noted an almost 10% difference in reported use of a contraceptive method at last intercourse. We judged the study as unclear risk of bias from the randomization process.

    Risk of bias due to deviations from the intended intervention

    We identified a low risk of bias from deviations from intended interventions.

    Missing outcome data

    A portion of both the copper and LNG‐IUD groups had missing pregnancy outcome data, which was revealed after the study was unblinded. The distribution of these missing outcomes was relatively equal between the two groups, likely leading to low risk of bias (21 in the copper group and 27 in the LNG group).

    There were also slightly different numbers of participants in each group who withdrew or discontinued the interventions (one withdrew from the copper arm and four discontinued the intervention in the LNG arm). This represents a small difference related to adherence, and overall, we judged these missing outcome data likely contributed a low risk of bias.

    Risk of bias in measurement of outcome

    The primary outcome was measured with a urine pregnancy test at the one‐month follow‐up. Survey data, and clinical chart reviews were used to assess pregnancy and adverse outcomes for the 48 participants with missing data, after the study was unblinded. While there may have been some recall bias, particularly related to the pain and bleeding questions, it is unlikely to contribute a significant risk of bias, therefore, we judged a low risk of measurement bias. 

    Risk of bias in selection of the reported result

    The results were reported as planned and described; we judged a low risk of reporting bias.

    Overall risk of bias

    Overall, with the issues related to randomization and imbalance between baseline characteristics of the two groups, we judged there was low to moderate concern of risk of bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Summary of findings table ‐ Levonorgestrel IUDs vs. Copper IUDs for individuals seeking emergency contraception after unprotected intercourse

    Levonorgestrel IUD versus copper IUD

    We used unadjusted data to develop our summary of findings table and describe unadjusted data in the results below. Turok 2021 reported adjusted data, but overall trends and conclusions were similar using unadjusted data. 

    Primary outcomes
    Pregnancy rates at one month

    One woman became pregnant in the LNG‐IUD group and no pregnancies occurred in the copper‐IUD group, within one month after placement of the IUD. 

    Turok 2021 performed a modified intention‐to‐treat (ITT) analysis, which included all participants receiving an IUD who reported the one‐month pregnancy outcome by any method (urine pregnancy test, survey data or chart review). The between‐group absolute difference in pregnancy rates was 0.3%, but the CI was wide and spanned null (95% CI −0.9 to 1.8; 1 study, 638 women; very low‐certainty evidence). A per protocol analysis was also performed and included only participants who were still using the IUD to which they were randomly assigned when the one‐month pregnancy outcome was reported (616 women). A sensitivity analysis was also performed which included only participants with one‐month pregnancy outcomes confirmed by a urine pregnancy test (590 women). All three analyses provided similar results (0.3% absolute difference, 95% CI −0.9 to 1.8). There may be little or no difference in pregnancy rates at one month between LNG‐IUD and Cu‐IUD users, but the evidence is very uncertain.

    Rates of failed insertions

    Twenty‐one women in the LNG‐IUD group and 20 women in the Cu‐IUD group experienced failed insertions. The estimated ITT risk ratio (RR) of failed insertion among all randomized participants was 1.05 (95% CI 0.58 to 1.90; 1 study, 711 women; very low‐certainty evidence). The evidence is very uncertain about the risk of failed insertion for LNG‐IUDs compared with CuIUDs.

    Secondary outcomes
    Rates of ectopic pregnancy

    The study did not measure this outcome.

    Rates of infection after placement

    The study did not measure this outcome.

    Rates of placements complicated by perforation

    The study did not measure this outcome.

    Rates of expulsion

    There were few reported expulsions in the LNG‐IUD (0.6%) and Cu‐IUD (0.9%) groups. Using study‐reported data, we calculated an RR of 0.67 (95% CI 0.11 to 4.00; 1 study, 655 women; very low‐certainty evidence). The study reported that the unadjusted difference in risk for expulsion was −0.3% (95% CI −1.6 to 1.0). The evidence is very uncertain regarding the effect of LNG‐IUDs versus Cu‐IUDs on expulsions.

    Removal and continuation rates

    The risk of women requesting IUD removal was a key acceptability domain reported in Turok 2021. Removal within one month was infrequent in the LNG‐IUD (3.1%) and Cu‐IUD (2.5%) groups. The RR computed using study reported data was 1.25 (95% CI 0.50 to 3.14; 1 study, 655 women; very low‐certainty evidence). The unadjusted risk difference (RD) reported in Turok 2021 was 0.6% (95% CI −1.9 to 3.1). The study did not report the reasons for removal of the IUDs and did not include continuation data. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of LNG IUDs versus Cu‐IUDs on IUD removal.

    Frequency of bleeding leading to removal

    Turok 2021 did not specifically address bleeding events leading to removal, but a few secondary outcomes focused on the number of bleeding and spotting days in the first month. We used these outcomes as a proxy. The unadjusted mean difference (MD) was 3.5 bleeding days more for the LNG‐IUD group compared with the Cu‐IUD group (95% CI 2.4 to 4.6; 1 study, 510 women; low‐certainty evidence), and 5.2 spotting days more for the LNG‐IUD group compared with the Cu‐IUD group (95% CI 3.7 to 6.4; 1 study, 514 women; low‐certainty evidence). The study did not report how frequently bleeding led to removal of the IUD. The evidence suggests LNG IUDs may increase the number of bleeding days and the number of spotting days in the first month slightly compared to Cu‐IUDs.

    Frequency of cramping leading to removal

    Turok 2021 did not specifically address cramping leading to removal as prespecified in our protocol, but we used general cramping and sharp pain outcomes as a proxy. The investigators assessed cramping from the time of insertion using a visual analog scale ranging from 0 to 100. The difference in unadjusted mean cramping scores was 9.9 points lower for the LNG‐IUD group compared with the Cu‐IUD group (95% CI −15.2 to ‐4.6; 1 study, 547 women; low‐certainty evidence). The evidence suggests LNG‐IUDs may result in slightly lower cramping scores after insertion compared to Cu‐IUDs.

    Patient satisfaction scores

    Turok 2021 measured participant satisfaction in the first month after IUD placement using a five‐point scale that ranged from very unsatisfied to very satisfied. There was no clear difference between the proportion of individuals who reported being very satisfied with their IUD (13.6% in LNG‐IUD group versus 16.3% in Cu‐IUD group; RR 0.84, 95% CI 0.57 to 1.22; 1 study, 615 women; low‐certainty evidence). The unadjusted RD was −2.9% (95% CI −8.4 to 2.6). Turok 2021 also compared the proportion of participants who were very unsatisfied with their IUD (6.5% in the LNG‐IUD group and 9.1% in the Cu‐IUD group) and with other aspects of the study. The unadjusted RDs all had wide CIs that included no difference. The evidence is very uncertain about the likelihood of users being very satisfied when using the LNG IUD versus the Cu‐IUD.

    LNG‐IUD versus any dedicated and effective oral EC method

    We did not identify any studies that examined LNG‐IUD versus any dedicated and effective oral EC method.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    The copper intrauterine device (IUD) is the most effective emergency contraceptive currently available. It has been well demonstrated to be safe and effective and provides ongoing contraception for individuals capable of becoming pregnant. The effectiveness of a progestin‐containing IUD for emergency contraception is unknown, and the evidence is very uncertain for determining whether the risk of failed insertion and other measures affecting the patient experience differs between the two IUD types. Including LNG‐IUDs in the options available to those seeking EC could be beneficial as it would provide them with an effective method for EC, as well as a popular method of contraception for the long term – one that also provides a number of non‐contraceptive benefits, such as improvement of dysmenorrhea or reduction in heavy bleeding. In this review, we assessed the evidence regarding the effectiveness of progestin‐containing IUDs for individuals seeking EC compared to other EC methods.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We identified one study that provided very low‐certainty evidence on the effectiveness of the LNG‐IUD compared with the Cu‐IUD for EC. Secondary outcomes were also supported with both very low‐certainty and low‐certainty evidence.

    The evidence we identified is limited in its applicability. The included study focused on the relevant population, namely those seeking EC within five days of unprotected intercourse, and reported key outcome data with pregnancy rates. However, the lack of longer‐term continuation rates, complication data, and detailed safety information, makes the evidence less complete and therefore less applicable.

    Quality of the evidence

    We applied the GRADE process to assess the certainty of evidence for each outcome of interest. 

    We judged the primary outcome, pregnancy, to be supported by very low‐certainty evidence. The included study was an RCT that recruited a fairly large number of women (N = 711) for a traditional EC trial. We had some concerns related to risk of bias, as the two comparison groups had different reasons for presenting for EC. These differences could have introduced bias into the results, and the imbalance in the groups suggested possible issues with the randomization scheme. The authors adjusted for this key difference in their analysis, but concerns remained about other potentially unmeasured confounders, given the imbalance in an important variable. Given these aggregate issues, we downgraded the evidence one level. The primary outcome of pregnancy was also a rare event, and confidence intervals were wide, suggesting imprecision of the results. We also only identified one study. These elements each led to a downgrade of one level, resulting in very low‐certainty evidence. We applied the same assessments, and therefore, judgments of very low‐certainty to outcomes related to failed insertions, expulsion, removal, and acceptability.

    We rated the other outcomes related to pain and bleeding as low‐certainty evidence, downgrading one level for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We used the standard methods of RoB 2 to assess risk of bias, and GRADE methodology to assess the certainty of the evidence. We used two review authors, who independently completed each of the critical steps in the review process. We are not aware of any biases that may have occurred during this process, but recognize that English and Chinese language papers were the only ones reviewed in detail, and this may have limited our results.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We did not identify any other reviews on this topic.
