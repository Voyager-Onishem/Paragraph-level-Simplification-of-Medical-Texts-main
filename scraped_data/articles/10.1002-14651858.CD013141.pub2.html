Early versus late parenteral nutrition for critically ill term and late preterm infants

    • Background

    Recently conducted randomised controlled trials (RCTs) suggest that late commencement of parenteral nutrition (PN) may have clinical benefits in critically ill adults and children. However, there is currently limited evidence regarding the optimal timing of commencement of PN in critically ill term and late preterm infants.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and safety of early versus late PN in critically ill term and late preterm infants.

    Search methods

    We used the standard search strategy of Cochrane Neonatal to search the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (5 April 2019), MEDLINE Ovid (1966 to 5 April 2019), Embase Ovid (1980 to 5 April 2019), EMCare (1995 to 5 April 2019) and MEDLINE via PubMed (1966 to 5 April 2019). We searched for ongoing or recently completed clinical trials, and also searched the grey literature and reference lists of relevant publications.

    Selection criteria

    We included RCTs comparing early versus late initiation of PN in term and late preterm infants. We defined early PN as commencing within 72 hours of admission, and late PN as commencing after 72 hours of admission. Infants born at 37 weeks' gestation or more were defined as term, and infants born between 34 and 36+6 weeks' gestation were defined as late preterm.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently selected the trials, extracted the data and assessed the risk of bias. Treatment effects were expressed using risk ratio (RR) and risk difference (RD) for dichotomous outcomes and mean difference (MD) for continuous data. The quality of the evidence was assessed using the GRADE approach.

    Main results

    Two RCTs were eligible for inclusion. Data were only available from a subgroup (including 209 term infants) from one RCT in children (aged from birth to 17 years) conducted in Belgium, the Netherlands and Canada. In that RCT, children with medium to high risk of malnutrition were included if a stay of 24 hours or more in the paediatric intensive care unit (PICU) was expected. Early PN and late PN were defined as initiation of PN within 24 hours and after day 7 of admission to PICU, respectively. The risk of bias for the study was considered to be low for five domains and high for two domains.

    The subgroup of term infants that received late PN had significantly lower risk of in‐hospital all‐cause mortality (RR 0.35, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.14 to 0.87; RD ‐0.10, 95% CI ‐0.18 to ‐0.02; number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) = 10; 1 trial, 209 participants) and neonatal mortality (death from any cause in the first 28 days since birth) (RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.88; RD ‐0.09, 95% CI ‐0.16 to ‐0.01; NNTB = 11; 1 trial, 209 participants).

    There were no significant differences in rates of healthcare‐associated blood stream infections, growth parameters and duration of hospital stay between the two groups. Neurodevelopmental outcomes were not reported. The quality of evidence was considered to be low for all outcomes, due to imprecision (owing to the small sample size and wide confidence intervals) and high risk of bias in the included studies.

    Authors' conclusions

    Whilst late commencement of PN in term and late preterm infants may have some benefits, the quality of the evidence was low and hence our confidence in the results is limited. Adequately powered RCTs, which evaluate short‐term as well as long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes, are needed.

    • Early versus late intravenous nutrition in critically ill term and late preterm babies

    Review question: to evaluate the benefits and risks of early (within 72 hours of admission) versus late (after 72 hours of admission) commencement of intravenous nutrition in unwell term babies (those born at 37 weeks of pregnancy or more) and late preterm babies (those born between 34 and 37 weeks of pregnancy).

    Background: when very unwell term babies and late preterm babies are unable to tolerate milk feeds due to their illness, it is necessary to give nutrition (such as glucose, amino acids and fat) to these babies via an intravenous drip (a tube that is inserted directly into a vein). Recent studies in sick children and adults suggest that it is better to administer only intravenous glucose initially and to delay giving intravenous amino acids and fat by about a week.

    Study characteristics: this review found two small studies that evaluated the effects of early versus late commencement of intravenous amino acids and fat in full term babies. However, relevant results were available from only one study, with evidence up to date as of 5 April 2019.

    Key results: late commencement of intravenous nutrition resulted in increased survival during hospital stay and in the first 28 days of birth.The timing of intravenous nutrition was not associated with an increased risk of hospital‐acquired blood stream infections, growth and duration of hospital stay. Neurodevelopmental outcomes were not reported. However, the quality of the evidence was considered to be low because the sample size was very small and the results were from only one study.

    Conclusions: while there were some benefits of late commencement of intravenous nutrition in term and late preterm babies, the quality of the evidence was low and so our confidence in the results is limited. Further trials are needed which investigate the best time to start intravenous nutrition in unwell newborn babies.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is insufficient evidence from randomised controlled trials (RCTs) to determine whether early or late commencement of parenteral nutrition (PN) affects the outcomes of critically ill term and late preterm infants.

    Implications for research

    Adequately powered RCTs in term and late preterm infants are needed. In such trials, infants should be stratified based on the underlying condition (medical and surgical), gestation (term and late preterm) and severity of critical illness.

    A prior survey of clinicians needs to be conducted to establish the current practice and beliefs. The results of such a survey should be taken into consideration while designing the trials and defining the interventions (i.e. 'early PN' and 'late PN') to ensure maximum generalisability and willingness to participate.

    Given that the multivariable analyses of the included study (van Puffelen 2018) found that late PN is associated with increased risk of hypoglycaemia, blood glucose levels should be diligently monitored in the future trials.

    In light of the results of a post‐hoc analysis of the included study (Vanhorebeek 2017; van Puffelen 2018), which suggested that early amino acids rather than lipids were associated with worse outcomes, future neonatal RCTs should analyse these interventions separately by having four arms: 1) early amino acids + early lipids; 2) early amino acids + late lipids; 3) late amino acids + early lipids; and 4) late amino acids + late lipids. It will be useful to include biochemical outcomes such as plasma amino acid and fatty acid profiles and biomarkers of oxidative stress to explore underlying pathways of benefits/harms.

    In addition to the short term outcomes, long‐term neurodevelopmental and growth outcomes should be evaluated in future trials.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Term and late preterm infants may suffer from various conditions that predispose them to critical illness. These include persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (Jain 2015), meconium aspiration syndrome (Chettri 2016), hypoxic‐ischaemic encephalopathy (Douglas‐Escobar 2015), sepsis (Dong 2017; Schrag 2016), critical congenital heart disease (Strobel 2015), congenital diaphragmatic hernia (Leeuwen 2014), and other surgical conditions (Rintala 2014).

    During periods of critical illness, provision of enteral nutrition is unachievable, which necessitates the use of parenteral nutrition (PN). PN is usually indicated in infants in whom enteral nutrition is either contraindicated or delivers insufficient total protein and energy requirements (Macdonald 2005). In very preterm infants (those with a gestational age of less than 32 weeks), the standard practice is early commencement of PN (Adamkin 2007; Bulbul 2012; Can 2012; Can 2013; Cleminson 2016; De Curtis 2012; Hay 2013; Liu 2015; Patel 2017; Repa 2016; Ribed 2013; Rigo 2013; Tagare 2013; Torer 2015; van Goudoever 2018). However, there is a lack of evidence regarding the optimal time to commence PN therapy in term and late preterm infants.

    The nutritional reserves, requirements and morbidities of critically ill term and late preterm infants are different from those of very preterm infants. Very preterm infants have higher energy requirements than term and late preterm infants. The ratio of the minimal metabolic rate (g/kg/d) to the non‐protein energy reserve (g/kg), which represents the number of days of energy reserve, is only two days at 28 weeks' gestation and increases to 20 days for term infants and 100 days for adults (Pierro 2008). The rate of protein loss in very preterm infants in the absence of amino acid intake is nearly double the rate in term infants (1.5 g/kg/d at 28 weeks' gestational age and 0.7 g/kg/d at term) (Denne 1996). Hence, the estimated protein requirements are higher in very preterm compared to term infants. Because of these differences, it is inappropriate to extrapolate nutritional recommendations for very preterm infants to term and late preterm infants.

    Description of the intervention

    Infants who are unable to tolerate enteral feeds always receive intravenous glucose and electrolytes. The term PN usually refers to intravenous administration of amino acids and lipids. Neonatal PN can be delivered via peripheral intravenous cannulae or central venous catheters (umbilical, percutaneous or surgically placed tunnelled catheters) (Ainsworth 2015; Cies 2014). A large RCT in adults defined early PN as administration within 48 hours of admission to the intensive care unit (ICU) (Casaer 2011), whereas a large paediatric RCT defined it as occurring within 24 hours (Fivez 2016). Both studies defined late PN as delaying the administration until day 8 of ICU admission. Studies in preterm infants have used various cutoffs between one and five days after birth (Simmer 2005; Trivedi 2013). For this Cochrane Review, we used 'within 72 hours of admission to the neonatal ICU (NICU)/paediatric ICU (PICU)' as a cutoff to define early and late PN.

    How the intervention might work

    Potential benefits of early parenteral amino acids

    Early parenteral amino acids may:

    • prevent catabolism, leading to improved growth and possibly improved neurodevelopmental outcomes (Ohnishi 2016; Roelants 2016; Schneider 2017; Singhal 2016; Yang 2016); and

    • decrease the frequency and severity of neonatal hyperglycaemia and stimulate growth through enhanced secretion of insulin and insulin‐like growth factors.

    Potential harms of early parenteral amino acids

    Early parenteral amino acids may:

    • lead to hyperammonaemia, azotaemia, and metabolic acidosis (Dounousi 2015; Guellec 2015; Uthaya 2016). It may also result in slower growth of head circumference (Uthaya 2016); and

    • lead to development of metabolic syndrome later in life, with detrimental effects such as insulin resistance and adiposity (Singhal 2016).

    Potential benefits of early parenteral lipids

    Early parenteral lipids may:

    • prevent essential fatty acid (EFA) deficiency. Even a short delay of three to seven days in supplying lipids to parenterally fed infants can lead to biochemical EFA deficiency (Friedman 1986; Gutcher 1991; Lee 1993);

    • improve long‐chain polyunsaturated fatty acid (LCPUFA) levels and probably improve neurodevelopmental outcomes (Alshweki 2015; de Jong 2015); and

    • prevent the development of pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) and right ventricular failure (Umar 2011).

    Potential harms of early parenteral lipids

    Early parenteral lipids may:

    • exacerbate free radical activity by providing the substrate, initiator, and catalysts for the chain reaction. Pierro 2002 and Basu 1999 have shown that parenteral nutrition is associated with increased free radical production both in critically ill infants in intensive care and in stable patients on the ward (similar findings were noted in Hasanoglu 2005);

    • increase the rate of sepsis due to coagulase‐negative staphylococci (Avila‐Figueroa 1998; Freeman 1990) and other nosocomial infections (Gilio 2000);

    • worsen respiratory function by decreasing arterial oxygenation in acute respiratory distress syndrome (Hwang 1990; Suchner 2001; Venus 1989);

    • increase the risk of kernicterus in infants with hyperbilirubinaemia due to free fatty acid displacing bilirubin from albumin‐binding sites (Amin 2009); and

    • worsen pulmonary hypertension (Aksnes 1996; Hammerman 1989; Prasertsom 1996).

    Other complications of parenteral nutrition

    Administration of PN is known to be associated with an increased risk of central line‐associated bloodstream infection (CLABSI), hospital‐acquired bloodstream infection (HABSI), extravasation injury, thrombosis, and many other complications (Bishay 2012; Donnell 2002; Dubbink‐Verheij 2017; Gonzalez‐Hernandez 2016; Kostogloudis 2015; Legeay 2015; Wilkins 2004).

    Evidence from critically ill paediatric and adult populations

    Results from an RCT of 4640 critically ill adults, which compared early initiation of PN (within 48 hours) versus late initiation of PN (on day 8 of admission), revealed that those in the late‐initiation group had a greater chance of being discharged alive from ICU and the hospital, had fewer infectious complications, and had a shorter duration of mechanical ventilation (Casaer 2011). In a multicentre RCT of 1440 critically ill children (Fivez 2016), it was reported that those children who received late PN (commenced on day 8 of ICU admission) had lower odds of new infection, shorter duration of PICU and hospital stay, higher likelihood of an earlier live discharge from the PICU, shorter duration of mechanical ventilation, and reduced need for renal replacement therapy. Hence, the overall suggestion is that early initiation of PN may worsen the outcomes of critically ill patients.

    Why it is important to do this review

    So far, no systematic reviews have addressed the issue of optimal timing of commencement of PN in critically ill term and late preterm infants. Evidence from systematic reviews is essential for formulating guidelines and so we conducted this Cochrane Review to help address the gap in the literature.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and safety of early versus late parenteral nutrition in critically ill term and late preterm infants.

    • We planned to conduct separate subgroup analyses for medical and surgical infants and for each condition within those subgroups.

    • We planned to conduct subgroup analyses to examine the influence of gestation (late preterm infants: 34 to 36+6 weeks' gestational age (GA) and term infants: ≥ 37 weeks' GA), given the known differences in short‐term and long‐term outcomes between these groups (Kugelman 2013).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs), quasi‐RCTs, and cluster‐RCTs were eligible for inclusion.

    Types of participants

    We included trials enrolling critically ill term or late preterm infants (≥ 34 weeks' gestation at birth until day 28 of life) who have a high likelihood of being unable to tolerate enteral feeds for at least three to five days and hence require parenteral nutrition.

    For this review, we defined critical illness as the presence of one or more of the following criteria.

    • Need for mechanical ventilation.

    • Use of inotropic agents.

    • Persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn (PPHN), defined as the presence of right‐to‐left or bidirectional shunting of blood at the foramen ovale or the ductus arteriosus (or both), as well as high pulmonary arterial/right ventricular systolic pressure on echocardiography (Nair 2014). In settings where point‐of‐care echocardiogram facilities were not available, we planned to use a difference in arterial partial pressure of oxygen (PO₂) ≥ 20 mmHg or oxygen saturation ≥ 5% to 10% between preductal (right upper limb) and postductal (either lower limb) to define PPHN (Nair 2014).

    • Use of inhaled nitric oxide or other pulmonary vasodilators.

    • Use of extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO).

    • Moderate to severe hypoxic‐ischaemic encephalopathy (HIE) based on modified Sarnat or Thompson classification, requiring therapeutic hypothermia (Sarnat 1976; Thompson 1997).

    • Severe neonatal sepsis (early or late): early‐onset sepsis to be defined as sepsis within the first 72 hours of birth (Shane 2014), and late‐onset sepsis as sepsis after 72 hours of birth.

    • Surgery under general anaesthesia for major cardiac, gastrointestinal or other anomalies.

    If the authors of the original RCTs used validated scoring systems such as the Clinical Risk Index for Babies (CRIB), Score for Neonatal Acute Physiology II (SNAP‐II), or Neonatal Therapeutic Intervention Scoring System (NTISS) to define critical illness, we planned to incorporate such information into the review (Gray 1992; International Neonatal Network 1993; Richardson 2001).

    Types of interventions

    We defined the early PN group as a group that receives PN within 72 hours of admission to the NICU/PICU. We chose a cutoff of 72 hours because experimental studies have shown that a delay longer than 72 hours may result in essential fatty acid and amino acid deficiency (Friedman 1976; Gutcher 1991; Lee 1993; Poindexter 2006). We defined the late PN group as a group that receives PN after 72 hours of admission to the NICU/PICU.

    We defined parenteral nutrition as administration of any quantity of intravenous amino acids or lipids, or both. These could have been administered via peripheral intravenous cannula or through any type of central venous access.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • In‐hospital all‐cause mortality: defined as death from any cause during the initial hospital stay

    • Neonatal mortality: defined as death in the first 28 days since birth from any cause

    Secondary outcomes

    • Healthcare‐associated bloodstream infection (HABSI), defined as positive culture on a blood sample drawn 48 hours after admission to the ICU

    • Other types of hospital‐acquired infection (e.g. ventilator‐associated pneumonia, cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) infection, urinary tract infection (UTI), surgical site infection and fungal infection)

    • Length of hospital stay (days)

    • Duration of respiratory support (defined as mechanical ventilation via endotracheal tube, or non‐invasive positive‐pressure ventilation (NIPPV), or continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP), or humidified high‐flow nasal prong (HFNP) therapy), in days

    • Duration of ventilation (defined as mechanical ventilation via endotracheal tube or NIPPIV), in days

    • Duration of inhaled nitric oxide therapy, in hours

    • Incidence of hypoglycaemia (plasma glucose < 45 mg/dL (2.5 mmol/L)

    • Incidence of hyperglycaemia (plasma glucose > 150 mg/dL (8.3 mmol/L)

    • Liver dysfunction (raised liver function tests, including total bilirubin (mg/dL), alkaline phosphatase (units/L), γ‐glutamyltransferase (units/L), alanine transferase (units/L), and aspartate aminotransferase (units/L))

    • Anthropometry at the time of discharge from the hospital (Z‐scores)

    • Anthropometry (change in Z‐scores from baseline)

    • Neurodevelopmental outcomes at 12, 18, and 24 months and early childhood, assessed using well‐recognised scales/tests such as Bayley Scales of Infant Development (BSID), Griffiths Mental Development Scales (GMDS), Brunet‐Lézine test, Movement Assessment Battery for Children (M‐ABC), and Weschsler Preschool and Primary Scales of Intelligence

    The following were added as additional secondary outcomes post hoc, after discussion with the Cochrane editors.

    • Death during the first week of admission

    • 90‐day mortality

    • Duration of ICU stay (days)

    • Healthcare‐associated infections (any type)

    • Highest plasma level of total bilirubin during hospital stay (mg/dl)

    • Highest plasma level of alkaline phosphatase during hospital stay (IU/L)

    • Highest plasma level of γ‐glutamyl‐transferase during hospital stay (IU/L)

    • Highest plasma level of alanine transferase (IU/L)

    • Highest plasma level of aspartate aminotransferase (IU/L)

    • Highest blood urea nitrogen levels during hospital stay

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We used the standard search strategy for the Cochrane Neonatal Group.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (5 April 2019) in the Cochrane Library

    • MEDLINE via PubMed (1996 to 5 April 2019).

    • MEDLINE via Ovid (1946 to 5 April 2019).

    • Embase (1980 to 5 April 2019).

    • EMCare (1995 to 5 April 2019).

    We used the following search terms: (parenteral nutrition[MeSH] OR parenteral nutrition*), plus database‐specific limiters for RCTs and neonates (Appendix 1). No language restrictions were applied.

    We also searched for ongoing or recently completed clinical trials via the following websites.

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov).

    • Australia and New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry (ANZCTR) (www.anzctr.org.au).

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trial Registry Platform (who.int/ictrp/en/).

    • International Standard Randomised Controlled Trial Number (ISRCTN) Registry (www.isrctn.com).

    • Clinical Trial Results (http://www.clinicaltrialresults.org/).

    Searching other resources

    We carefully read the reference lists of relevant publications, Cochrane Reviews, guidelines, and review articles to identify potentially eligible studies. We searched conference proceedings (online abstracts) for annual meetings of the Pediatric Academic Societies (2000 to present), the European Society for Pediatric Research (2009 to present), the Royal College of Paediatrics and Child Health (2009 to present), and the Perinatal Society of Australia and New Zealand (2009 to present). We also searched the grey literature via Opengrey (http://www.opengrey.eu/), Mednar‐North Grey Literature Collection (mednar.com/mednar/desktop/en/search.html) and National Technical Reports Library (https://ntrl.ntis.gov/NTRL/).

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methods of the Cochrane Neonatal Group.

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (KM and UR) independently searched for relevant studies by using the search strategies outlined above. We screened titles/abstracts to identify potentially relevant citations and subsequently obtained the full texts of eight potentially relevant studies to assess them for inclusion. We independently assessed the eligibility of studies by reviewing those eight full texts. When we were uncertain about the eligibility of the study for inclusion, we resolved this through discussion among all review authors.

    The details of studies excluded from the review have been provided in the Characteristics of excluded studies table. The details of the study selection process are given in a PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 1).

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (KM and UR) separately extracted, assessed, and coded the data from each study using a piloted form specifically developed for this review. For each included study, we collected information on design, methods, participants, interventions, outcomes, and treatment effects. We contacted the study authors of van Puffelen 2018a and Makay 2007 for provision of additional data or clarification of published data. For each study, one review author (UR) entered final data into Review Manager 5.3 (RevMan 2014), and the two review authors (KM and SR) checked the data.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (KM and UR) independently assessed risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) of all included trials using the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool for the following domains (Higgins 2011).

    • Sequence generation (selection bias).

    • Allocation concealment (selection bias).

    • Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias).

    • Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias).

    • Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias).

    • Selection reporting (reporting bias).

    • Any other bias.

    See Appendix 2 for a detailed description of risk of bias under each domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We reported risk ratios (RRs) and risk differences (RDs) for dichotomous data, with respective 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We determined the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) for analyses with a statistically significant difference in the RD. We reported continuous data by using mean difference (MD) and associated 95% CIs.

    For studies that reported medians and interquartile ranges (IQR), we calculated means and standard deviations using the formula by Wan and colleagues (Wan 2014).

    Had a continuous outcome been measured using different scales, we would have calculated the standardised mean difference.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We planned to consider an infant only once in an analysis. We planned to exclude infants with multiple enrolments, as we would not be able to address the unit of analysis issues that may arise.

    No cluster‐RCT was identified for inclusion in this review. If we had identified a cluster‐RCT, we would have undertaken analyses at the level of the individual infant using recalculated effective sample sizes taking into account the number of clusters in each group and intracluster correlation coefficients (ICC) (Higgins 2011).

    We planned to contact the study authors of cluster RCTs to request information on ICCs from their studies. If ICCs were not available from some included studies, we planned to use the mean ICC from available ones.

    Dealing with missing data

    If participant dropouts lead to missing data, we planned to conduct intention‐to‐treat analyses. We endeavoured to obtain missing data from study authors when possible.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to estimate treatment effects of individual trials and examine heterogeneity between trials by inspecting forest plots and quantifying the impact of heterogeneity using I² statistics. We planned to use the following criteria when describing the percentage of heterogeneity:

    • < 25%: no heterogeneity;

    • 25% to 49%: low heterogeneity;

    • 50% to 74%: moderate heterogeneity; and

    • ≥ 75%: high heterogeneity.

    If we detected moderate or high statistical heterogeneity (I² ≥ 50%), we planned to explore possible causes (e.g. differences in study quality, participants, intervention regimens, or outcome assessments).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to assess publication bias using the funnel plot (Egger 1997) if at least 10 studies were included in the meta‐analysis.

    Data synthesis

    We performed analyses using Review Manager 5.3, as provided by Cochrane (RevMan 2014). For estimates of relative risks and risk differences, we used the Mantel‐Haenszel method. For estimates of mean differences, we used the inverse variance method. For all analyses, we used the fixed‐effect model.

    Had we found cluster‐RCTs that reported study estimates which were unadjusted and adjusted for clustering, we would have used adjusted estimates in the meta‐analyses. For cluster‐RCTs that did not report post‐intervention study estimates (and a relevant measure of variance) that accounted for clustering, we planned to calculate a design effect and an effective sample size using study data (number of clusters, number of participants analysed) and the ICC reported by one of the included studies.

    We planned to enter reported post‐intervention outcome data (e.g. mean and standard deviation) and author‐calculated effective sample sizes into Review Manager 5.3 to calculate individual‐level adjusted study estimates to enable inclusion in meta‐analyses.

    Quality of evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook (Schünemann 2013), to assess the quality of evidence for the following (clinically relevant) outcomes: in‐hospital mortality, neonatal mortality, HABSI, healthcare‐associated infections (any type), length of hospital stay, duration of ventilation, and incidence of hypoglycaemia.

    Two review authors (KM and SR) independently assessed the quality of evidence for each of the outcomes above. We considered evidence from RCTs as high quality but downgraded the evidence by one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of evidence, precision of estimates, and presence of publication bias. We used the GRADEpro Guideline Development Tool (GRADEpro GDT) to create a ‘Summary of findings’ table to report the quality of evidence.

    The GRADE approach yields an assessment of the quality of a body of evidence as one of the following four grades.

    • High quality: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate quality: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low quality: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited; the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low quality: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    The haemodynamic and respiratory statuses of neonates with surgical conditions are usually different from those of neonates with pure medical conditions. For example, most surgical neonates are ventilated for general anaesthesia rather than lung disease, whereas infants with medical conditions usually have associated lung pathology such as hyaline membrane disease, pneumonia, or meconium aspiration syndrome. Hence we planned to conduct these subgroup analyses.

    In addition, given the heterogenous nature of the individual conditions that lead to critical illness in term and late preterm infants, we planned to conduct subgroup analysis separately for each condition within medical and surgical subgroups. We will also planned to conduct subgroup analysis for term (≥ 37 weeks' GA) and late preterm (34 to 36+6 weeks' GA) infants because of the known higher risks for mortality and morbidity in late preterm infants compared to term infants (Kugelman 2013).

    We were unable to conduct any of the above subgroup analyses in this review due to there being insufficient data.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to perform sensitivity analysis to explore the influence of the following factors on effect size.

    • Repeating the analysis after excluding unpublished studies.

    • Repeating the analysis while taking account of risk of bias, as specified.

    We planned to undertake post hoc sensitivity analyses as required based on the findings of the meta‐analysis. We were unable to conduct any of the planned sensitivity analyses due to there being insufficient data.

    Results of the search

    Our initial broad search yielded 3695 citations. After removing 1422 duplicates and excluding 2265 studies based on titles and abstracts, we read the full texts of eight studies in detail. Finally, two studies were identified as eligible for inclusion in this review. The details of the included studies are summarised in the Characteristics of included studies table. Details of the six excluded studies are summarised in the Characteristics of excluded studies table. Figure 1 illustrates the screening and the study selection process.

    Included studies

    We included two studies (Makay 2007; van Puffelen 2018) in this review. However, relevant information was available from only one RCT (number of participants (n) = 209 term infants) (van Puffelen 2018). This study was a secondary subgroup analysis of the Paediatric Early versus Late Parenteral Nutrition In Critical Illness (PEPaNIC) multicentre paediatric trial (Fivez 2016), conducted in Belgium, the Netherlands and Canada. In that RCT, children (from birth to 17 years of age) with medium to high risk of malnutrition (score of 2 or more on the Screening Tool for Risk on Nutritional Status and Growth) were included if a stay of 24 hours or more in PICU was expected. Early PN (the control group) was defined as initiation of PN within 24 hours after admission to the PICU. In the late PN group (the intervention group), PN was withheld until the morning of day 8 in the PICU.

    The results for 209 term infants (98 of whom received early PN, and 111 of whom received late PN) from the PEPaNIC trial (Fivez 2016), reported by van Puffelen and colleagues (van Puffelen 2018), were included in this review.

    Enteral nutrition was commenced early in both groups and increased according to individual unit's protocols. Macronutrient and caloric targets for the three study sites (Belgium, the Netherlands and Canada) varied. Micronutrients (trace elements, minerals, and vitamins) were started from day 2 and continued until the enteral nutrition provided reached 80% of the caloric target in all study sites. In the two study sites (Leuven and Rotterdam), insulin infusion was used to achieve tight control of blood glucose levels. In Leuven, insulin was started in both groups to achieve blood glucose levels of 2.7 mmol/L to 4.4 mmol/L. In Rotterdam, all infants received an insulin infusion designed to achieve blood glucose levels of 4 mmol/L to 8 mmol/L. In Edmonton, infants received insulin infusion when blood glucose concentrations exceeded 10 mmol/L.

    The primary endpoints in van Puffelen 2018 were 'incidence of PICU‐acquired infections' and 'duration of PICU dependency' (number of days in the PICU) and likelihood of earlier live discharge from PICU. Secondary efficacy endpoints were 'duration of mechanical ventilatory support' (number of days and likelihood of earlier live weaning from ventilation), 'maximum plasma level of urea', 'duration of hospital stay' (number of days and likelihood of earlier live discharge from hospital), and 'direct health‐care costs'. Secondary safety endpoints were 'death during the first week', '90 day mortality' and 'hypoglycaemia during the first week'.

    Baseline characteristics of the infants in the early PN group were similar to the late PN group for gender, gestational age, birth weight, weight for age Z scores, Pediatric Logistic Organ Dysfunction (PELOD) score, diagnostic groups, mechanical ventilation on admission and haemodynamic support on admission. However, the Paeditric Index of Mortality 2 (PIM 2) and risk of mortality calculated based on PIM2, were significantly higher in the early PN group than in the late PN group (P = 0.016 and P = 0.011, respectively).

    Additional data were provided by the authors of van Puffelen 2018 for the following outcomes for this review: 'in‐hospital all‐cause mortality', 'neonatal mortality', 'duration of hospital stay', 'duration of respiratory support', 'highest blood urea nitrogen levels', 'Z score for weight at discharge' and 'changes to Z scores for weight from baseline'.

    For the outcomes 'duration of PICU dependency' and 'duration of mechanical ventilation', authors reported the results as medians and interquartile ranges (IQRs) and were unable to provide the mean and standard deviation (SD). Hence, we calculated the mean and SD from the reported medians and IQRs using the formula by Wan and colleagues (Wan 2014).

    The authors reported the results of the univariate as well as multivariable analyses. For multivariable analyses, the original investigators adjusted pre‐defined risk factors such as severity of critical illness, estimated risk of mortality, diagnosis group and weight on admission.

    Makay 2007 was a single‐centre RCT of 72 infants in Turkey. In the early PN group, amino acids (1 g/kg/day) were commenced within the first eight hours after birth and lipid emulsion (0.5 g/kg/day) was commenced from the next day. In the late PN group, 0.5 g/kg/day of amino acid and 0.5 g/kg/day of lipid emulsion were commenced after 72 hours. For both groups, amino acids and lipid emulsion were increased by 0.5 g/kg/day to a maximum 3 g/kg/day. The outcome measures were energy intake, changes in body weight, time of first stool passage, number of daily stool output, relative changes in serum bilirubin levels at 24, 48 and 72 hours, need for phototherapy and duration of phototherapy. We contacted the authors for the additional information but there was no response.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This Cochrane Review included data from a subgroup analysis of a paediatric RCT. It found that late PN in term and late preterm infants may be associated with decreased mortality, decreased hospital‐acquired infections and reduced length of hospital stay and duration of mechanical ventilation. However, the evidence was considered to be of low quality given the various limitations of the included study. There was no effect on duration of ICU stay, incidence of hypoglycaemia and physical growth. Information on neurodevelopmental outcomes was not available.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The body of evidence for this review was very limited as it was based on the results of only one RCT (van Puffelen 2018). While the included study (van Puffelen 2018) was relevant to the review question, it was a secondary subgroup analysis of the PEPaNIC multicentre paediatric trial (which included participants aged 0 to 18 years) (Fivez 2016), which makes the evidence even weaker.

    In the included study, early and late PN were defined as "within 24 hours" and "after 7 days" of PICU admission, respectively (van Puffelen 2018). These extreme timings of commencement of PN are practiced only in a few paediatric and neonatal units and hence the study design is very unlikely to be applicable in term and late preterm infants (Mehta 2018). It was highlighted in the worldwide survey of nutrition of 156 PICUs that only approximately 20% of the units commence PN within 24 hours of admission and only 3.5% of the units commence PN after seven days of admission (Kerklaan 2016). Furthermore, no data exist on the current timing of commencement of PN for term and late preterm infants in NICUs.

    There were variations on how glucose homeostasis in study infants was achieved across the three study sites of the included RCT (van Puffelen 2018). The use of different insulin infusion regimens, with different target blood glucose levels, for the study infants in the trial sites may have affected the clinical outcomes. In two of the study sites (Leuven and Rotterdam), insulin infusion was used to tightly control blood glucose levels. This 'tight glycaemic control' has been reported to be associated with significant increase in hypoglycaemia in critically ill infants and children admitted to PICU (Agus 2017; Chen 2018; Macrae 2014; Srinivasan 2014; Vlasselaers 2009; Zhao 2018). Even though our analysis found no association between the timing of commencement of PN and the incidence of hypoglycaemia, the authors of the included RCT (van Puffelen 2018) reported higher incidence of hypoglycaemia in the first week in the late PN group on multivariable analysis (van Puffelen 2018). Potential for neonatal hypoglycaemia with the late commencement of PN is an important issue of concern, given its association with adverse neurodevelopmental outcomes (Mahajan 2017; Qiao 2019; Shah 2019; Tan 2017; Wickstrom 2018).

    The types and compositions of amino acids and lipid emulsions, as well as the caloric targets, varied across the study centres in the included RCT (van Puffelen 2018). This would have lead to different types and doses of amino acids and lipid emulsions being administered to infants and may have affected the clinical outcomes (Clark 2007; Jiang 2019).

    We found low‐quality evidence that late commencement of PN reduced in‐hospital all‐cause mortality, neonatal mortality and 90‐day mortality. However, it is important to note that these results were based on only one study (van Puffelen 2018) and that the baseline risk of mortality was lower in the group that received late PN compared to the group that received early PN (6.7% versus 9.9% respectively; P = 0.011). Hence it is not surprising that there was lower mortality in the group that received late PN. The authors of van Puffelen 2018 also reported that there was no difference in mortality between the two groups on multivariable analysis, once the baseline risk factors were adjusted for.

    In the included study (van Puffelen 2018), infection rates were 31% with early PN compared to 16% with late PN. The results of this RCT may not be generalisable to the units that have lower or higher incidences of infection.

    We were unable to evaluate the long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes of early versus late PN in this review.

    Based on the results of one RCT in children (aged 0 to 18 years) (Fivez 2016), the recently updated ESPGHAN/ESPEN/ESPR/CSPEN guidelines recommend that clinicians consider "withholding parenteral nutrition, including amino acids, for 1 week in critically ill term infants while providing micronutrients can be considered (LOE 1+, RG B, conditional recommendation)" (van Goudoever 2018). However, given the multiple and significant limitations to the available evidence in this Cochrane Review, we are unable to make such a recommendation. Another important reason for caution is the risk of hypoglycaemia associated with the late commencement of PN that was found on multivariable analysis of the included RCT (van Puffelen 2018).

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, we considered the quality of evidence to be low on the basis of our GRADE analysis, due to the risk of bias of included studies and imprecision (the sample size was small — one study with 209 participants — and the confidence intervals were wide). We were unable to assess heterogeneity as there was only one study included in our analysis. We were unable to evaluate some of the outcomes such as liver function, duration of nitric oxide, head circumference, length and neurodevelopmental outcomes. We were unable to perform sensitivity analyses and subgroup analyses due to there being insufficient data. See summary of findings Table for the main comparison.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The review included the results from the subgroup analysis of a RCT, which was not explicitly described in the protocol methods.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    To our knowledge, there are no other systematic reviews comparing early versus late PN in term and late preterm infants. The only study that addressed this issue was van Puffelen 2018 which has been included in this review. They concluded that withholding PN for one week of critical illness in term infants, while administering micronutrients, is superior to early initiation of PN from a short‐term clinical and health‐economical perspective. They also cautioned that withholding PN for one week significantly increased the risk of hypoglycaemia and hence long‐term effects of hypoglycaemia need to be investigated before late PN can be confidently recommended for this population.

    As highlighted in the results section of this Cochrane Review, the directions and sizes of the effect estimates from our analyses were often different to the results of the multivariable analyses in the included study (van Puffelen 2018). We found that infants given late PN had significantly lower incidence of 90‐day mortality and overall incidence of HAI, but van Puffelen 2018 found that the timing of PN commencement did not affect these outcomes on multivariable analyses. Furthermore, our analyses found that the timing of commencement of PN did not affect duration of hospital stay and incidence of hypoglycaemia. On the other hand, van Puffelen 2018 found that late PN was associated with shorter duration of hospital stay and higher incidence of hypoglycaemia on multivariable analyses.

    Our results in favour of late PN for duration of ICU stay and duration mechanical ventilation were in agreement with the results of multivariable analyses in van Puffelen 2018. Furthermore, our analysis reporting no significant difference between the two groups in regards to death during first week of admission was also in agreement with the multivariable analysis results of van Puffelen 2018.

    For individual types of infections, van Puffelen 2018 reported results from univariable analyses and they were in agreement with our review. For HABSI, van Puffelen 2018 reported results of univariable analysis and found that late PN was associated with lower incidence of HABSI (P = 0.048). Our analysis found that the timing of PN was not associated with incidence of HABSI (P = 0.05).

    We were unable to compare some of our results to those of van Puffelen 2018 (e.g. in‐hospital all‐cause mortality, neonatal mortality, duration of respiratory support, highest blood urea nitrogen and growth outcomes ) as result were not reported in the manuscript of van Puffelen 2018 , but the raw data was kindly provided by the authors upon our request.
