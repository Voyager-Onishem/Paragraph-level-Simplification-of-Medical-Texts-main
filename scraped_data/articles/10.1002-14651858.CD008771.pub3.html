Early versus late administration of amino acids in preterm infants receiving parenteral nutrition

    • Background

    Observational studies in preterm newborns suggest that delay in administering amino acids (AA) could result in a protein catabolic state and impact on growth and development.

    Objectives

    The objective of this review was to compare the efficacy and safety of early versus late administration of intravenous AA in neonates born at < 37 weeks of gestation.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and trial registries in March 2023. We checked the reference lists of included studies and studies/systematic reviews where subject matter related to the intervention or population examined in this review.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing early administration of AA with late administration in premature newborn infants. We defined early administration of AA solution as the administration of AA in isolation or with total parenteral nutrition within the first 24 hours of birth, and late administration as the administration of AA in isolation or with total parenteral nutrition after the first 24 hours of birth.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methodological procedures. We used the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of the evidence.

    Main results

    Nine studies (383 participants) were eligible for inclusion in the review. All study participants were born at < 37 weeks of gestation and were inpatients in neonatal intensive care units.

    No studies reported growth during the first months of life as assessed by difference in weight. Early administration of AA may have little or no effect on growth in the first month of life as measured by length (mean difference (MD) 0.00, 95% confidence interval (CI) −0.41 to 0.41; 1 study; 21 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and head circumference (MD 0.05, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.14; 2 studies; 87 participants; low‐certainty evidence). No studies reported the discharge weight outcome. Early administration of AA may result in little to no difference in neurodevelopmental outcome assessed by Mental Developmental Index (MDI) of < 70 at two years of age (odds ratio 0.83, 95% CI 0.21 to 3.28; 1 study; 111 participants; low‐certainty evidence). No studies reported all‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge.

    Early administration of AA may result in a large increase in positive nitrogen balance in the first three days of life (MD 250.42, 95% CI 224.91 to 275.93; 4 studies; 93 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    Authors' conclusions

    Low‐certainty evidence suggests that there may be little to no difference between early and late administration of AA in growth (measured by length and head circumference during the first month after birth) and neurodevelopmental outcome (assessed by MDI of < 70). No RCTs reported on weight in the first month of life, mortality (all‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge), or discharge weight. Low‐certainty evidence suggests a large increase in positive nitrogen balance in preterm infants who received AA within 24 hours of birth. The clinical relevance of this observation is unknown.

    The number of infants in the RCTs included in the review was small, and there was clinical heterogeneity amongst trials. Adequately powered trials in infants < 37 weeks' gestation are required to determine optimal timing of initiation of AA.

    We identified two ongoing studies. Both studies will be recruiting infants ≥ 34 weeks of gestation and may or may not add to the outcome data for this review.

    • What are the benefits and risks of giving amino acids early to babies who were born before 37 weeks of pregnancy?

    Key message

    Due to lack of strong evidence, the benefits of giving amino acids early to babies who were born before 37 weeks of pregnancy are unclear.

    What are amino acids?

    Amino acids are the building blocks that combine to form proteins. Our body makes some amino acids, but it cannot make the nine essential amino acids that we need from our diet. Protein is crucial for healthy growth and development. Newborn babies ingest essential amino acids from breast and formula milk.

    What is prematurity, and what are some of the risks of prematurity in babies?

    Premature babies have limited reserves of energy and may not be able to feed soon after birth. This often results in a need for their nutrition to be administered in a vein (parenteral nutrition).

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if giving amino acids within the first 24 hours of birth compared to after the first 24 hours of birth to babies born before 37 weeks of pregnancy improved growth and development, decreased the chance of death, and affected some normally occurring chemicals in the blood.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies in which babies who were born before 37 weeks of pregnancy randomly received amino acids early (within 24 hours of birth) or late (after 24 hours of birth).

    What did we find?

    We found 9 reports (383 babies) in our search. However, not all of our outcomes of interest were reported in every study. Limited data from a few babies suggest that early amino acids may not result in any improvement in growth. Early amino acids may make little or no difference to chemicals in the blood. They may also result in a large gain in protein in the first three days after birth; however, what this means in relation to the health of the baby is unclear. The available evidence is not strong enough to support early initiation of amino acids in babies born before 37 weeks of pregnancy.

    How up‐to‐date is the evidence?

    The evidence is current to March 2023.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Low‐certainty evidence suggests that there may be little to no difference between early and late administration of amino acids (AA) in growth (measured by length and head circumference during the first month after birth) and neurodevelopmental outcome (assessed by Mental Development Index (MDI) of < 70). No randomised controlled trials reported the outcomes of weight in the first month of life, mortality (all‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge), and discharge weight. Low‐certainty evidence suggests a large increase in positive nitrogen balance in preterm infants who received AAs within the first 24 hours of birth. The clinical relevance of this observation is unknown.

    We are very uncertain about the effect of early administration of AA on bicarbonate level within the first 24 hours of birth.

    The number of infants included in the review was small, and there was clinical heterogeneity amongst the included studies. Adequately powered trials in infants < 37 weeks' gestation are required to determine optimal timing of initiation of AA.

    We identified two ongoing studies. Both studies will be recruiting infants ≥ 34 weeks of gestation and may or may not add to the outcome data for this review.

    Implications for research

    There is a need for adequately powered randomised controlled trials to assess the efficacy and safety of early administration of AAs in premature infants. Important short‐term outcomes such as growth during the first month of life, mortality, sepsis, duration of hospital stay, and biochemical profile should be included. Moreover, other outcomes listed in the 'Core Outcomes in Neonatology' should be incorporated into the research plan ( Webbe 2020 ). Investigators should design parallel‐group randomised controlled trials (randomised either at individual or cluster level).

    Background

    Nutritional interventions are a cornerstone of the management of premature infants in a neonatal intensive care unit. Nutritional goals for the preterm infant are to provide nutrients to approximate the rate of growth and composition of weight gain to that of a normal foetus of the same postmenstrual age whilst maintaining normal concentrations of nutrients in blood and other tissues ( Pediatric Nutrition 2020 ). This goal is difficult to achieve, and the weight at term in premature infants is consistently below those born at full term ( Greenbury 2021 ).

    Birth of a premature newborn is a nutritional emergency and a challenge for treating clinicians. Early parenteral nutrition is started within a few hours after delivery to prevent excessive catabolism ( UpToDate 2023 ). A preterm infant's ability to manage the volume of intravenous fluids, renal immaturity, sugar and electrolyte disturbances, and associated morbidities dictate the intake of nutrients.

    Description of the condition

    The foetus requires amino acids (AA) for the processes of protein synthesis, carbon accretion, oxidative metabolism, and biosynthesis, which ultimately determine growth rate in utero ( Vaughan 2017 ). Amino acid uptake by the foetus exceeds that needed for protein accretion, and the excess is oxidised, contributing significantly to foetal energy production ( Thureen 2005 ).

    After birth, infants can lose 10% to 20% of weight ( Thureen 2005 ), and although a significant proportion of this weight loss is extracellular water, the remainder represents a failure to accrete lean mass or fat, both. Protein losses in neonates receiving isolated intravenous glucose increase with decreasing gestational age ( Denne 2007 ). This catabolic state of nutrition in a preterm infant could potentially lead to early postnatal growth failure and a widening gap in lean body mass between neonates and foetuses of the same gestational age. The nutritional deficits may be difficult, if not impossible, to recoup and may impact on both short‐ and long‐term morbidities ( Denne 2007 ; Ziegler 2002 ).

    Administration of early AA was reported to be associated with improved growth outcomes ( Kotsopoulos 2006 ; Poindexter 2006 ). Review articles that include observational studies have reported early administration of AA to be safe and recommended infusion of relatively high amounts of AA soon after birth ( De Curtis 2012 ; Denne 2007 ; Vlaardingerbroek 2009 ).

    Optimising early protein intake may contribute to promoting long‐term brain health in preterm‐born children ( Duerden 2021 ). In a population‐based cohort study of preterm infants born at less than 30 weeks’ gestation, high early AA intake was independently associated with a greater likelihood of surviving with improved cognitive outcomes at 5 years of age ( Rozé 2021 ). In this study, early AA intake was associated with increases in white matter area and increased anisotropy of several white matter tracts assessed by magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) at term. On the other hand, Cormack 2019 reported that the relationship between early protein intake and later neurodevelopmental outcomes, or proxy outcomes, such as head growth or MRI findings, remains unclear, and whether increased early protein intake improves or worsens later outcomes is unknown.

    Some risks have been reported with early and high protein intake. High protein intake soon after birth as part of optimal parenteral nutrition could induce metabolic disturbances in preterm infants ( Guellec 2015 ). High doses of AA in preterm infants could lead to cholestasis ( Wang 2023 ), hypophosphataemia ( Sung 2019 ), and hypercalcaemia ( Bonsante 2013 ).

    Description of the intervention

    After birth, the continuous supply of nutrients from the placenta is interrupted. Preterm infants require nutritional support immediately after birth to prevent catabolism and support normal growth, often in the setting of increased energy requirements due to postnatal environmental conditions and critical illness ( Stremming 2023 ).

    Parenteral nutrition is frequently administered to preterm infants to manage their metabolic needs in the interim whilst enteral feeds are gradually established. Amino acids are an integral part of parenteral nutrition and are administered to achieve growth, maintain and gain lean mass, maintain ideal body composition, net positive balance, and brain maturation.

    Amino acids provide scaffolding for the growth of all cells, tissues, and organs, as well as the structural links amongst cells. Amino acids in particular act as metabolic signals to regulate metabolism ( Hay 2010 ).

    How the intervention might work

    Intravenous AA in premature newborns can transiently stimulate protein synthesis and suppress breakdown ( Kalhan 2007 ). Replenishing protein intravenously can avoid catabolism, achieve protein accretion, and improve growth. Reversal of catabolism has been a consistent finding in numerous studies, despite differences in the composition of the AA used ( Denne 2007 ). In an observational study, early administration of AA in preterm infants < 1500 g was associated with improved weight gain, suggesting that nutrition that included AA may be critical during the first 24 hours of life ( Valentine 2009 ). Improved growth with early higher AA was noted by Scattolin 2013 as well.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Several reports have suggested protein catabolism with lack of early AA supplementation and the negative impact of this catabolism on growth and development ( Denne 2007 ; Mitton 1992 ; Thureen 1998 ; Van Goudoever 1995 ). Recommendations have been made to commence AA intake from the first day of life or as soon as possible after birth ( ESPGHAN 2018 ; Hay 2018 ); however, there is no definitive evidence available ( Bolisetty 2020 ). Variability in practice has also been reported with AA administration commencing from the first to fifth day after birth, with a wide‐ranging initial dose ( Lapillonne 2013 ). Additionally, in critically ill term neonates, withholding parenteral nutrition for one week was clinically superior to standard care of initiating parenteral nutrition within 24 hours for short‐term outcomes ( van Puffelen 2018 ). It is therefore important to critically review the evidence and evaluate the safety, efficacy, and balance of benefits versus risks of early supplementation of AAs.

    Objectives

    The objective of this review was to compare the efficacy and safety of early versus late administration of intravenous AA in neonates born at < 37 weeks of gestation.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs), quasi‐RCTs, and cluster‐RCTs. The control and experimental groups differed only in the timing of the initiation of AAs, with other components of parenteral nutrition remaining the same.

    Types of participants

    We included all neonates born at less than 37 weeks of gestation.

    We excluded neonates with metabolic diseases affecting protein metabolism.

    Types of interventions

    We compared early administration of AA solution with late.

    We defined early administration of AA solution as the administration of AAs with or without any other parenteral nutrition intake within the first 24 hours of birth, and late administration as the administration of AAs with or without any other parenteral nutrition intake after the first 24 hours of birth.

    Other parenteral nutrition included intravenous dextrose, solutions containing any combination of dextrose, electrolytes, trace elements administered with or without lipids.

    Types of outcome measures

    The outcome measures were as described below.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Growth during the first month of life, measured by changes in:

      1. weight in g/kg/week;

      2. length in cm/week;

      3. head circumference in cm/week.

    2. Discharge weight.

    3. Neurodevelopmental outcome assessed with any standard tool.

    4. All‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge from hospital.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Biochemistry:

      1. nitrogen balance, assessed by measuring total urinary nitrogen excretion in the first seven days after birth and subtracting it from the nitrogen intake over the same period, and then identifying it as positive or negative;

      2. metabolic acidosis, measured by pH and bicarbonate levels in the first seven days after birth;

      3. plasma ammonia and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) levels in the first seven days after birth;

      4. alkaline phosphatase and direct bilirubin levels in the first seven days after birth;

      5. serum albumin level;

      6. serum calcium and phosphate levels;

      7. blood glucose level and hypoglycaemia in the first seven days after birth.

    2. Other secondary outcomes:

      1. time to regain birthweight in days;

      2. sepsis (positive bacterial culture in cerebral spinal fluid, urine, or blood) during the inpatient stay;

      3. chronic lung disease (oxygen requirement at or beyond a postmenstrual age of 36 weeks);

      4. duration of hospital stay in days;

      5. time to full feeds in days.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The Cochrane Neonatal Information Specialist developed the search strategies.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases without language, publication type, or publication status restrictions. We conducted searches for clinical trials without date limits; we limited searches for systematic reviews from 2021 forward. We searched the following databases on 20 March 2023 (see Appendix 1 for detailed strategies and search dates):

    1. Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2023, Issue 2), via Wiley;

    2. Ovid MEDLINE (MEDALL) (1946 to 17 March 2023);

    3. Embase via Ovid (1980 to week 11, 2023).

    Searching other resources

    We identified trial registration records using CENTRAL and by independent searches of the following on 20 March 2023:

    1. US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov ( clinicaltrials.gov );

    2. World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) ( trialsearch.who.int/Default.aspx ).

    Search strategies are provided in Appendix 1 .

    We screened the reference lists of included and related (e.g. in the subject area of our review but not eligible for inclusion) studies and related systematic reviews (e.g. reviews including the population or intervention examined in our review) for studies not identified by the database searches.

    Data collection and analysis

    We collected information regarding the randomisation, blinding, intervention, stratification, and whether the trial was single or multicentre for each included study, analysing the clinical outcomes noted above in Types of outcome measures .

    Selection of studies

    We used Covidence for screening ( Covidence ). We used Cochrane's Screen4Me to reduce the screening burden for the authors of the current review ( Marshall 2018 ; Noel‐Storr 2020 ; Noel‐Storr 2021 ; Thomas 2021 ).

    Screen4Me comprises three components, as follows.

    1. Known assessments (a service that matches records in the search results to records that have been screened by Cochrane Crowd and labelled as 'RCT' or 'not an RCT').

    2. The RCT classifier (a machine‐learning model that distinguishes RCTs from non‐RCTs).

    3. Cochrane Crowd (Cochrane’s crowdsourcing platform, where contributors from around the world help to identify randomised trials and other types of healthcare‐related research).

    We added any references categorised as non‐RCTs through the known assessments and the RCT classifier to the irrelevant segment of Covidence.

    This approach means that references have been categorised, which would assist in deduplication when the review is updated in the future, and for verification purposes should questions arise about the accuracy of Screen4Me categorisation. The results of Screen4Me are presented in Figure 1 , and the disposition of references incorporated into the PRISMA flow diagram in Figure 2 ( Liberati 2009 ).

    We entered data into Review Manager Web ( RevMan Web 2022 ).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (AT, VJ) independently assessed risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) in the included trials using the Cochrane RoB 1 tool ( Higgins 2017 ), for the following domains.

    1. Sequence generation (selection bias)

    2. Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    4. Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    5. Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    6. Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    7. Any other bias

    We resolved any disagreements by discussion. A more detailed description of risk of bias for each domain is presented in Appendix 2 .

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    We analysed dichotomous using odds ratio (OR) and risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We did not calculate the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome or the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome, as there was no statistically significant reduction (or increase) in risk difference ( Higgins 2022 ).

    Continuous data

    We analysed continuous data using mean difference (MD) for continuous variables when outcomes were measured in the same way between trials; we used the standardised mean difference (SMD) to combine trials that measured the same outcome but used different methods. We converted continuous data presented as median and interquartile range (IQR) to mean and estimated the standard deviation (SD).

    We used Review Manager Web for statistical analyses ( RevMan Web 2022 ).

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis in our review was the participating infant, nonetheless:

    1. if the unit of analysis was not the same as the unit of randomisation, such as in cluster‐randomised trials, we planned to adjust for clustering using the guidance in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions ( Higgins 2022 );

    2. in the event of repeated observations on the same study participant (such as episodes of sepsis), we treated the data as dichotomous, with each study participant counted once.

    None of the studies included in the review were randomised at the cluster level, therefore unit of analysis was not an issue.

    Dealing with missing data

    We intended to carry out analysis on an intention‐to‐treat basis for all included outcomes.

    We planned to contact trial authors when missing data were considered to be 'not missing at random'; however, no such study was identified.

    We planned to make explicit the assumptions of any methods used to deal with missing data.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We used the I 2 statistic to quantify inconsistency across studies, grading heterogeneity as follows:

    1. 0% to 40%: not important heterogeneity;

    2. 30% to 60%: moderate heterogeneity;

    3. 50% to 90%: substantial heterogeneity;

    4. 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    We assessed statistical heterogeneity by visual inspection of forest plots, direction and magnitude of effects, and the degree of overlap between CIs. The Chi 2 statistic generated in forest plots with a P value of < 0.10 was considered significant ( Higgins 2022 ).

    We assessed clinical and methodological diversity by exploring variability in study design, outcome measurement tools, and risk of bias ( Higgins 2022 ).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned that if a sufficient number of studies were pooled (> 10), we would generate a funnel plot to visually inspect the risk of publication bias, whereby more pronounced asymmetry of the funnel plot may be indicative of a substantial overestimation of the intervention effect ( Higgins 2022 ). However, generation of a funnel plot was precluded by an insufficient number of included studies.

    Data synthesis

    We performed meta‐analysis using a fixed‐effect model. Use of the generic inverse variance method was not required, as no cluster‐RCTs were included in the review ( Higgins 2022 ).

    If included studies used different scales for continuous variables, then we used the SMD, where we assessed the impact of using the highest versus the lowest of the available SD on the overall estimate of effect.

    If a study used median for continuous variables because data were skewed, we used the statistical conversions suggested by Wan 2014 for imputing values of mean and SDs from lower quartile, median and upper quartile summary statistics ( Higgins 2022 ).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We did not perform subgroup analyses, as the number of infants included in the review was small.

    If sufficient data were available, we planned to perform subgroup analyses (restricted to the primary comparisons) as follows.

    1. Premature infants with a gestational age < 30 weeks versus premature infants with a gestational age ≥ 30 weeks.

    2. Birthweight:

      1. premature very low‐birthweight infants (less than 1500 g) versus premature infants with birthweight of 1500 g or more;

      2. premature extremely low‐birthweight infants (less than 1000 g) versus premature infants with birthweight of 1000 g or more.

    3. High (≥ 2 g/kg/day at commencement) or low (< 2 g/kg/day at commencement) intake

    4. AA solution in isolation versus AA solution with glucose or lipids, or both.

    5. Premature infants born with intrauterine growth restriction versus premature infants born with gestational age‐appropriate birthweight.

    Sensitivity analysis

    Insufficient data precluded sensitivity analysis. In the case of sufficient data, we planned to perform the following sensitivity analyses:

    1. determine the impact of risk of bias on the overall effect estimate by adding high risk of bias studies to low risk of bias studies and comparing the results ( Higgins 2022 );

    2. determine the impact of heterogeneity on the overall estimate of effect by removing studies that contributed to heterogeneity from the analyses and comparing the results ( Higgins 2022 ).

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook ( Schünemann 2013 ), to assess the certainty of the evidence for the following (clinically relevant) outcomes.

    1. Growth during first month of life assessed with weight.

    2. Growth during first month of life assessed with crown‐heel length.

    3. Growth during first month of life assessed with head circumference.

    4. Discharge weight.

    5. Neurodevelopmental outcome assessed by any standard tool.

    6. All‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge from hospital.

    7. Nitrogen balance.

    Two review authors (AT, VJ) independently assessed the certainty of the evidence. We considered evidence from RCTs as high certainty, downgrading one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of the evidence, precision of estimates, and presence of publication bias. We used GRADEpro GDT software to create summary of findings Table 1 to report the certainty of the evidence ( GRADEpro GDT ).

    The GRADE approach results in an assessment of the certainty of a body of evidence as one of four grades, as follows.

    1. High certainty: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    2. Moderate certainty: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    3. Low certainty: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited: the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    4. Very low certainty: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    Results of the search

    The trial searches identified 5227 references. After removal of 1553 duplicates, 3674 records were available for screening. We used Cochrane’s Screen4Me automatic classifier. Screen4Me's Known Assessments and the RCT classifier eliminated 1331 references; the remaining 2343 titles/abstracts were screened by the review authors. We eliminated 2314 records based on title/abstract and reviewed 29 full texts and trial registry records. We included 9 studies (1 new: van den Akker 2014 ); excluded 18 studies (2 new: Liu 2015 ; Suganuma 2020 ); and categorised 2 studies as ongoing ( ACTRN12622001297718 ; Moon 2020 ). Details of the Screen4Me process are shown in Figure 1 ; an overall study flow diagram is presented in Figure 2 .

    Included studies

    We included 9 reports from 7 RCTs (383 participants) ( Blanco 2008 ; Heimler 2010 ; Murdock 1995 ; Rivera 1993 ; Saini 1989 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). Two studies, van den Akker 2006 ; van den Akker 2014 , reported on participants from the study by te Braake 2005 . All nine trials included premature infants who in the 'early' arm had AA administered within the first 24 hours of birth. All studies were single‐centre studies. Three studies were performed in the USA, two in the UK, one in the Netherlands, and one in China.

    Participants

    The number of study participants in the individual studies ranged from 17 to 135. The gestational age of study participants ranged from ≥ 24 to 34 weeks. Three studies reported specific criteria for gestational age ( Blanco 2008 ; Heimler 2010 ; Saini 1989 ). Heimler 2010 included infants born at < 34 weeks of gestation, and Saini 1989 included infants born at < 30 weeks. Blanco 2008 excluded infants born at ≤ 24 weeks of gestation.

    Four studies reported birthweight as an additional criterion for inclusion ( Blanco 2008 ; Murdock 1995 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). Blanco 2008 recruited infants with a birthweight of < 1000 g; Murdock 1995 recruited infants weighing < 2000 g at birth; te Braake 2005 included infants with a birthweight of < 1500 g; and Tang 2009 reported infants with a birthweight of between 1000 g and 2000 g.

    Intervention

    The included studies differed in their approach to the administration of AA.

    Blanco 2008 : the control group received 0.5 g/kg/day of intravenous AAs (Aminosyn PF; Abbott Laboratories, Chicago, Illinois) with 40 mg/kg/day of cysteine hydrochloride at 24 to 36 hours of life, with increases of 0.5 g/kg/day every 24 hours to a maximum of 3.0 kg/day. The intervention arm received 2 g/kg/day of AA within the first 24 hours of birth, with increases of 1 g/kg/day every day to reach 4 g/kg/day. All infants were prescribed intravenous lipids.

    Heimler 2010 : the intervention arm received 1.5 g/kg/day of AAs within 24 hours of birth along with intravenous glucose, calcium gluconate, and vitamins. The AA infusion rate was advanced by 0.5 g/kg/day, up to 2.5 g/kg/day by day three and continued at this rate during the trial. The control arm received intravenous glucose, calcium gluconate, and vitamins for three days, and AA infusion was added at three days age at 1 g/kg/day, advanced by 0.5 g/kg/day up to a maximum of 2.5 kg/day by day seven.

    Murdock 1995 : infants were allocated to three arms. The first arm received intravenous glucose for two days; the second arm received glucose and intravenous AA at 1 g/kg/day on day one and 1.4 g/kg/day on day two (Vamin 9, Pharmacia, Milton Keynes, UK); whilst the third arm received glucose 10%, intravenous AA, and lipids. We did not include the third arm in this review.

    Rivera 1993 : the intervention arm received AA at 1.5 g/kg/day within 24 hours of birth, and the same rate was continued for three days. The control arm received intravenous glucose for three days. The AA solution used in this study was Aminosyn PF (Abbott Laboratories, Chicago, Illinois) with cysteine hydrochloride additive, which was omitted inadvertently from the nutrient solutions administered to the first four infants. None of the infants in the study received enteral feeds or parenteral lipid infusion.

    Saini 1989 : the intervention arm received intravenous glucose and AA (Vamin 9, KabiVitrum) within 24 hours of birth, whilst the control arm received intravenous glucose only, with AA added after 72 hours. The rate of AA infusion in the groups was increased progressively over three days from 1 g/kg/day to 3 g/kg/day. Intravenous lipid was introduced in both regimens after seven days. Six infants in the early group had periods of up to 48 hours when they could not receive AAs for a variety of reasons.

    Tang 2009 : infants were allocated to three arms. The first arm received 2.4 g/kg/day of AA within 24 hours of birth, with increments of 1.2 g/kg/day to a maximum of 3.6 g/kg/day. The second arm received 1.0 g/kg/day of AA, 24 hours after birth, which was increased by 0.5 g/kg/day until a maximum of 3.0 g. The third arm received 0.5 g/kg/day of AA at day three, with increments of 0.5 g/kg/day until a maximum of 3.0 g/kg/day. We did not include the third arm in this review.

    te Braake 2005 : the intervention arm received glucose and 2.4 g/kg/day of AA within 24 hours of birth, whilst the control arm received glucose only. AA infusion continued at a uniform rate in the intervention arm, whilst in the control arm, the infusion rate was increased to 2.4 g/kg/day over two subsequent days. Intravenous lipids were administered in both groups from day two onward. The AA infusion in this study was Primene 10% (Baxter, Clintec Benelux NV, Brussels, Belgium).

    Outcomes

    Three studies reported anthropometric measures of growth during the first month of life ( Heimler 2010 ; Saini 1989 ; Tang 2009 ). Saini 1989 reported change in weight in g/kg/day, occipitofrontal head circumference in cm/week, and crown heel length in cm/week, in the first 10 days. Heimler 2010 and Tang 2009 reported change in occipitofrontal head circumference as an increment over the first two weeks and in cm/week, respectively.

    van den Akker 2014 reported survival without major disabilities at the corrected age of two years. Infants born at a gestation of < 32 weeks were eligible for this follow‐up study. Eighteen infants died before discharge, and three were lost to follow‐up. The secondary outcomes were neurodevelopmental scores in infants without major disabilities and anthropometric measurements in all surviving infants. Neurodevelopmental outcome was assessed by measuring the Mental Developmental Index (MDI) and Psychomotor Developmental Index (PDI) of the Bayley Scales of Infant Development (2nd edition) ( Bayley 1993 ). Neurodevelopmental outcome following stratification for negative versus positive nitrogen balance on the second day of postnatal age and correlation of neonatal urea concentration with developmental scores in surviving infants without major disability at two years were reported. No differences in mortality and developmental outcome were observed in the early and late groups.

    Heimler 2010 , Rivera 1993 , Saini 1989 , te Braake 2005 , and van den Akker 2006 reported mean difference in nitrogen balance. te Braake 2005 reported nitrogen balance in the form of a bar chart, without actual values. Heimler 2010 measured nitrogen excretion by 24‐hour urine collections obtained on day three before the administration of AA to the late group. Faecal nitrogen losses were assumed to be negligible. Rivera 1993 measured nitrogen excretion in urine between 48 and 72 hours of age. In eight infants, urine collections were of less than 24 hours. Faecal nitrogen losses were assumed to be negligible. Saini 1989 carried out serial metabolic balance studies of nitrogen for 10 days by timed 24‐hour collections of urine and faeces. Nitrogen balance data was reported by van den Akker 2006 on the second postnatal day with urine samples collected over a 12‐hour period.

    Six studies reported biochemical parameters such as BUN, sodium bicarbonate level, pH, serum ammonia, glucose, and bilirubin ( Blanco 2008 ; Heimler 2010 ; Murdock 1995 ; Rivera 1993 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ).

    Tang 2009 reported duration of hospital stay, time to regain birthweight, and time to full enteral feeds.

    For further details, see Characteristics of included studies .

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 18 studies for the following reasons.

    1. In five studies ( Anderson 1979 ; Bassoiuny 2009 ; Black 1981 ; van Lingen 1992 ; Weinstein 1987 ), the age at randomisation differed from that defined in the protocol for this review ( Trivedi 2010 ).

    2. In three studies ( Chessex 1985 ; Kadrofske 2006 ; Thureen 2003 ), the difference was in the dose of AA and not the timing.

    3. In three studies ( Gunn 1978 ; Ibrahim 2004 ; Makay 2007 ), the intake of lipids differed.

    4. Two studies were not RCTs ( Jadhav 2007 ; Liu 2015 ).

    5. The study groups of one study differed in the intake of carbohydrates ( Wilson 1997 ).

    6. In three studies ( Suganuma 2020 ; te Braake 2008 ; Van Goudoever 1995 ), the control arm did not receive AA.

    7. In one study ( van den Akker 2007 ), numerical values of the outcome of interest (albumin) were not reported.

    For further details, see Characteristics of excluded studies .

    There were no studies awaiting classification.

    Allocation

    Blanco 2008 reported that assignment to a treatment group was carried out by the clinical pharmacist with cards in sealed sequential envelopes. Murdock 1995 and Tang 2009 reported random allocation but did not mention the method of randomisation. Heimler 2010 and Rivera 1993 mentioned an envelope method of randomisation. Saini 1989 mentioned sequential allocation but provided no further details. te Braake 2005 mentioned that the trial was randomised but did not provide any further details. van den Akker 2006 reported a subset of participants from te Braake 2005 , and described their trial as randomised and open. Selection bias for van den Akker 2014 was the same as for te Braake 2005 .

    Blinding

    Six trials did not report blinding of intervention and outcome measurements ( Blanco 2008 ; Heimler 2010 ; Murdock 1995 ; Rivera 1993 ; Saini 1989 ; Tang 2009 ). te Braake 2005 and van den Akker 2006 reported that their trials were open. The assessors who performed developmental assessment in the van den Akker 2014 study were blinded to group allocation.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Except for van den Akker 2014 , the outcomes studied by the trials included in this review were short term. The rate of attrition in van den Akker 2014 was very low. Ninety‐seven per cent of surviving infants in this study visited the follow‐up outpatient clinic for neurodevelopmental assessment.

    We could identify no obvious bias from incomplete outcome data.

    Selective reporting

    The protocol for the trial by Blanco 2008 was registered with ClinicalTrials.gov. There was no mention of registration of protocols by any of the other included studies.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Publication bias was not obvious for the nitrogen balance or BUN estimates within the first two days of age. The study population of van den Akker 2014 was that of the surviving infants included in the te Braake 2005 study. We could identify no other potential sources of bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Summary of findings table ‐ Early amino acids compared to Late amino acids for neonates born at <37 weeks of gestation

    Comparison: early versus late administration of AA

    Primary outcomes
    1. Growth during the first month of life

    • Difference in weight was not reported.

    • One study reported on length ( Saini 1989 ). Early administration of AA may result in little or no difference in length (mean difference (MD) 0.00, 95% confidence interval (CI) −0.41 to 0.41; 1 study; 21 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1 ).

    • Two studies reported on head circumference ( Saini 1989 ; Tang 2009 ). Early administration of AA may result in little or no difference in head circumference (MD 0.05, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.14; 2 studies; 87 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2 ).

    Heimler 2010 reported no difference in occipitofrontal circumference in the two weeks after birth. The format of the presented data on occipitofrontal circumference prevented its inclusion in the meta‐analysis.

    2. Discharge weight

    Not reported.

    3. Neurodevelopmental outcome

    One study reported on adverse neurodevelopmental outcome, defined by MDI of < 70 ( van den Akker 2014 ). Early administration of AA may result in little to no difference in neurodevelopmental outcome (odds ratio (OR) 0.83, 95% CI 0.21 to 3.28; 1 study; 111 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3 ).

    Although long‐term neurodevelopmental outcome did not differ between early and late AA supplementation groups, stratification per gender revealed that males had higher odds of achieving normal outcome if supplemented with AA from birth onwards (crude OR 3.80, 95% CI 1.27 to 11.40). Adjusted OR was 6.17 with 95% CI 1.01 to 38.46. MDI was lower by 10.10 points (95% CI 1.60 to 18.6) in females without disability who received early AA. PDI did not differ in infants without major disabilities, both as a group and after stratification by gender. Male infants had a higher adjusted OR of 7.87 (95% CI 1.31 to 47.62) when they had a positive nitrogen balance on day two of postnatal age.

    4. All‐cause mortality at 28 days and before discharge from hospital

    Not reported.

    Secondary outcomes
    1. Biochemistry

    Nitrogen balance

    Four studies reported on nitrogen balance ( Heimler 2010 ; Rivera 1993 ; Saini 1989 ; van den Akker 2006 ). Early administration of AA may result in a large increase in positive nitrogen balance. Although the overall assessment of the certainty of evidence was low, all studies consistently reported positive nitrogen balance in recipients of early AA (MD 250.42, 95% CI 224.91 to 275.93; 4 studies; 93 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4 ).

    A negative nitrogen balance was recorded in infants who received late AA. The P value for the Chi 2 statistic for assessment of heterogeneity was significant (0.06), and the I 2 statistic showed a moderate heterogeneity (59%).

    Metabolic acidosis

    Two studies reported on pH within the first 24 hours of birth ( Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). Mild metabolic acidosis was seen in both early and late AA groups with a pH just below 7.35. The mean difference in pH in the two groups was not significant (MD −0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.01; 2 studies; 201 participants; Analysis 1.5 ).

    One study reported on pH at two days after birth ( te Braake 2005 ). Mild metabolic acidosis was seen in both early and late AA groups with a pH just below 7.35. The mean difference in pH in the two groups was not significant (MD −0.01, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.01; 1 study; 135 participants; Analysis 1.6 ).

    Three studies reported bicarbonate levels within the first 24 hours of birth ( Blanco 2008 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). The mean bicarbonate level within the first 24 hours after birth did not differ between groups (MD −0.17, 95% CI −0.80 to 0.47; 3 studies; 262 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.7 ).

    Two studies reported bicarbonate levels at two days ( Blanco 2008 ; te Braake 2005 ). The levels, though normal, were lower in the early AA group (MD −0.66, 95% CI −1.30 to −0.03; 2 studies; 196 participants; Analysis 1.8 ).

    Two studies reported bicarbonate levels at three to four days of life ( Blanco 2008 ; Tang 2009 ). The levels, though normal, were higher in the early AA group (MD 1.60, 95% CI 0.52 to 2.69; 2 studies; 127 participants; Analysis 1.9 ).

    Plasma ammonia

    One study reported ammonia levels on day one ( Rivera 1993 ). The levels were normal, and did not differ between groups (MD 18.00, 95% CI −7.69 to 43.69; 1 study; 14 participants; Analysis 1.10 ).

    Three studies reported ammonia levels on days three and four ( Heimler 2010 ; Rivera 1993 ; Tang 2009 ). The levels were normal, and did not differ between groups (MD −1.86, 95% CI −7.90 to 4.19; 3 studies; 96 participants; Analysis 1.11 ).

    Blood urea nitrogen (BUN)

    Four studies reported BUN levels in the first two days ( Blanco 2008 ; Rivera 1993 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). BUN levels were elevated in the early AA group (P < 0.001) (MD 2.06, 95% CI 1.50 to 2.62; 4 studies; 285 participants; Analysis 1.12 ). There was substantial heterogeneity (I 2 = 90%).

    Four studies reported BUN levels on days three and four ( Heimler 2010 ; Rivera 1993 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). The levels were significantly elevated in the early AA group (P < 0.001) (MD 2.12, 95% CI 1.40 to 2.84; 4 studies; 242 participants; Analysis 1.13 ). There was substantial heterogeneity (I 2 = 79%).

    Three studies reported BUN levels on days six and seven ( Blanco 2008 ; Tang 2009 ; te Braake 2005 ). BUN levels were elevated on days six and seven in the early AA group (P < 0.001) (MD 2.44, 95% CI 1.59 to 3.29; 3 studies; 262 participants; Analysis 1.14 ). There was substantial heterogeneity (I 2 = 90%).

    Alkaline phosphatase and direct bilirubin

    Tang 2009 reported levels of direct bilirubin on days one and four. The levels did not differ on day one (MD −0.90, 95% CI −3.85 to 2.05; 1 study; 66 participants; Analysis 1.15 ) and day four (MD −0.50, 95% CI −3.15 to 2.15; 1 study; 66 participants; Analysis 1.16 ).

    Neither group had cholestasis (direct serum bilirubin > 34 mmol/L).

    Alkaline phosphatase level was not reported.

    Serum albumin level

    Not reported.

    Serum calcium and phosphate level

    Not reported.

    Blood glucose and hypoglycaemia

    te Braake 2005 reported blood glucose levels on days one and two. On day one, mean blood sugar levels did not differ between early and late AA groups (MD 0.40, 95% CI −1.36 to 0.56; 1 study; 135 participants; Analysis 1.17 ). On day two, mean blood sugar was lower in the early AA group (MD −0.90, 95% CI −1.58 to −0.22; 1 study; 135 participants; Analysis 1.18 ). However, mean blood glucose was normal in both study groups on days one and two ( te Braake 2005 ).

    One study reported on the incidence of hypoglycaemia ( Murdock 1995 ). No difference was observed in the incidence of hypoglycaemia between study groups (risk ratio 0.61, 95% CI 0.34 to 1.08; 1 study; 21 participants; Analysis 1.19 ).

    Heimler 2010 also reported no difference in mean blood sugar level between groups. Data from this study were not included in the meta‐analysis as the time of the test was not reported.

    2. Other secondary outcomes

    Time to regain birthweight in days

    Two studies reported the number of days to regain birthweight ( Heimler 2010 ; Tang 2009 ). Tang 2009 reported that the mean number of days to regain birthweight was less in infants who received early AA (11.7 days) compared with those who received late AA (14.1 days). No difference was seen in Heimler 2010 . The meta‐analysis showed a P value of 0.02 favouring early administration of AA (MD −2.39, 95% CI −4.48 to −0.31; 2 studies; 83 participants; Analysis 1.20 ).

    Sepsis (positive bacterial culture in cerebral spinal fluid, urine, or blood) during inpatient stay

    Infection as an outcome was reported by Tang 2009 . However, its definition was not described.

    Chronic lung disease (oxygen requirement at or beyond a postmenstrual age of 36 weeks)

    Chronic lung disease was reported by Tang 2009 . However, the definition of chronic lung disease was not described.

    Duration of hospital stay in days

    One study reported duration of hospital stay in days ( Tang 2009 ). Duration of hospital stay was less in those who received early AA, with a borderline P value of 0.05 (MD −7.20, 95% CI −14.37 to −0.03; 1 study; 66 participants; Analysis 1.21 ).

    Time to full feeds in days

    One study reported time to full feeds in days ( Tang 2009 ). Infants who received early AA required fewer days to reach enteral nutrition compared with those who commenced on AA late. The mean number of days required to reach enteral nutrition in infants in the early AA group was 13.9 compared with 16.1 in the late AA group (MD −2.20, 95% CI −4.38 to −0.02; 1 study; 66 participants; Analysis 1.22 ).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Primary outcomes

    We found that early administration of AA may result in little or no difference in length and head circumference during the first month of life and adverse neurodevelopmental outcome at two years of age. Differences in weight during the first month of life, discharge weight, all‐cause mortality at 28 days, and mortality before discharge were not reported.

    Secondary outcomes

    Early administration of AA may result in a large increase in positive nitrogen balance. Nitrogen accretion in early life compared to nitrogen loss makes physiological sense and emulates foetal growth; however, its potential clinical significance is unclear. Although levels of bicarbonate differed between groups on days two and three to four of life, these findings are of uncertain clinical significance as the levels were normal in both groups.

    Mildly low pH was observed within the first 24 hours of birth and on day two. The difference between groups was not significant.

    Elevated levels of BUN were noted in the early AA group in the first two days after birth and on days three to four of life and days six to seven. The elevated levels do not necessarily mean intolerance to AA; they could be a reflection of oxidation of AA, just like in utero, where AAs are partly oxidised and partly used for protein synthesis.

    Plasma ammonia and direct bilirubin levels did not differ between groups. Mean blood glucose was normal on days one and two in the early and late AA groups. One study showed that duration of hospital stay, days to enteral nutrition, and days to regain birthweight were less in the early AA group ( Tang 2009 ).

    The number of infants in the included studies was small, with the certainty of evidence assessed as low to very low ( summary of findings Table 1 ).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    All participants were premature infants with low birthweight. Mean gestation age was less than 33 weeks, and birthweight was less than 2000 g. Positive nitrogen balance seen in association with early administration of AA had statistical significance, P < 0.001. Positive nitrogen balance was consistent across all the trials that reported nitrogen balance as an outcome. The clinical significance of this observation is unclear. Blood sugar and bicarbonate levels were normal in premature infants irrespective of timing of initiation of AA.

    The data for this review come mostly from short‐term studies with few study participants. Given the low to very low certainty of the evidence and the small numbers in the study population, applicability of the current evidence is not strong.

    Quality of the evidence

    Several limitations in the quality of evidence should be noted. Only three studies described their method of randomisation ( Blanco 2008 ; Heimler 2010 ; Rivera 1993 ). Sequence generation was not described. Blinding of early administration AAs was not performed. Blinding of allocation to outcome assessors was not described, except for blinding of the follow‐up team by van den Akker 2014 . Only one trial reported registration of protocol ( Blanco 2008 ).

    The number of study participants was small, and significant clinical heterogeneity was observed amongst the included studies. The inclusion criteria for participants, intervention, and outcomes studied were not identical. The mean gestational age of infants included in this review ranged from 26.3 to 32.8 weeks. The mean birthweight ranged from 768 g to 1635 g. Early AA administration was carried out within the first 24 hours of birth; however, the protocol for late administration was unique to each included study.

    The dosage of AA administered was different in all of the included studies. Early AA ranged from 1 g/kg/day to 2.4 g/kg/day. Some studies administered the same amount of lipids to both groups. As a result, the amount of energy provided to the treatment and control arms varied considerably amongst studies. In addition, the included trials used different preparations of AAs.

    Given the small number of infants included in the review, we did not perform subgroup analyses.

    The overall certainty of evidence was low to very low, downgraded mainly for risk of bias and imprecision.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We used standard Cochrane Neonatal methods in conducting this systematic review. A Cochrane Information Specialist drafted the search strategy and performed the search. No language restrictions were applied, and an appropriate range of sources were searched to identify published and ongoing studies.

    We believe that the protocol for this review was implemented appropriately, and that any differences between the protocol and the review were not a source of bias ( Differences between protocol and review ). During the title/abstract screening phase, there were no marginal decisions regarding the exclusion of studies.

    Although only one additional study, van den Akker 2014 , was included in this update of an earlier review, Trivedi 2013 , we believe that our search strategy has identified all relevant reports.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Similar observations were reported in a systematic review by Leenders 2018 . The authors of Leenders 2018 reported that early administration of parenteral AA is well tolerated. Higher nitrogen balance and serum BUN level were reported in infants who received early AA. No significant benefits on growth were reported.
