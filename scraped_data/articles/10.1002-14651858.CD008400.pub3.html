Acupuncture for mumps in children

  • Jing He
  • Pengli Jia
  • Min Zheng
  • Mingming Zhang
  • Hua Jiang
Authors' declarations of interest

    • Background

    Mumps is an acute, viral illness transmitted by respiratory droplets and saliva. A number of studies published in China have suggested that acupuncture is beneficial for children with mumps but the literature reporting the benefits or harms of acupuncture for mumps has not been systematically reviewed.

    Objectives

    To determine the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for children with mumps.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL (2014, Issue 11), MEDLINE (1950 to November week 3, 2014), EMBASE (1974 to December 2014), CINAHL (1981 to December 2014), AMED (1985 to December 2014), the Chinese BioMedical Literature Database (CBM) (1979 to November 2014), China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) (1979 to November 2014), Chinese Technology Periodical Database (CTPD) (1989 to November 2014) and Wanfang database (1982 to November 2014). We also handsearched a number of journals (from first issue to current issue).

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials comparing acupuncture with placebo acupuncture, no management, Chinese medication, Western medication or other treatments for mumps. Acupuncture included either traditional acupuncture or contemporary acupuncture, regardless of the source of stimulation (body, electro, scalp, fire, hand, fine needle, moxibustion).

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently extracted data. We identified no trials for inclusion in this updated review.

    Main results

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Authors' conclusions

    We could not reach any conclusion about the efficacy and safety of acupuncture as we identified no trials for inclusion in this review. More high‐quality research is needed.

    • Acupuncture for mumps in children

    Review question
    We aimed to determine the effectiveness and safety of using acupuncture to treat mumps in children.

    Background
    Acupuncture has been used to treat children with mumps for hundreds of years in China. Benefits attributed to acupuncture include decreased swelling and pain, and shortening of the disease duration. According to traditional Chinese medicine, health is achieved by maintaining an uninterrupted flow of Qi, or energy, along 14 meridians. Mumps is caused by 'wind warmth evil' (epidemic heat) and 'pyretic toxicity' accumulated in the Shaoyang and Yangming meridians, thus the flow of Qi, sputum and 'heat evil' stagnate in and around the ears and the cheeks. Acupuncture can help expel 'wind warmth evil', clear pathogenic heat, remove toxic substances, act as an anti‐inflammatory, alleviate pain and re‐establish the normal flow of Qi, thus restoring internal balance.

    Study characteristics
    No trials met our inclusion criteria.

    Key results
    This update aimed to systematically review all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that examined the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for children with mumps. The evidence is current to December 2014. Although acupuncture has been widely used in China for children with mumps and quite a number of trials claiming to be RCTs have been published, we identified no eligible trials. Although no trials were included, we reviewed the studies we found and there were no reports of adverse events with acupuncture as a treatment for children with mumps. Therefore, we cannot draw any definite conclusions about the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for children with mumps.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is no evidence to determine the efficacy and safety of acupuncture in the treatment of children with mumps, although the excluded studies suggest that acupuncture is effective in improving swelling and pain of the parotid gland and assisting the body temperature to return to normal. We cannot make any recommendations for practice and nor can the results be generalised to clinical practice.

    Implications for research

    We need randomised controlled trials (RCTs) to evaluate the effectiveness of acupuncture in the treatment of children with mumps. These trials should pay greater attention to methodological design, including randomisation, blinding of participants, other care providers and outcome assessors, and clarification of standard control treatment, and should use valid and reliable outcome measures to minimise performance and assessment bias (although blinding of acupuncturists is difficult). Future trials need to improve the quality of reporting and should report adverse effects of acupuncture and long‐term follow‐up in order to measure the incidence of complications in mumps.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Mumps (epidemic parotitis) is an acute, vaccine‐preventable viral illness transmitted by respiratory droplets and saliva, which is caused by an organism called a paramyxovirus (Kaye 2008). Mumps is transmitted via the mouth by tiny drops of saliva from talking, sneezing, sharing drinks, kissing or coughing. Mumps manifests with painful swellings in or around the neck, ear or cheek and is accompanied by fever, chills, headache, sore throat, loss of appetite and weakness (Govoni 2006; Sullivan 1985). Approximately 1765 cases of mumps were reported to the Iowa Department of Public Health, USA in 2006 (Campos 2006).

    Mumps can result in a variety of complications such as meningitis, encephalitis, arthralgia, orchitis, mastitis, oophoritis and pancreatitis (Govoni 2006; Kanra 2004). In high‐income countries mumps is less common because of large‐scale vaccination programmes (Dayan 2008; Kaye 2008). Isolation of mumps cases is a good way to prevent spreading in the community (Kaye 2008; MacDonald 2007). Prior to the introduction of the vaccine in 1967, mumps mainly occurred in children aged five to nine years (Behrman 2003). Routine vaccination has proven highly effective in reducing the incidence of mumps and is currently used in most high‐income countries. However, there have been outbreaks of mumps in vaccinated populations (Hviid 2008). Mumps is particularly prevalent in low‐income countries where vaccination programmes are inconsistent and the morbidity is high. For example, the Harbin study in China showed that the average incidence of mumps was 36.85/100,000. The proportion of mumps cases in children aged 6 to 15 years was 76% (Xu 2008).

    The incubation period for mumps is usually 16 to 18 days but it can range from 12 to 25 days. A person is infectious from two days before symptoms start to nine days after symptoms have resolved (Campos 2006). In general, mumps is not a serious illness but some children suffer complications such as meningitis, encephalitis, arthralgia, orchitis, mastitis, oophoritis and pancreatitis (Govoni 2006; Kanra 2004). Deafness can also occur (Tsubota 2008). In 1981, during an outbreak of mumps in a middle school in Ohio, USA, in those affected 100% had parotitis, 81.1% had fever, 61.8% had headache, 44.6% had earache, 9.1% had orchitis (boys and men ≥ 12 years old) and 3.8% had oophoritis (girls and women ≥ 12 years old) (Kevin 1985). There has been a recent increase in mumps orchitis among pubertal and postpubertal males (Davis 2010).

    Description of the intervention

    Acupuncture has been used to treat children with mumps for hundreds of years in China (Zhao 2003). From the perspective of traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), mumps is called 'Zhasai' in the Song Dynasty. Benefits attributed to acupuncture include decreasing swelling and pain, and shortening of the duration of the disease (Zhao 2003). It has been reported that many types of acupuncture, including body acupuncture, ear acupuncture, electroacupuncture, three‐edged needle (prick acupoints to cause bleeding), moxibustion with rush pith (a special moxibustion) and moxibustion with moxa, may be beneficial in the treatment of mumps. Whether single or integrated acupuncture is applied, some relevant studies have indicated that this type of treatment is easy, safe and affordable for patients, and that the rate of effectiveness is as high as 90% to 100% (Chi 2001; Wei 2008; Zhao 2003).

    Acupuncture and Chinese medicinal herbs are popular treatments for mumps in China. Chinese physicians and acupuncturists generally believe that herbs and acupuncture are more effective than Western medicines in alleviating symptoms and shorten the duration of mumps with fewer side effects (Shu 2012; Zhao 2003).

    There are other treatments designed to alleviate mumps symptoms and prevent complications (Chen 1983; Hviid 2008). Conventional treatments include antipyretics and antiviral medicines taken during febrile periods to relieve pain. These drugs can also shorten and reduce the duration and morbidity of complications in epidemic parotitis (Liu 1997; Liu 2006; Tortorec 2008). However, some drugs including interferon or glucocorticosteroids may have serious side effects and are more expensive than other antiviral drugs, such as moroxydine hydrochloride (Zhao 2003). In China, practitioners consider antivirals and antipyretics including moroxydine hydrochloride and Kang san xian yan zhu she ye (anti‐parotid injection) as conventional treatments to alleviate symptoms and these drugs have been widely used to treat mumps (BDS 2010; Xue 2005; Zhang 2010; Zhao 2003).

    How the intervention might work

    Acupuncture is based on a complex theoretical framework (Zhang 1988). According to traditional Chinese medicine (TCM), health is achieved by maintaining an uninterrupted flow of Qi, or energy, along 14 meridians. Mumps is caused by a kind of special 'evil' ‐ 'wind warmth evil' (pestilent toxicity). The evil Qi is liable to invade the Shaoyang meridian (shallow meridian) and Yangming meridian (deeper meridian), thus the flow of Qi, sputum and 'heat toxicity' stagnate in and around the ears and the cheeks (Han 2008). Acupuncture can help expel 'wind warmth evil', clear pathogenic heat, remove toxic substances, act as an anti‐inflammatory, alleviate pain and re‐establish the normal flow of Qi, thus restoring internal balance (Lu 2008). In China, a number of studies have shown that acupuncture is the sole treatment for mumps in clinical practice (Gui 1995; Long 2000; Song 1989). The mechanism of acupuncture in the treatment of mumps remains unknown but it is thought that acupuncture affects the immune response (Guo 2006), and could improve the microcirculation of blood to affected areas (Liu 2004).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Acupuncture is a relatively simple, inexpensive and safe treatment compared to other conventional interventions. Acupuncture is well accepted by Chinese patients and is widely used to alleviate the symptoms of mumps (Zhao 2003). Studies have shown that acupuncture can significantly reduce the degree of parotid gland swelling within two to three days, reduce body temperature back to normal within two days and reduce complications including meningitis, encephalitis, arthralgia, orchitis, mastitis, oophoritis and pancreatitis (Chi 2001; Lu 2008). To our knowledge, this is the first systematic review to examine empirical evidence of the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for mumps.

    Objectives

    To determine the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for children with mumps. We intended to test the following hypotheses.

    1. Acupuncture improves the symptoms of mumps. Symptoms included swelling, pain in or around the neck, ears and cheeks and elevated body temperature.

    2. Acupuncture shortens the duration of mumps.

    3. Acupuncture reduces complications of mumps (meningitis, encephalitis, arthralgia, orchitis, mastitis, oophoritis and pancreatitis).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing acupuncture with no treatment, sham acupuncture or Western medicine. We excluded quasi‐RCTs or controlled trials using any other non‐random allocation methods.

    Types of participants

    Children aged one to 15 years, with typical mumps symptoms. Mumps must have been diagnosed according to the following criteria:

    1. participants with typical symptoms: acute unilateral or bilateral swelling of parotid or other salivary glands;

    2. participants infected by other mump patients or in epidemic areas or during an epidemic season; and

    3. participants diagnosed according to laboratory tests.

    We excluded the following:

    1. participants with serious skin infections;

    2. suppurative parotitis;

    3. other kinds of cardiac, lung, liver or kidney diseases; and

    4. non‐epidemic parotitis.

    Types of interventions

    Studies evaluating all forms of acupuncture therapy, including body acupuncture, ear acupuncture, acupressure, laser acupuncture, phonopuncture, electroacupuncture and moxibustion (including moxibustion with rush pith).

    We included both traditional acupuncture along classical meridian points and contemporary acupuncture in non‐meridian or trigger points, regardless of the source or methods of stimulation (for example, hand, needle, laser or electrical stimulation). Acupuncture can be given on its own, or as part of a comprehensive regimen involving a number of different strategies. Comprehensive methods refer to acupoint pressing, acupoint injection, moxibustion, medicine for internal use and/or the external application of medicines such as herbs (Yin 2008; Zhao 2003).

    The control interventions included no treatment, sham acupuncture or Western medicine alone. The treatment comparisons to be investigated included:

    1. acupuncture alone compared with no treatment;

    2. acupuncture alone compared with sham acupuncture;

    3. acupuncture alone compared with baseline medication; and

    4. acupuncture plus baseline medication compared with baseline medication alone.

    We excluded trials that only compared different manipulation methods or different acupoints of acupuncture.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Swelling and pain in the parotid gland area.

    2. Time until the body temperature returns to normal.

    3. Number of days absent from school.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Incidence of complications including meningitis, encephalitis, arthralgia, orchitis, mastitis, oophoritis, pancreatitis, etc.

    2. Number of adverse effects, including dizziness, bleeding, difficulty in tolerating the discomfort associated with needling and infection, which were presumed to be caused by acupuncture.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    For this 2014 update of the review we searched the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL 2014, Issue 11) (accessed 4 December 2014), which includes the Acute Respiratory Infections Group's Specialised Register, MEDLINE (January 2012 to November week 3, 2014), EMBASE (January 2012 to December 2014), CINAHL (2011 to December 2014), AMED (2011 to December 2014), the Chinese BioMedical Literature Database (CBM) (2012 to November 2014), China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) (2012 to November 2014), Chinese Technology Periodical Database (CTPD) (2012 to November 2014) and Wanfang database (2012 to November 2014). We handsearched a number of journals (from first issue to current issue).

    Previously we searched the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL 2012, Issue 4) (accessed 8 May 2012), which includes the Acute Respiratory Infections Group's Specialised Register, MEDLINE (1950 to April week 4, 2012), EMBASE (1974 to May 2012), CINAHL (1981 to May 2012), AMED (1985 to May 2012), the Chinese BioMedical Literature Database (CBM) (1979 to May 2012), China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) (1979 to May 2012), Chinese Technology Periodical Database (CTPD) (1989 to May 2012) and Wanfang database (1982 to May 2012).

    We used the search strategy described in Appendix 1 to search MEDLINE and CENTRAL. We did not use a filter to identify randomised trials as there were too few results. We adapted the search strategy for EMBASE (Appendix 2); CINAHL (Appendix 3); AMED (Appendix 4); Chinese BioMedical Literature Database (Appendix 5); CNKI (Appendix 6); CTPD (Appendix 7) and Wanfang (Appendix 8).

    Searching other resources

    We screened the reference lists of the identified studies and reviews for additional references. We contacted trial authors about the effectiveness of acupuncture in mumps patients by mailing and asking them for any additional unpublished studies relevant to this systematic review. We asked authors of abstracts of possible eligible studies for full reports. We also handsearched some major Chinese medical journals relevant to acupuncture (from the first issue to current issue), including the following journals:

    • Shanghai Journal of Acupuncture and Moxibustion;

    • Acupuncture Research;

    • Journal of Clinical Acupuncture and Moxibustion;

    • Chinese Acupuncture and Moxibustion.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (JH, PLJ) independently checked the titles and abstracts of trials identified by the search for inclusion in the review, based on the selection criteria outlined above. We retrieved the full‐text articles if there was any doubt as to whether the article should be excluded. A third review author (ZMM) reviewed the information to decide on inclusion or exclusion of a trial or through discussion in cases of disagreement between the two review authors.

    Data extraction and management

    At least two review authors (JH, PLJ) independently extracted information on participants, methods, interventions, outcomes and results by using a specially designed data extraction form and entered information into Review Manager 5.3 for statistical analysis (RevMan 2014). We contacted trial authors for any missing data.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (JH, PLJ) independently assessed the validity and methodological quality of studies according to the 'Risk of bias' tool developed by The Cochrane Collaboration (Higgins 2011):

    1. random sequence generation;

    2. allocation concealment;

    3. blinding of participants and personnel;

    4. blinding of outcome assessment;

    5. incomplete outcome data;

    6. selective reporting; and

    7. other bias.

    A third author (ZMM) resolved disagreements as required.

    Measures of treatment effect

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Unit of analysis issues

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Dealing with missing data

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    No study met our inclusion criteria, therefore we could not assess clinical heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Data synthesis

    No study met our inclusion criteria, so we could not perform data synthesis.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    No study met our inclusion criteria, so we could not conduct a subgroup analysis and investigate heterogeneity.

    Sensitivity analysis

    No study met our inclusion criteria, so we could not conduct a sensitivity analysis.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    See: Characteristics of excluded studies and Studies awaiting classification tables.

    We retrieved a total of 86 trials (79 for our first published review in 2012 and seven trials for this 2014 update) involving mumps and acupuncture therapy through electronic searching and handsearching. After excluding all duplicates, we retrieved 30 citations from the electronic searches. After reading the titles and abstracts, we excluded 17 citations because they were not randomised controlled trials (RCTs) or did not relate to this review. The remaining nine studies claimed to be RCTs (Chi 2001; Guo 2006; He 1994; Jin 2006; Liu 2004; Lu 2008; Shan 2001; Si 2001; Yu 1999). We examined these nine studies in detail and contacted the trial authors by telephone or e‐mail for confirmation of the methodological process. None of the studies met our inclusion criteria. In this 2014 updated review, we were unable to contact the author of the Jin 2006 study to confirm whether it was a real RCT and therefore we placed it in the Studies awaiting classification section.

    Included studies

    No study met our inclusion criteria.

    Excluded studies

    In the first publication of this review (He 2012), we excluded three studies after contacting the trial authors who confirmed that the study designs were not true RCTs (Chi 2001; He 1994; Liu 2004). No baseline data were described in one study (Shan 2001). Acupuncture was used in all groups in one trial (Yu 1999), and two trials included participants who were over 15 years of age (Lu 2008; Si 2001). In this 2014 update review we also excluded Guo 2006, which had been included in the first publication of this review. The reason for this exclusion was that we agreed with the comments of a feedback submitter, who suggested that it was doubtful that a random number sequence generator was used (Feedback 1).

    Studies awaiting classification

    One study is awaiting classification as we have been unable to contact the trial authors to obtain information regarding the study design at the time of updating this review (Jin 2006).

    Ongoing studies

    We are not aware of any ongoing studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We could not carry out a 'Risk of bias' assessment as no trials were eligible for inclusion.

    Effects of interventions

    We did not identify any trial that fulfilled our inclusion criteria.

    Discussion

    Acupuncture has been widely used in China to treat children with mumps and a number of trials claiming to be randomised controlled trials (RCTs) have been published. However, there is a lack of well‐designed, methodologically sound trials and most of the studies we identified had been inadequately reported. We did not identify any RCTs for inclusion. Evidence from excluded trials suggested that acupuncture may improve the swelling and pain of the parotid gland and return the body temperature to normal within three to five days after treatment. The recovery rate in the acupuncture group suggests a trend towards better outcomes. However, the acupuncture group had a longer time to cure than the Western medicine group. The possible reason is that acupuncture stimulates the body's immune system and needs more time to produce an anti‐inflammatory effect, while Western medicine results in direct antiviral activity and an anti‐inflammatory effect (Zhao 2003). The result was that the control group experienced a reduced fever more quickly than the acupuncture group (BDS 2010; Zhang 2010). Current evidence is limited and no firm conclusions can be made on the efficacy and safety of acupuncture for children with mumps, and so these results may not be generalised to clinical practice.

    Although we did not identify any good quality randomised, double‐blind, controlled trials of acupuncture for the treatment of children with mumps, and most of the purported RCTs reported comparable baseline data including age, duration of disease and the timing of interventions, there was no clear distinction between inclusion and exclusion criteria, no standard efficacy criteria and no specific description of primary and secondary outcomes that would have an impact on the results. No adverse effects from acupuncture were reported during treatment or after long‐term follow‐up, which is an important issue. Future studies should have a follow‐up time of at least three months to measure clinically important outcomes. In addition, we also suggest that reducing the incidence of complications, particularly male sterility, should be considered as an important outcome in the treatment of mumps.

    Reporting of the excluded studies did not fully comply with the Consolidated Standards of Reporting Trials (CONSORT) statement (Wang 2011). There was no clear information about the methods of allocation concealment, sequence generation or blinding in the trials. The baseline characteristics of the acupuncture and control groups were not reported separately. We think that it is important to compare the key baseline characteristics of the two groups to evaluate the success of randomisation, especially for trials with a small number of participants. The effect size and its precision were not summarised by commonly used measurements, such as risk ratio. Additionally, the excluded studies did not fully follow the reporting guidelines of the Standards for Reporting Interventions in Controlled Trials of Acupuncture (STRICTA) criteria (Fei 2007). There was also poor reporting of the needle type. The studies did not specify the practitioner's background, which the STRICTA criteria suggest should be reported as the providers' training and experience will influence the effects of acupuncture.

    Summary of main results

    Currently no evidence is available to assess the efficacy and safety of acupuncture in the treatment of mumps in children. The excluded studies suggest that acupuncture may improve swelling and pain of the parotid gland and assist the body temperature to drop or return to normal at days three to five after treatment. However, because of the poor methodological quality of the trials, we cannot draw any conclusions about the efficacy and safety of acupuncture. More high‐quality research is needed.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The evidence is inadequate to allow any conclusion about the efficacy and safety of acupuncture.

    Quality of the evidence

    The quality of the evidence is poor.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We attempted to retrieve all RCTs of acupuncture for treating mumps in children. However, we only found nine trials in Chinese and one of these is awaiting classification. Our review does not include data from unpublished trials, hence it is possible that some negative results might have been missed.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We did not identify any other trials reviewing acupuncture for mumps in children.
