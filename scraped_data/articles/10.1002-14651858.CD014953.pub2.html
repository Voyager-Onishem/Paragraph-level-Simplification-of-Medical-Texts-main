Virtual reality training for cataract surgery operating performance in ophthalmology trainees

    • Background

    Cataract surgery is the most common incisional surgical procedure in ophthalmology and is important in ophthalmic graduate medical education. Although most ophthalmology training programs in the United States (US) include virtual reality (VR) training for cataract surgery, comprehensive reviews that detail the impact of VR training on ophthalmology trainee performance are lacking.

    Objectives

    To assess the impact of VR training for cataract surgery on the operating performance of postgraduate ophthalmology trainees, measured by operating time, intraoperative complications, postoperative complications, supervising physician ratings, and VR simulator task ratings.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL (which contains the Cochrane Eyes and Vision Trials Register), Ovid MEDLINE, Embase.com, PubMed, LILACS, ClinicalTrials.gov, and the World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP). We did not use any date or language restrictions in the electronic search for trials. We last searched the electronic databases on 14 June 2021.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing VR training to any other method of training, including non‐VR simulation training (e.g., wet laboratory training), didactics training, or no supplementary training in postgraduate ophthalmology trainees.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methodology. Primary outcomes were operating times in the operating room and intraoperative complications. Secondary outcomes were operating times in simulated settings, simulator task ratings, and supervising physician ratings, either in the operating room or simulated settings.

    Main results

    We included six RCTs with a total of 151 postgraduate ophthalmology trainees ranging from 12 to 60 participants in each study. The included studies varied widely in terms of geography: two in the US, and one study each in China, Germany, India, and Morocco. Three studies compared VR training for phacoemulsification cataract surgery on the Eyesi simulator (VRmagic, Mannheim, Germany) with wet laboratory training and two studies compared VR training with no supplementary training. One study compared trainees who received VR training with those who received conventional training for manual small incision cataract surgery on the HelpMeSee simulator (HelpMeSee, New York, NY). Industry financially supported two studies. All studies had at least three domains judged at high or unclear risks of bias. We did not conduct a meta‐analysis due to insufficient data (i.e., lack of precision measurements, or studies reported only P values). All evidence was very low‐certainty, meaning that any estimates were unreliable.

    The evidence for the benefits of VR training for trainees was very uncertain for primary outcomes. VR‐trained trainees relative to those without supplementary training had shorter operating times (mean difference [MD] −17 minutes, 95% confidence interval [CI] −21.62 to −12.38; 1 study, n = 12; very low‐certainty evidence). Results for operating time were inconsistent when comparing VR and wet laboratory training: one study found that VR relative to wet laboratory training was associated with longer operating times (P = 0.038); the other reported that two training groups had similar operating times (P = 0.14). One study reported that VR‐trained trainees relative to those without supplementary training had fewer intraoperative complications (P < 0.001); in another study, VR and conventionally trained trainees had similar intraoperative complication rates (MD −8.31, 95% CI −22.78 to 6.16; 1 study, n = 19; very low‐certainty evidence).

    For secondary outcomes, VR training may have similar impact on trainee performance compared to wet laboratory and greater impact compared to no supplementary training, but the evidence was very uncertain. One study reported VR‐trained trainees relative to those without supplementary training had significantly reduced operating time in simulated settings (P = 0.0013). Another study reported that VR‐trained relative to wet laboratory‐trained trainees had shorter operating times in VR settings (MD −1.40 minutes, 95% CI −1.96 to −0.84; 1 study, n = 60) and similar times in wet laboratory settings (MD 0.16 minutes, 95% CI −0.50 to 0.82; 1 study, n = 60). This study also found the VR‐trained trainees had higher VR simulator ratings (MD 5.17, 95% CI 0.61 to 9.73; 1 study, n = 60). Results for supervising physician ratings in the operating room were inconsistent: one study reported that VR‐ and wet laboratory‐trained trainees received similar supervising physician ratings for cataract surgery (P = 0.608); another study reported that VR‐trained trainees relative to those without supplementary training were less likely to receive poor ratings by supervising physicians for capsulorhexis construction (RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.15 to 0.57). In wet laboratory settings, VR‐trained trainees received similar supervising physician ratings compared with wet laboratory‐trained trainees (MD −1.50, 95% CI −6.77 to 3.77; n = 60) and higher supervising physician ratings compared with trainees without supplementary training (P < 0.0001). However, the results for all secondary outcomes should be interpreted with caution because of very low‐certainty evidence. 

    Authors' conclusions

    Current research suggests that VR training may be more effective than no supplementary training in improving trainee performance in the operating room and simulated settings for postgraduate ophthalmology trainees, but the evidence is uncertain. The evidence comparing VR with conventional or wet laboratory training was less consistent.

    • Can virtual reality training for cataract surgery supplement or replace other training methods for ophthalmology trainees?

    Key messages
    We did not find enough evidence to show that virtual reality (VR) training improves cataract surgery performance among ophthalmology trainees compared to wet lab or conventional training.

    What is virtual reality training?
    VR training uses computer technology to simulate a three‐dimensional interactive environment.

    How does cataract surgery training work in ophthalmology trainees?
    Most postgraduate ophthalmology trainees in the United States (US) transition gradually from performing specific cataract surgery (replacing the cloudy lens inside the eye with an artificial one) steps in a wet laboratory (practicing on cadaver or artificial tissues) or using VR simulators to performing cataract surgery in the operating room.

    Why is this question important?
    VR training is becoming increasingly common in postgraduate ophthalmology training, but comprehensive reviews of the impact of VR training on trainees’ cataract surgery performance are lacking.

    What did we want to find out?
    The primary aim of this review was to determine whether VR training improved operating room performance (measured by operating time in the operating room, intraoperative [during the operation] complications, or postoperative [after the operation] complications) in cataract surgery for postgraduate ophthalmology trainees. The secondary aim was to identify the impact of VR training on operating time in simulated settings, supervising physician ratings in the operating room or simulator settings, or VR simulation task ratings.

    How did we identify and evaluate the evidence?
    We searched for studies that compared VR training to other training methods such as traditional wet laboratory training or no supplementary training in a postgraduate ophthalmology trainee population.

    What did we find?
    VR relative to conventional or wet laboratory training did not impact operating times, the rate of intraoperative complications, or supervising physician ratings in the operating room. However, compared to trainees without supplementary training, VR‐trained trainees received higher supervising physician ratings in the operating room. The quality of evidence for all outcomes was very low.

    What does this mean?
    VR training is a promising intervention for teaching cataract surgery, but more rigorous, evidence‐based studies are needed to gauge its impact on key outcomes such as intraoperative and postoperative complications. 

    How up‐to‐date is the evidence?
    The evidence is current up to 14 June 2021.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    In the few and small, low‐quality trials identified for inclusion in this review, virtual reality (VR)‐trained trainees relative to those without supplementary training had shorter operating times in the operating room and simulated settings, fewer intraoperative complications in the operating room, higher supervising physician ratings in the operating room and simulated settings, and higher VR simulation ratings, although the certainty of evidence was very low. However, VR training did not improve performance when compared to conventional or wet laboratory training, as indicated by similar operating times in the operating room and wet laboratory simulated settings, similar intraoperative complications in the operating room, similar supervising physician ratings in the operating room and wet laboratory simulated settings, and similar VR simulation ratings. Relative to wet laboratory training, VR training only improved operating time in VR simulated settings and VR simulation ratings. No information was available on postoperative complications.

    Reduced operating time, reduced intraoperative complications, and higher‐rated operating performance may reduce costs and improve cost‐effectiveness of cataract surgery for patients and healthcare organizations (Abell 2014; Hosler 2012; Moore 2013). Additionally, reduced intraoperative complications may improve quality of life for patients (Heemraz 2016).

    Implications for research

    Further research is necessary to determine the impact of VR training for cataract surgery on the operating performance of postgraduate ophthalmology trainees by using a rigorous study design and measuring critical outcomes. Study investigators should report trials with appropriate details by following the CONSORT statement.

    Background

     

    Description of the condition

    Cataract surgery is the most frequently performed incisional surgical procedure in ophthalmology, with more than 9.5 million operations performed worldwide each year (Micieli 2011). Cataract surgery is an integral part of ophthalmology training; the Accreditation Council for Graduate Medical Education (ACGME) requires that postgraduate ophthalmology trainees in the US perform at least 86 cataract surgeries as the primary surgeon in order to graduate; additionally, trainees must complete 350 cataract procedures to graduate in the United Kingdom (Fahim 2016; Review Committee for Ophthalmology 2021). In the interest of patient safety, most postgraduate ophthalmology training programs transition trainees gradually from performing specific cataract surgery steps in a wet laboratory or using virtual reality (VR) simulators to performing cataract surgery on patients in the operating room (Lotfipour 2017).

    Description of the intervention

    Most postgraduate ophthalmology training programs in the US include VR training, which uses computer‐generated simulation for trainees to practice surgery (Paul 2018). VR training methods vary widely among programs, including length, timing, and amount of VR training; degree of attending physician supervision; whether training is required; and assessment of trainee performance.

    How the intervention might work

    VR provides students with a real‐time, immersed surgical training experience. Students can observe and explore clinical cases through VR without any risk to patient welfare. Applying VR technology allows students to actively observe and interact with a simulated patient, imitating real‐world situations for students to practice their surgical skills.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Many surgical specialties have integrated the use of VR training. For instance, its use in laparoscopic surgery has been associated with decreased operating time and improved surgical performance of laparoscopic surgery trainees (Nagendran 2013). Due to the highly versatile nature of VR, many ophthalmology training programs have adapted VR training specifically for cataract surgery, including use of the Eyesi (VRmagic, Mannheim, Germany) and MicroVisTouch (ImmersiveTouch, Chicago, Illinois) VR platforms (Paul 2018; Ting 2016).

    Given the growing role of VR training in cataract surgery, it is important to review the current evidence evaluating its impact on trainee performance and patient outcomes in live surgery. However, there is a lack of comprehensive reviews of VR and ophthalmology trainee outcomes. There is no Cochrane Review on this subject. Limitations of published reviews include incomplete search strategies (e.g., omitted comprehensive searches of educational and medical literature databases, conference proceedings, or both), inadequate assessment of risk of bias and quality of evidence, and the inclusion of other types of ophthalmic surgery and non‐VR types of simulation training (Lam 2013; Lee 2020; Thomsen 2015).

    Objectives

    To assess the impact of VR training for cataract surgery on the operating performance of postgraduate ophthalmology trainees, measured by operating time, intraoperative complications, postoperative complications, supervising physician ratings, and VR simulator task ratings.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included only randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in this review, irrespective of language or publication status. We excluded cohort studies, case‐control studies, and quasi‐randomized studies.

    Types of participants

    We included trials in which participants were postgraduate ophthalmology trainees, generally known as trainees, excluding students who were in rotations and ophthalmologists who had completed their specialty training. 

    Types of interventions

    We included trials comparing VR training to any other method of training, including non‐VR simulation training, didactics training, and no supplementary training. Non‐VR simulation training included mannequin simulators (mannequin heads without any computer‐generated sensing), wet laboratory (animal, cadaver, or artificial tissues), task‐trainer simulators (specialized models for repetitive practice placement), and didactics training focused on improving the knowledge base of students.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    There was no single measure of trainee cataract surgery competence. We measured trainee surgical performance in patients with cataract in the operating room using the following outcomes.

    • Trainee operating time in the operating room.

    • Intraoperative complications (e.g., posterior capsular rupture, vitreous loss, or iris trauma), defined as complications during the surgery.

    • Postoperative complications (e.g., wound leak, retained lens material, dislocated intraocular lens implant, clinically significant cystoid macular edema, and endophthalmitis), defined as complications up to 90 days following cataract surgery (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services 2016; Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services 2018).

    We did not exclude studies from the review solely because there were no available outcome data.

    Secondary outcomes

    To determine trainee cataract surgery performance in the simulated setting, we measured the following outcomes.

    • Trainee operating time in a simulated setting.

    • Simulator task ratings provided by the VR simulator.

    • Supervising physician ratings, either in the operating room or simulated settings.

    Given the lack of standardization in simulation and supervising physician ratings, we planned to calculate the standardized mean difference for these two measures.

    Adverse effects

    For patients, adverse effects of cataract surgery include intraoperative (during surgery) and postoperative (up to 90 days following surgery) complications both of which were Primary outcomes. To our knowledge, there are no known adverse effects on trainees.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We conducted the systematic review in accordance with standard Cochrane methodology.

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Eyes and Vision Information Specialist searched the following databases for randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials. There were no restrictions to language or year of publication. The date of the search was 14 June 2021.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2021, Issue 6) (which contains the Cochrane Eyes and Vision Trials Register) in the Cochrane Library (searched  14 June 2021) (Appendix 1).

    • MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 14 June 2021) (Appendix 2).

    • Embase.com (1947 to 14 June 2021) (Appendix 3).

    • PubMed (1946 to 14 June 2021) (Appendix 4).

    • LILACS (Latin American and Caribbean Health Science Information database (1982 to 14 June 2021) (Appendix 5).

    • US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov; searched 14 June 2021) (Appendix 6).

    • World Health Organisation (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (www.who.int/ictrp; searched 14 June 2021) (Appendix 7).

    Searching other resources

    We searched the reference lists of the studies included in the review. 

    Data collection and analysis

    We conducted the review according to the published protocol and reported any deviations from it in the Differences between protocol and review section of the systematic review (Lin 2021).

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors independently screened the titles and abstracts resulting from the searches using Covidence (Covidence). A third review author resolved disagreements. All citations not considered relevant at this stage were not documented, other than to be counted and noted in a flow chart. We obtained full‐text copies of potentially relevant trials.

    Two review authors independently assessed the full‐text copies for inclusion based on the Criteria for considering studies for this review. A third review author resolved disagreements. We corresponded with investigators to clarify study eligibility, as appropriate. We were not masked to the names of the authors, institutions, or journal publication when we did this.

    We listed all excluded studies in the Characteristics of excluded studies table and provided a brief justification for exclusion in the Excluded studies section.

    For potentially eligible studies identified on trial registers, we did the following.

    • When the study had a completion date before 2019, we looked for publications of the trial and attempted to contact the investigators when necessary to obtain published or unpublished data from the trial. If eligible, we included the study in the review irrespective of whether we could identify a publication.

    • When the study had a completion date between 2019 and 2021, or after 2021, we documented the study in the Characteristics of ongoing studies table.

    • We had planned that when only a subset of participants in a study was eligible for inclusion we would contact the investigators to obtain relevant data. However, we did not identify any such studies in this review.

    When studies had multiple publications, we collated the reports of the same study so that each study, rather than each report, was the unit of interest for the review, and such studies had a single identifier with multiple references.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors extracted data independently using an online form developed by Cochrane Eyes and Vision (Covidence). A third review author resolved discrepancies. We contacted trial investigators to request missing data. All data were imported directly into Review Manager Web (Review Manager Web), and one review author checked entries for accuracy.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors independently assessed risk of bias in each included study using Cochrane's risk of bias tool (Higgins 2011).

    We resolved disagreements by discussion.

    We specifically considered and reported on the following domains.

    • Selection bias (random sequence generation, allocation concealment): was the sequence of allocation generated using a random procedure? Was the allocation concealed to people recruiting/enrolling participants and to participants?

    • Performance bias (masking of participants and researchers): were the recipients of care unaware of their assigned intervention? Were people providing care unaware of the assigned intervention?

    • Detection bias (masking of outcome assessors): were people evaluating outcomes unaware of the assigned intervention?

    • Attrition bias: were the rates of follow‐up and compliance similar in the groups? Was the analysis by intention‐to‐treat and were there any postrandomization exclusions?

    • Selective outcome reporting bias: is there any evidence that the outcomes that were measured have not been reported?

    We graded each domain as low risk of bias, high risk of bias, or unclear risk of bias (lack of information or uncertainty of potential for bias). We contacted trial investigators for clarification of parameters that were graded as 'unclear'.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We calculated the mean difference (MD) for the following continuous outcomes: trainee operating time in the operating room, trainee operating time in simulated settings (wet laboratory or VR), intraoperative complications, postoperative complications, VR simulator task ratings, and supervising physician ratings when data were available. We did not check for the skewness of continuous data due to the lack of studies included in the analysis (Altman 1996).

    Since we did not perform any meta‐analyses, we did not compare continuous outcomes across different scales. In review updates, we will use the standardized mean difference (SMD) for continuous outcome on different scales, such as for simulator ratings and supervising physician ratings. The SMD expresses the size of the intervention effect in each study relative to the variability observed in that study. If one scale increases with severity while another decreases, we will ensure that all the scales point in the same direction either by multiplying the mean values of studies using one type of scale by –1 or by subtracting the mean from the maximum possible value for the scale.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis in all included studies was the individual. Participants were randomized to either VR training or comparator (e.g., wet laboratory, no supplementary training, or both), and the analyses were performed based on the participant. There were no unit‐of‐analysis errors identified. 

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted trial investigators of included studies if data were unclear or incomplete. We did an available‐case analysis using the data as reported when we did not receive any response within two weeks.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We examined the overall characteristics of the studies, specifically, the types of participants and types of interventions, to assess the extent to which the studies were sufficiently similar to make pooling study results sensible.

    Since we did not perform any meta‐analyses, we did not examine the forest plots of study results to determine how consistent the results of the studies were, particularly considering the size and direction of effects. Similarly, we did not calculate the I² statistic, which indicates the percentage of the variability in effect estimates that was due to heterogeneity rather than sampling error (chance) (Higgins 2002). In review updates, when meta‐analysis is conducted, we will consider I² values over 50% to indicate substantial heterogeneity and consider the Chi² P value. As this approach may have low power when there are few studies, we will consider P < 0.1 to indicate statistical significance of the Chi² test.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We used the risk of selective outcome reporting bias assessment tool to look for selective or incomplete reporting. We did not construct funnel plots or consider tests for asymmetry for assessment of publication bias, according to Chapter 13 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Page 2020), because we included fewer than 10 trials.

    Data synthesis

    We followed Chapter 10 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions for data synthesis and analysis (Higgins 2019). However, we did not pool data using a random‐effects model in Review Manager Web as we did not conduct any meta‐analyses. If data were sparse (e.g., fewer than three trials), we planned to use a fixed‐effect model, which provides a more robust estimate of effect in the case of sparse data.

    If there was inconsistency among individual study results such that a pooled result might not be a good summary of the individual trial results (e.g., the effects were in different directions), we did not pool the data but described the pattern of the individual study results.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to compare the effect of treatment in the following subgroups if there were sufficient trials.

    • Different levels of trainees' prior cataract surgery experience.

    • Different methods of VR training (structured or unstructured; combinations with other types of training; different software).

    We did not perform subgroup analysis due to the sparsity of the data.

    Sensitivity analysis

    In review updates, we will perform sensitivity analyses on primary outcomes for studies with lower methodological quality (high risk of bias in one or more domains) or studies funded by industry if there are enough studies to perform sensitivity analyses.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We prepared a summary of findings table presenting relative and absolute risks (see summary of findings Table 1). Two review authors independently graded the overall certainty of the evidence for each outcome using the five GRADE considerations (i.e., risk of bias, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) (www.gradeworkinggroup.org/). We included the following outcomes in the summary of findings table: trainee operating time in the operating room, intraoperative complications, postoperative complications, trainee operating time in a simulated setting, VR simulator task ratings, and supervising physician ratings.

    Results

    Description of studies

    A study selection flow diagram is shown in Figure 1.

    Figure 1

    Flow diagram.

    Results of the search

    We searched the electronic databases in June 2021, yielding 460 records. After removal of duplicates, we screened 355 titles and abstracts. We retrieved for further screening 20 full‐text reports, of which, we included six studies (seven records), identified two ongoing studies (two records), and excluded eight studies (11 records). One ongoing study was started in July 2021 and is estimated to be complete in June 2022 (NCT04908072), and another ongoing study was started in March 2018 and was expected to end in March 2021 (ISRCTN15327117).

    Included studies

    We included six parallel group RCTs with a total of 151 participants ranging from 12 (Adnane 2020) to 60 (Hu 2021) in each trial. Participants were postgraduate ophthalmology trainees in university hospitals, tertiary care hospitals, private practices, or a combination of these. Two studies were conducted in the US (Daly 2013; Folgar 2007), and one study each in China (Hu 2021), Germany (Feudner 2009), India (Nair 2021), and Morocco (Adnane 2020).

    Three studies compared VR training on the VRmagic Eyesi simulator versus wet laboratory training for phacoemulsification cataract surgery (Daly 2013; Folgar 2007; Hu 2021). Two studies compared VR training on the VRmagic Eyesi simulator to no supplementary training, where trainees did not receive supplemental training during the study period, for phacoemulsification cataract surgery (Adnane 2020; Feudner 2009). One study compared VR training on the HelpMeSee simulator to conventional patient‐based training for manual small incision cataract surgery (Nair 2021).

    One study was financially supported by VRmagic GmbH, although it was stated that the sponsor had no role in the research design or conduct (Feudner 2009). Another study was funded by HelpMeSee Inc and was co‐authored by an employee of HelpMeSee Inc (Nair 2021).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded eight studies after a full‐text review. We documented the reasons in the Characteristics of excluded studies table. In summary, we excluded seven studies due to lacking interventions of interest (e.g., no VR simulator) and one study due to lacking the study design of interest (e.g., not an RCT).

    Risk of bias in included studies

    The summary of risk of bias is shown in Figure 2. Our assessments of the risk of bias for the six included studies are described in the Characteristics of included studies table.

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 2

    Allocation

    Most included studies (83.3%) did not describe how participants were randomized. One study used a computer‐generated blocked random number sequence with a fixed block size of two, and the study investigators were not aware of these assignments before participants were enrolled in the study (Nair 2021). We judged the study at low risk of selection bias. We judged the remaining five studies as having unclear risks of bias for this domain because the method of random sequence generation and allocation concealment before randomization were not described.

    Blinding

    The nature of the intervention prevented masking of participants and study personnel; therefore, we judged all studies at high risk of performance bias. Three trials were at low risks of detection bias because outcome assessors were adequately masked (Daly 2013; Feudner 2009; Nair 2021). The remaining three studies did not provide sufficient information to permit judgment on masking of outcome assessors (Adnane 2020; Folgar 2007; Hu 2021). We judged these studies to have an unclear risk of detection bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    We judged four studies (66.7%) at low risk of attrition bias because there were no missing outcome data and they were free of incomplete outcome reporting (Adnane 2020; Daly 2013; Feudner 2009; Hu 2021). We judged one study at high risk of bias for this domain as two participants (16.7%) in the simulator group and three participants (25%) in the control group were not included in the final analysis (Nair 2021). The remaining one study did not provide sufficient information to permit judgment on attrition bias (i.e. unclear risk of bias) (Folgar 2007).

    Selective reporting

    Five studies provided insufficient information to permit judgment on selective outcome reporting and were judged at unclear risk of bias (Adnane 2020; Daly 2013; Feudner 2009; Folgar 2007; Hu 2021). One study demonstrated inadequate reporting bias because not all outcomes specified in the trial registry were reported in the published article (Nair 2021). 

    Other potential sources of bias

    We did not assess this domain in accordance with updated Cochrane Handbook (Higgins 2019).

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Virtual reality training compared with wet laboratory or no supplementary training in ophthalmology trainees for cataract surgery

    All the evidence was very uncertain because studies were small, poorly reported, and possibly yielded inconsistent results.

    Trainee operating time in the operating room

    One RCT provided data for operating time in the operating room (Adnane 2020). In this study, six VR‐trained trainees had significantly shorter operating times than six trainees who did not receive supplementary training (MD −17 minutes, 95% CI −21.62 to −12.38; Analysis 1.1). 

    An additional two studies measured this outcome for VR and wet laboratory training, but we were unable to perform meta‐analysis because one study did not provide precision measured (Daly 2013), and another study only reported the P value (Folgar 2007). Daly 2013 found that VR‐trained trainees had significantly longer operating times (mean 9.48 minutes) than wet laboratory‐trained trainees (mean 4.70 minutes) (P = 0.038), and Folgar 2007 found similar operating times between VR‐ and wet laboratory‐trained trainees (P = 0.14).

    The certainty the evidence for these outcomes was very low, downgraded one level due to inconsistency of results, one level due to imprecision of results, and one level due to risk of bias because most domains were judged to be unclear.

    Intraoperative complications, defined as complications during the surgery

    One RCT measured intraoperative complications (Nair 2021). The number of total errors made during each trainee's first 20 cataract surgery procedures was similar among VR‐trained (mean 9.25, 95% CI 0 to 18.95) and conventionally trained trainees (mean 17.56, 95% CI 6.63 to 28.49) (MD −8.31, 95% CI −22.78 to 6.16; n = 19; Analysis 1.2). Intraoperative complications included uveal prolapse, buttonhole incision, premature entry, Descemet membrane detachment, laceration of lateral walls or roof of tunnel, corneal endothelial touch, contact with iris, and contact with lens.

    Another RCT reported results for this outcome, but we could not combine the data because the authors did not report the number of patients with complications (Adnane 2020). The authors reported that VR‐trained trainees had a smaller number of posterior capsular ruptures (37 cases) than trainees without supplementary training (12 cases) (P < 0.001).

    The certainty of the evidence was very low for this outcome, downgrading one level due to inconsistency of results, one level due to imprecision of results, and one level due to risk of bias.

    Postoperative complications, defined as complications up to 90 days following cataract surgery

    No included studies quantified postoperative complications. 

    Trainee operating time in a simulated setting

    One RCT examined operating time in simulated settings (Hu 2021). Thirty trainees trained through VR spent less time on VR surgery (MD −1.40 minutes, 95% CI −1.96 to −0.84) and similar amounts of time on wet laboratory surgery (MD 0.16 minutes, 95% CI −0.50 to 0.82) than 30 trainees who trained in wet laboratories (Analysis 1.3).

    Another study reported results for this outcome, but we could not combine the data due to the lack of precision measured (e.g., standard deviation); we did not receive additional data after contacting study investigators (Feudner 2009). The authors reported that VR‐trained trainees improved in terms of operating time compared to trainees who did not receive supplementary training (P = 0.0013).

    The certainty of the evidence was very low for this outcome, downgrading one level due to inconsistency of results, one level due to imprecision of results, and one level due to risk of bias.

    Simulator task ratings

    One RCT measured simulator ratings (Hu 2021). Trainees who received VR training performed significantly better on VR simulators than controls who underwent wet laboratory training (range 0 to 100; MD 5.17, 95% CI 0.61 to 9.73; n = 60; Analysis 1.4). 

    The certainty of the evidence was very low, downgrading one level due to inconsistency of results, one level due to imprecision of results, and one level due to risk of bias.

    Supervising physician ratings in the operating room

    Two RCTs that measured supervising physician ratings in the operating room did not report standard deviations and were not included in the analysis. One study reported that VR and wet laboratory‐trained trainees had similar performance (P = 0.608) (Daly 2013), and the other reported that VR‐trained trainees were less likely to have a very small or poorly centered capsulorhexis (10 cases) than trainees without supplementary training (34 cases) (RR 0.29, 95% CI 0.15 to 0.57; Analysis 1.5) (Adnane 2020).

    Supervising physician ratings in simulated settings 

    One RCT reported data for supervising physician ratings in a wet laboratory setting (Hu 2021). Trainees undergoing VR training received similar scores compared with wet laboratory‐trained trainees (MD −1.50, 95% CI −6.77 to 3.77; n = 60; Analysis 1.6). 

    Another study that measured supervising physician ratings in a wet laboratory setting did not report standard deviations and could not be included in this analysis (Feudner 2009). The authors reported that VR‐trained trainees significantly improved their performance compared with trainees without supplementary training (P < 0.0001). 

    The certainty of the evidence for supervising physician rating was very low, downgraded one level due to inconsistency of results, one level due to imprecision of results, and one level due to risk of bias.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We identified six relevant studies that included a total of 151 postgraduate ophthalmology trainees. Five studies used the Eyesi VR simulator for phacoemulsification training, and one used the HelpMeSee Eye Surgery simulator for manual small incision cataract surgery. 

    Although the studies examined trainee operating time, intraoperative complications, simulator ratings, and supervising physician ratings, meta‐analysis was not possible for any outcomes due to poor reporting of the outcome data. No studies reported the effects of VR training on postoperative complications. 

    When compared with trainees without supplementary training, VR‐trained trainees had shorter operating times, fewer intraoperative complications, and higher supervising physician ratings in the operating room, but the certainty of evidence was very low. The MD in operating time of 1.4 minutes (95% CI −1.96 to −0.84) may not be clinically important. There were no significant benefits in operating room performance for trainees who received VR training relative to conventional or wet laboratory training, as measured by operating times, intraoperative complications, and supervising physician ratings. There were a few studies that measured relevant outcomes but did not provide sufficient data to be included in the analysis. Further large trials with appropriately detailed reporting of outcomes are needed to draw a robust conclusion.

    Likewise, in VR‐simulated settings, VR‐trained trainees had shorter operating times and higher supervising physician ratings than trainees without supplementary training, but again the certainty of evidence was very low. Relative to wet laboratory‐trained trainees, VR‐trained trainees had shorter operating times in VR settings and higher VR simulator ratings; however, both groups had similar operating times and supervising physician ratings in wet laboratory settings.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The findings of this review are applicable to postgraduate ophthalmology trainees and the types of VR training used in included trials, which were conducted in China, Germany, India, Morocco, and the US. However, our findings were limited by small sample sizes, imprecise description of interventions and study designs, and heterogeneity in interventions and outcome assessments. We attempted to contact study investigators for additional data but received no further information.

    The evidence indicates that VR relative to no supplementary training improved trainee performance on cataract surgery in the operating room and simulated settings, although the certainty of evidence was very low. However, VR relative to conventional forms of training such as wet laboratory training did not improve surgical outcomes in the operating room or in wet laboratory simulated settings; VR training improved outcomes in VR simulated settings compared to wet laboratory training.

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, the certainty of the evidence for primary outcomes was very low due to the imprecision of results related to the small sample sizes and the limitations of the study design, implementation, and reporting. All included studies had at least three domains at high or unclear risks of bias. Five studies did not describe how randomization was adequately performed; neither protocol nor trial registry was publicly available for these studies (Adnane 2020; Daly 2013; Feudner 2009; Folgar 2007; Hu 2021). Masking of participants and study personnel was not possible due to the nature of the intervention, although three studies masked outcome assessors (Daly 2013; Feudner 2009; Nair 2021).

    Potential biases in the review process

    We used the standard methodology required by Cochrane to minimize bias and followed Methodological Expectations of Cochrane Intervention Reviews (MECIR) standards to report new Cochrane Intervention Reviews (editorial-unit.cochrane.org/mecir) in conducting this review and reporting our findings. An information specialist from Cochrane Eyes and Vision performed a highly sensitive search to identify studies. None of the authors had any financial conflicts of interest.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    In agreement with this review, previous reviews have similarly used narrative synthesis to determine that VR training is validated or associated with improved performance (or both) but were constrained by poor quality of evidence and the lack of investigation of patient outcomes (Lam 2013; Thomsen 2015; Lee 2020). Other systematic reviews were also unable to conduct meta‐analyses due to study heterogeneity (Thomsen 2015).
