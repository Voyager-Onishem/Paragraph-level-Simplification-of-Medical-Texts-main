Perioperative local anaesthesia for reducing pain following septal surgery

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español

    • Background

    Septal surgery is a well‐established procedure used to treat nasal obstruction due to deviation of the nasal septum, which is carried out under local or general anaesthesia. Local anaesthesia is used for postoperative pain control, but its effectiveness and safety are unclear.

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness of perioperative local anaesthesia for reducing pain in septal surgery and to evaluate the risk of associated complications.

    Search methods

    The Cochrane ENT Information Specialist searched the Cochrane ENT Trial Register; Central Register of Controlled Trials; Ovid MEDLINE; Ovid Embase; CINAHL; Web of Science; ClinicalTrials.gov; ICTRP and additional sources for published and unpublished trials. The date of the search was 9 January 2018.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials and cluster‐randomised controlled trials involving adults or children (or both) who underwent septal surgery. We included studies comparing local anaesthesia versus no treatment/placebo. We also included studies comparing different types of local anaesthesia to each other (i.e. local injection, the addition of an anaesthetic agent to nasal packing, where used, and sphenopalatine ganglion block).

    Data collection and analysis

    We used the standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane. The primary outcome was postoperative pain intensity at 12, 24 and 48 hours measured by visual analogue scale (VAS) or another pain outcome tool including numerical or verbal rating scales. Secondary outcomes were requirement for additional analgesia, duration of hospitalisation and adverse effects (postoperative bleeding and postoperative vomiting). We used GRADE to assess the quality of the evidence for each outcome; this is indicated in italics.

    Main results

    We included seven randomised controlled trials involving 493 participants. In all studies the participants were adults undergoing septoplasty. These studies were heterogeneous and the quality of the body of evidence ranged from low to very low. Few of the studies provided reliable data for the primary outcome in this review.

    Local anaesthetic injection versus no treatment/placebo

    Two studies (142 participants) compared local anaesthetic injection versus placebo but these studies did not report postoperative pain at 12, 24 or 48 hours. It is unclear whether local anaesthetic injection changed the risk of vomiting (odds ratio (OR) 3.10, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.12 to 79.23; 60 participants; one study) (low‐quality evidence). Neither study reported the requirement for additional analgesia, duration of hospitalisation or uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    Local anaesthetic application via nasal packing versus no packing/packing with placebo

    Four studies (301 participants) used nasal packing postoperatively and compared the addition of local anaesthetic to the pack versus packing with a placebo added. Compared with packing with placebo, the addition of local anaesthetic to nasal packing reduced the pain score on a VAS (ranging from 0 to 100) at 12 hours (mean difference (MD) ‐16.95, 95% CI ‐22.27 to ‐11.62; 151 participants; two studies; I2 = 49%) (low‐quality evidence) and at 24 hours postoperatively (MD ‐7.53, 95% CI ‐9.76 to ‐5.29; 268 participants; four studies; I2 = 83%) (very low‐quality evidence). These studies did not report postoperative pain at 48 hours. The addition of local anaesthetic to nasal packing decreased the requirement for additional analgesia (OR 0.15, 95% CI 0.07 to 0.34; 151 participants; two studies; I2 = 15%) (moderate‐quality evidence). No studies reported duration of hospitalisation, postoperative vomiting or uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    No studies compared the addition of local anaesthetic to nasal packing versus no packing.

    Sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment/placebo

    One study (50 participants) compared sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment but this study did not report postoperative pain, requirement for additional analgesia, duration of hospitalisation, vomiting or uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    Authors' conclusions

    The addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packs (if these are being used) following septal surgery may reduce postoperative pain within the first 12 hours, compared to nasal packing with a placebo added. The effect is uncertain at 24 hours because the quality of the evidence is very low. Evidence was lacking for other outcomes, including adverse effects. There is a lack of evidence about the effects of local anaesthesia added to nasal packing compared to no nasal packing. There is also a lack of evidence about the effects of local anaesthesia given by injection and the effects of sphenopalatine ganglion block.

    • Perioperative local anaesthesia for reducing pain following septal surgery

    Review question

    Does local anaesthesia at the time of the operation (perioperative) reduce pain after surgery to the nasal septum and are there any adverse effects?

    Background

    Septal surgery is used to treat nasal obstruction due to deviation of the nasal septum. Local anaesthesia is used to decrease pain after the operation and there are several types (e.g. local injection, addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packing, if this is being used to manage bleeding after the operation, and regional nerve block). Most surgeons agree on the use of local anaesthesia, but the choice of which type to use depends on the surgeon's preference.

    Study characteristics

    The evidence is current to January 2018. We included seven studies, recruiting 493 patients. The participants were adults having septoplasty in all of the included studies and the percentage of females ranged from 20.7% to 58.3%. Two studies, recruiting 142 participants, assessed local anaesthetic injection. Four studies, recruiting 301 participants, used nasal packing postoperatively and assessed the addition of a local anaesthetic to the nasal pack. One study, recruiting 50 participants, assessed a regional nerve block. No studies were funded by the anaesthetic drug manufacturer.

    Key results

    Local anaesthetic injection compared to no treatment/placebo

    The main outcome we looked at was the effect on reducing pain at 12, 24 and 48 hours postoperatively. Two studies assessed local injection of anaesthesia, but neither reported on pain at these times. It is unclear whether local injection increased postoperative vomiting. Neither study reported uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    Local anaesthetic application via nasal packing compared to no packing/packing with placebo

    Four studies that used nasal packing after the operation assessed the addition of a local anaesthetic to the pack compared to packing with a placebo added. Four of these studies reported pain at 12 or 24 hours (or both) postoperatively. Local anaesthetic added to nasal packing reduced pain by 17.0 points on a 100‐point scale at 12 hours postoperatively and by 7.5 points at 24 hours postoperatively. These studies did not report on pain at 48 hours postoperatively. Local anaesthetic application by nasal packing decreased the need for additional analgesia (painkillers) postoperatively. No studies reported postoperative vomiting or uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    No studies evaluated local anaesthetic application via nasal packing compared to no packing.

    Regional nerve block compared to no treatment/placebo

    One study compared a regional nerve block with no treatment, but this study did not report postoperative pain or any of our other outcomes.

    Quality of the evidence

    We graded the quality of evidence for the use of local anaesthetic injection as low, which means that further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and is likely to change the estimate. We downgraded the quality of the evidence because of the small number of patients and events. We graded the quality of evidence for the use of local anaesthetic applied to nasal packing as low at 12 hours postoperatively and very low at 48 hours. We downgraded the quality of the evidence because of the poor conduct of the studies and because the results were not similar across studies. We did not grade the quality of evidence for the use of regional nerve block because none of our review outcomes were reported by the one study looking at this.

    Conclusion

    The addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packs (if these are being used) following septal surgery may reduce postoperative pain within the first 12 hours postoperatively compared to nasal packing with a placebo added. However, our review revealed a lack of evidence on which to base comparisons of the various types of local anaesthesia. This review should prompt further research comparing local anaesthesia by injection, as an addition to nasal packing and as a regional nerve block following septal surgery.

    • Implications for practice

    The addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packs (if these are being used) following septal surgery may reduce postoperative pain within the first 12 hours, compared to nasal packing with a placebo added. The effect is uncertain at 24 hours because the quality of the evidence is very low. Evidence was lacking for other outcomes, including adverse effects. There is a lack of evidence about the effects of local anaesthesia added to nasal packing compared to no nasal packing. There is also a lack of evidence about the effects of local anaesthesia given by injection and the effects of sphenopalatine ganglion block.

    Implications for research

    The results of this review indicate that when nasal packing is being used after septal surgery, the addition of a local anaesthetic agent may decrease postoperative pain intensity. However, the numbers of participants per treatment arm in each included study were limited and the studies had risks of bias in terms of their methodology. Small studies and those of low quality are thought to be at increased risk of bias. Additional high‐quality studies with larger cohorts may solve this problem.

    This review revealed a lack of evidence for the comparison of local anaesthetic added to nasal packing versus no packing. Nasal packs are sometimes used to reduce postoperative bleeding but the value of packing after septal surgery is not clear and was not evaluated in this review.

    Our review revealed a lack of evidence comparing different methods of administration of local anaesthesia. This should prompt further research comparing local anaesthesia by injection, the addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packing (where used) and sphenopalatine ganglion block following septal surgery.

    Background

    available in
  • English
  • Español

    • Description of the condition

    Septal surgery is a well‐established procedure used to treat a deviated nasal septum, which can be a contributor to nasal obstruction (Neskey 2009). Septal surgery is considered when breathing problems or snoring do not improve with non‐surgical management. It is sometimes performed in conjunction with functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS) to improve sinus mucociliary flow and ventilation.

    Postoperative pain and bleeding are common post‐surgical complications of septal surgery. Additionally, the nasal packs that are sometimes used to reduce postoperative bleeding can increase the patient's pain and discomfort (Prabhu 2007). Thus, effective analgesic strategies are needed to reduce pain after surgery. Combinations of local and systemic anaesthesia may afford adequate postoperative pain control. Local anaesthetic options include the injection or local application (via nasal packing) of an anaesthetic agent, or a sphenopalatine block/regional block (Buchanan 2005; Kesimci 2012). The choice depends on the surgeon's preference, but also on the efficacy and safety of each technique.

    Description of the intervention

    Local anaesthesia is used to decrease the pain resulting from surgery and nasal packing. Lidocaine, bupivacaine or ropivacaine are often used for local anaesthesia during septal surgery (Karaman 2011). In this review we will focus on local anaesthesia by injection, the addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packing where this is used and sphenopalatine block (Buchanan 2005; Kesimci 2012).

    How the intervention might work

    Local injection of an anaesthetic agent at the surgical site is often used before septal surgery to obtain adequate pain control. The sites include the nasal septum mucosa, nasal septum cartilage and bone.

    Local application is achieved via the nasal packs that are sometimes used to manage postoperative bleeding, which can be infiltrated with anaesthetic agents. These agents come into contact with the surface of the sinus and paranasal sinus and provide an anaesthetic effect.

    A sphenopalatine ganglion block is administered via transpalatal injection of the pterygopalatine fossa. This injection provides anaesthetic agents to the posterior septum, middle turbinate, sphenoid sinus and posterior ethmoid cavity (Kennedy 2012). The analgesic effects obtained depend on the concentration, dose and type of anaesthetic agent.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Perioperative local anaesthesia is often used for postoperative pain management in septal surgery. However, relevant systematic reviews, including Cochrane systematic reviews, on this topic are currently lacking. The effectiveness and safety of local anaesthesia in septal surgery is therefore unclear. Moreover, it is also unclear which procedure for the administration of local anaesthesia is better for obtaining pain relief. It is therefore important to establish the effectiveness of local anaesthesia in septal surgery to inform evidence‐based practice. The aim of this review is to accumulate quality evidence on the topic, to contribute to the literature on adequate postoperative pain management in septal surgery.

    available in

    • Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and cluster‐randomised controlled trials. If a cluster‐randomised controlled trial included only two clusters, we planned to exclude it. We excluded quasi‐randomised controlled trials.

    Types of participants

    We included all adult and child patients undergoing septal surgery in any setting for any indication. We included studies in which participants also underwent turbinate surgery.

    We excluded studies in which participants underwent septorhinoplasty because the surgical incision of septorhinoplasty is different from septal surgery. We excluded studies in which participants also underwent FESS. We excluded studies in which patients received nasal polypectomy, antral lavage or submucous diathermy in addition to septal surgery because these procedures would affect postoperative pain.

    Types of interventions

    Anaesthesia included local injection, addition to nasal packing where this is used and sphenopalatine ganglion block using commercially available agents. We defined a sphenopalatine ganglion block as a regional block performed via the oral cavity and through the descending/greater palatine canal. We excluded discontinued agents.

    Local anaesthetic agents have different durations of action and we classified them as short‐ or long‐acting as follows:

    • short‐acting: duration of action of less than four hours, e.g. procaine, chloroprocaine, tetracaine, lidocaine, mepivacaine, prilocaine and tramadol;

    • long‐acting: duration of action of four hours or more, e.g. bupivacaine, levobupivacaine and ropivacaine (Miller 2011).

    We excluded head‐to‐head RCTs comparing the same local anaesthetic agent with a different concentration.

    We considered the following main comparison pairs:

    • local injection versus no treatment/placebo;

    • local application via nasal packing versus no packing/packing with placebo

    • sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment/placebo.

    In addition, we also considered the following comparison pairs:

    • local injection plus local application via nasal packing versus no treatment/placebo;

    • local injection plus sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment/placebo;

    • local application via nasal packing plus sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment/placebo;

    • local injection versus local application via nasal packing;

    • local injection versus sphenopalatine ganglion block;

    • local application via nasal packing versus sphenopalatine ganglion block.

    Types of outcome measures

    We analysed the following outcomes in the review, but we did not use them as a basis for including or excluding studies.

    We considered postoperative pain at 12 and 24 hours as the main outcome for our 'Summary of findings' tables.

    Primary outcomes

    • Postoperative pain intensity at 12, 24 and 48 hours measured by:

      • visual analogue scale (VAS);

      • other pain outcome tools including numerical rating scales and verbal rating scales.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Requirement for additional analgesia (number of patients who require additional analgesia).

    • Duration of hospitalisation.

    • Adverse effects:

      • postoperative bleeding (number of patients who have continuous bleeding that cannot be controlled with a temporary gauze and requires repacking);

      • postoperative vomiting (number of patients who vomit).

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The Cochrane ENT Information Specialist conducted systematic searches for randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials. There were no language, publication year or publication status restrictions. The date of the search was 9 January 2018.

    Electronic searches

    The Information Specialist searched:

    • the Cochrane ENT Trials Register (searched 9 January 2018);

    • the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (searched 9 January 2018);

    • Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE(R) Daily and Ovid MEDLINE(R) (1946 to 9 January 2018);

    • Ovid EMBASE (1974 to 9 January 2018);

    • Ovid CAB Abstracts (1910 to 9 January 2018);

    • EBSCO CINAHL (1982 to 9 January 2018);

    • LILACS, lilacs.bvsalud.org (searched 9 January 2018);

    • KoreaMed (searched via Google Scholar 9 January 2018);

    • IndMed, www.indmed.nic.in (searched 9 January 2018);

    • PakMediNet, www.pakmedinet.com (searched 9 January 2018);

    • Web of Knowledge, Web of Science (1945 to 9 January 2018);

    • ClinicalTrials.gov, (searched via the Cochrane Register of Studies 9 January 2018);

    • World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP), www.who.int/ictrp (searched 9 January 2018).

    In searches prior to 2017, we also searched PubMed as a top up to Ovid MEDLINE 1946 to February 2015.

    The Information Specialist modelled subject strategies for databases on the search strategy designed for CENTRAL. Where appropriate, they were combined with subject strategy adaptations of the highly sensitive search strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials (as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions Version 5.1.0, Box 6.4.b) (Handbook 2011). Search strategies for major databases including CENTRAL are provided in Appendix 1.

    Searching other resources

    We scanned the reference lists of identified publications for additional trials and contacted trial authors where necessary. In addition, the Information Specialist searched Ovid MEDLINE to retrieve existing systematic reviews relevant to this systematic review, so that we could scan their reference lists for additional trials. The Information Specialist also ran non‐systematic searches of Google Scholar to retrieve grey literature and other sources of potential trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (AK and ToF) independently screened any titles and abstracts from the searches. We obtained the full text of studies that potentially met the eligibility criteria. The same two authors independently assessed the eligibility of the studies from the full text. We resolve disagreements by discussion and we consulted a third author (TaF) if disagreement was not resolved by discussion.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (TaF and YK) independently extracted data using a standardised data extraction form developed by the review authors. We resolved disagreement by discussion and consulted a third author (ToF) if disagreement was not resolved. The extraction form included the following information:

    • source: year of publication, citation and contact details;

    • method: study design, study duration, sequence generation, concealment and blinding;

    • study eligibility criteria, including participant population and surgery;

    • participants: total number, setting, age, sex and country;

    • interventions: total number of intervention groups, type of intervention, type of local anaesthetic drug, and use and type of vasoconstrictors;

    • confounding factors: usage of perioperative corticosteroid, type of anaesthesia (local or anaesthesia) and type of nasal packing;

    • outcome/measurement;

    • miscellaneous: funding source and reference to relevant studies.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    TaF and YK independently assessed the risk of bias of the included studies and considered the following, as noted in theCochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions Version 5.1.0 (Handbook 2011):

    • sequence generation;

    • allocation concealment;

    • blinding of participants and personnel;

    • blinding of outcome assessment;

    • incomplete outcome data;

    • selective outcome reporting; and

    • other sources of bias.

    We used the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool in RevMan 5.3 (RevMan 2014), which involved describing each of these domains as reported in the study and then assigning a judgement about the adequacy of each entry: 'low', 'high' or 'unclear' risk of bias.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous outcomes, we calculated the risk ratios of observable differences between the interventional and comparative groups, with 95% confidence intervals. For continuous outcomes, we calculated the mean difference and 95% confidence interval (CI). When visual analogue scales (VAS) ranged from 0 to 10, we converted them to the same 0 to 100 scale. If a continuous outcome was measured using different scales (VAS, numerical rating scale or verbal rating scale), we calculated the standardised mean difference and 95% CI and performed a meta‐analysis.

    Unit of analysis issues

    In future updates, if we include cluster‐RCTs for meta‐analysis, we will use the intracluster correlation co‐efficient (ICC) to adjust the sample sizes of the data available from the trials; if not, we will use the ICC from a similar trial or study with a similar population using the methods in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Handbook 2011). We will use generic inverse‐variance methods to perform meta‐analysis to estimate the effect and standard error of the trial. If we use an ICC from another source, we will conduct sensitivity analysis to check the effect of variation in the ICC. If little heterogeneity exists, we will try to combine individual RCTs and cluster‐RCTs. We will also conduct sensitivity analysis to investigate the effects of the randomisation unit.

    If included studies had multiple interventions, we analysed the relevant intervention group only. If there were more than two intervention groups, we split the control group into two or more groups with smaller sample sizes to make reasonably independent comparisons. If we include multiple control group (placebo and no intervention) RCTs in the future updates, we will combine groups to create single pair‐wise comparisons.

    Dealing with missing data

    We specified levels of attrition for included trials. We performed sensitivity analysis to check the impact of including trials with high levels of attrition data on the intervention effect. For missing or unavailable data, we contacted the study authors. For data synthesis, we used available case analysis.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We used the I2 statistic to assess the magnitude of heterogeneity (Higgins 2002). We conducted pre‐specified subgroup analysis if substantial heterogeneity existed (defined as an I2 value greater than 60% and either Tau2 was greater than zero, or there was a low P value (less than 0.10) in the Chi2 test for heterogeneity).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We had planned to assess reporting bias using a funnel plot, however we only included four studies at most in any one analysis. In the future updates, we will assess the possibility of publication bias and other possible biases using Egger's asymmetry test if 10 or more studies are included in an analysis (Egger 1997).

    Data synthesis

    We conducted meta‐analysis using forest plots with the Review Manager software for those studies reporting similar outcome measures (RevMan 2014). We used a fixed‐effect model to conduct meta‐analysis if the included studies were reasonably similar or had small sample sizes or there were few studies, but otherwise we had planned to use a random‐effects model. We presented the results as the average treatment effect with a 95% confidence interval.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Local anaesthetic agents have different durations of action and we undertook following subgroup analysis for the primary outcome:

    • short‐acting anaesthetic agent versus long‐acting anaesthetic agent.

    We classified anaesthetic agents as short‐ or long‐acting as follows (short‐acting: duration of action of less than four hours, e.g. procaine, chloroprocaine, tetracaine, lidocaine, mepivacaine, prilocaine and tramadol; long‐acting: duration of action of four hours or more, e.g. bupivacaine, levobupivacaine and ropivacaine (Miller 2011)).

    We took into consideration several potential sources of heterogeneity. If statistical heterogeneity had been identified in the primary outcome measures, we had planned to undertake the following subgroup analyses where possible:

    • use of vasoconstrictors versus no use of vasoconstrictors;

    • septal surgery without turbinate surgery versus septal surgery with turbinate surgery;

    • use of perioperative corticosteroid versus no use of corticosteroid;

    • use of nasal packs versus no use of nasal packs.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We conducted sensitivity analysis for the primary outcomes based on the 'Risk of bias' assessment for incomplete outcome data (high attrition rates, defined as > 10%). We performed the sensitivity analysis with or without studies with high attrition rates in the analyses to see if there was any difference in the overall results.

    GRADE and 'Summary of findings' table

    TaF and YK independently used the GRADE system to assess the quality of the body of evidence. The quality of evidence reflects the extent to which we are confident that an estimate of effect is correct and we applied this in the interpretation of results. There are four possible ratings: high, moderate, low and very low. A rating of high quality of evidence implies that we are confident in our estimate of effect and that further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the estimate of effect. A rating of very low quality implies that any estimate of effect obtained is very uncertain.

    The GRADE approach rates evidence from RCTs that do not have serious limitations as high quality. However, several factors can lead to the downgrading of the evidence to moderate, low or very low. The degree of downgrading is determined by the seriousness of these factors:

    • study limitations (risk of bias);

    • inconsistency;

    • indirectness of evidence;

    • imprecision; and

    • publication bias.

    We constructed 'Summary of findings' (SoF) tables for the three comparisons 'Local anaesthetic injection compared to no treatment/placebo', 'Local application via nasal packing compared to no treatment/placebo' and 'Sphenopalatine ganglion block compared to no treatment/placebo'.

    We presented the outcomes postoperative pain at 12, 24 and 48 hours postoperatively, duration of hospitalisation and adverse effects in the 'Summary of findings' (SoF) tables.

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    The search identified 3255 studies and we removed 1937 duplicates. Of the remaining 1318 citations, we identified 44 studies as potentially eligible for inclusion after title and abstract screening. We assessed these 44 studies by full‐text screening and identified seven studies that met the inclusion criteria (Figure 1). We excluded 37 studies after full‐text assessment (Excluded studies). No studies are awaiting assessment and we did not identify any ongoing studies.

    Included studies

    See Characteristics of included studies.

    We included seven studies in the review (Dadgarnia 2016; Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014).

    Design

    All of the included studies were randomised controlled trials. We did not identify any cluster‐randomised controlled trials. One study had four arms (Karaman 2011), two studies had three arms (Yildiz 2016; Younas 2014), and the others had two arms.

    Sample size

    The numbers of participants in the included studies were as follows: 50 (Dadgarnia 2016), 60 (Ekmekci 2013), 82 (Gungor 2016), 143 (Karaman 2011), 60 (only 37 patients were in groups compared in this review) (Yildiz 2016), 41 (Yilmaz 2010), and 120 (only 80 patients were in groups compared in this review) (Younas 2014). Yilmaz 2010 and Ekmekci 2013 calculated a sample size, but the other studies did not describe sample size calculation.

    Setting

    Dadgarnia 2016 was undertaken in Iran and Younas 2014 in Pakistan. The other studies were undertaken in Turkey. Septal surgery was performed under general anaesthesia in all seven studies (Dadgarnia 2016; Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014).

    Participants

    The participants were adults in all of the included studies. Gungor 2016 included participants aged 17 to 58, Karaman 2011 included participants aged 17 to 57, and Younas 2014 included participants aged 18 to 55. The other studies showed the mean age of participants instead of a range. The mean ages of included participants were 28.5 ± 10.3 in Dadgarnia 2016, 33.7 ± 11.2 (local anaesthesia group) and 36.3 ± 12.5 (control group) in Ekmekci 2013, 30.5 ± 10.6 (local anaesthesia group) and 32.8 ± 10.6 (control group) in Yildiz 2016, and 31 ± 8.9 (local anaesthesia group) and 33 ± 7.5 (control group) in Yilmaz 2010. Karaman 2011 did not state how many participants were male or female. In the other studies the percentage of females ranged from 20.7% to 58.3%.

    Five studies used nasal packing after the septal surgery (Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014). The type of nasal packing was Merocel in four studies (Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010) and unclear in Younas 2014. Two studies did not use nasal packing (Dadgarnia 2016; Ekmekci 2013).

    With regard to the type of surgery, all studies evaluated patients undergoing septal surgery. No study evaluated those undergoing septal surgery with turbinate surgery.

    Intervention

    The included studies varied in the type of local anaesthesia used. Two studies used local injection (Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016). Local anaesthesia was used for infiltration pre‐incisionally in Ekmekci 2013 and the timing of local anaesthesia was unclear in Gungor 2016. The local anaesthetic was applied via nasal packing in four studies (Karaman 2011; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014; Yildiz 2016). Of these studies, three used Merocel tampons (Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010), and the details of nasal packing were not provided in one study (Younas 2014). The anaesthetic agents were applied only once after surgery in most studies. However, Karaman 2011 applied the anaesthetic at the end of septal surgery and it was reapplied at eight hours postoperatively. One study evaluated a sphenopalatine ganglion block (Dadgarnia 2016). We did not identify any studies comparing combinations of multiple types of local anaesthesia versus no treatment/placebo. We did not identify any studies comparing different types of local anaesthesia.

    Five studies evaluated short‐acting anaesthetic agents (Dadgarnia 2016; Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Younas 2014). Two studies evaluated long‐acting anaesthetic agents (Karaman 2011; Yilmaz 2010). Tramadol was evaluated in one study (Ekmekci 2013).

    Outcomes

    Five studies measured our primary outcome, postoperative pain intensity (Ekmekci 2013; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014), but one study did not measure postoperative pain intensity at 12 and 24 hours as we required for this review (Ekmekci 2013). Gungor 2016 evaluated whether local anaesthesia improved the septoplasty procedure (e.g. mucosal injuries and the quality of the surgical field) and did not measure postoperative pain. Dadgarnia 2016 evaluated the efficacy of local anaesthesia for haemostasis and did not measure postoperative pain.

    Requirement for additional analgesia was measured in one study (Karaman 2011). Duration of hospitalisation was not reported in any studies. Postoperative vomiting (number of patients who vomit) was measured in one study (Ekmekci 2013). Postoperative bleeding (number of patients who had continuous bleeding that could be controlled with a temporary gauze and required repacking) was not reported in any studies.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 37 studies after full‐text assessment. Four studies evaluated local anaesthesia in septal surgery but did not have a placebo/no treatment comparison (Bizakis 2004; Drivas 2007; Erkul 2010; Yilmaz 2008a). Some studies included patients having FESS (Ahmed 2007; Demiraran 2008; Javer 2009; Koputan 2012; Ma 2014; Wang 2009), or septorhinoplasty (Apuhan 2011; Bicer 2011; Boselli 2016; Cekic 2013; Granier 2009; Sener 2008), and we thus excluded them. The details of the excluded studies are presented in the Characteristics of included studies table.

    Kuo 1995 recruited participants undergoing septal surgery, but there was no exclusion of those with trimming of the turbinates, nasal polypectomy, antral lavage or submucous diathermy in addition to septal surgery. According to our definition, studies with participants undergoing FESS were excluded in the protocol, but we did not define how nasal polypectomy, antral lavage or submucous diathermy would be dealt with. However, these procedures would affect postoperative pain and were thus excluded.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    The risk of bias was unclear in most studies. See the 'Risk of bias' tables in the Characteristics of included studies section and Figure 2 and Figure 3 for the specific judgements.

    Allocation

    Sequence generation

    Most studies did not provide an adequate description of the randomisation process (unclear risk of bias). However, Gungor 2016, Karaman 2011 and Yildiz 2016 described in detail the methods used to generate the random sequence (low risk).

    Allocation concealment

    Only three studies provided an adequate description of allocation concealment and had a low risk of bias (Dadgarnia 2016; Ekmekci 2013; Yildiz 2016). We judged the other four studies as 'unclear'.

    Blinding

    Blinding of participants and personnel

    The surgeons were blinded to the group allocation (low risk) in five studies (Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016; Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010). One study selected no intervention as the control and surgeons were unblinded (high risk) (Dadgarnia 2016). One study did not provide information on surgeon blinding (unclear risk of bias) (Younas 2014).

    Blinding of outcome assessment

    Postoperative pain was measured by the participants themselves and most studies did not mention blinding of outcome assessment (unclear risk of bias) (Dadgarnia 2016; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014). Karaman 2011 stated that patients were unaware of allocation (low risk). A blinded assessor measured postoperative pain in two studies (low risk) (Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016).

    Incomplete outcome data

    Due to the nature of the interventions and outcomes, few patients were lost to follow‐up in most studies. There was no loss to follow‐up in five studies (Dadgarnia 2016; Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014), while 5% (3/60) and 23.7% (34/143) were lost to follow‐up in Yildiz 2016, and Karaman 2011, respectively. We judged Karaman 2011 to have a high risk of bias. We judged all other studies low risk for this domain.

    Selective reporting

    The included studies did not have published protocols and it was difficult to make a clear judgement about reporting bias (unclear risk of bias). Five of the included studies reported postoperative pain (our primary outcome) although Gungor 2016 and Dadgarnia 2016 did not evaluate pain in the participants.

    Other potential sources of bias

    In one study, the number of patients in each allocation was unbalanced (high risk) (Karaman 2011). We had no additional concerns about bias in the other studies (low risk).

    Local anaesthetic injection versus no treatment/placebo

    Two studies compared local injection versus placebo (Ekmekci 2013; Gungor 2016).

    Primary outcome: postoperative pain intensity at 12, 24 and 48 hours

    Postoperative pain intensity was not measured in Gungor 2016 but it was measured in Ekmekci 2013, in which a local injection of tramadol was used. Although the use of intravenous or intramuscular tramadol is approved, local injection is off‐label. Ekmekci 2013 reported that local anaesthetic injection did not reduce postoperative pain at 12 and 24 hours, but no numeric data were reported. Postoperative pain at 48 hours was not reported.

    Secondary outcomes
    Requirement for additional analgesia (number of patients who require additional analgesia)

    Neither study reported the requirement for additional analgesia in terms of the number of patients who required this. Ekmekci 2013 measured the requirement for additional analgesia in terms of total opioid consumption at the end of 24 hours and found no difference between the local anaesthetic injection and placebo groups. However, the details of consumption were not reported.

    Duration of hospitalisation

    Neither study reported the duration of hospitalisation.

    Adverse effects

    Bleeding

    Neither study reported the number of participants with uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    Vomiting

    Ekmekci 2013 measured the number of patients who vomited. Local anaesthetic injection did not change the risk of vomiting (odds ratio (OR) 3.10, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.12 to 79.23; 60 participants; one study) (low‐quality evidence) (Analysis 1.1).

    Local anaesthetic application via nasal packing versus no packing/packing with placebo

    Four studies evaluated local anaesthetic application via nasal packing versus packing with placebo (Karaman 2011; Yildiz 2016; Yilmaz 2010; Younas 2014). No studies evaluated local anaesthetic application via nasal packing versus no packing.

    Primary outcome: postoperative pain intensity at 12, 24 and 48 hours

    Local application via nasal packing was shown to reduce postoperative pain measured on a visual analogue scale (VAS, ranging from 0 to 100) at 12 hours (mean difference (MD) ‐16.95, 95% CI ‐22.27 to ‐11.62; 151 participants; two studies; I2 = 49%) (Analysis 2.1) (low‐quality evidence) and at 24 hours postoperatively (MD ‐7.53, 95% CI ‐9.76 to ‐5.29; 268 participants; four studies; I2 = 83%) (very low‐quality evidence) (Analysis 2.2). When using a random‐effects model, the MD was ‐16.85 (95% CI ‐24.33 to ‐9.38) at 12 hours and ‐5.33 (95% CI ‐11.04 to 0.37) at 24 hours postoperatively. Postoperative pain at 48 hours was not reported in these studies.

    Karaman 2011 applied the anaesthetic agents repeatedly and this could be a factor contributing to the high I2 value in these analyses. Sensitivity analysis omitting this study, which also had high attrition, showed no clear difference in postoperative pain at 24 hours (MD ‐1.74, 95% CI ‐5.46 to 1.99; 158 participants; three studies; I2 = 43%). However, pain intensity at 12 hours postoperatively was similar in the analyses both with and without this study.

    In a subgroup analysis of short‐acting versus long‐acting anaesthetic agents, long‐acting agents reduced the pain score measured on a VAS (ranging 0 to 100) at 12 hours (MD ‐18.90, 95% CI ‐25.21 to ‐12.59; 112 participants; two studies; I2 = 56%) and 24 hours postoperatively (MD ‐11.54, 95% CI ‐14.51 to ‐8.57; 112 participants; two studies; I2 = 79%). Short‐acting agents reduced the pain score (range 0 to 100) at 12 hours (MD ‐12.10, 95% CI ‐22.03 to ‐2.17; 39 participants; one study) but did not reduce it at 24 hours postoperatively (MD ‐2.27, 95% CI ‐5.67 to 1.13; 156 participants; three studies = 3; I2 = 42%) (Analysis 2.1; Analysis 2.2).

    We were unable to carry out any of our other planned subgroup analyses.

    Secondary outcomes
    Requirement for additional analgesia (number of patients who require additional analgesia)

    Local application via nasal packing was shown to reduce the requirement for additional analgesia (odds ratio (OR) 0.15, 95% CI 0.07 to 0.34; 151 participants; two studies; I2 = 15%) (moderate‐quality evidence) (Analysis 2.3). When using a random‐effects model, the OR was 0.15 (95% CI 0.06 to 0.35).

    Duration of hospitalisation

    No studies reported the duration of hospitalisation.

    Adverse effects

    Bleeding

    No studies reported the number of participants with uncontrollable postoperative bleeding.

    Vomiting

    No studies reported the number of patients who vomited.

    Sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment/placebo

    One study compared a sphenopalatine ganglion block versus no treatment (Dadgarnia 2016).

    Primary outcome: postoperative pain intensity at 12, 24 and 48 hours

    This study did not report postoperative pain.

    Secondary outcomes
    Requirement for additional analgesia (number of patients who require additional analgesia)

    This study did not report the requirement for additional analgesia.

    Duration of hospitalisation

    This study did not report the duration of hospitalisation.

    Adverse effects

    Dadgarnia 2016 did not report uncontrollable bleeding or vomiting.

    • Summary of main results

    This review includes seven studies (493 participants) examining the effectiveness and safety of perioperative local anaesthesia in septal surgery. Two of the seven studies evaluated local injection of perioperative local anaesthesia, four studies evaluated the addition of local anaesthesia to nasal packing and one study evaluated a sphenopalatine ganglion block. Many of the studies included in this review involved small numbers of participants and had a high risk of bias in terms of methodological quality. The results of this review should therefore be interpreted cautiously with regard to the implication in a clinical setting.

    The addition of anaesthetic agents to nasal packing decreased the pain intensity score at 12 and 24 hours postoperatively compared to nasal packing alone. In subgroup analysis, long‐acting agents reduced the pain intensity score at 12 and 24 hours postoperatively, while short‐acting agents reduced the pain intensity score at 12 hours but not at 24 hours postoperatively. No studies compared the addition of local anaesthetic agents to nasal packing versus no packing. No studies evaluating local injection of anaesthetic agents or sphenopalatine ganglion block reported this outcome.

    The addition of local anaesthetic agents to nasal packing decreased the requirement for additional analgesia compared to nasal packing alone. No studies evaluating local anaesthetic injection or sphenopalatine ganglion block reported this outcome.

    No studies reported the duration of hospitalisation.

    No studies reported postoperative bleeding (the number of patients with continuous bleeding that could not be controlled with temporary gauze and required repacking).

    Local injection of anaesthetic agents increased postoperative vomiting (the number of patients who vomit). No studies evaluating local application of anaesthetic agents via nasal packing or sphenopalatine ganglion block reported this outcome.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The studies in this review measured postoperative pain intensity, but three of the included studies did not report the primary outcome of this review, postoperative pain. We tried to contact the study authors and we obtained unpublished data from one study (Yildiz 2016). This review presents pooled data for the effects of local anaesthesia but the results of each pooled analysis were calculated from only a few studies. No studies directly comparing the different methods of administration of local anaesthesia were included in this review.

    Quality of the evidence

    The evidence for the analgesic efficacy of perioperative local anaesthesia is based on studies of very low methodological quality (summary of findings Table for the main comparison; summary of findings Table 2). There was considerable heterogeneity among the studies, likely due to differences in the type of surgery and use of vasoconstrictors. We identified substantial heterogeneity in Analysis 2.1 and Analysis 2.2, which may have been due to the use of vasoconstrictors. Pre‐surgical local infiltration of a vasoconstrictor was reportedly used in all patients in the study Yilmaz 2010, but vasoconstrictor infiltration was not used in Karaman 2011. Most of the included studies did not calculate a sample size and eight studies involved fewer than 50 participants per treatment arm. Therefore, the pooled results should be interpreted with caution.　

    The overall certainty of the body of evidence relating to the efficacy of local anaesthesia in septal surgery is low. The certainty of the evidence (as assessed using GRADE) for the use of local anaesthetic application via nasal packing to decrease postoperative pain is low because of the heterogeneity of the studies and high attrition in an included study (Karaman 2011). The certainty of the evidence for the use of local anaesthetic injection to avoid adverse effects (postoperative vomiting) is low because only a single study with a small sample size was identified that measured this outcome.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We have tried to avoid bias during the review process. We conducted a systematic search in multiple electronic databases regardless of language or date of publication. Two review authors independently completed each assessment of eligibility and data extraction. However, the included studies used remedication when pain relief was insufficient. It was therefore difficult to avoid the influence of the effect of remedication and any effects of local anaesthesia should be combined with those of remedication. We could not carry out most of our planned subgroup analyses, with the exception of duration of action of local anaesthesia (short‐ versus long‐acting).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We are not aware of any other reviews that have studied this question.
