    • Background

    To support patientâcentred care, healthcare organisations increasingly offer patients access to data stored in the institutional electronic health record (EHR).

    Objectives

    Primary objective

    1. To assess the effects of providing adult patients with access to electronic health records (EHRs) alone or with additional functionalities on a range of patient, patientâprovider, and health resource consumption outcomes, including patient knowledge and understanding, patient empowerment, patient adherence, patient satisfaction with care, adverse events, healthârelated quality of life, healthârelated outcomes, psychosocial health outcomes, health resource consumption, and patientâprovider communication.

    Secondary objective

    1. To assess whether effects of providing adult patients with EHR access alone versus EHR access with additional functionalities differ among patient groups according to age, educational level, or different status of disease (chronic or acute).

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, CINAHL, and Scopus in June 2017 and in April 2020.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials and clusterârandomised trials of EHR access with or without additional functionalities for adults with any medical condition.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methodological procedures.

    Main results

    We included 10 studies with 78 to 4500 participants and followâup from 3 to 24 months. Nine studies assessed the effects of EHR with additional functionalities, each addressing a subset of outcomes sought by this review. Five studies focused on patients with diabetes mellitus, four on patients with specific diseases, and one on all patients. All studies compared EHR access alone or with additional functionalities plus usual care versus usual care only. No studies assessing the effects of EHR access alone versus EHR access with additional functionalities were identified. Interventions required a variety of data within the EHR, such as patient history, problem list, medication, allergies, and lab results. In addition to EHR access, eight studies allowed patients to share selfâdocumented data, seven offered individualised disease management functions, seven offered educational diseaseârelated information, six supported secure communication, and one offered preventive reminders.

    Only two studies were at low or unclear risk of bias across domains. Metaâanalysis could not be performed, as participants, interventions, and outcomes were too heterogeneous, and most studies presented results based on different adjustment methods or variables. The quality of evidence was rated as low or very low across outcomes. Overall differences between intervention and control groups, if any, were small. The relevance of any small effects remains unclear for most outcomes because in most cases, trial authors did not define a minimal clinically important difference. Overall, results suggest that the effects of EHR access alone and with additional functionalities are mostly uncertain when compared with usual care.

    Patient knowledge and understanding: very lowâquality evidence is available from one study, so we are uncertain about effects of the intervention on patient knowledge about diabetes and blood glucose testing.

    Patient empowerment: lowâquality evidence from three studies suggests that the intervention may have little or no effect on patient empowerment measures.

    Patient adherence: lowâquality evidence from two studies suggests that the intervention may slightly improve adherence to the process of monitoring risk factors and preventive services. Effects on medication adherence are conflicting in two studies; this may or may not improve to a clinically relevant degree.

    Patient satisfaction with care: lowâquality evidence from three studies suggests that the intervention may have little or no effect on patient satisfaction, with conflicting results.

    Adverse events: two small studies reported on mortality; one of these also reported on serious and other adverse events, but sample sizes were too small for small differences to be detected. Therefore, lowâquality evidence suggests that the intervention may have little to no effect on mortality and other adverse events.

    Healthârelated quality of life: only very lowâquality evidence from one study is available. We are uncertain whether the intervention improves diseaseâspecific quality of life of patients with asthma.

    Healthârelated outcomes: lowâquality evidence from eight studies suggests that the intervention may have little to no effect on asthma control, glycosylated haemoglobin (HbA1c) levels, blood pressure, lowâdensity lipoprotein or total cholesterol levels, body mass index or weight, or 10âyear Framingham risk scores. Lowâquality evidence from one study suggests that the composite scores of risk factors for diabetes mellitus may improve slightly with the intervention, but there is uncertainty about effects on ophthalmic medications or intraocular pressure.

    Psychosocial health outcomes: no study investigated psychosocial health outcomes in a more than anecdotal way.

    Health resource consumption: lowâquality evidence for adult patients in three studies suggests that there may be little to no effect of the intervention on different measures of healthcare use.

    Patientâprovider communication: very lowâquality evidence is available from a single small study, and we are uncertain whether the intervention improves communication measures, such as the number of messages sent.

    Authors' conclusions

    The effects of EHR access with additional functionalities in comparison with usual care for the most part are uncertain. Only adherence to the process of monitoring risk factors and providing preventive services as well as a composite score of risk factors for diabetes mellitus may improve slightly with EHR access with additional functionalities. Due to inconsistent terminology in this area, our search may have missed relevant studies.

    As the overall quality of evidence is very low to low, future research is likely to change these results. Further trials should investigate the impact of EHR access in a broader range of countries and clinical settings, including more patients over a longer period of followâup, as this may increase the likelihood of detecting effects of the intervention, should these exist. More studies should focus on assessing outcomes such as patient empowerment and behavioural outcomes, rather than concentrating on healthârelated outcomes alone. Future studies should distinguish between effects of EHR access only and effects of additional functionalities, and investigate the impact of mobile EHR tools. Future studies should include information on usage patterns, and consider the potential for widening health inequalities with implementation of EHR access. A taxonomy for EHR access and additional functionalities should be developed to promote consistency and comparability of outcome measures, and facilitate future reviews by better enabling crossâstudy comparisons.

    What are electronic health records?

    Healthcare providers maintain healthârelated information about a patient in an electronic health record, which is held in a digital form and is accessed by a computer. Healthcare professionals use these records to access all healthârelated information for a patient's care, whenever and wherever they need it. Information may include a person's medical history, medicines, allergies, test results, and immunisation history.

    Some healthcare providers let patients see their own electronic health records, usually by giving them webâbased access. Patients may also be offered other webâbased services, such as healthârelated reminders, secure messaging, and general educational health information.Â 

    Why we did this Cochrane Review

    Access to electronic health records might encourage patients to talk with a healthcare professional about their health and potential treatments, and might help them to take part in decisionâmaking. We wanted to find out whether access to electronic health records benefits patients or causes any potentially unwanted effects.Â 

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that looked at patientsâ access to electronic health records. We also looked for studies where access came with extra services. We were interested in changes in:

    1. how much patients knew, and understood, about their healthcare;
    2. whether patients felt more in control of their care (empowerment);
    3. taking medicines, or keeping up with monitoring (prevention) programmes;
    4. patients' satisfaction with their care;
    5. how patients rated their wellâbeing (quality of life);
    6. patients' health;
    7. patients' levels of anxiety, worry, or depression;
    8. how often patients used healthcare services (numbers of phone calls or visits);
    9. communication between patients and their healthcare providers; and
    10. whether patients experienced any unwanted effects.

    Search date: we included evidence published from 2000 up to April 2020.

    What we found

    We found 10 relevant studies, published between 2000 and 2016, that enrolled from 78 to 4500 adults. These studies took place in the USA (seven studies), Canada (two), and Japan (one). Five studies were conducted in doctors' offices and five in hospital clinics. People taking part in the studies were followed up for three months to two years. Two studies were funded in part by pharmaceutical companies.

    The studies focused on patients with type 2 diabetes (five studies), asthma (one study), glaucoma (one study), congestive heart failure (one study), and hypertension (one study); one study focused on patients who visited their doctor for any reason.Â 

    Studies compared usual care plus access to electronic health records against usual care alone. In nine studies, access to electronic health records came with extra services.

    We could not combine study results because of differences in how studies were conducted, types of patients enrolled, and how results were measured, so we had to evaluate them separately.

    What are the main results of our review?

    Compared with usual care, we are uncertain whether access to electronic health records affected:

    1. patients' knowledge and understanding of diabetes and of blood glucose testing (evidence from one study in 379 patients with diabetes); or

    2. how often patients communicated with their healthcare provider (one study in 107Â patients).

    Compared with usual care, access to electronic health records may make little to no difference in:

    1. patients feeling empowered (three studies; 601 patients) or satisfied with their care (threeÂ studies; 903 patients); or

    2. how many patients died or reported serious unwanted effects (two studies; 486 patients).

    Four studies (in 5466 patients) looked at how well patients kept up with monitoring programmes and continued to take their medicines. Although access to electronic health records may slightly improve keeping up with monitoring programmes, studies assessing how patients continued to take their medications showed different results, so we are unclear about effects of the intervention.

    Accessing electronic health records may not have affected how often patients used healthcare services (three studies).

    No studies reported on any unwanted effects nor on whether access to health records may have affected patients' anxiety, worry, or depression.

    How confident are we about our results?

    We are not confident in our results because of the small number of studies found. Our search may have missed some relevant studies because of differences in terms used for electronic health records. In addition, we identified limitations in the ways most studies were designed or conducted. Further evidence is likely to change our results.Â 

    Key messages

    Access to electronic health records (and extra services) may provide little to no benefit for patients' feelings of empowerment or satisfaction, nor for risk factors for diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and high pressure inside the eye (a risk factor for glaucoma). Such access may slightly increase how many patients keep up with monitoring for risk factors.

    Future studies should use upâtoâdate technologies such as mobile devices to find out:

    1. effects of access to electronic health records;

    2. for which groups of patients access is most suitable; and

    3. which extra services should be included.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    This review found lowâquality evidence for mostly little to no effect of EHR access and additional functionalities on a range of outcomes, compared with usual care. We found that EHR access may slightly improve adherence to the processes of monitoring risk factors and receiving preventive services. As monitoring is important for patient adherence and for clinical outcomes, this finding supports the need to further investigate the impact of EHR access on these processes.

    Based on these results and their uncertainty, at the moment it cannot be suggested that healthcare organisations that do not yet offer EHR access to patients should introduce this as a means of fostering patient empowerment and improving patient outcomes.

    Further research studies in various settings and with various functionalities for various groups of patients are needed. Providers also need to ascertain whether the underlying health information technology infrastructure is in place and allows patients access, and whether clinical staff have the resources to provide ongoing patient management when offering additional functionalities (e.g. responding to patient messages, monitoring patient health data). Finally, providers need to ascertain what might contribute to sustained high usage rates of the intervention, for example, by carefully designing userâfriendly interfaces. In this context, equity issues need to be considered during implementation, training, and support (e.g. by considering various levels of computer access, digital literacy, or health literacy).

    Implications for research

    Further research is required in several areas. A taxonomy for EHR access and additional functionalities is needed to provide a better description of the functionalities and context of EHR access, as this will support comparison and aggregation of evidence. Studies not only should compare EHR access to usual care but also should compare different functionalities with one another to determine the most effective functionalities and combinations for different settings and patient groups. Pragmatic randomised trials may improve understanding of the effectiveness of EHR access in a realâworld setting and may provide evidence to support clinical or policy decisions on its adoption. The impact of EHR access should be investigated in a broader range of countries (i.e. outside the United States) and clinical settings (i.e. also in inpatient areas), with larger numbers of patients and longer periods of followâup, as this may increase the likelihood of detecting effects, if they exist. More studies should focus on the impact of EHR access on patient empowerment and patient activation and on an even broader range of behaviourârelated outcomes (e.g. seeking additional information before an appointment, documenting fitness activities), in addition to healthârelated outcomes, as according to selfâefficacy theory, changes in behaviourârelated outcomes may be visible earlier than changes in healthârelated outcomes. Additionally, as the included studies used various instruments, metaâreviews should be conducted to review, assess, and recommend suitable instruments for measuring patient empowerment and patient activation. Future studies should distinguish more clearly between effects of EHR access only and effects of additional functionalities (such as reminders or secure communication) and should investigate the impact of increasing use of mHealth (mobile health tools for patients) for easier EHR access. Future studies should present more information on usage patterns and on issues of equity by considering factors that may influence the use or nonâuse of EHRs when patients are provided access. Given that EHR access seems likely to become "usual care" in some countries soon, research should focus not only on whether an effect is measurable, but on which factors contribute to any effect (e.g. by combining qualitative and quantitative research). In this context, the theoretical assumptions underpinning any impact of EHR access may need to be revised by adding variables to better reflect the complex relationships between EHR access, patient empowerment, and healthârelated outcomes.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    This review looks at the effects of access to an institutional electronic health record (EHR) on adult patients, irrespective of their diagnosis or the type of healthcare organisation where they are treated.

    Description of the intervention

    The progress of modern information technology (IT) is changing healthcare. Healthcare depends strongly on the collection, storage, distribution, and analysis of healthârelated information (Winter 2011). Healthârelated information concerning a specific patient is typically managed by an electronic health record (EHR). An EHR is defined as the "electronic collection of healthârelated data relating to one subject of care, i.e. the patient" (Winter 2011). An EHR is maintained by a healthcare provider and provides healthcare professionals with realâtime, patientâcentred access to all healthârelated data relevant for patient care whenever and wherever needed.

    Healthârelated data within an EHR comprise, among other things, clinical assessments, laboratory results, radiology findings, nursing documentation, allergy information, medication information, and discharge letters. These data are called 'clinical data', as they are related to clinical care. However, an EHR may also contain general healthârelated data, such as data on lifestyle habits (e.g. eating, drinking, physical activity).

    Patientâcentred care has gained importance in both research and clinical practice (Scholl 2014). Patientâcentred care may have positive effects on clinical outcomes (Rathert 2013). However, the concept of patientâcentredness is not well defined and is based, among other things, on concepts such as patient empowerment, patient participation, or shared decisionâmaking (Castro 2016; Scholl 2014).

    Two important activities that foster patientâcentred care are patient information (e.g. sharing of information and knowledge between clinician and patient) and patient involvement in care (e.g. active participation of patients in consultations and shared decisionâmaking) (Scholl 2014). One precondition for both patient information and patient involvement is patients having access to information about their clinical history and recent treatment. When patients have access to these healthârelated data, it is expected that they may be better able to contribute to shared decisionâmaking (Rigby 2015).

    In many countries, patients have the legal right to access their own health information. Webâbased EHR access would enable patients to exercise this right. To better facilitate patientâcentred care, healthcare organisations have started to offer patients access to their data that are stored in the institutional EHR. Patients can access the data, read and print them, and download or integrate the data into any (electronic or paperâbased) type of patientâheld record (Ammenwerth 2012). This EHR access by patients is typically webâbased, allowing them to independently access these healthârelated data via the Internet from the community.

    In addition to offering access to healthârelated data (in this review, called 'EHR access alone'), a healthcare organisation may offer further webâbased services to the patient, including medication refill requests, appointment bookings, secure messaging, personal healthârelated reminders, individual therapeutic recommendations, personal diaries, and social networking with other patients. In this review, this is called 'EHR access with additional functionalities'.

    The interface providing EHR access (alone or with additional functionalities) is called a patient portal (Rigby 2015). A patient portal is defined as a webâbased application that allows patients to access their healthârelated data stored in the EHR of a healthcare organisation. It may offer additional functionalities. To allow a more specific description of the intervention, we used the following list of typical functionalities in which the first denotes EHR access alone and thus is the focus of this review; the remaining denote additional functionalities that may be offered to the patient: access (access to healthârelated data), remind (preventive health maintenance reminders), request (transactional services), communicate (biâdirectional messaging service), share (patient selfâdocumentation), manage disease (individualised disease management functions), and educate (general educational healthârelated information).

    EHR access is the focus of the review; however, as EHR access is very often combined with additional functionalities, we analysed all studies that include EHR access, with or without one or more of the additional functionalities listed above.

In these cases, the target of the intervention is not the patient, but relatives or other informal caregivers, and a direct patientârelated outcome (e.g. for patient empowerment) cannot easily be expected. For this review, we focused on adult patients as users only, excluding studies in which relatives or informal caregivers were identified as major targets of the intervention.

    Besides healthcare organisations, EHR access may be offered on a national scale. Several countries, including Austria, Australia, Denmark, and Sweden, have already started eHealth projects to make selected healthârelated data from various healthcare organisations available to their citizens via the web (Ammenwerth 2020; Moen 2013). These national initiatives often focus on EHR access alone. For example, the Austrian EHR offers patients access to discharge letters, lab findings, radiological reports, and medication information. The Australian EHR allows patients access to diagnoses, medications, brief medical history, and pathology results (Ammenwerth 2020). We planned to include such nationalâlevel interventions in the review, as they combine healthârelated data from more than one healthcare organisation, and thus their impact may be quite different from EHR access provided by a specific healthcare organisation.

    This review uses the term EHR (electronic health record) throughout to denote the electronic collection of healthârelated data about one recipient of care. In the literature, other terms may be used to refer to related concepts, such as electronic medical record (EMR) or personal health record (PHR). The World Health Organization (WHO) uses the following definition: "EMRs are inâhouse electronic versions of the traditional paper charts that collect, store and display patient information," and "EHRs include additional information about the broader spectrum of health from all clinicians involved in an individual's care and can be shared electronically with other authorized health professionals" (WHO 2016). A PHR can be defined as "an electronic application through which individuals can access, manage, and share their health information" (Markle 2003). But as said, these definitions are not consistently used in the literature. Thus, to promote consistency, we use the term 'EHR' throughout this review, while acknowledging that other terms may be in use. We included all studies that evaluate patient access independently of how study authors name the institutional application (e.g. EHR, EMR, PHR). We used the list of functionalities as described above to consistently collect detailed information on the type and functionality of the application.

    How the intervention might work

    No uniform theory exists on how EHR access alone or with additional functionalities might contribute to patientâcentred care and related concepts such as patient empowerment or patient participation. However, some qualitative studies and reviews suggest possible mechanisms through which these and other outcomes may be affected.

    As defined, EHR access alone gives patients access to their healthârelated data. This transparency of information may help patients to ensure the accuracy of the stored data (e.g. by identifying missing or erroneous medication data). It may also enable patients to better manage their care (e.g. by allowing patients to monitor their laboratory values) and may improve patient empowerment (e.g. by helping patients to identify topics on which they would like more information, by allowing patients to ask questions about their healthcare). Transparent information can also improve patient adherence (e.g. regarding medication intake or adherence to guidelines). Such changes may, in turn, lead to improved clinical outcomes (Liyanage 2019; Mold 2015; OtteâTrojel 2014; OtteâTrojel 2015).

    The additional functionalities described above that can be delivered together with EHR access may have further or additive effects. Reminders (e.g. to support medicine intake, to remind patients of scheduled examination attendance) may increase the effectiveness of presented information, support patient adherence, and facilitate shared decisionâmaking. Easy communication with a provider (e.g. by secure eâmail) may enhance interpersonal continuity of care, strengthen trust and the doctorâpatient relationship, and improve patient satisfaction and clinical outcomes. Diseaseâspecific healthârelated information may improve the patient's knowledge and understanding of health and treatment options. Also, patient selfâdocumentation may reveal and help to decrease adverse events (e.g. by allowing the doctor to better monitor medication and related effects). Finally, providing organisational information may make the patient's navigation through the healthcare system easier, decrease costs, and improve service convenience for the patient (Ammenwerth 2012; Liyanage 2019; Mold 2015; OtteâTrojel 2014; OtteâTrojel 2015).

    In contrast, as a possible adverse effect, EHR access may increase feelings of confusion and anxiety when patients read clinical information that is unclear to them (Mold 2015). In addition, EHR access, like many other eHealth solutions, is created for "people like me". These eHealth solutions, therefore, may only address the needs of "wellâeducated and wellâtoâdo [wealthy] users rather than the needs of the most disadvantaged in society (the disempowered, disengaged, and disconnected)" (Showell 2013). For example, a study on the patient portal of Kaiser Permanente Georgia showed that portal users were significantly more likely to be white and to have a higher level of education (Roblin 2009).

    Thus, different levels of access and of proficiency in the use of IT, but also different levels of health literacy to appropriately interpret the content provided in the EHR, may influence the frequency of use and the potential benefits of access to EHR. Education or socioeconomic status, therefore, might have an influence on the benefit of EHR access (Graetz 2016). In addition, physical and mental illnesses might restrict access and use. All the above indicate that EHR access might have the potential to widen health disparities (i.e. make health inequities worse). It seems likely that people with chronic conditions who use healthcare services more frequently, and for whom longâterm and regular monitoring of their disease might be an issue, may benefit more than people who have only occasional contact with the healthcare system (Riippa 2014).

    Any benefits attained from EHR access are closely linked to the quantity of data made accessible. Usually, national EHR systems first need to be populated with patient data, which requires time. Institutional EHR systems, in contrast, normally allow immediate access to healthârelated data that have already been in the EHR system for some time. We would not expect that an impact evaluation of EHR access would be conducted and published when the EHR does not yet include some healthârelated data for the majority of targeted patients; still, we carefully looked for information on the quantity of data for how many patients was made available in the EHR, intending to exclude studies in which the EHR comprised only data for at least 20% of the intended target group.

    This review examines EHR access as offered by a healthcare organisation. Whether or not this offer is accepted and used by patients also depends on the usability of the interface that is offered to the patient. Thus, this review also aims to assess information on features of the EHR, including any reported usability issues.

    EHR access differs in several ways from earlier attempts to give patients paper copies of their patient records. First, EHR access is possible via the Internet and thus is independent of place (as Internet access is ubiquitous) and time (Internet access is possible 24 hours a day/7 days a week). Second, EHR access is provided automatically to the patient, but providing paperâbased copies may involve additional workload for the provider. Third, EHR access can be combined with additional functionalities, thus holding the potential to be tailored to patients' needs and to promote more responsive, patientâcentred care.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Currently, evidence regarding the effects of providing EHR access alone or with additional functionalities to adult patients is insufficient. Thus, healthcare organisations do not know whether it is worthwhile to invest in providing EHR access to their patients. Also, patients do not know which effects they can expect when using EHR access in different configurations. It is especially unclear which effects and adverse effects EHR access alone or with additional functionalities may confer, and which groups of patients may benefit from these interventions. Additionally, it is not sufficiently clear whether providing EHR access has only beneficial effects, or whether this access needs to be supplemented with additional functionalities to be effective.

    The impact of providing patients with EHR access alone or with additional functionalities has not been addressed, to our knowledge, by Cochrane Reviews; however, a number of Cochrane Reviews have examined related topics (Adoun 2011; Boyle 2014; GonÃ§alves Bradley 2015; Goyder 2015; Murray 2005; Nasser 2010). Murray 2005 reviewed Interactive Health Communication Applications for people with chronic disease while focusing on information packages for patients as the intervention; this is not related to EHR access. Nasser 2010 reviewed patient record systems while focusing on the impact of electronic patient records in dental practice. This review compared paperâbased records with electronic records, and patientâheld records with practiceâheld records. However, the review authors did not analyse in greater detail EHR access to practiceâheld records, which is included in the scope of our review. Adoun 2011 reviewed promoting sexual and reproductive health and preventing HIV infection; however, these review authors focused on providing promotional and behavioural information on sexual health via Internet/mobile tools, rather than providing EHR access. Boyle 2014 reviewed the use of EHRs to support smoking cessation, while focusing on documenting and monitoring tobacco status and providing cessation assistance as the intervention. This compares with the current review, which assesses access to EHR and its effects on outcomes but is restricted to smokingârelated data. Goyder 2015 reviewed clinical communication between healthcare professionals and focused on eâmail communication as the intervention; however, this is not related to EHR access. In summary, Cochrane Review has specifically focused on effects of patient access to an EHR. Consequently, there is a gap in evidence regarding the question that this review seeks to answer.

    There currently exist a number of related systematic reviews on the effects of EHR access alone or with additional functionalities.

    Ammenwerth 2012 reviewed four controlled studies on EHR access alone or with additional functionalities (three RCTs, one cohort study). This review found insufficient evidence to determine effects of the intervention on any specific outcomes. However, indications suggested better patient adherence and lower health resource consumption. These review authors did not compare effects of providing EHR access alone with effects of providing EHR access with additional functionalities.

    Goldzweig 2013 reviewed 14 randomised controlled trials (RCTs) on EHR access alone or with additional functionalities and on the PHR (personal health record). This review found mixed or unclear evidence regarding patient satisfaction, patient outcomes, utilisation, and efficiency. The review authors provided a structured list of available additional functionalities besides EHR access but did not compare effects of providing EHR access alone versus effects of providing EHR access with additional functionalities.

    Giardina 2014 reviewed 20 RCTs on the impact of EHR access alone. Outcomes were equivocal with respect to effectiveness (e.g. physical health outcomes, psychosocial health outcomes, health behaviour), patientâcentredness (e.g. patient satisfaction, patient involvement in care), and efficiency. This review did not focus only on EHR, but also on paperâbased records and patientâheld records. It did not describe the intervention in a structured way.

    OtteâTrojel 2014 reviewed 32 evaluation studies on EHR access alone or with additional functionalities (including not only RCTs but also observational and qualitative studies). The larger number of RCTs reflects the fact that this review also included studies in which the intervention was only one part of a more complex intervention (e.g. a diseaseâmanagement programme), or in which the intervention offered only specific communication functionalities without EHR access. This realist review identified possible effects on several outcomes, including patient empowerment and health resource consumption.

    Irizarry 2015 reviewed EHR access alone or with additional functionalities in general. The review authors included all types of studies (including descriptive and qualitative studies). Their review found 120 studies and summarised study findings with regard to adoption by patients, provider endorsement, health literacy of patients, usability of the user interface, and functionalities offered. This paper did not systematically review the effects of EHR access but did provide a narrative review of success criteria.

    Mold 2015 reviewed 17 controlled studies on the impact of EHR access alone or with additional functionalities in primary care. The review authors concluded that EHR access led to improved satisfaction, improved selfâcare, and better communication and engagement with clinicians, but found variable effects on faceâtoâface contacts. They focused their review on primary care only and did not distinguish between EHR access alone or with additional functionalities.

    Rathert 2017 reviewed 41 studies on the impact of EHR on patientâprovider communication. Only four of the included studies focused on patient access to the EHR, all of which were nonâRCTs.

    Mold 2018 reviewed 28 studies on the use and impact of having online access to computerised medical records and services for patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus in primary care. This review found that online access appears to be safe and is associated with improved glycaemic control but found lack of rigorous evidence on positive health outcomes for other complications such as blood pressure. This review focused only on patients with diabetes in primary care.

    Kelly 2018 reviewed 17 mostly qualitative studies evaluating design, use, and impact of inpatient portals. The review authors found only limited evidence supporting the role of inpatient portals in improving patient and caregiver engagement, knowledge, communication, care quality, and safety. This review focused on inpatient portals only.

    Han 2019 reviewed 24 studies, including 10 RCTs, evaluating the psychoâbehavioural and clinical outcomes of patient portal interventions. The review authors provided a qualitative synthesis of available evidence. They found evidence that portals generally support improved health knowledge and other psychosocial outcomes, such as decisionâmaking and selfâefficacy, as well as behavioural outcomes, such as medication adherence and cancer screening. This review did not distinguish between EHR access alone or with additional functionalities.

    In summary, available reviews do not provide a clear picture of the effects of EHR access alone or with additional functionalities in enabling evidenceâbased decisions on their use.

    Objectives

    Primary objective

    1. To assess the effects of providing adult patients with access to electronic health records (EHRs) alone or with additional functionalities on a range of patient, patientâprovider, and health resource consumption outcomes, including patient knowledge and understanding, patient empowerment, patient adherence, patient satisfaction with care, adverse events, healthârelated quality of life, healthârelated outcomes, psychosocial health outcomes, health resource consumption, and patientâprovider communication.

    Secondary objective

    1. To assess whether effects of providing adult patients with EHR access alone versus EHR access with additional functionalities differ among patient groups according to age, educational level, or different status of disease (chronic or acute).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    RCTs and clusterâRCTs only.

    Types of participants

    We included studies with adult patients (aged 18 years and older) who were given access to their EHR by a healthcare organisation. We included studies independent of the medical conditions of patients. We excluded studies with patients younger than 18 years, as effects of patientâcentred care on children and their parents are expected to be different from those on adult patients. We planned to exclude studies in which relatives or informal caregivers were mentioned as a major target of the intervention, but we did not identify any such studies.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies assessing effects of access to the EHR, alone or with additional functionalities.

    'Access to EHR alone' covers EHR functionality 1 from the following list; 'access to EHR with additional functionalities' covers at least one of the functionalities from 2 to 7.

    1. Access: access to healthârelated data (e.g. viewing test results, visit notes, information about condition or treatment, medical history) (this is the focus of this review).

    2. Remind: preventive health maintenance reminders (e.g. for immunisation, mammography, screening tests).

    3. Request: transactional services (e.g. repeat prescription, appointment booking, referral requests).

    4. Communicate: biâdirectional messaging service (e.g. secure messaging for nonâurgent medical questions and administrative concerns).

    5. Share: patient selfâdocumentation (e.g. manage medication list, approve content of clinical notes, upload blood pressure measurements, maintain personal diary).

    6. Manage disease: individualised disease management functions (e.g. individual guidelines, generation of an individual care plan).

    7. Educate: general educational clinical information.

    We included studies independent of study setting, that is, which type of healthcare organisation is offering EHR access (e.g. primary care, secondary care, national level).

    We included only studies in which EHR access was provided via an electronic interface and independent of a given location; this means that access is typically provided via an Internetâbased application and is not provided only at dedicated computer terminals at the hospital.

    We included EHR access in the context of disease management programmes or other multiâcomponent interventions if it was possible to isolate and report the effects of EHR access interventions from the larger intervention.

    We planned to include the following comparisons.

    1. EHR access alone or with additional functionalities with usual care versus usual care only.

    2. EHR access alone versus EHR access with additional functionalities.

    To summarise, we included studies matching the following conditions.

    1. Patients could access their own EHR anytime and anywhere (i.e. typically webâbased).

    2. Patients were the main users (i.e. not healthcare professionals or informal caregivers or relatives of the patient).

    3. EHR could contain any type of patientâspecific healthârelated data (both longitudinal healthârelated data and episodic data such as data related to pregnancy).

    4. EHR was offered by any healthcare provider (e.g. primary care physician, healthcare organisation, national healthcare system).

    We excluded studies under the following conditions.

    1. No function for EHR access was offered to the patient.

    2. Patient access was offered only to paperâbased records (e.g. printouts).

    3. Patient access was offered only to general educational information.

    4. Access was not possible from the patient's home or community.

    5. Access was not possible for a majority of eligible patients due to missing data in the EHR (e.g. the EHR contained patients' data for < 20% of the targeted patient population).

    Types of outcome measures

    We focused on five main primary outcome categories and five secondary outcome categories, each of which included a number of potential outcomes and outcome measures.

    Two review authors independently assigned the outcomes reported in each included study to review outcome categories and resolved any differences in categorisation by involving a third review author.

    Primary outcomes

    We expected that EHR access alone or with additional functionalities had the potential to impact the following outcome categories relevant to decisionâmakers and thus included them as primary outcomes.

    1. Patient knowledge and understanding (e.g. diseaseârelated knowledge).

    2. Patient empowerment* (e.g. patient autonomy, selfâmanagement, selfâefficacy, patient participation).

    3. Patient adherence (e.g. attendance rates, treatment adherence, active reduction of risk factors).

    4. Patient satisfaction with care (e.g. with treatment, with the process, with the patientâprovider relationship, with the intervention).

    5. Adverse events (e.g. patient anxiety due to unclear information, delay in treatment due to misleading information).

    *The category 'patient empowerment' covers all outcomes related to this concept. As there conceptual clarity is lacking in patient empowerment research (Riesling 2017), this category includes various concepts.

    Outcomes reported in the 'Summary of findings' table reflect all primary outcomes listed above.

    Secondary outcomes

    As secondary outcomes, we chose the following outcome categories.

    1. Healthârelated quality of life.

    2. Healthârelated outcomes.

    3. Psychosocial health outcomes (e.g. anxiety, confusion, depression, worry).

    4. Health resource consumption (e.g. number of phone calls, number of visits, number of hospitalisations, provider workload, visit length).

    5. Patientâprovider communication (e.g. quality of communication, quantity of communication).

    The timing of outcome assessment depends on how often EHR access is used and on observed outcomes. Thus it was not appropriate to define the general timing of outcome assessment. However, we deemed that EHR access must have been offered for at least one month before data were collected. If studies used repeated measurements, we limited outcome measurement to one time point considered most relevant for the outcome; this typically was the outcome measurement taken at longest followâup.

    For five outcomes (patient empowerment, adherence, patient satisfaction, a composite outcome of risk factors, health resource consumption), we found multiple contributions from the same study. We did not select a single outcome for analysis from these categories because in all cases, we considered the additional information useful to better judge the clinical relevance of reported effects. Furthermore, as we did not perform metaâanalyses, there was no necessity to choose a single outcome for inclusion in metaâanalysis.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following electronic databases: Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), in the Cochrane Library; MEDLINE (OvidSP); Embase (OvidSP); PsycINFO (OvidSP); Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature (CINAHL) (EBSCO); and SCOPUS in June 2017 and in April 2020. We limited searches to studies from the years 2000 to 2020 because EHR access via a patient portal was not available before that time (Irizarry 2015). We did not apply language restrictions, and we considered only published studies as eligible for inclusion. We tailored the search strategy to each of these databases and report them in Appendix 1 to Appendix 2.

    Searching other resources

    We searched a range of further literature sources, such as Proceedings of the World Conference on Medical Informatics (MedInfo), Proceedings of the Medical Informatics Europe conference (MIE), and Proceedings of the American Medical Informatics Association conference (AMIA). To be sure to also identify studies that did not use the chosen search terms in title or abstract, or were not indexed at all in electronic libraries, we handsearched the following journals from 2000 to April 2020: Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association ; International Journal of Medical Informatics ; Applied Clinical Informatics ; Journal of Medical Internet Research ; BMC Medical Informatics and Decision Making ; and Telemedicine and eHealth. We searched reference lists of identified relevant studies and of other systematic reviews on relevant topics. We did not identify any further studies. We searched the online trial register ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov) and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) portal (www.who.int/ictrp/en), in June 2017 and again in April 2020, for ongoing and recently completed studies.

    Data collection and analysis followed the published protocol (Ammenwerth 2017); differences from the protocol are summarised in the Differences between protocol and review section.

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors independently screened all titles and abstracts identified from searches to determine whether they met the inclusion criteria. We retrieved the full text of any papers identified as potentially relevant by at least one review author. Two review authors independently screened fullâtext articles for inclusion or exclusion, with discrepancies resolved by discussion and by consultation with a third review author if necessary.     Data extraction and management

    One review author extracted data from included studies using a predefined and piloted data collection form. A second review author checked the collected data for accuracy. We resolved any discrepancies by discussion until we reached consensus or through consultation with a third review author when necessary. We developed and piloted the data extraction form using the Cochrane Consumers and Communication Group (CCCG) Data Extraction Template (available at cccrg.cochrane.org/author-resources).

    Extracted data included the following study characteristics.

    1. Methods: study design, geographic location, study period, aim of study, description of intervention and control group, clinical setting, consumer involvement, informed consent, ethical approval.

    2. Participants: description of participants, methods of recruitment, baseline characteristics of intervention and control groups, inclusion and exclusion criteria, group differences at baseline.

    3.     4. Outcomes: data on primary and secondary outcomes and instruments used; adverse events.

    5. Identification: sponsorship, correspondence with study authors, additional published or unpublished data.

    One review author entered all extracted data into Review Manager (RevMan 2014), and a second review author checked the data for accuracy against the data extraction sheets.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    We assessed and reported on the methodological risk of bias of included studies in accordance with the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011), along with guidelines of the Cochrane Consumers and Communication Review Group (CCCG 2014), which recommend explicit reporting of the following individual elements for RCTs: random sequence generation; allocation sequence concealment; blinding of participants and personnel; blinding of outcome assessment; completeness of outcome data; selective outcome reporting; and other sources of bias. For clusterâRCTs, we assessed and reported risk of bias associated with an additional domain: selective recruitment of cluster participants under "other biases". We reported issues with comparability of intervention and control groups at baseline, especially in combination with unclear allocation concealment, under selection bias.

    We considered blinding separately for objective and subjective outcomes, as blinding may have the potential to affect subjective versus objective outcome measures differently. We judged each item as being at high, low, or unclear risk of bias, as set out in the criteria provided by Higgins 2011, and we provided a quote from the study report and a justification for our judgement for each item in the risk of bias section of the 'Summary of findings' table.

    We excluded studies that were rated as having high risk of bias for random sequence generation, as they cannot be considered RCTs. This decision is based on growing empirical evidence that this factor is a particularly important potential source of bias (Higgins 2011). We included studies scored as having high or unclear risk of bias for allocation concealment. It was not possible to conduct a sensitivity analysis because we could not perform metaâanalysis. For judgement about high or unclear risk of bias, we used the criteria in Section 8.5 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    In all cases, two review authors independently assessed the risk of bias of included studies, with disagreements resolved by discussion to reach consensus. We contacted study authors for additional information about the included studies or for clarification of study methods if required. We did not receive further information that could be included in the review.

    We incorporated results of the risk of bias assessment into the review through standard tables, systematic narrative descriptions, and commentary about each of the elements, leading to an overall assessment of risk of bias of included studies and a judgement about the internal validity of review results.

Only if effect estimates were not directly reported, we calculated them as differences between study groups.Â 

    Due to large methodological heterogeneity in measuring and analysing study outcomes, we did not perform metaâanalyses.

The relevant target readership of the review is thus Chief Information Managers and Chief Executive Officers of healthcare organizations (such as hospitals). However, patients will also be interested to understand whether EHRs are effective to inform their decisions about whether to use EHR access if offered by a healthcare provider. In addition, administrators at local government bodies may be interested, as far as they are concerned with the funding of healthcare organizations. To help ensure accessibility to a range of audiences, the protocol and review received input from a consumer referee according to standard CCCG editorial practices.

    Study design

    The 10 included studies consist of eight RCTs (Ahmed 2016; Holbrook 2009Kashiwagi 2014Krist 2012McCarrier 2009Ralston 2009Ross 2004; Tang 2013) and two clusterârandomised trials (Grant 2008; Wagner 2012). Length of followâup was between three months and 24 months.

    Sample size

    We found considerable variation in the sample size of studies. The median sample size was 214, with a range from 78 participants in McCarrier 2009 to 4500 participants in Krist 2012 (Table 1).

    Setting

    Seven of the included studies were performed in the United States (Grant 2008; Krist 2012; McCarrier 2009; Ralston 2009; Ross 2004; Tang 2013; Wagner 2012), two in Canada (Ahmed 2016; Holbrook 2009), and one in Japan (Kashiwagi 2014). All studies were conducted in the year 2011 or earlier. Five studies took place in primary care settings (Grant 2008; Holbrook 2009; Krist 2012; Tang 2013; Wagner 2012), and five were conducted in outpatient clinics or specialised practices (Ahmed 2016; Kashiwagi 2014; McCarrier 2009; Ralston 2009; Ross 2004).

    Participants

    Five studies focused on patients with diabetes mellitus (type 2 diabetes mellitus: Grant 2008Holbrook 2009Ralston 2009Tang 2013; type 1 diabetes mellitus: McCarrier 2009); one each on patients with asthma (Ahmed 2016), glaucoma (Kashiwagi 2014), congestive heart failure (Ross 2004), and hypertension (Wagner 2012); and one on patients with office visits (Krist 2012). No study focused explicitly on patients with multiâmorbidities.

    Eight studies reported mean age of participants for intervention and control groups (Grant 2008; Holbrook 2009; Kashiwagi 2014; McCarrier 2009; Ralston 2009; Ross 2004; Tang 2013; Wagner 2012). The weighted mean age of participants in the intervention groups of these eight studies was 56.8 years. Means ranged from 36.8 to 62.6 years. The weighted mean age in the control group was 56.2 years. Means ranged from 37.8 to 64.6 years. Two studies reported participant age in categories. The calculated median age in one study was 40.2 years in the intervention group and 47.8 years in the control group (Ahmed 2016). The calculated median age in the other study was 49 years in both groups (Krist 2012).

    Interventions

    Although all studies involved interventions that provided adult patients with access to an EHR, we found considerable variation in the way EHR access was provided and in the functionalities offered (Table 3). Five interventions were motivated by the Chronic Care Model (CCM) (Ahmed 2016; Grant 2008; McCarrier 2009; Ralston 2009; Tang 2013); two of these were also motivated by the selfâefficacy theory (Ahmed 2016; McCarrier 2009). One study was motivated by consumer engagement and patient activation theory (Wagner 2012). The remaining four studies did not mention any underlying theory (Holbrook 2009; Kashiwagi 2014; Krist 2012; Ross 2004).

    Allocation

    Seven studies reported an acceptable method of random sequence generation for the intervention assignment (Figure 3). Five studies reported that a computer or a random number generator generated the random sequence, one study reported use of "a randomization programme based on Pocockâs âminimizationâ procedure" (Tang 2013), and another study reported that the study statistician developed the sequence (McCarrier 2009). Three studies did not provide an adequate description of the randomisation process, so we classified these as having unclear risk of bias. Measures to conceal allocation appeared satisfactory in two studies and were not reported in seven studies; of these we classified six studies as having unclear risk of bias. We classified the seventh study as high risk because comparison of baseline characteristics suggested that randomisation did not work (Kashiwagi 2014). McCarrier 2009 reported allocation concealment to study staff during recruitment but not to the study coâordinator; therefore, we classified this study as having unclear risk of bias.

    Blinding

    Blinding of patients is very difficult for this type of intervention, as a placebo situation cannot easily be created and implemented. Blinding of clinical staff is also very challenging, as most treating physicians will get to know if a patient uses the intervention. For example, for interventions with disease management functionalities, the physician will get automatic information or information from the study nurse about the patient. Also, for all types of interventions, during the next visit, the patient may just tell the physician, discuss selfâdocumented data with the physician, or ask a question on information provided by the intervention.

    For objective outcomes such as lab values, we classified risk of performance bias as unclear for nine studies, as it was not clear whether nonâblinding could have influenced the outcome. We classified risk as low for one study that used parameters such as mortality, emergency room visits, and hospitalisation (Ross 2004), as we did not expect an impact of nonâblinding on these outcomes. For patientâreported outcomes, we classified nonâblinding of patients as high risk for performance bias for all six studies, as knowledge about group allocation may have influenced the answers on patient questionnaires used to assess empowerment, selfâefficacy, or patient health, among other outcomes.

    For objective outcomes, we classified blinding of outcome assessment as low risk for eight studies when data were collected from automatically generated data (e.g. from lab tests, EHR data), were calculated via algorithms, or were collected from a blinded outcome assessor. We classified two studies as having unclear risk: Krist 2012 collected some service delivery information via mailing. In Kashiwagi 2014, a potentially unblinded physician performed the ophthalmologic assessment. For patientâreported outcomes, we classified risk of detection bias as high for all six studies, as study authors did not report information on blinding of outcome assessors.

    Incomplete outcome data

    For objective outcomes, we rated eight studies at low risk with respect to attrition bias. We rated one study at high risk because loss to followâup was high with a range between 24% and 64% lost for all clinical variables (Holbrook 2009). We rated another study at unclear risk because no numbers of patients analysed at followâup were reported (Ross 2004). Data for resource use came from chart review without indication of completeness. For mortality, we assumed complete followâup because activities in addition to chart review such as telephone calls and eâmails were reported to complete followâup.

    For patientâreported outcomes, we considered attrition bias as high risk for three studies. All three studies had high rates of attrition (25% or higher) in at least one study arm, and two of the three studies were unbalanced on attrition between study arms. Ahmed 2016 was rated at high risk due to differential loss to followâup in intervention groups for the Miniâasthma Quality of Life Survey (followed up 57% of intervention group versus 92% of control group). Wagner 2012 was rated at high risk because there was high loss to followâup for all outcomes (followed up 69% of intervention group versus 75% of control group) except HbA1c. Ross 2004 was rated at high risk because of high loss to followâup (followed up 70% of intervention group versus 81% of control group). The intervention group showed greater loss to followâup but with no indication that this was related to the intervention.

    Selective reporting

    We considered reporting bias to be at unclear risk for nine studies, as we found no published protocols for these studies.Â For Ahmed 2016, a study protocol was available. We considered this study to have low risk for reporting bias.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We considered other potential sources of bias, and we classified seven studies at low risk for these criteria. We classified three studies at unclear risk: for Grant 2008, we found it unclear whether study authors took clustering into account in the statistical analysis. If this was not taken into account, a unit of analysis error would occur. The other clusterârandomised trial took clustering into account (Wagner 2012). Both studies had low risk of selective recruitment. In one study (Holbrook 2009), there was a mistake in the presentation of clinical outcomes in the paper. Another study presented a small inconsistency with regard to reported and calculable values of change in the number of medications (Kashiwagi 2014).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Despite the rising interest in patientâcentred information systems and their impact on patient empowerment and healthârelated outcomes, we found only 10 randomised controlled trials investigating the impact of electronic health record (EHR) access, with nine of them offering various additional functionalities, and one study â Kashiwagi 2014 â merely providing EHR access. The type of information offered to patients and additional functionalities offered were diverse among these 10 studies. We could not perform metaâanalyses, as included patients, interventions, and outcomes were too heterogeneous, and most studies presented results based on different adjustment methods or variables. We were also unable to compare effects of different types of additional EHR functionality or effects for different types of patients.

    Only 6 of the 10 included studies reported on variables defined as primary outcomes for this review: patient knowledge and understanding, patient empowerment, patient satisfaction, patient adherence, and adverse events. Overall differences, if any, were small between intervention and control groups. The relevance of any small effects remains unclear for most outcomes because in most cases, trial authors did not define a minimal clinically important difference (MCID), and evidence was of low or very low quality.

    Overall, our review suggests that effects of EHR access alone and with additional functionalities are mostly uncertain.

    Patient knowledge and understanding

    We found very lowâquality evidence from one study, and so we are uncertain whether the EHR intervention influences patient knowledge about diabetes and about blood glucose testing.

    Patient empowerment

    We found lowâquality evidence from three studies suggesting that the intervention may have little to no effect on patient empowerment measures.

    Patient adherence

    We found lowâquality evidence from four studies suggesting that the intervention may slightly improve adherence to the process of monitoring risk factors and preventive services, but effects on medication adherence are conflicting, and these may or may not improve to a clinically relevant degree with the intervention.

    Patient satisfaction

    We found lowâquality evidence from three studies suggesting that the intervention may have little to no effect on patient satisfaction, with conflicting results.

    Adverse events

    Two small studies reported mortality, and one of these also reported on serious and other adverse events, but sample sizes were too small to detect small differences. Therefore, we found lowâquality evidence suggesting that the intervention may have little to no effect on mortality or other adverse events.

    Secondary outcomes

    Eight studies reported on risk factors for diabetes mellitus, cardiovascular disease, or glaucoma. These studies provided lowâquality evidence suggesting that the intervention may have little to no effect on glycosylated haemoglobin (HbA1c) levels, blood pressure (systolic or diastolic), lowâdensity lipoprotein (LDL) or total cholesterol levels, body mass index (BMI) or weight, or 10âyear Framingham Risk scores. The heterogeneity of study populations in these studies (patients with hypertension, patients with diabetes mellitus) and different lengths of followâup may contribute to inconsistency.

    We found lowâquality evidence from one study suggesting that the composite scores of risk factors for diabetes mellitus may improve slightly with the intervention, but that there may be little to no effect on composite scores for asthma in one further study. We are uncertain about effects of the intervention on ophthalmic medication use or intraocular pressure.

    Three studies reported on health resource consumption, providing lowâquality evidence to suggest there may be little to no effect of the intervention on different measures of health resource consumption. Inconsistency in study results is evident, but the increase in emergency department visits for the intervention group in one study was probably due to chance. One study reported on patientâprovider communication, and we are uncertain about effects of the intervention on measures of communication. No study investigated psychosocial health outcomes in a more than anecdotal way.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The 10 studies included in our systematic review offer a broad range of different types of information to patients along with various additional functionalities. Most of the studies (seven) came from the USA, and nearly all (nine) focused only on certain groups of patients (among these, five studies on patients with diabetes). All studies took place in primary care settings, outpatient clinics, or specialised practices. The limited range of countries in which these trials were conducted and the inclusion of specific groups of patients in most trials mean that the results reported here may not apply beyond these populations and settings.

    Five studies were published before 2010, and five studies after 2010. The most recent study was published in 2016. This seems to indicate that there has not been much interest in conducting randomised studies on the impact of EHR access with or without additional functionalities in recent years. In the United States, the requirement to implement certified EHR systems under the "Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Actâ, which included the need to give patients access to their own data, may have made it seemingly unfeasible, unnecessary, or unethical to conduct randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in a situation where EHR access is already available for routine care on a broad scale. As most earlier RCTs came from the United States, this may explain the small number of new trials reported in recent years. Another possible explanation for lack of recent RCTs is publication bias (i.e. studies conducted but not published due to negative or inconclusive results).

    Seven studies reported that many patients in the intervention groups did not use the intervention at all or used the EHR only inconsistently. Low or inconsistent use of the EHR may explain the limited impact of the intervention on the broad range of outcomes assessed by this review. Most studies did not report sufficient data about usage of the intervention to enable further investigation of the impact of low or variable usage rates on outcomes.

    Three studies compared characteristics of users and nonâusers, for example, regarding age, health literacy, and digital literacy; results were inconsistent. If there exist differences between those who use the intervention and those who do not, there is the risk that the intervention may widen health inequalities.

    Some of the instruments used to measure patient empowerment (such as the Patient Activation Measure or the Patient Empowerment Scale) may have been unable to detect small changes in this outcome. Also, in most included trials, the study period may have been too short and the number of included patients might have been too small to allow detection of any impact of the intervention on patient empowerment.

    Patients in control groups (usual care) may have received quite variable types of care. Six studies compared the intervention to "usual" or "standard" care, without giving details on how this usual care was performed. One study compared the intervention to usual care in which an asthma nurse provided education and followâup as needed, one study compared the intervention to a personal health record that allowed review and update of family history and general preventive services, one study compared the intervention to a support system that was available to patients only while they were examined by ophthalmologists, and one study compared the intervention to a situation with receiving annual reminders for preventive services, HbA1c test results, and online messages. The care that the usual care groups in these four studies received may be closer to the intervention than the usual care received in the other six studies. In any case, the usual care groups cannot be considered to be homogenous across included studies. This variability of usual care in the comparison groups may limit the possibility of detecting an impact of the intervention for this review.

    Eight studies focused on changes in healthârelated outcomes. EHR access is meant to increase patient empowerment, and this may have an impact on healthârelated outcomes, but this relationship may be complex and may be dependent on a range of other variables such as severity of disease, quality of the patientâprovider relationship, age, and health literacy. This large number of interacting variables that influence healthârelated outcomes may also help to explain why the included RCTs were not, overall, able to detect effects of the intervention on healthârelated outcomes.

    All but one study offered additional functionalities besides EHR access. Thus we could not distinguish effects of EHR access from effects of additional functionality such as disease management functions. As we most often found little or no impact of EHR access with additional functionalities, we might assume that providing EHR access alone would have a similarly minimal effect on outcomes.

    Overall, we do not know whether study findings are generalisable to other settings (e.g. inpatient care), to other countries (outside the United States), and to other clinical or patient groups, or to patients with multiâmorbidities. EHR access may show greater impact on patient empowerment and healthârelated outcomes in other settings, for example, among those who are younger or have high levels of health or computer literacy, in studies of longer duration, in studies with more patients, or in studies that use more specific instruments to detect an impact. EHR access with additional and interacting functionalities is a complex intervention, and conducting RCTs to evaluate the impact of each functionality alone or in combination on a broad range of outcomes in a large variety of inpatient and outpatient settings may not be possible with rigorous RCTs alone. Here, more pragmatic trials may be useful. Pragmatic trials are designed to show the realâworld effectiveness of an intervention in broad patient groups to inform a clinical or policy decision on the intervention (Ford 2016).

    Quality of the evidence

    We identified only 10 RCTs and overall lowâquality evidence on the impact of EHR access, combined with additional functionalities, on a range of outcomes.

    Risk of bias was an issue for many of the included studies. We did not assess any of the studies as having high risk of bias in relation to random sequence generation, but we considered one study as having high risk of selection bias. Whether a random sequence and concealed allocation were used in this study is unclear, but considerable imbalance in the distribution of baseline variables indicates that randomisation procedures did not work well. Blinding of patients and staff about EHR access is almost impossible, thus only one study achieved blinding of patients. However, it is unclear whether differences in performance may influence objective outcomes such as blood test results; thus we considered risk of bias unclear in these cases. For mortality, hospitalisations, and emergency department visits for patients with congestive heart failure, we assumed low risk of performance bias. For subjective outcomes such as a selfâefficacy survey or patientâreported outcomes, however, differences in performance may have influenced results; thus we considered risk of bias to be high in all studies that applied subjective outcomes. Four studies showed high risk of bias due to possible attrition bias. Overall, we considered eight studies to have high risk of bias in at least one area. Risk of bias was a reason for downgrading the level of evidence for 12 of 17 outcomes, for inconsistent results in 8 of 17 outcomes, for indirectness most often due to limited transferability of results because of a small spectrum of settings or diseases in study populations in nine of 17 outcomes, and for imprecision due most often to small sample sizes for 8 of 17 outcomes.

    Overall, as the overall quality of evidence is very low to low, future research is likely to change these results.

    Potential biases in the review process

    Searching for studies on EHR access and additional functionalities was hindered by the fact that the terminology in this field is still very heterogeneous, and interventions are often poorly or inconsistently described and indexed. We searched for a large number of synonyms and related terms, but we cannot be sure that we have identified all suitable studies. The fact that many of the identified studies are already several years old may particularly indicate the potential for issues in identifying more recent studies in this area. For updates of this review, even broader search terms should be chosen, including outcomes from recent research.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Our findings are in line with those of earlier systematic reviews, which found overall insufficient evidence for any clear impact of EHR access on patientârelated outcomes (e.g. Ammenwerth 2012; Coughlin 2017; deLusignan 2014; Giardina 2014; Goldzweig 2013; Han 2019; Kelly 2018). A recent metaâreview that included 20 systematic reviews also found only mixed evidence that Internetâbased digital health interventions for patients may improve health outcomes (Zanaboni 2018).

    The most recent systematic review included 20 RCTs and focused on the impact of providing patients access to EHRs (Neves 2020). Review authors found eight RCTs that provided patient access to institutional EHRs; these eight RCTs are also included in our review. The remaining 12 RCTs analysed in Neves 2020 focused on EHRs without institutional EHR access (such as EHRs that allow patients to upload blood pressure and to receive feedback from a physician). The authors of this review found inconsistent results for patientâcentredness outcomes, no change or a reduction in healthcare usage indicators, and a reduction in absolute values of systolic blood pressure; however, the latter was lost in sensitivity analyses including only studies at low risk of bias. Review authors conducted a metaâanalysis of six studies and found a beneficial effect of â0.316% absolute reduction in HbA1c levels (CI â0.540 to â0.093) with the intervention: this was judged as clinically relevant. Results of this metaâanalysis are broadly consistent with our findings of effect estimates that ranged between â0.7% and â0.19% HbA1c. Due to the stated minimal clinically important difference (MCID) of 0.5% to 1.0% provided by Tang 2013, we considered this difference not clinically relevant. Neves 2020 noted that only half of the RCTs had an overall low risk of bias score, and that additional studies are needed to establish robust conclusions.

    Very few other reviews have found positive effects of EHR access. Kruse 2015 included 27 mostly nonârandomised studies, one of which was also eligible for our review. These review authors found a positive impact on medication adherence and a decrease in office visits, among other findings. Mold 2015 included 17 randomised and nonârandomised studies, one of which was also eligible for our review. These review authors found improved selfâcare, better communication and engagement with clinicians, and higher uptake of preventive services, but focused on EHR access only in primary care. Mold 2018 included 28 studies, of which 4 studies were also eligible for inclusion in our review. Review authors found that EHR access is associated with improved glycaemic control but is inconclusive regarding its impact on blood pressure control in diabetes patients; these results correspond to the results of our review for diabetes patients.

    To summarise, although research has suggested that EHR access may increase patient empowerment and improve patient adherence and clinical outcomes (Liyanage 2019; Mold 2015; OtteâTrojel 2014; OtteâTrojel 2015), our review as well as other reviews have not identified consistent evidence of effects on these and several other outcomes.

    The number of RCTs included in the various earlier systematic reviews on EHR access differs, with typical numbers between 5 and 20, depending on included time period, clinical setting (primary or secondary care), included patient groups (e.g. only diabetes patients, patients independent of their diagnoses), included functionality (EHR access with or without additional functionality or PHR access only), and included study designs (RCT, quasiâexperimental, and/or descriptive or qualitative studies). For example, Coughlin 2017 reviewed studies on web portals for diabetes patients and found five RCTs, none of them evaluating EHR access. Giardina 2014 reviewed studies on providing patients access to their medical records and found 20 RCTs, including studies with access to paperâbased printouts. Neves 2020 reviewed studies on the impact of sharing EHRs with patients and found 20 RCTs, including studies in which only patients uploaded data to a shared EHR. In our review, we identified 10 RCTs that focused on EHR access only or with additional functionalities, independent of patient diagnosis.

    Our review did not identify any adverse effects of EHR access on any observed outcome. The Zanaboni 2018 metaâreview also found no reviews that mentioned serious adverse consequences. Sample sizes of studies reporting on mortality and other rare adverse events might have been too small to detect rare adverse effects.

    Several reviews have noted that patients often did not consistently use the offered functionalities (AbdâAlrazaq 2019; Antonio 2019; Coughlin 2017; Goldzweig 2013; Irizarry 2015; Mold 2015; Sun 2018). This may explain difficulties in measuring impact. Reasons for limited uptake may include lack of technical abilities, awareness, and training;,different levels of health literacy, and privacy and security concerns (Powell 2017SagaguchiâTang 2017).

    Our review contributes to the state of research in several ways. We based our findings on RCTs only. We did not restrict our review to certain settings or patient groups. We systematically described additional functionalities besides EHR access, which allows better comparison of the outcomes of studies for our review and for future reviews. We conducted a systematic assessment of risk of bias and found several sources of bias, despite restricting our systematic review to RCTs.
