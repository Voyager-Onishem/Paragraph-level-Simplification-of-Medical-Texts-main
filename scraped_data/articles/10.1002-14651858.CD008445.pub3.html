Intraoperative mild hypothermia for postoperative neurological deficits in people with intracranial aneurysm

  • Luying Ryan Li
  • Chao You
  • Bhuwan Chaudhary
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 22 March 2016 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD008445.pub3
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Rupture of an intracranial aneurysm causes aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage, which is one of the most devastating clinical conditions. It can be classified into five Grades using the Hunt‐Hess or World Federation of Neurological Surgeons (WFNS) scale. Grades 4 and 5 predict poor prognosis and are known as 'poor grade', while grade 1, 2, and 3 are known as 'good grade'. Disturbances of intracranial homeostasis and brain metabolism are known to play certain roles in the sequelae. Hypothermia has a long history of being used to reduce metabolic rate, thereby protecting organs where metabolism is disturbed, and may potentially cause harm.

    Objectives

    To assess the effect of intraoperative mild hypothermia on postoperative death and neurological deficits in people with ruptured or unruptured intracranial aneurysms.

    Search methods

    We updated the search in the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Register (August 2015), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL 2015, Issue 8), WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP; December 2015), MEDLINE (1950 to September 2015), EMBASE (1980 to September 2015), Science Citation Index (1900 to September 2015), and 11 Chinese databases (September 2015). We also searched ongoing trials registers (September 2015) and scanned reference lists of retrieved records.

    Selection criteria

    We included only randomised controlled trials that compared intraoperative mild hypothermia (32°C to 35°C) with control (no hypothermia) in people with ruptured or unruptured intracranial aneurysms.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently selected trials and assessed the risk of bias for each included study. We presented data as risk ratio (RR) and risk difference (RD) with 95% confidence intervals (CI).

    Main results

    We included three studies, enrolling 1158 participants. Each study reported an increased rate of recovery with intraoperative mild hypothermia, but the effect sizes were not sufficient for certainty. A total of 1086 of the 1158 participants (93.8%) had good grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage. Seventy‐six of 577 participants (13.1%) who received hypothermia and 93 of 581 participants (16.0%) who did not receive hypothermia were dead or dependent (RR 0.82; 95% CI 0.62 to 1.09; RD ‐0.03; 95% CI ‐0.07 to 0.01, moderate‐quality evidence) after three months.

    Reported unfavourable outcomes did not differ between participants with or without hypothermia. The quality of evidence for these outcomes remains unclear because the outcomes were reported in a variety of ways. No decompressive craniectomy or corticectomy was reported. Thirty‐six of 577 (6.2%) participants with hypothermia and 40 of 581 (6.9%) participants without hypothermia had infarction. Thirty‐four of 577 (6%) participants with hypothermia and 32 of the 581 (5.5%) participants without hypothermia had clinical vasospasm (temporary deficits).

    Duration of hospital stay was not reported. Only one study with 112 participants reported discharge destinations: 43 of 55 (78.2%) participants with hypothermia and 39 of 57 (68.4%) participants in the control group were discharged home. The remaining participants were discharged to other facilities.

    Thirty‐nine of 577 (6.8%) participants with hypothermia and 39 of 581 (6.7%) participants without hypothermia had infections. Six of 577 (1%) participants with hypothermia and 6 of 581 (1%) participants without hypothermia had cardiac arrhythmia.

    Authors' conclusions

    It remains possible that intraoperative mild hypothermia could prevent death or dependency in activities of daily living in people with good grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage. However, the confidence intervals around this estimate include the possibility of both benefit and harm. There was insufficient information to draw any conclusions about the effects of intraoperative mild hypothermia in people with poor grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage or without subarachnoid haemorrhage. We did not identify any reliable evidence to support the routine use of intraoperative mild hypothermia. A high‐quality randomised clinical trial of intraoperative mild hypothermia for postoperative neurological deficits in people with poor grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage might be feasible.

    • Cooling the brain during surgery to prevent death or severe disability in people with brain aneurysms

    Review question

    We reviewed the evidence about the effect of cooling the brain during surgery for brain aneurysms. We found three studies of acceptable quality and analysed the results to see if cooling the brain during open‐skull surgery for brain aneurysms prevents death or severe disability.

    Background

    Intracranial aneurysms are bulges on the cerebral arterial wall. Rupture of an intracranial aneurysm is often life‐threatening. People with intracranial aneurysms are classified as good grade or poor grade, based on their clinical manifestations. Surgery is a common option to treat this problem, but it can cause further damage to the brain. Theoretically, cooling the brain reduces its metabolic activity, thus protecting it during an operation. Research on animals supports this theory in general.

    Study characteristics

    The updated evidence is current to August 2015. We found only three appropriate studies with a total of 1158 participants to include. Data primarily came from one high‐quality study with 1000 participants.

    Key results

    Our analysis showed that in good grade patients, it remains possible that cooling the brain during surgery might prevent death or dependency in everyday activities. Very limited information was available for those without bleeding or those with poor grade bleeding. Unfavourable outcomes did not differ between participants with or without hypothermia. The quality of evidence for these outcomes remains unclear because they were reported in a variety of ways.

    Quality of the evidence

    We do not have enough data to be certain about the effect of cooling on death or dependency. The evidence for other outcomes was more uncertain, since we could not combine the data due to variation in the definition and reporting of outcome data. Data in our analyses were primarily from one high quality trial on good grade participants.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    In people with good‐grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage, it remains possible that intraoperative mild hypothermia could prevent death or dependency in activities of daily living. However, the confidence intervals around this estimate include the possibility of both benefit and harm. In people with poor grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage or without subarachnoid haemorrhage, there was insufficient information to allow us to draw any conclusions. We did not identify any reliable evidence to support the routine use of intraoperative mild hypothermia.

    Implications for research

    A high‐quality randomised controlled trial of intraoperative mild hypothermia to prevent post‐operative neurological deficits in people with poor‐grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage appears to be justified.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    An intracranial aneurysm forms at a weakened spot on the wall of a main intracranial artery and enlarges with circulating blood pressure, similar to the inflation of a balloon. It is usually located on the basilar surface of the brain. However, it is not known if or when it will rupture. The rupture of intracranial aneurysms is not only disastrous for patients, but is also a devastating situation for clinicians. Sudden internal bleeding within the skull, as well as the resulting complications, has caused death in 8.3% to 66.7% of those participants reported in various studies. A marked variance in case‐fatality rates has been recognised among studies conducted on different populations: for example, the case fatality in Japan is 8.3%, 11.5% lower than the case fatality rate of Europe, the USA, Australia, and New Zealand at 19.8% (Hop 1997; Nieuwkamp 2009; Vega 2002).

    Fortunately, not all intracranial aneurysms are life‐threatening. In fact, an intracranial aneurysm is not a rare condition. Although reports on the prevalence of unruptured intracranial aneurysms vary widely from 2 to 90 per 1000 people (Griffiths 1996; McCormick 1965), a prospective angiography study has estimated it at 6% in the general population (Rinkel 1998). The risk of rupture for an intracranial aneurysm has been linked to a series of factors including: old age, female gender, the ethnicity of the person, whether the size of the aneurysm is larger than one centimetre in diameter, posterior circulatory location, and probably the non‐spherical configuration of the circle of Willis and direct blood flow into the cavity of the aneurysm (Rinkel 1998; Rooij 2009; Wermer 2007; Wiebers 2003). Among those factors, the size of the unruptured aneurysm is of particular significance. For example, one international study indicated that the cumulative risk of rupture for aneurysms that were less than 10 mm in diameter at diagnosis was less than 0.05% per year, while 6% of those with a diameter more than 25 mm were likely to burst in the first year of diagnosis (Investigators 1998).

    As a matter of course, the rupture of an intracranial aneurysm will be followed by the leakage of blood into the extravascular space, which most commonly takes the form of a diffused subarachnoid haemorrhage (Suarez 2006), occasionally accompanied by an intraventricular haemorrhage (Rosen 2007), or more rarely, an intra‐parenchymal haematoma (Abbed 2003). The blood clot causes severe damage to the brain. However, a much more hazardous consequence of the rupture of a cerebral aneurysm is the deleterious cascades of damage upon the vascular system, following the disturbance of the auto‐regulation of the intracranial blood circulation. Vascular malfunction, in turn, causes ischaemic damage to the neurons. The pathophysiological response by the circulatory system to the rupture is a profound reduction and fluctuation of blood flow to the brain tissue, which causes a hypoxic insult to it through an alteration of the levels of certain intermediate substances (Prunell 2004; Sehba 2006). The brain is extremely vulnerable to oxygen deficiency, thus, deprivation of oxygen will lead to necrosis and apoptosis of affected neurons, giving rise to life‐threatening brain oedema and leaving the patient with neurological deficits (Claassen 2002). There are different scales to assess the clinical severity of subarachnoid haemorrhage, based on manifestations of patients, such as Hunt‐Hess grade (Hunt 1968), and the World Federation of Neurological Sciences (WFNS) grade (Teasdale 1988). Both scales divide people with aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage into five grades, with Grades 4 and 5 classified as 'poor grades' because they predict dismal prognoses. The other three grades are known as 'good grades'.

    Unruptured intracranial aneurysms are usually asymptomatic, unless they are big enough to compress the nearby nerves or brain tissues. The resulting manifestations from mass effect include headache, seizure, visual loss, and facial pain (Raps 1993). It has been reported that severe headache occurring in an unusual location could be a warning symptom of impending aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage (Ostergaard 1991). The benefit of screening for, or treatment of, unruptured intracranial aneurysms is controversial and must be weighed cautiously against a surgery‐related morbidity and mortality rate of about 15%, especially for older people (Gerlach 2007; Investigators 1998; Study Group 1999). One recent cost‐effective analysis from the Netherlands concluded that patients' life expectancy, risk of rupture, and utility of awareness of an untreated aneurysm are the outcomes that mainly define cost‐effectiveness for treating unruptured aneurysms in that region (Greving 2009).

    Treatment to prevent re‐bleeding of a ruptured aneurysm, or the rupture of a vulnerable unruptured intracranial aneurysm, is either surgical clipping with craniotomy or endovascular coiling. The choice of treatment is based on the general condition of the patient and the location of the aneurysm, as well as the specific circumstances in different hospitals (Johnston 2002; Takao 2008; Vega 2002). For surgical treatment of ruptured intracranial aneurysms, one of the priorities for surgeons is to prevent and manage the ischaemic events that result from cerebral vasospasm, or occasionally, from the temporary deliberate occlusion of the feeding artery to facilitate the clipping process (Haley Jr 1992; Kassell 1984). As we have mentioned, the ensuing oedema of the brain after anoxic damage is a devastating emergency, which sometimes warrants a decompressive corticectomy or a craniectomy to prevent lethal brain herniation (Jha 2003). Inevitably, both infarction and decompressive removal of the brain tissue will be followed by certain neurological deficits.

    Description of the intervention

    Profound intraoperative hypothermia, with a temperature lower than 28°C, was attempted in the 1950s to protect brain function from surgery‐related ischaemic damage. However, this method has not been generally accepted due to its fatal complications (Chouhan 2006; Todd 2005). In the 1980s, the interest in hypothermia was re‐ignited when the application of mild hypothermia in place of deep hypothermia was introduced. In clinical practice, the target temperature varies from 32°C to 35°C; the method and timing of cooling and re‐warming are also quite variable.

    Numerous animal experiments have demonstrated a beneficial effect on brain protection from ischaemic damage by mild hypothermia (Busto 1987; Busto 1989; Clifton 1991; Dempsey 1987; Dietrich 1990; Minamisawa 1990; Patel 1994; Shimohata 2007; Zhang 1993; Zhao 2007). However, it has been reported that hypothermia can predispose the patient to wound infection and lead to a longer stay in hospital (Kurz 1996). Animal experiments have also shown a decreased resistance to bacterial infection of the skin under deliberate hypothermia (Sheffield 1994a; Sheffield 1994b). More serious than potential infections, hypothermia probably disturbs the electrical equilibrium of the myocardium and causes dangerous cardiac arrhythmia, or even cardiac arrest (Freysz 1989).

    How the intervention might work

    Experiments on animals have proven that mild hypothermia suppresses the complex harmful biochemical cascades related to inflammation and alters blood‐brain barrier permeability after experimental stroke and brain damage (Deng 2003). The activity of a great number of identified biochemical agents associated with ischaemic damage has been shown to be influenced by mild hypothermia in favour of neuronal protection (Busto 1989; Dempsey 1987; Dietrich 1990; Patel 1994; Shimohata 2007; Zhao 2007). A diminished degree of brain oedema following ischaemia due to mild hypothermia has also been specifically demonstrated in rats (Dempsey 1987; Kawanishi 2008; Schubert 2008; Török 2009). Studies on human patients have already shown that mild intraoperative hypothermia reduces the production of reactive oxygen intermediates by polymorphonuclear leukocytes, thereby attenuating the damage to cells during the process of inflammation (Wenisch 1996).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Although numerous studies on the effect of hypothermia for brain‐protection have been done during the past decades, we still lack a consensus on whether intraoperative hypothermia is effective in reducing neurological deficits relating to the surgical treatment of intracranial aneurysms. A systematic review will inform decision making or suggest that further studies are required.

    Objectives

    To assess the effect of intraoperative mild hypothermia on postoperative neurological deficits in people with ruptured and unruptured intracranial aneurysm.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing mild hypothermia with no hypothermia.

    Types of participants

    People with ruptured or unruptured intracranial arterial aneurysm undergoing craniotomy.

    Types of interventions

    Intraoperative mild hypothermia (32°C to 35°C) compared with control (no hypothermia). We did not specify the methods or restrict any co‐intervention in inducing and maintaining hypothermia.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    The primary outcome was death or dependency in activities of daily living (ADL) three months post‐operatively, recorded at the end of scheduled follow‐up, quantified through standard scales, for example the Rankin scale (Wilson 2005), the Barthel Index (Sulter 1999), or the Glasgow Outcome Scale (GOS; Jennett 1975). The GOS divides recovery into five levels:

    1. dead;

    2. vegetative state;

    3. severe disability (able to follow commands but unable to live independently);

    4. moderate disability (able to live independently but unable to return to work or school);

    5. good recovery (able to return to work or school).

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Decompressive craniectomy or corticectomy performed to reduce intracranial pressure during the surgery.

    2. Newly developed postsurgical vasospasm or infarction during hospitalisation.

    3. Duration of hospitalisation.

    4. Destination of discharge (home or to another hospital).

    5. Postoperative infection of the wound, the lung, the urinary tract, or the skin within one week after surgery.

    6. Cardiac arrhythmia or arrest within one week after surgery.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    See the 'Specialized register' section in the Cochrane Stroke Group module. We did not restrict the searches by date, publication status, or language, and arranged translation of studies where necessary.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Register, which was last searched for this update by the Managing Editor in August 2015. We also searched the following electronic bibliographic databases:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2015, Issue 8);

    • MEDLINE (1950 to September 2015; Appendix 1);

    • EMBASE (1980 to September 2015);

    • ISI Web of Science: Science Citation Index Expanded (SCI‐EXPANDED), Social Sciences Citation Index (SSCI), Conference Proceedings Citation Index‐Science (CPCI‐S) (1900 to September 2015);

    • The WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP; http://www.who.int/ictrp/en/)

    • Chinese Bio‐Medical Literature & Retrieval System (CBM; 1979 to September 2015)

    • China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI; searched September 2015);

    • Chinese Dissertations database (CDDB; searched September 2015);

    • Chinese MD and DD Dissertations in CNKI (searched September 2015);

    • Chinese Science and Technology Document Databases (CSTDB; searched September 2015);

    • Chinese scientific periodical database of VIP Information (searched September 2015);

    • Chinese Social Science Citation Index (searched September 2015);

    • Wanfang Data (http://www.wanfangdata.com/; searched September 2015).

    The MEDLINE search strategy was developed by the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Search Co‐ordinator.

    We used the following search terms for EMBASE and Science Citation Index: "hypothermia" and "intracranial AND 'aneurysm'/exp" and used the same words translated into Chinese for the Chinese databases.

    Searching other resources

    In an effort to identify further published, unpublished and ongoing trials not available in the major databases, we:

    • checked reference lists of all relevant articles;

    • searched the following ongoing trials and research registers (searched September 2015):

      • Stroke Trials Registry (http://www.strokecenter.org/trials/);

      • ClinicalTrials.gov (http://clinicaltrials.gov/);

      • Current Controlled Trials (http://www.controlled‐trials.com/);

    • searched the Internet using Baidu.com.

    We had planned to search the Chinese Stroke Trials Register, but this was not available to us.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (LR and BC) independently screened the titles and abstracts of the records obtained from the searches of the electronic databases and excluded obviously irrelevant studies. We obtained the full manuscripts of the remaining records for further checking and the same two review authors independently examined the designs of these studies, and then included those matching the inclusion criteria for reviewing. The two review authors resolved any disagreements through discussion with the third review author (CY). See Figure 1 for the flow of studies identified.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (LR and BC) independently extracted data relevant to the objective of this review from the reports of included studies. The outcome data of interest included case fatality and the rate of severe disability (dependency in activities of daily living measured by the standard scales mentioned above), as well as the number of participants in the treatment and control groups. We also extracted information on the secondary outcomes listed above if they were reported.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    We assessed and reported sources of bias using Cochrane's tool for assessing risk of bias. We addressed the following domains (Higgins 2011):

    • random sequence generation;

    • allocation concealment;

    • blinding of participants and personnel;

    • blinding of outcome assessment;

    • incomplete outcome data;

    • selective outcome reporting.

    We scored each domain as 'low risk', 'high risk' or 'unclear risk' of bias and reported them in a 'Risk of bias' table. We summarised and presented the judgments made in those domains (Figure 2; Figure 3).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We calculated risk ratio (RR) and 95% confidence interval (CI) of death or dependency in ADL for each study.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We had planned to address unit of analysis issues when the number of observations in the analysis did not match the number of units that were randomised. However, none of the three included studies had unit of analysis issues.

    Dealing with missing data

    We recorded losses to follow‐up. We regarded exclusions in the middle of a study as missing data. We sought reasons for missing data and made judgements on the influence of missing data according to information obtained from the study reports. We judged missing data to be random if they were deemed to be unrelated to outcome.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed clinical and methodological diversity based on the study authors' judgement. When the confidence intervals for the results of individual studies have poor overlap, this generally indicates the presence of statistical heterogeneity; we looked at the baseline parameters of participants from each study, the methods used to achieve mild hypothermia, and data on the body temperature of participants during surgery. We also tested for statistical heterogeneity using the I² statistic.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If there had been enough relevant studies, we had planned to produce funnel plots to investigate reporting bias. However, as we only included three studies, we were unable to do so.

    Data synthesis

    We generated a summarised RR and 95% CI for death or dependency in ADL using a random‐effects meta‐analysis provided by Review Manager 5.3 (RevMan 2014).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We did not perform subgroup analyses for this update.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We recalculated the estimated risk ratio for Todd 2005 and compared it with the summarised estimation. We tested both the random‐effects and fixed‐effect models for the statistical estimation of risk difference.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    Our searches of the electronic databases in English and Chinese identified 19 trials from the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Register, and 1701 records from the other databases (165 from CENTRAL, 741 from MEDLINE, 610 from EMBASE, 176 from Science Citation Index, and 9 from Chinese databases; Figure 1). We found one relevant report in the Chinese databases and one ongoing study from the searches of the trials registers.

    Included studies

    Of the 11 seemingly relevant studies, only three met the inclusion criteria.

    One study conducted in India included people with both good grade and poor grade subarachnoid haemorrhage (Chouhan 2006). The other two studies were, in fact, two stages of one trial performed by the same team of researchers in the USA (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005). They were both multicentre studies, with Hindman 1999 being the preliminary study and Todd 2005 the final study. All participants in Todd 2005 were newly enrolled. There was no duplication of data between these two studies.

    The sample sizes of the three included studies varied from 70 to 1001 participants. They all reported that participants from both the hypothermia and normothermia groups had comparable baseline characters of age, gender, weight, and other important factors. Chouhan 2006 reported the results of 47 out of the 70 randomised participants; the exclusions were stated to be due to failures in achieving target temperature at the time of aneurysm clipping. Hindman 1999 had intended to include 120 participants from four centres, with each contributing 30 participants, but one centre withdrew after having entered four participants; another substitute centre then contributed 20 participants instead. Of the 114 participants actually enrolled, 62 were operated on before the rupture of aneurysms, and three were lost to follow‐up. Todd 2005 enrolled 1001 participants, all with aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage, and one was lost to follow‐up. For more information, please refer to the Characteristics of included studies table.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded eight studies that initially appeared to be relevant because they turned out to be focusing on methods to achieve hypothermia and not to study the effects of hypothermia on neurological outcome. We excluded another study identified from the Chinese databases because none of the information needed to assess the quality of the study was reported and only one author was listed (Zhang 2013).

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Chouhan 2006 had incomplete data, thus, the risk of bias was relatively high.

    In Hindman 1999, there were methodological flaws, which resulted in two unclear assessments for sequence generation and incomplete data; therefore, we assessed the overall risk of bias as high.

    We evaluated the risk of bias of Todd 2005 as low (Figure 2; Figure 3).

    Allocation

    Only one of the three included studies specifically reported the method used for randomised sequence generation (Todd 2005) and allocation concealment. The randomised sequence was generated by a permuted‐block scheme and the allocation was concealed in an opaque envelope, which was opened by the anaesthesiologist only after induction of anaesthesia.

    Blinding

    Surgeons in all three included studies were adequately blinded to the group assignments. Whether or not the participants and outcome assessors were adequately blinded was not reported.

    Incomplete outcome data

    In Chouhan 2006, 23 participants were excluded because of failures in achieving the target temperature at the time of aneurysm clipping; three participants were lost to follow‐up in Hindman 1999; and one participant was lost to follow‐up in Todd 2005.

    Selective reporting

    All three included studies were free of selective reporting.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We did not suspect any other potential source of bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings for the main comparison

    Primary outcomes

    Death or dependency in activities of daily living in all patients

    In total, 76 of 577 (13.2%) and 93 of 581 (16.0%) incidents of death or dependency occurred in the hypothermia and control groups, respectively. Since the studies contained both good‐grade and poor‐grade patients, we used a random‐effects meta‐analysis, which resulted in a summarised RR of 0.82 (95% CI 0.62 to 1.09; P = 0.17; Analysis 1.1). The RD was ‐0.03 (95% CI ‐0.07 to 0.01; P = 0.17; Analysis 1.2).

    We did not detect significant statistical heterogeneity: Tau² = 0.00; Chi² = 0.11, df = 2 (P = 0.94); I² = 0% (Analysis 1.1). We recalculated the estimated risk ratio for Todd 2005 and compared it with the summarised estimation as a sensitivity analysis: the results were very similar, 0.83 (95% CI 0.61 to 1.12) versus 0.82 (95% CI 0.62 to 1.09).

    Secondary outcomes

    The three studies also reported unfavourable outcomes. There was no clinically significant difference in the occurrence of these events between participants with or without hypothermia. For details, please refer to summary of findings Table for the main comparison.

    1. Decompressive craniectomy or corticectomy performed to reduce intracranial pressure during the surgery

    No decompressive craniectomy or corticectomy was reported. 

    2. Newly developed postsurgical vasospasm or infarction during hospitalisation

    Thirty‐six of 577 (6.2%) participants with hypothermia had postoperative infarction; 40 of 581 (6.9%) participants without hypothermia had infarction. Thirty‐four of 577 (6%) participants with hypothermia had clinical vasospasm (temporary deficits); and 32 of the 581 (5.5%) participants without hypothermia had clinical vasospasm.

    3. Duration of hospitalisation

    None of the studies reported duration of hospital stay.

    4. Destination of discharge (home or to another hospital)

    Only one study with 112 participants reported discharge destinations (Hindman 1999). Forty‐three of 55 (78.2%) participants with hypothermia were discharged home; 39 of 57 (68.4%) participants in the control group were discharged home. The remaining participants were discharged to other facilities.

    5. Postoperative infection of the wound, the lung, the urinary tract, or the skin within one week after surgery.

    Thirty‐nine of 577 (6.8%) participants with hypothermia had infections; 39 of 581 (6.7%) participants without hypothermia had infections.

    6. Cardiac arrhythmia or arrest within one week after surgery

    Six of 577 (1%) participants with hypothermia and 6 of 581 (1%) participants without hypothermia had cardiac arrhythmia.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    All three studies, with a total of 1158 participants, investigating the effect of intraoperative mild hypothermia for postoperative neurological outcomes, reported better neurological recovery with this treatment, but the statistical estimation of the effect failed to reach certainty.

    Chouhan 2006 reported that neurological recovery was better in the hypothermic group (GOS Grade V in 66% versus 47%); Hindman 1999 reported a greater incidence of good long‐term outcomes in the hypothermic group (71% versus 57%); and Todd 2005 also reported better recovery in the hypothermia group (GOS grade V in 66% versus 63%).

    A random‐effects meta‐analysis summarising the three RCTs resulted in an estimated risk ratio very close to the recalculated one from the data in Todd 2005, which contributed 1000 of the 1158 included participants and accounted for 85.2% of the weight of the meta‐analysis (Analysis 1.1).

    The three included studies also reported unfavourable outcomes; there was no clinically significant difference in the occurrence of these events between participants with and without hypothermia. For details, please refer to summary of findings Table for the main comparison.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    All three studies examined the effects of intraoperative mild hypothermia, but primarily in people with a good‐grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage. Postoperative neurological outcomes, quantified by the GOS three months after surgery, were reported by two studies (Hindman 1999; Todd 2005), the other study followed participants until discharge (Chouhan 2006). All three studies used good recovery (GOS Grade V) as the indicator for the neuroprotective effect of intraoperative mild hypothermia.

    In this review, we focused on postoperative neurological deficits rather than good recovery. In our analysis of the data, we defined people with a GOS Grade 3 or 2 as dependent in activities of daily living; Grade 1 meant death.

    Readers should note that the Chouhan 2006 study included all grades of participants, but followed them for less than three months and had missing data. Their data accounted for only 4.9% of the weight of our meta‐analysis. So, data for the 13 poor grade participants had little influence on the result. Chouhan 2006 also reported that in subarachnoid haemorrhage Grade II participants, all eight (100%) in the hypothermic group and four out of 10 (40%) in the normothermic group, had good neurological recovery (GOS Grade V). This appears to support the rationale of investigators in Todd 2005, who investigated the effect in good grade participants only.

    The other two studies were two stages of one multicentre trial. Hindman 1999, the preliminary stage, included good grade participants and participants without subarachnoid haemorrhage. The second stage of the study, Todd 2005, included only good grade participants and had a large sample size, which carried 85.2% of the weight in our meta‐analysis.

    Of the 1158 participants included in our meta‐analysis, 13 were poor grade while 59 did not have a subarachnoid haemorrhage. Therefore, the result of our meta‐analysis basically reflects the effectiveness of intraoperative mild hypothermia on postoperative neurological deficits in people with good grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage, not in all people with cerebral aneurysm.

    The number of participants without subarachnoid haemorrhage was relatively small in our review. Subgroup analysis on this group would not have reached a conclusion. However, due to its similar clinical scenarios to good grade participants, we speculate that outcomes for this group of people would not be so different.

    As the neuro‐intensive care field develops, there is a trend to perform surgery or endovascular therapy in the early stage of aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage. Clinically, this implies that more poor grade patients are going to be operated on in some medical centres. Although there was no significant statistical difference in the seven poor grade people who received intraoperative mild hypothermia, we found no clinical evidence to exclude the possibility that this treatment might be effective in this subgroup of people.

    The metabolism and autoregulation of the neural or neurovascular system in poor grade people are thought to be under more severe disturbances or damage. We presume this might render the brain or neurovascular system more vulnerable to surgery. In our opinion, the majority of animal models used to investigate the effect of hypothermia for neurological protection are more likely to mimic poor grade people, and they often demonstrate favourable results (Busto 1987; Busto 1989; Clifton 1991; Dempsey 1987; Dietrich 1990; Minamisawa 1990; Patel 1994; Shimohata 2007; Zhang 1993; Zhao 2007). Chouhan 2001 reported that their preliminary study observed better neurological recovery with intraoperative mild hypothermia in poor grade people, but in Chouhan 2006, a sample size of 13 was obviously not large enough for any conclusion to be drawn with certainty.

    Not all the target secondary outcomes were addressed by all of the studies, but complications reported in their separate cohorts were rare, and not attributable to mild hypothermia.

    Quality of the evidence

    Although data in our analysis were primarily derived from one high‐quality study on people with good grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage, the overall quality of evidence remains moderate. For those without subarachnoid haemorrhage or those with poor grade subarachnoid haemorrhage, it is inappropriate to make clinical decisions based on these analyses. For secondary outcomes, the ways in which they were reported were so various that the summarised results could only have limited reliability.

    Potential biases in the review process

    Due to the limited access to all databases, we did not carry out a small fraction of the planned database searches.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We did not find any reviews on the same topic. Although a systematic review of 23 trials on hypothermia for people with head trauma concluded there was no evidence that hypothermia treatment was effective (Sydenham 2009), no evidence exists to reject a future high‐quality trial focusing on intraoperative mild hypothermia for people with poor grade aneurysmal subarachnoid haemorrhage.
