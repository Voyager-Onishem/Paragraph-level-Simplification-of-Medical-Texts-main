Early versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for labour

Abstract

    • Background

    Pain during childbirth is arguably the most severe pain some women may experience in their lifetime. Epidural analgesia is an effective form of pain relief during labour. Many women have concerns regarding its safety. Furthermore, epidural services and anaesthetic support may not be available consistently across all centres. Observational data suggest that early initiation of epidural may be associated with an increased risk of caesarean section, but the same findings were not seen in recent randomised controlled trials. More recent guidelines suggest that in the absence of a medical contraindication, maternal request is a sufficient medical indication for pain relief during labour. The choice of analgesic technique, agent, and dosage is based on many factors, including patient preference, medical status, and contraindications. There is no systematically reviewed evidence on the maternal and foetal outcomes and safety of this practice.

    Objectives

    This systematic review aimed to summarise the effectiveness and safety of early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia in women. We considered the obstetric and fetal outcomes relevant to women and side effects of the treatments, including risk of caesarean section, instrumental birth and time to birth.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group's Trials Register (12 February 2014), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (The Cochrane Library 2014, Issue 1), MEDLINE (January 1966 to February 2014), Embase (January 1980 to February 2014) and reference lists of retrieved studies.

    Selection criteria

    We included all randomised controlled trials involving women undergoing epidural labour analgesia that compared early initiation versus late initiation of epidural labour analgesia.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed trials for inclusion, extracted the data and assessed the trial quality. Data were checked for accuracy.

    Main results

    We included nine studies with a total of 15,752 women.The overall risk of bias of the studies was low, with the exception of performance bias (blinding of participants and personnel).

    The nine studies showed no clinically meaningful difference in risk of caesarean section with early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for labour (risk ratio (RR) 1.02; 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.96 to 1.08, nine studies, 15,499 women, high quality evidence ). There was no clinically meaningful difference in risk of instrumental birth with early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for labour (RR 0.93; 95% CI 0.86 to 1.01, eight studies, 15,379 women, high quality evidence ). The duration of second stage of labour showed no clinically meaningful difference between early initiation and late initiation of epidural analgesia (mean difference (MD) ‐3.22 minutes; 95% CI ‐6.71 to 0.27, eight studies, 14,982 women, high quality evidence). There was significant heterogeneity in the duration of first stage of labour and the data were not pooled.

    There was no clinically meaningful difference in Apgar scores less than seven at one minute (RR 0.96; 95% CI 0.84 to 1.10, seven studies, 14,924 women, high quality evidence ). There was no clinically meaningful difference in Apgar scores less than seven at five minutes (RR 0.96; 95% CI 0.69 to 1.33, seven studies, 14,924 women, high quality evidence ). There was no clinically meaningful difference in umbilical arterial pH between early initiation and late initiation (MD 0.01; 95% CI ‐0.01 to 0.03, four studies, 14,004 women, high quality evidence ). There was no clinically meaningful difference in umbilical venous pH favouring early initiation (MD 0.01; 95% CI ‐0.00 to 0.02, four studies, 14,004 women, moderate quality evidence).

    Authors' conclusions

    There is predominantly high‐quality evidence that early or late initiation of epidural analgesia for labour have similar effects on all measured outcomes. However, various forms of alternative pain relief were given to women who were allocated to delayed epidurals to cover that period of delay, so that is it hard to assess the outcomes clearly. We conclude that for first time mothers in labour who request epidurals for pain relief, it would appear that the time to initiate epidural analgesia is dependent upon women’s requests.

    • Early versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for labour

    Epidural analgesia involves the injection of medication just outside the spinal column. It is an effective form of pain relief during labour. The intensity of the pain increases as labour progresses. Epidural analgesia is an invasive procedure with side effects and more rarely complications. Reported side effects include muscle weakness, nausea, shivering, itching and headache. Epidural analgesia with low concentration infusions of bupivacaine has been shown to not increase the incidence of caesarean section but may increase the incidence of instrumental vaginal delivery and the duration of second stage of labour (Sia 2004). This Cochrane systematic review summarised the best available evidence (published before 12 February 2014) regarding the effectiveness and safety of early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for both spontaneous and augmented labour. Our meta‐analysis involved nine randomised controlled studies with a total of 15,752 women giving birth to their first baby and found no differences in the risk of caesarean section and instrumental birth with early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia for pain relief during labour. Although the effects of early or late initiation of epidural analgesia on the duration of the second stage of labour are similar, we are unable to rule out early initiation leading to an appreciably shorter duration of labour. There was a lot of variation (heterogeneity) between the results of the studies for the duration of the first stage of labour. For the baby, Apgar scores and cord pH were not different. We conclude that it would appear to be advantageous to initiate epidural analgesia for labour early, when requested by the woman.

    The studies varied in the definition of early initiation and late initiation of epidural analgesia. Early initiation is typically defined as with cervical dilatation of less than 4 cm to 5 cm, and late initiation with cervical dilatation of 4 cm to 5 cm or more. The dose, concentration and technique of epidural analgesia also varied between the studies. The groups randomised to receive late initiation of epidural analgesia differed in the analgesia they received before the epidural analgesia.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    This systematic review found high‐quality evidence that early and late initiation of epidural analgesia confers similar effects on the risk of caesarean and instrumental birth, the duration of the second stage of labour, Apgar scores less than seven at one and five minutes and umbilical arterial pH; and moderate quality evidence that early and late initiation of epidural analgesia confers similar effects on umbilical venous pH. The findings of this review support the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists recommendation (ACOG 2006) 'In the absence of a medical contraindication, maternal request is a sufficient medical indication for pain relief during labor'.

    Implications for research

    There remain unanswered questions regarding the impact of neuraxial analgesia on the duration of first stage of labour especially with a combined spinal epidural technique utilising intrathecal opioids. Intrathecal fentanyl may reduce the maternal concentration of circulating adrenaline leading to decreased tocolysis resulting in faster labour, while systemic opioids may decrease uterine activity in active labour. Hence a well‐designed adequately powered randomised controlled trial is required to determine the effects of neuraxial versus systemic opioids on uterine activity in labour. All clinically‐relevant side effects should be evaluated. Reporting standards should adhere to the CONSORT guidelines.

    Background

    Pain during childbirth is arguably the most severe some women may experience in their lifetimes (Khan 1993; Melzack 1984). Many factors influence how individual women experience and respond to this pain, the intensity of which will increase as labour progresses (Haines 2012). Epidural analgesia is an effective form of pain relief during labour (Khan 1993; Thorp 1996), although it is an invasive procedure with side effects and, more rarely, serious complications. Reported side effects of epidural analgesia include muscle weakness, nausea, shivering, itching and headache; also many women have concerns regarding its safety. In the Cochrane review comparing epidural analgesia with other forms of pain relief, women who received epidurals reported better pain relief than those who had other types of analgesia (Anim‐Somuah 2011). However, having an epidural was also associated with a longer second stage of labour, greater need for augmentation of labour, greater incidence of hypotension, problems passing urine, fever, and immobility for a period of time after the birth (Anim‐Somuah 2011). Epidural analgesia did not appear to influence the risk of caesarean section (Anim‐Somuah 2011; Liu 2004).

    The use of neuraxial and/or epidural analgesia varies between countries and regions. Neuraxial analgesia is used by approximately one‐third of women in Australia (Chan 2006). In one European study epidural use ranged from 0% in Perugia and up to 98% in Barcelona (Alran 2002).

    Description of the condition

    Whether to use analgesia during labour, and if so what to use, are choices women will make based on their individual preferences. Women who wish to have an epidural for analgesia will request this when they feel the need for pain relief, which will often be in early labour. However, observational data have suggested that early initiation of epidural analgesia may be associated with an increased risk of caesarean section (Lieberman 1996; Thorp 1991; Thorp 1993). In a retrospective study, Thorp 1991 reported the greatest effect of epidural analgesia on the risk of caesarean section for dystocia was for nulliparous women with slow cervical dilatation (less than 1 cm/hour) in early labour who had an epidural inserted before they reached 5 cm of cervical dilatation (20.6% versus 3.4%). This increase could not be accounted for by adjusting for other possibly confounding variables. In another retrospective study, Lieberman 1996 reported the caesarean rate among women receiving epidural analgesia was 17% (168 of 991) compared with 4% (30 of 742) among those who did not receive epidural analgesia. The greatest increase in caesarean risk was when epidural analgesia was administered earlier in labour, but overall there was more than a two‐fold increase in caesarean rate. Retrospective studies such as these are prone to bias and confounding factors; a further limitation is that the studies were based on the use of the older high‐dose regimens for epidural analgesia. The newer low‐dose regimens may have better outcomes.

    Description of the intervention

    Epidural analgesia involves placing a thin catheter through a needle inserted into the epidural space.The catheter is used to administer local anaesthetics and/or opioids into the epidural space on an intermittent and/or continuous basis throughout labour and birth. The drugs act locally on nerve roots exiting the spinal cord thereby providing segmental analgesia. In addition, the epidural catheter may be utilised to administer higher doses of drugs as needed, or for instrumental or cesarean birth. The catheter may also be left in situ postpartum and used to manage pain after birth.

    The combined spinal‐epidural (CSE) technique involves the use of local anaesthetics in the intrathecal space via a spinal needle that passes through an epidural needle. This is followed by the placement of an epidural catheter that allows the subsequent instillation of anaesthetic agents into the epidural space. CSE analgesia is commonly practised, but there seems to be little overall difference between this technique and conventional epidural, despite a slightly faster onset of pain relief for CSE and less pruritus (itching) with conventional epidural (Simmons 2007).

    In 2002, the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists Task Force on caesarean birth recommended that when feasible, obstetric practitioners should delay the administration of epidural analgesia in nulliparous women until cervical dilatation reaches 4 cm to 5 cm and that other forms of analgesia be used until that time (ACOG 2002).

    Poor neonatal outcomes are indicated by persistently poor Apgar scores e.g. less than seven at five minutes; and fetal acidosis as indicated by low umbilical cord arterial and/or venous pH, e.g. less than 7.20.

    How the intervention might work

    Retrospective and non‐randomised cohort studies are not adequate study designs to study the effect of timing of labour epidural analgesia due to selection bias and confounding factors (Ohel 1994; Rogers 1999; Sharma 2003; Vahratian 2004). Several randomised controlled trials to date have addressed obstetric and fetal outcomes. Randomised trials since 1994 have not reported a causal relationship between the timing of initiation of epidural analgesia and the risk of caesarean birth (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009).

    For example, in one trial 449 nulliparous term women in early labour with less than 3 cm of cervical dilatation were randomly assigned to receive either immediate initiation of epidural analgesia at first request, or delayed initiation of epidural analgesia after the cervix had dilated to at least 4 cm (Ohel 2006). The risk of caesarean section was not significantly different between the two groups (13% versus 11%). The mean duration from randomisation to full dilatation was significantly shorter in the early compared to the late epidural group: 5.9 hours and 6.6 hours respectively. When questioned after birth regarding their next labour, the women indicated a preference for early initiation of epidural analgesia; 78% of women in the late epidural group preferred to be in the early epidural group for their next birth while 7% of women in the early epidural group preferred to be in the late epidural group for their next birth. In another trial, women were randomly assigned to receive intrathecal fentanyl or systemic hydromorphone at the first request for analgesia; epidural analgesia was initiated for the intrathecal group at the second request for analgesia and in the systemic group at a cervical dilatation of 4 cm or greater or at the third request for analgesia (Wong 2005). Neuraxial analgesia in early labour did not appear to increase the risk of caesarean birth, provided better analgesia and resulted in a shorter duration of labour than systemic analgesia. Epidural analgesia lasting more than five hours in women in labour is associated with elevated maternal temperature, which is a concern with early initiation of epidural analgesia, and which may lead to a longer duration of epidural infusion (Lieberman 1997).

    There are clearly differing views about the effects of early initiation of epidural analgesia. Previous concerns that this practice might increase the risk of caesarean section may have been exaggerated by the limitations of retrospective data. Also, the newer techniques using low‐doses for epidural analgesia may have less impact on the progress of labour than the older high‐dose techniques.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Retrospective data have suggested that early initiation of epidural analgesia using the older high‐dose techniques may increase the risk of caesarean section. A recent systematic review by Marucci 2007 on this topic included five randomised controlled trials, one prospective cohort study and three retrospective cohort studies. However, the use of cohort studies may lead to biases in the results. The review did not include the two newer studies by Wang 2009 and Wong 2009. This review assesses the comparative effects of early and late initiation of epidural analgesia utilising randomised controlled trials only. The results of this review will facilitate decision making on the timing of the initiation of epidural analgesia for women during labour, and may improve the woman's outcome and satisfaction.

    Objectives

    This systematic review aimed to summarise the effectiveness and safety of early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia in women. We considered the obstetric and fetal outcomes relevant to women and side effects of the treatments, including risk of caesarean section, instrumental birth and time to birth.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials. Cluster‐randomised trials were eligible for inclusion but none were identified. We did not include quasi‐randomised trials or cross‐over trials.

    Types of participants

    We included pregnant term women requesting epidural analgesia in labour, regardless of whether the labour was spontaneous or induced. We excluded women in preterm labour, with multiple pregnancy or malposition of fetus including breech presentations.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies that compared early versus late initiation of epidural analgesia (including combined spinal‐epidural). Studies that compared epidural versus non‐epidural or no analgesia in labour are the subject of another review (Anim‐Somuah 2011). Early initiation is typically defined as cervical dilatation of less than 4 cm to 5 cm, as defined by the authors of the trials. Late initiation is typically defined as cervical dilatation of 4 cm to 5 cm or more, as defined by the authors of the trials. We contacted the investigators of the trials if raw data were required.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Incidence of caesarean section (dichotomous)

    2. Incidence of instrumental birth (dichotomous)

    3. Duration of first stage of labour from randomisation (continuous)

    4. Duration of second stage of labour (continuous)

    Secondary outcomes
    Neonatal outcomes

    1. Apgar scores less than seven at one minute (dichotomous)

    2. Apgar scores less than seven at five minutes (dichotomous)

    3. Umbilical cord arterial pH (continuous)

    4. Umbilical cord venous pH (continuous)

    5. Naloxone administration (dichotomous)

    6. Meconium staining (dichotomous)

    7. Neonatal hypoglycaemia (dichotomous)

    8. Birth trauma (dichotomous)

    9. Neonatal intensive care unit admission (dichotomous)

    10. Long‐term neonatal morbidity (dichotomous)

    Maternal outcomes

    1. Indication for caesarean section (dichotomous)

    2. Use of oxytocin augmentation (dichotomous)

    3. Malposition (dichotomous)

    4. Time of administration of pain relief to time level of pain relief is satisfactory (continuous)

    5. Women's satisfaction with pain relief in labour (continuous/dichotomous)

    6. Need for additional analgesia (dichotomous)

    7. Postpartum haemorrhage (dichotomous)

    8. Maternal hypotension (dichotomous)

    9. Maternal fever (dichotomous)

    10. Breastfeeding and problems with breastfeeding (dichotomous)

    11. Economic outcomes (length of hospital stay)

    12. Long‐term maternal morbidity (backache, neurological deficit) (dichotomous)

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group’s Trials Register by contacting the Trials Search Co‐ordinator (12 Feburary 2014).

    The Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group Trials Register is maintained by the Trials Search Co‐ordinator and contains trials identified from: 

    1. monthly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL);

    2. weekly searches of MEDLINE;

    3. weekly searches of Embase;

    4. handsearches of 30 journals and the proceedings of major conferences;

    5. weekly current awareness alerts for a further 44 journals plus monthly BioMed Central email alerts.

    Details of the search strategies for CENTRAL, MEDLINE and Embase, the list of handsearched journals and conference proceedings, and the list of journals reviewed via the current awareness service can be found in the ‘Specialized Register’ section within the editorial information about the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group. 

    Trials identified through the searching activities described above are given a topic code (or codes) linked to review topics. The Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group Trials Search Co‐ordinator searches the register for each review using these codes rather than keywords. 

    In addition, we searched CENTRAL (The Cochrane Library 2014, Issue 1) (Appendix 1), MEDLINE (via OvidSP) (January 1966 to Feburary 2014) (Appendix 2); and Embase (via OvidSP) (January 1980 to Feburary 2014) (Appendix 3).

    Searching other resources

    We searched the reference lists of retrieved studies.

    We did not apply any language restrictions.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (B Sng (BS), Y Lim (YL)) independently assessed for inclusion all the potential studies we identified as a result of the search strategy (Criteria for considering studies for this review). We resolved any disagreement through discussion or, if required, we consulted a third author (A Sia (AS)).

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (BS, YL) independently extracted the data using a standardised form. We extracted information pertaining to the study design, method of randomisation, use of allocation concealment, reporting of the study setting and participants, inclusion and exclusion criteria, sample size, interventions and outcomes. Outcome data included caesarean section, instrumental birth, duration of first stage of labour from randomisation, duration of second stage of labour, Apgar scores at one and five minutes, umbilical cord artery pH and umbilical cord vein pH. Data were entered into Review Manager software (RevMan 2014) and checked for accuracy

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (BS, YL) independently assessed the risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). We resolved any disagreement by discussion or by involving a third assessor (AS).

    (1) Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups.

    We assessed the method as:

    • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

    • high risk of bias (any non‐random process, e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (2) Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment and will assess whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered sealed opaque envelopes);

    • high risk of bias (open random allocation; unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes, alternation; date of birth);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (3.1) Blinding of participants and personnel (checking for possible performance bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind study participants and personnel from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We considered that studies were at low risk of bias if they were blinded, or if we judged that the lack of blinding would be unlikely to affect results. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for participants;

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for personnel.

    (3.2) Blinding of outcome assessment (checking for possible detection bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind outcome assessors from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed methods used to blind outcome assessment as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias.

    (4) Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias due to the amount, nature and handling of incomplete outcome data)

    We described for each included study, and for each outcome or class of outcomes, the completeness of data including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We stated whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. Where sufficient information is reported, or could be supplied by the trial authors, we re‐included missing data in the analyses which we undertook.

    We assessed methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data; missing outcome data balanced across groups);

    • high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups; ‘as treated’ analysis done with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (5) Selective reporting (checking for reporting bias)

    We described for each included study how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (where it is clear that all of the study’s pre‐specified outcomes and all expected outcomes of interest to the review have been reported);

    • high risk of bias (where not all the study’s pre‐specified outcomes have been reported; one or more reported primary outcomes were not pre‐specified; outcomes of interest are reported incompletely and so cannot be used; study fails to include results of a key outcome that would have been expected to have been reported);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (6) Other bias (checking for bias due to problems not covered by (1) to (5) above)

    We described for each included study any important concerns we had about other possible sources of bias.

    (7) Overall risk of bias

    We made explicit judgements about whether studies are at high risk of bias, according to the criteria given in the Handbook (Higgins 2011). With reference to (1) to (6) above, we assessed the likely magnitude and direction of the bias and whether we considered it likely to impact on the findings. We planned to explore the impact of the level of bias through undertaking sensitivity analyses ‐ seeSensitivity analysis.

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we presented results as summary risk ratio with 95% confidence intervals.

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we used the mean difference if outcomes are measured in the same way between trials. We planned to use the standardised mean difference to combine trials that measured the same outcome, but used different methods.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The woman was the unit of analysis in all of the studies.

    Cluster‐randomised trials

    We did not identify any cluster‐randomised trials for inclusion in this review. If we identify any cluster‐randomised trials for inclusion in future updates we will include them in the analyses along with individually randomised trials. We will adjust their sample sizes or standard errors using the methods described in the Handbook [Section 16.3.4 or 16.3.6] using an estimate of the intracluster correlation co‐efficient (ICC) derived from the trial (if possible), from a similar trial or from a study of a similar population. If we use ICCs from other sources, we will report this and conduct sensitivity analyses to investigate the effect of variation in the ICC. If we identify both cluster‐randomised trials and individually‐randomised trials, we plan to synthesise the relevant information. We will consider it reasonable to combine the results from both if there is little heterogeneity between the study designs and the interaction between the effect of intervention and the choice of randomisation unit is considered to be unlikely.

    We will also acknowledge heterogeneity in the randomisation unit and perform a sensitivity analysis to investigate the effects of the randomisation unit.

    Dealing with missing data

    We attempted to contact the authors if key information was unavailable in the publications.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We evaluated clinical heterogeneity by qualitatively appraising differences in study characteristics such as participants, interventions, outcomes assessed and study methodology. Quantitative pooling of the data was first justified by a consensus clinical judgement of sufficient clinical homogeneity. We informally evaluated and investigated the degree of statistical heterogeneity by visual inspection of forest plots and more formally by using the T2, I2 and Chi2 statistics. We regarded heterogeneity as substantial if I2 is greater than 30% and either T2 is greater than zero, or there is a low P value (< 0.10) in the Chi2 test for heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We checked the methodology and study protocols of the primary studies where available. If in future updates there are 10 or more studies in the meta‐analysis, we will investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We will assess funnel plot asymmetry visually. If asymmetry is suggested by a visual assessment, we will perform exploratory analyses to investigate it.

    Data synthesis

    We carried out statistical analysis using the Review Manager software (RevMan 2014). There was clinical heterogeneity sufficient to expect that the underlying treatment effects differ between trials, and/or substantial statistical heterogeneity was detected, therefore, we used random‐effects meta‐analysis to produce an overall summary as an average treatment effect across trials was considered clinically meaningful. The random‐effects summary was treated as the average range of possible treatment effects and we discussed the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. We did not combine trials if the average treatment effect was not clinically meaningful.

    As we used random‐effects analyses, the results were presented as the average treatment effect with 95% confidence intervals, and the estimates of T2 and I2.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We performed the following pre‐planned subgroup analyses for the primary outcomes.

    1. Epidural technique: epidural alone compared with combined spinal‐epidural technique

    2. Local anaesthetic alone compared with local anaesthetic with opioid regimens

    3. Low‐dose compared with high‐dose local anaesthetic regimens

    4. Labour: spontaneous compared with induced labour or both

    5. Parity: nulliparous compared with multiparous

    Furthermore, we carried out subgroup analyses for the duration of first and second stage of labour (Analysis 1.3; Analysis 1.4) based on different analysis populations, as some studies excluded women who had undergone emergency caesarean section from further analysis, while others did not. Hence, these two subgroups of studies would have a different calculated duration of labour, as women who had undergone caesarean section would have a truncated duration of labour.

    We will perform the subgroup analysis for nulliparous versus multiparous women if trials including multiparous women are included in future versions of this review.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We did not perform sensitivity analysis as quality of studies is consistent across different studies (Risk of bias in included studies). We will consider performing sensitivity analysis in future updates of this review if required. Sensitivity analysis for trial quality will involve analysis based on rating of selection bias and attrition bias; we will exclude studies of poor quality from the analysis (those rated as unclear or high risk of bias) in order to assess for any substantive difference to the overall result. The sensitivity analysis for compliance would be based on trials where women did not receive their allocated treatment, or they received an additional form of analgesia to that allocated. These sensitivity analyses will use only the primary outcomes.

    We identified a total of 1493 citations from the database searches. After screening by title and abstract, we obtained full texts for 20 citations that were judged potentially eligible for inclusion in the review. We included nine studies (11 reports) and excluded nine studies.

    Included studies

    We included nine studies involving 15,752 women (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). All nine studies enrolled healthy nulliparous women at term with singleton fetus in vertex presentation and excluded women with complicated pregnancies. All trials reported the following primary outcome measure:

    1. the number of women who underwent caesarean section.

    The other outcomes included number of women who underwent instrumental birth, duration of first stage of labour, duration of second stage of labour, Apgar score less than seven at one minute, Apgar score less than seven at five minutes, umbilical arterial pH, umbilical venous pH.

    There were variations in the definition of early initiation and late initiation of epidural analgesia based on cervical dilatation. Two studies used cervical dilatation of 5 cm as the cut‐off (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b). Five studies used cervical dilatation of 4 cm as the cut‐off including Luxman 1998, Ohel 2006, Wang 2009; Wong 2005; Wong 2009. One study (Parameswara 2012) used 2 cm as the cut‐off. As there were no raw data, we have analysed using the varying definitions of early and late initiation in the original article.

    The dose, concentration and technique of initiation of epidural analgesia varied between the studies. Six studies utilised opioids such as fentanyl (Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009) and sufentanil (Wang 2009); while the other three did not use adjuvant opioids (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998).

    There was also variability in the technique of regional block with three studies (Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009) performing the combined spinal epidural technique (delivering drugs directly into the spinal fluid with a small catheter placed in the space surrounding the spinal column) and hence administering drugs intrathecally before initiating epidural analgesia. Four studies did not use a continuous background epidural infusion, instead administering bolus drugs at the women's request (Luxman 1998) or by woman‐controlled epidural analgesia (Wang 2009; Wong 2005; Wong 2009).

    The analgesia received by the groups randomised to receive late initiation of epidural analgesia varied between the studies. Two studies (Luxman 1998; Parameswara 2012) did not administer any analgesia to the group receiving late initiation of epidural analgesia until epidural analgesia had begun. One study (Wang 2011) administered intrathecal analgesia to all women prior to randomisation for early or delayed epidural analgesia. The remainder of the studies used systemic opioids such as intravenous nalbuphine (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b), intravenous pethidine with promethazine (Ohel 2006), intramuscular meperidine (Wang 2009) and intravenous and intramuscular hydromorphone (Wong 2005; Wong 2009).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded nine studies as they did not fulfil our inclusion criteria. One trial studied the effect of epidural fentanyl administered during the early period of the first stage of induced labour in nulliparous women and not the timing of epidural analgesia (Chen 2000). Three were retrospective cohort studies of early compared to late initiation of epidural analgesia (Lieberman 1996; Ohel 1994; Thorp 1991). One study utilised a prospective non‐randomised study design (Sharma 2003; Vahratian 2004). One study examined the effect epidural analgesia on maternal fever and neonatal outcomes (Lieberman 1997). The primary intervention in one study was active management of labour compared with control protocols, with secondary analysis of early and late timing in initiation of epidural analgesia (Rogers 1999). One study compared epidural analgesia with narcotic analgesia on obstetric outcomes (Thorp 1993).

    Allocation

    Seven studies utilised computer‐generated random numbers for randomisation and sequential opaque envelopes for allocation (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009) and were rated as low risk of bias. One study used the order of arrival of women for randomisation but did not describe the method of allocation (Luxman 1998) and was rated as unclear risk of bias. The Parameswara 2012 was also judged to be at unclear risk of bias as the trial report did not describe the method of randomisation and allocation.

    Blinding

    None of the studies were blinded. For the women and attending clinician, blinding would be difficult as management of the early group and late group was different as the late group had either no pain treatment (Luxman 1998; Parameswara 2012) or systemic opioids (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). In one study, women received intrathecal opioids for initial analgesia followed by early or late administration of epidural bupivacaine and fentanyl (Wang 2011).

    Incomplete outcome data

    All nine studies had a low risk of allocation bias ‐ all outcome data (recruitment and attrition data) were reported with no missing data. Analyses were performed using the intention‐to‐treat principle (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009).

    Selective reporting

    All nine studies (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009) reported all prespecified outcomes and were judged to be at low risk of bias.

    Other potential sources of bias

    All nine studies (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009) appeared to be free of other bias.

    Early initiation of epidural analgesia versus late initiation of epidural anaesthesia

    Primary outcomes
    Incidence of caesarean section

    Nine studies, which analysed a total of 15,499 women, reported the incidence of caesarean section (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Parameswara 2012; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). The pooled results showed no difference in the risk of caesarean birth (average risk ratio (RR) 1.02; 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.96 to 1.08, random‐effects) between early and late initiation of epidural analgesia (Analysis 1.1). There was low heterogeneity (I2: 0%). There was no statistical difference when comparing subgroups for epidural only versus combined spinal‐epidural (CSE) (Analysis 1.16), local anaesthetic only versus opioid‐containing regimens (Analysis 1.17), high‐dose versus low‐dose regimens (Analysis 1.18) and spontaneous versus induced labour, or both (Analysis 1.19).

    Incidence of instrumental birth

    Eight studies, which analysed a total of 15,379 women, reported the incidence of instrumental birth (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). The pooled results showed no statistically significant difference in the risk of instrumental birth (average RR 0.93; 95% CI 0.86 to 1.01, random‐effects) between early and late initiation of epidural analgesia (Analysis 1.2). There was low heterogeneity (I2: 0%). There was no statistical difference between subgroups for epidural only versus CSE (Analysis 1.20), local anaesthetic only versus opioid‐containing regimens (Analysis 1.21), high‐dose versus low‐dose regimens (Analysis 1.22) and spontaneous versus induced labour, or both (Analysis 1.23).

    Duration of first stage of labour

    Seven studies, which analysed a total of 14,384 women, reported the mean duration of first stage of labour with the time starting from randomisation (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005). Studies were grouped on the basis of whether women undergoing caesarean section were excluded from the denominator or not (Analysis 1.3). No pooling of data was undertaken due to clinical and outcome heterogeneity arising from the differences in defining the end of first stage of labour. Results varied from a shorter first stage of labour amongst the early group by 91 minutes to a longer first stage of labour by 45 minutes (Analysis 1.3). For various subgroup analyses the statistical heterogeneity was high with the smallest I2 value of 58%, therefore we did not pool the results.

    Duration of second stage of labour

    Eight studies, which analysed a total of 14,982 women, reported the duration of second stage of labour (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). Studies were grouped on the basis of whether women undergoing caesarean section were excluded from the denominator or not (Analysis 1.4). Studies that reported the mean after excluding women who underwent caesarean section showed no effect on the duration of second stage of labour in the early initiation group (Analysis 1.4.1). In contrast, the analysis of all recruited women showed significantly shorter duration in the early initiation group (Analysis 1.4.2). As there was low heterogeneity (I2: 21%), the results were pooled and there was no difference in the duration of second stage of labour (mean difference (MD) ‐3.22; 95% CI ‐6.71 to 0.27, random‐effects) between early and late initiation of epidural analgesia (Analysis 1.4). There was no statistical difference between subgroups for epidural only versus CSE (Analysis 1.28), spontaneous versus induced labour, or both (Analysis 1.31), local anaesthetic only versus opioid‐containing regimens (Analysis 1.29), and high‐dose versus low‐dose regimens (Analysis 1.30)

The pooled results showed no difference in Apgar scores less than seven at one minute (RR 0.96; 95% CI 0.84 to 1.10) between early and late initiation of epidural analgesia (Analysis 1.5).     Four studies, which analysed a total of 14,004 women, reported the umbilical cord arterial pH (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Wang 2009; Wong 2005). The pooled results showed no difference in umbilical artery pH (MD 0.01; 95% CI ‐0.01 to 0.03) between early and late initiation of epidural analgesia (Analysis 1.7). There was considerable heterogeneity (I2: 76%), which mainly arose from extremely narrow confidence intervals. Pooling of results was done despite heterogeneity, considering that it was clinically inconsequential.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    All nine included studies did not show any difference in the risk of caesarean section or instrumental birth, duration of second stage of labour, Apgar scores at one minute and five minutes, umbilical artery pH or umbilical venous pH, naloxone administration to infant, meconium‐stained amniotic fluid, use of oxytocin augmentation, materna hypotension, maternal fever, malposition at delivery and malposition at vaginal delivery between early initiation and late initiation of epidural analgesia. There was significant heterogeneity in the data for duration of first stage of labour.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Four trials (Chestnut 1994b; Luxman 1998; Wang 2009; Wong 2005) studied women with spontaneous labour; two trials (Chestnut 1994a; Wong 2009) studied women with augmented labour via intravenous oxytocin; and one trial (Ohel 2006) studied women with both spontaneous and induced labour. None of the studies studied multiparous women in labour.

    Quality of the evidence

    All nine studies reported random allocation but only seven described the method of randomisation and allocation concealment with low risk of selection bias (Chestnut 1994a; Chestnut 1994b; Ohel 2006; Wang 2009; Wang 2011; Wong 2005; Wong 2009). There was an unclear risk of selection bias in the remaining two studies (Luxman 1998; Parameswara 2012). There was a low risk of detection bias, attrition bias, reporting bias or other potential bias.

    None of the studies were described as blinded, therefore there was high risk of performance bias.

    The quality of the evidence was high for most of the outcomes listed in summary of findings Table for the main comparison. We downgraded the quality of the evidence for the first stage of labour two levels to low due to inconsistency, and for umbilical vein pH (one level) due to imprecision.

    Potential biases in the review process

    Potential biases in the review process can arise from clinical heterogeneity due to the following factors: differing definitions of early and late with regards to the degree of cervical dilatation, the dose, concentration and technique of initiation of epidural analgesia, and the analgesia received by women in the groups randomised to receive late initiation of epidural analgesia; details of which have been provided in Data synthesis.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We did not find any systematic reviews of randomised controlled trials comparing early initiation versus late initiation of epidural analgesia in labour.
