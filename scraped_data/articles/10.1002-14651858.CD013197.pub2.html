    • Background

    Laparoscopic surgery is the preferred option for many procedures. To properly perform laparoscopic surgery, it is essential that sudden movements and abdominal contractions in patients are prevented, as it limits the surgeon's view. There has been a growing interest in the potential beneficial effect of deep neuromuscular blockade (NMB) in laparoscopic surgery. Deep NMB improves the surgical field by preventing abdominal contractions, and it is thought to decrease postoperative pain. However, it is uncertain if deep NMB improves intraoperative safety and thereby improves clinical outcomes.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of deep neuromuscular blockade versus no, shallow, or moderate neuromuscular blockade during laparoscopic intraâ or transperitoneal procedures in adults.

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. The latest search date was 31 July 2023.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised clinical trials (irrespective of language, blinding, or publication status) in adults undergoing laparoscopic intraâ or transperitoneal procedures comparing deep NMB to moderate, shallow, or no NMB. We excluded trials that did not report any of the primary or secondary outcomes of our review.

    We used standard Cochrane methods. Our primary outcomes were 1. allâcause mortality, 2. healthârelated quality of life, and 3. proportion of participants with serious adverse events. Our secondary outcomes were 4. proportion of participants with nonâserious adverse events, 5. readmissions within three months, 6. shortâterm pain scores, 7. measurements of postoperative recovery, and 8. operating time. We used GRADE to assess the certainty of evidence for each outcome.

    Three trials compared deep versus shallow NMB. The trials did not report on mortality and healthârelated quality of life.     There was insufficient evidence to draw conclusions about the effects of deep NMB compared to moderate NMB on allâcause mortality and serious adverse events.     • Does deep muscle relaxation during laparoscopic surgery improve outcomes?

    Key messages

    â Deep muscle relaxation during laparoscopic surgery may not affect mortality (death) and morbidity (ill heath), but we are very uncertain about the results.

    â Deep muscle relaxation probably makes little to no difference in healthârelated quality of life (wellâbeing) up to four days after the surgery.

    What is deep muscle relaxation in laparoscopic surgery?

    Surgery of the abdomen is increasingly performed by laparoscopy. Laparoscopy is an operation using a camera inserted through one or several small cuts (incisions) (usually 0.5 cm to 1.5 cm) in the skin, for example, near the belly button. To improve the surgeon's working conditions, there has been a growing interest in the potential beneficial effect of deep muscle relaxation (deep neuromuscular blockade) to prevent patients' movements and abdominal contractions. There is evidence that surgeons rate working conditions higher when using deep neuromuscular blockade. This could result in improvement in patient's outcomes, such as a reduction in mortality, fewer complications, or fewer unwarranted events. This review compares deep neuromuscular blockade to different levels of superficial or no neuromuscular blockade.

    What did we want to find out?

    Does deep muscle relaxation during laparoscopic surgery improve patients' outcomes?

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that compared deep muscle relaxation to one of the following levels of more superficial muscle relaxation:

    â no muscle relaxation;

    â shallow muscle relaxation;

    â moderate muscle relaxation.

    What did we find?

    We found 42 studies that involved 3898 people who underwent any type of laparoscopic surgery in the abdomen. In most cases, these were surgeries to the gut or stomach (20 studies). The studies were conducted across the world. Most studies compared deep muscle relaxation to moderate muscle relaxation (38 studies). Followâup of complications and mortality ranged from three days to 60 days after surgery. Pharmaceutical companies or forâprofit organisations funded 22 of the studies.

    Main results

    Mortality and serious unwanted effects almost did not occur in the comparison of deep muscle relaxation and moderate muscle relaxation. Therefore, it is not possible to conclude whether deep muscle relaxation reduces any of those outcomes. There are probably no differences in healthârelated quality of life shortly after surgery and in duration of surgery. It is uncertain whether deep muscle relaxation changes pain scores after surgery, duration of hospital stay, or number of readmissions.

    We found too few studies for the comparison of deep muscle relaxation to shallow muscle relaxation and no muscle relaxation to draw conclusions.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    For most outcomes, our confidence in the evidence is limited. This is due to the low number of unwanted effects reported and concerns about how some of the studies were conducted.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up to date to 31 July 2023.

    Implications for practice

    There is very lowâcertainty evidence that there is no beneficial effect of deep neuromuscular blockade (NMB) regarding mortality and serious adverse events intraoperatively and up to three months postoperative.

    Deep NMB likely results in little to no difference in healthârelated quality of life compared to moderate NMB. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of deep NMB on nonâserious adverse events, length of hospital stay, or pain scores.

    Implications for research

    We need further randomised clinical trials with adequate reporting of all (serious) adverse events and postoperative complications to evaluate whether deep NMB has a beneficial effect on safety.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    In an attempt to reduce the impact of surgery, there has been a tremendous increase in the number of laparoscopic surgeries, as laparoscopy is associated with quicker convalescence, reduced postoperative pain, and shorter duration of surgery (Buia 2015; Chen 2014; Cheng 2014; Fonouni 2014; Galaal 2012; Keus 2006; Keus 2010; Reza 2006; Vennix 2014; Wilson 2011). The necessary elevation of intraâabdominal pressure during laparoscopic surgery affects vascular and haemodynamic systems, thereby temporarily decreasing intraâabdominal perfusion (Junghans 1997; Leister 2004; Schilling 1997; Sodha 2016). To perform laparoscopic surgery effectively, it is essential that sudden movements and abdominal contractions in patients are prevented, as it limits the surgeon's view. Intraâabdominal pressure is often increased when surgical sight is not optimal. Nonetheless, there is increasing evidence that lowering intraâabdominal pressure has advantages for patient outcomes (ReijndersâBoerboom 2023).

    Description of the intervention

    Neuromuscular blocking agents (NMBAs) are used during surgery to facilitate endotracheal intubation and intraoperatively to improve surgical conditions. NMBAs can be divided into nonâdepolarising and depolarising agents. Only nonâdepolarising agents are used to obtain deep NMB. The method of action of nonâdepolarising agents such as cisatracurium, vecuronium, atracurium, and rocuronium is competitive binding to acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction of skeletal muscles, preventing acetylcholineâinduced depolarisation and thereby contraction of the muscles (AppiahâAnkam 2004).

    Depth of muscle relaxation is measured by acceleromyography by means of the soâcalled trainâofâfour (TOF) twitch stimulation (FuchsâBuder 2009). To quantify the amount of muscle relaxation, the ulnar nerve is supramaximally stimulated four times, and the response of muscle twitches is measured. With an increasing depth of muscle paralysis induced by nonâdepolarising agents, the response to stimulation fades. In the case of moderate neuromuscular blockade (NMB), one to three twitches appear, which corresponds to approximately 75% to 90% of receptors blocked. When TOF response is absent, the postâtetanic count (PTC) measures the response after a tetanic stimulus (FuchsâBuder 2007). Deep NMB is defined as no TOF response and a PTC of one or two (Albers 2019).

    A major concern about the use of NMBAs, especially after the application of deep NMB, is the occurrence of residual muscle paralysis. Residual NMB is associated with increased mortality and morbidity due to, for example, the risk of pulmonary complications. However, after the introduction of sugammadex, a reversal agent for nonâdepolarising NMBAs, recovery of muscle relaxation is quicker, and the occurrence of residual paralysis decreases substantially (Hristovska 2017). Sugammadex binds to free plasma nonâdepolarising NMBAs resulting in a quick reversal of muscle paralysis, enabling the use of NMB until the end of surgery.

    How the intervention might work

    It is thought that deep NMB provides better intraoperative surgical conditions by improving the surgical field and preventing abdominal contractions and sudden movements. This was confirmed in previous systematic reviews on deep NMB (Bruintjes 2017; Madsen 2015; Park 2018; Raval 2020). Improvement of surgical conditions could improve intraoperative safety and reduce intraâ and postoperative complications.

    The depth of NMB is measured by the abductor pollicis longus, a peripheral muscle that belongs to the muscle groups that recover slowest from muscle relaxation (Leikin 2007; Pansard 1987). Recovery of function at the ulnar nerve is used as the gold standard that extubation can be performed safely. However, it is known that different muscle groups recover differently from NMBAs. Muscles of the diaphragm and abdomen recover more quickly, resulting in possible abdominal contractions with moderate NMB (Hemmerling 2007; Mencke 2013).

    Deep NMB can prevent unnecessary raising of intraâabdominal pressure during laparoscopy. There is a growing interest in performing laparoscopic surgery under lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum to reduce ischaemiaâreperfusion injury (Gurusamy 2014; Hua 2014; Ãzdemirâvan Brunschot 2016). Deep NMB can facilitate an adequate surgical field while operating with lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum (Kim 2016; Koo 2018; Ozdemirâvan Brunschot 2018; Yoo 2015).

    Last, it is proposed that deep NMB may have a positive impact on postoperative inflammation. By binding various nicotinic acetylcholine receptors in the antiâinflammatory cascade, a deep NMB itself could have antiâinflammatory effects (Fanelli 2016). Moreover, due to the easier performance of surgery, surgeryâinduced cellular stress and damage are reduced, consequently reducing the postoperative inflammation reaction.

    Why it is important to do this review

    The effect of deep NMB has been studied in several randomised clinical trials. Previously published systematic reviews and metaâanalyses found evidence that deep NMB in laparoscopic procedures provides better intraoperative surgical field rating scores. However, due to a lack of sufficient evidence, clinical outcomes such as safety and quality of recovery could not be assessed (Bruintjes 2017; Madsen 2015; Park 2018; Raval 2020). With the expansion of the body of evidence due to recently published randomised clinical trials, we aimed to assess with the best level of evidence for the effects of deep NMB in adults undergoing laparoscopic abdominal procedures regarding clinical outcomes.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised clinical trials irrespective of format, language, or publication date. We included unpublished trials only if methodological descriptions and trial data were provided.     Types of participants

    We included adults (aged 18 years and older) undergoing laparoscopic intraperitoneal and transperitoneal surgery.

    Types of interventions

    We included all trials comparing deep NMB to moderate, shallow, or no NMB. We made the following comparisons.

    • Deep NMB versus moderate NMB

    • Deep NMB versus shallow NMB

    • Deep NMB versus no NMB

    We included any type of nonâdepolarising NMBA.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (LMCJ and VB) independently extracted the following data using standardised data collection forms which were first tested on one study in the review.

    • Study design (parallel, crossâover trial, or clusterârandomised trial)

    • Participants: number of participants, sex, age, weight, body mass index

    • Surgery: type of laparoscopy, pressure level of pneumoperitoneum

    • Intervention: level of NMB (and specification) of experimental and control group, type and amount of NMBA used, type and amount of reversal agent used

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (LMCJ, KIA, VB) independently assessed the risk of bias (high, low, or unclear risk of bias) for each included study. In accordance with the protocol, we used the RoB 1 tool (Bruintjes 2018; Higgins 2017). We reported the following domains.

    Random sequence generation

    • Low risk of bias: the study authors performed sequence generation using computer randomânumber generation or a randomânumber table. Drawing lots, tossing a coin, shuffling cards, and throwing dice were adequate if an independent person not otherwise involved in the study performed them.

    • Unclear risk of bias: the study authors did not specify the method of sequence generation.

    • High risk of bias: the sequence generation method was not random. We planned to include such studies only for the assessment of harms.

    Allocation concealment

    • Low risk of bias: the participant allocations could not have been foreseen in advance of or during enrolment. A central and independent randomisation unit controlled allocation. The investigators were unaware of the allocation sequence (e.g. if the allocation sequence was hidden in sequentially numbered, opaque, and sealed envelopes).

    • Unclear risk of bias: the study authors did not describe the method used to conceal the allocation, so the intervention allocations may have been foreseen before or during enrolment.

    • High risk of bias: it was likely that the investigators who assigned the participants knew the allocation sequence. We planned to include such studies only for the assessment of harms.

    Blinding of participants and personnel

    • Low risk of bias: any of the following: blinding of participants and key study personnel ensured, and it was unlikely that the blinding could have been broken; or (rarely) no blinding or incomplete blinding, but the review authors judged that the outcome was not likely to be influenced by lack of blinding, such as allâcause mortality or SAEs.

    • Unclear risk of bias: any of the following: insufficient information to permit judgement of 'low risk' or 'high risk'; or the trial did not address this outcome.

    • High risk of bias: any of the following: no blinding or incomplete blinding, and the outcome was likely to be influenced by lack of blinding; or blinding of key study participants and personnel attempted, but likely that the blinding could have been broken, and the outcome was likely to have been influenced by lack of blinding.

    Blinding of outcome assessment

    • Low risk of bias: any of the following: blinding of outcome assessment ensured, and unlikely that the blinding could have been broken; or (rarely) no blinding of outcome assessment, but the review authors judged that the outcome measurement was not likely to be influenced by lack of blinding, such as allâcause mortality or SAEs.

    • Unclear risk of bias: any of the following: insufficient information to permit judgement of 'low risk' or 'high risk'; or the trial did not address this outcome.

    • High risk of bias: any of the following: no blinding of outcome assessment, and the outcome measurement was likely to be influenced by lack of blinding; or blinding of outcome assessment, but likely that the blinding could have been broken, and the outcome measurement was likely to be influenced by lack of blinding.

    Incomplete outcome data

    • Low risk of bias: missing data were unlikely to make treatment effects depart from plausible values. The study used sufficient methods, such as multiple imputation, to handle missing data.

    • Unclear risk of bias: there was insufficient information to assess whether missing data in combination with the method used to handle missing data were likely to induce bias in the results.

    • High risk of bias: the results were likely to be biased due to missing data.

    Selective outcome reporting

    • Low risk of bias: the trial reported at least the following outcomes: allâcause mortality, healthârelated quality of life, and SAEs. If the original trial protocol was available, the outcomes should have been those set out in that protocol. If we obtain the trial protocol from a trial registry (e.g. ClinicalTrials.gov), the outcomes sought should have been those stated in the original protocol if the trial protocol was registered before or at the time that the trial was begun. If the trial protocol was registered after the trial had begun, we would not consider those outcomes to be reliable.

    • Unclear risk of bias: not all predefined or clinically relevant and reasonably expected outcomes were reported fully, or it was unclear whether data on these outcomes were recorded or not.

    • High risk of bias: one or more predefined or clinically relevant and reasonably expected outcomes were not reported, despite the fact that data on these outcomes should have been available and even recorded.

    Other bias

    • Low risk of bias: the trial appeared to be free of other components that could have put it at risk of bias, for example, inappropriate control or dose or administration of control, baseline differences, and early stopping. We also took into account if studies reported on calibration of the trainâofâfour watch, defined deep NMB as no responses to trainâofâfour and at least one response to postâtetanicâcount, and if they mentioned the use of additional amounts of neuromuscularâblocking agent as an escape in the moderate neuromuscular group in case of insufficient surgical field.

    • Unclear risk of bias: the trial may or may not have been free of other components that could have put it at risk of bias or unintendedly influence the outcomes.

    • High risk of bias: there were other factors in the trial that could have put it at risk of bias, for example, inappropriate control or dose or administration of control, baseline differences, early stopping, and inappropriate measurement of neuromuscular monitoring as this could result in unintended intergroup differences in level of NMB.

    We resolved disagreements between the assessing review authors by discussion or by consulting a third review author (MW).

    Overall risk of bias

    If a trial was at low risk of bias in all domains, we considered it at low risk of bias overall. If a trial was at 'unclear risk of bias' or 'high risk of bias' in one or more of the specified individual domains, we considered it at high risk of bias overall (Higgins 2017).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We planned to perform a metaâanalysis for the three different comparisons. However, only the first and second comparison (deep NMB versus moderate NMB and deep NMB versus shallow NMB) had a sufficient number of trials available to perform the metaâanalysis. For dichotomous outcomes (proportion of participants with SAE, readmission rate, etc.), we calculated the risk ratio (RR) and 95% confidence interval (CI) or Peto odds ratio (OR) and 95% CI depending on the number of events per group. For continuous data (e.g. healthârelated quality of life), we calculated the mean differences (MDs) with 95% CIs. All trials reporting healthârelated quality of life used the same scale, so it was not necessary to use a standardised mean difference. We regarded pain scores as a continuous outcome to prevent losing important information and, therefore, measured MDs.

    It was planned to calculate the hazard ratio (HR) for longâterm mortality. However, combined mortality was still too low to calculate this outcome. We used the randomâeffects metaâanalysis for our main analysis and the fixedâeffect model as sensitivity analysis. For dichotomous outcomes with sparse events, we presented the Peto fixedâeffect model.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the individual participant undergoing a laparoscopic intraâ or transperitoneal procedure. We planned to take into account crossâover and clusterârandomised trials, but none were identified.

    Dealing with missing data

    We performed analysis on an intentionâtoâtreat basis. Whenever possible, we analysed all participants in the group to which they were randomised. We contacted authors of trials with missing or unclear data.

    When we were unable to obtain information from the investigators regarding continuous data, we calculated the mean and standard deviation from the median and interquartile range according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2022; Wan 2014). When calculation of the standard deviation was not possible, we imputed the standard deviation as the highest standard deviation in the remaining trials in the outcome. We used sensitivity analyses to assess the impact of including such studies.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed heterogeneity amongst trials using the I2 statistic. An I2 statistic greater than 50% represented substantial heterogeneity. In these analyses we performed subgroup analyses to explore heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We included both published and unpublished studies. Moreover, we searched for published protocols and trial registrations for the included studies. As we included more than 10 studies, we visually assessed reporting bias by use of funnel plots (Egger 1997).

    Data synthesis

    We only performed metaâanalyses on the first and second comparison (deep NMB versus moderate NMB and deep NMB versus shallow NMB) where the studies were sufficiently similar for pooling, using Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2023). We calculated the MDs with 95% CIs for continuous outcomes and RR or OR with 95% CIs for dichotomous variables. We presented the randomâeffects model for most outcomes as there was significant heterogeneity amongst trials, and it was the most conservative model. If metaâanalysis was not appropriate due to an insufficient number of eligible studies, we provided a narrative description.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to conduct the following subgroup analyses to identify sources of heterogeneity.

    • Trials at low risk of bias compared to trials at high risk of bias.

    • Type of laparoscopic procedures: trials with hepatobiliary surgery compared with trials with gastrointestinal surgery, compared with trials with gynaecological surgery, compared with trials with urological surgery.

    • Level of pressure of pneumoperitoneum: trials with lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum (10 mmHg or less) compared to trials with standardâpressure pneumoperitoneum (greater than 10 mmHg).

    • Body mass index: trials with healthy weight body mass index (18.5 kg/m2 to 25 kg/m2) compared to trials with overweight or obese participants (greater than 25 kg/m2). No trials reported underweight participants.

    Subgroup analyses on trials with low and high risk of bias and on type of laparoscopic procedure have not been conducted, as it led to fewer than five trials per subgroup for the individual outcomes. We assessed differences between subgroups by performing a test for heterogeneity across subgroups and analysing the I2 statistic.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We performed a sensitivity analysis by imputing data for binary outcomes using various scenarios, such as bestâworst and worstâbest scenarios. Due to the low number of incidents in the mortality analysis and the readmission analysis, we did not perform these analyses for these outcomes. For continuous data, we performed an analysis excluding all studies with imputed data. As we identified no suitable clusterârandomised clinical trials, we did not conduct an analysis with the adjusted effect estimates.

    Assessment of imprecision with Trial Sequential Analysis

    Cumulative metaâanalyses are at risk of producing random errors due to sparse data and multiple testing of accumulating data (Brok 2008; Brok 2009; Thorlund 2009; Thorlund 2010; Wetterslev 2008; Wetterslev 2009; Wetterslev 2017); therefore, Trial Sequential Analysis can be applied as a secondary analysis to control this risk. Trial Sequential Analysis considers the choice of statistical model (fixedâeffect or randomâeffects metaâanalysis) and diversity, and provides the Trial Sequential Analysisâadjusted CIs (Thorlund 2017; TSA 2017).

    The underlying assumption of Trial Sequential Analysis is that testing for statistical significance may be performed each time a new trial is included in the metaâanalysis. The trial references are arranged automatically by the programme according to the year of publication; if more than one trial is published in a year, the programme sorts out the references alphabetically, according to the last name of the first author. Based on the calculated diversityâadjusted required information size (DARIS; i.e. the number of participants needed in a metaâanalysis to detect or reject a certain intervention effect), we can construct the trial sequential monitoring boundaries for benefit, harm, and futility (Brok 2008; Brok 2009; Thorlund 2010; Wetterslev 2008; Wetterslev 2009; Wetterslev 2017). These boundaries determine the statistical inference one may draw regarding the cumulative metaâanalysis that does not reach the DARIS; if the trial sequential monitoring boundary for benefit or harm is crossed before the DARIS is reached, firm evidence can be established, and further trials may be superfluous. If the boundaries for benefit or harm are not crossed, it is most probably necessary to continue running trials to detect or reject a certain intervention effect. However, if the cumulative Zâcurve crosses the trial sequential monitoring boundaries for futility, no additional trials may be required.

    In our Trial Sequential Analysis for the dichotomous outcomes, we based the DARIS on event proportion in the control group, assuming a plausible relative risk ratio of 10%, risk of type I error of 1.25% for the seven primary outcomes and 0.83 for the 11 secondary outcomes (Jakobsen 2014), risk of type II error of 10% (power 90%), and the diversity of trials included in the metaâanalysis (Castellini 2018; Jakobsen 2014).

    For continuous data, we used a risk of type I error of 1.25% for the seven primary outcomes and 0.83% for the 11 secondary outcomes (Jakobsen 2014), a risk of type II error of 10% (power 90%), and the diversity of trials included in the metaâanalysis. For healthârelated quality of life, as assessed by the quality of recovery questionnaires, we considered 10 points a minimal relevant difference (MIREDIF) (De Oliveira 2011; Myles 2016; Ãzdemirâvan Brunschot 2016). For pain, we used a MIREDIF of 1 point on the visual analogue scale (VAS) (Todd 1996). For length of hospital stay, we used a MIREDIF of one day. For the duration of surgery, we considered a MIREDIF of 15 minutes. A control group variance for each of the continuous outcomes was used as observed in the trials and the diversity observed in the metaâanalysis. We used the DerâSimonian randomâeffects model with Trial Sequential Analysis software, version 0.9.5.10 beta (TSA 2017).

    In Trial Sequential Analysis, assessment of imprecision is downgraded by two levels if the accrued number of participants is below 50% of the DARIS, and one level if between 50% and 100% of DARIS. We did not downgrade if futility or DARIS was reached or crossed by the cumulative Zâscore. A more detailed description of Trial Sequential Analysis and the software programme can be found at www.ctu.dk/tsa/ (Thorlund 2017).

    GRADE considers the assessment of five domains: risk of bias, inconsistency of the effect, imprecision of effect estimates, indirectness of the evidence, and risk of publication bias.     Regarding risk of bias, we used the overall judgement for an outcome. 'Low' risk of bias indicates 'no limitation (the certainty was downgraded)'; and 'high' risk of bias (including 'unclear' risk of bias) indicates either 'serious limitation' or 'very serious limitation (the certainty was downgraded two levels)'.

    The levels of evidence are defined as 'high', 'moderate', 'low', or 'very low'. These grades are defined as follows.

    • High certainty: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate certainty: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low certainty: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited: the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low certainty: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate: the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    We justified all decisions to downgrade the certainty of evidence of the included studies using the footnotes, and where necessary, provided comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review.     Intervention

    One trial compared deep NMB to no NMB (Blobner 2014), three trials compared deep NMB to shallow NMB (Madsen 2016; Putz 2016; StaehrâRye 2014), and the remaining trials compared deep NMB to moderate NMB. Most trials defined deep NMB as PTC one to two or as PTC below five; however, three trials defined deep NMB as any PTC count with TOF of zero (Abu Yazed 2019; Lee 2022; Putz 2016). Most trials obtained deep NMB by the administration of rocuronium. Only DiazâCambronero 2020 did not exactly report the agent used. All trials administered sugammadex at 2 mg/kg to 8 mg/kg to reverse deep NMB.

    Comparisons

    Most trials defined moderate NMB as a TOF count of one to two. Five trials defined moderate NMB as TOF one to three (Barrio 2017; Bruintjes 2019; Coviello 2023; Gu 2021; Lee 2022). Three trials aimed for a TOF of one in the moderate group (Lowen 2022; Ozdemirâvan Brunschot 2018; Kathopoulis 2022). FuchsâBuder 2019 aimed for a TOF of one to four, DiazâCambronero 2020 and Rosenberg 2017 for a TOF of two to four. Shallow NMB was defined as induction dose NMBA with only rescue dose at request.     Discussion

    Summary of main results

    In this systematic review, we compared deep NMB to various levels of more superficial or no NMB. We included 42 trials with 3898 participants contributing to one or more of the outcomes.

    Metaâanalysis of 38 trials comparing deep NMB to moderate NMB revealed with very low certainty evidence that there might be no difference in shortâterm mortality and morbidity between the two treatment groups. Regarding shortâterm healthârelated quality of life, there is likely no difference between treatment groups. A mean difference of 4.53 points (95% CI 0.96 to 8.09) on the Quality of Recoveryâ40 questionnaire was found to favour deep NMB. This is lower than the mean clinically important difference of 6 to 10 points on the 40â to 200âpoint scale (De Oliveira 2011; Myles 2016; Ãzdemirâvan Brunschot 2017). Accordingly, this small difference is not clinically relevant and does not support the routine application of deep NMB over moderate NMB. The same holds true for the length of hospital stay and (also sparsely reported) the number of readmissions, as there was no difference between groups for these outcomes.

    Deep NMB may reduce pain scores one and 24 hours after surgery, but the evidence is very uncertain. Moreover, for both outcomes, the MD was lower than the minimal clinically important difference at 1 point on the visual analogue scale (Todd 1996), and there was substantial heterogeneity in these outcomes. Predefined subgroup analyses showed no reduction and, therefore, no explanation for heterogeneity, but there was a significant subgroup difference in postoperative pain scores one hour after surgery between laparoscopic surgery performed under standard or low intraâabdominal pressure. A considerable number of the assessed trials used deep NMB to facilitate the use of lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum. One recent systematic review and metaâanalysis showed lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum leads to lower pain scores in a broad group of laparoscopic procedures (ReijndersâBoerboom 2023). Subgroup analyses on the level of NMB could not be performed in that review due to a lack of trials reporting on the level of NMB used. However, subgroup analysis of our metaâanalysis supports the small observed difference in pain scores that can likely be attributed to low intraâabdominal pressure and not deep NMB.

    We identified only three trials for the comparison of deep NMB and shallow or no NMB. All three trials included in the deep NMB versus shallow NMB performed the strategy in the control group closest to 'dailyâpractice', as all participants in the control group received rocuronium during induction of anaesthesia and additional boluses only upon request. Mortality and healthârelated quality of life events did not occur or were not reported in these trials. As it is unusual not to apply any NMBA in laparoscopic surgery, it is unsurprising that only one trial was identified for the comparison with no NMB. The evidence on the effect of deep NMB compared to shallow or no NMB on SAEs is very uncertain.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The review of the first comparison of deep NMB versus moderate NMB consisted of 38 trials performed in a wide range of countries. Trials included participants undergoing several types of elective intraâ and transperitoneal laparoscopic surgery. Therefore, the conclusions are applicable to a wide range of hepatobiliary, gastrointestinal, gynaecological, and urological surgeries. Nonetheless, taking into account the considerable heterogeneity between trials, it is important to keep in mind that some surgical procedures (e.g. surgeries close to the diaphragm or procedures hindered by contraction of the abdominal wall) may benefit more from muscle relaxation than others. Postoperative mortality and perioperative complications are two of the five core outcome measures that are considered critically important in perioperative and anaesthesia research (Boney 2022). Only 12 trials including 1390 participants reported shortâterm mortality (within three months after surgery); two deaths occurred. This number is in line with the mortality rates after elective laparoscopic colorectal surgery (0.5%), gastrectomy (0.09%), and donor nephrectomy (0.03%) as reported in the majority of respective trials (Guerrier 2018; Munasinghe 2015; Segev 2010). Remarkably, less than half of the trials reported intraâ and postoperative (serious) adverse events and complications. For the trials that did assess adverse events and SAEs, there appeared to be a high level of underâreporting. From validation of the International ClassIntra score for intraâ and postoperative complications, we know that for all types of surgery combined, 24% of patients experience at least one intraoperative adverse event and 33% experience at least one postoperative complication (DellâKuster 2020). The European Association of Endoscopic Surgeons classification of intraoperative surgical (not including the anaesthesia complications) adverse events in laparoscopic surgery reveals 60% grade I (nonâconsequential), 37% grade II (requiring minor corrective action), 2.4% grade III (major correction or change in postoperative care), and 0.1% grade IV (lifeâthreatening) complications (Francis 2018). Therefore, in the combined 3.4% intraoperative SAEs and 5.8% intraoperative adverse events presented in this metaâanalysis, a large part of these events is missing despite the fact that patients who receive NMB are constantly being monitored. Analysis of the available adverse event and SAE data suggests no benefit from the deep or moderate NMB, but we are hesitant to draw this conclusion when a considerable part of the supporting information was evidently incomplete. This is unfortunate considering the consensus that these core outcomes are the most critically important to perioperative and anaesthesia research. However, reporting biases are likely to favour deep NMB rather than the control group. One large ongoing trial evaluating adverse events and SAEs as primary endpoints for deep versus standard NMB in 922 participants will likely provide more certainty (Honing 2021). However, the trial is being performed in people undergoing laparoscopy at standardâpressure pneumoperitoneum, so the accompanying effect of lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum will still remain unclear.

    As a result of the low number of trials in the second (deep NMB versus shallow NMB) and third (deep NMB versus no NMB) comparisons, no definitive conclusion can be formed on the effect of deep NMB on the three primary outcomes.

    Quality of the evidence

    The overall certainty of the evidence was moderate to very low (summary of findings Table 1; summary of findings Table 2; summary of findings Table 3; Table 1). Most trials were at unclear risk of bias, with high rates of selective reporting bias. Moreover, due to the low number of events, the certainty of the evidence was downgraded because of the wide CIs of the effect estimates. For several outcomes, there was significant heterogeneity in the analyses. We analysed heterogeneity by performing subgroup analyses, but this did not result in a lower I2 statistic. Therefore, we downgraded the certainty of the evidence.

    Another serious possible weakness in the evidence was that only seven out of 42 trials reported the adequacy of the intended intervention (Albers 2022; FuchsâBuder 2019; Honing 2020; Martini 2014; StaehrâRye 2014; Torensma 2016; Yoo 2015). Adequate deep NMB requires frequent monitoring and can prove challenging even for experienced anaesthesiologists. The potency of NMBAs is influenced by many factors such as age, body mass index, race, and the type of anaesthetic used, so the required dose is highly variable between patients (AhlstrÃ¶m 2021; Collins 2000; Moerer 2005; Oris 1993; StÃ¤uble 2015). Without neuromuscular monitoring data, the accuracy of the intervention remains questionable. Moreover, a consistent moderate NMB is presumably already deeper than conventional clinical practice and may also explain why there was no difference for moderate versus deep NMB.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The search process was conducted according to the standard methodology of Cochrane HepatoâBiliary Group. Therefore, we believe that this review is comprehensive in identifying all eligible studies. We searched trial registries to identify unpublished trials to explore reporting bias, but none of them could be included as none provided methodological descriptions and trial data. At least two review authors independently performed identification of trials for inclusion and extraction of data, thus minimising errors. The large number of predefined outcomes may have led to a multiple comparison bias. We attempted to minimise this effect by performing sensitivity analyses and subgroup analyses only as predefined in the protocol. We imputed the mean and standard deviation as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2022), which may have introduced bias. Sensitivity analyses, by excluding these trials, did not change the results.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Several systematic reviews have evaluated the effect of deep NMB (Bruintjes 2017; Liu 2023; Madsen 2015; Park 2018; Raval 2020). The main outcome of all these reviews was the effect of deep NMB on surgical conditions. All five systematic reviews concluded that deep NMB enables better surgical vision during surgery. As the improvement of surgical conditions by deep NMB could lead to higher safety and lower mortality, our review aimed to investigate this by solely focussing on clinical outcomes. Here, we found too little evidence to support a higher safety or lower mortality for deep NMB. The previous systematic reviews and metaâanalyses did not cover these outcomes. Bruintjes 2017, Liu 2023, and Raval 2020 did report a metaâanalysis of five, 10, and six trials, respectively, on the effect of deep NMB on postoperative pain scores. All stated lower pain scores after surgery in the deep NMB groups. However, in our review, evidence about the effect of deep NMB on pain scores was very uncertain. The observed effect is presumably due to the accompanying lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum that was used.

    Altogether, there was a very limited certainty of evidence regarding deep NMB versus moderate, shallow, or no NMB in laparoscopic surgery and routine application of deep NMB is not supported. The major impediments to this conclusion are poor reporting of core outcome measures such as mortality, adverse events, and SAEs, confounding of intraâabdominal pressure, and possible variation in effect between different abdominal surgical procedures. It is paramount for future trials to accurately monitor and describe complications according to standardised classifications such as ClassIntra and ClavienâDindo. Moreover, further evaluation of the optimal depth of NMB to facilitate lowâpressure pneumoperitoneum for different types of laparoscopic surgery is still warranted.
