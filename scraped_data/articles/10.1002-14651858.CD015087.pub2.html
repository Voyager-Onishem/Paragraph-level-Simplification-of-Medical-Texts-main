Diclofenac for acute postoperative pain in children

    • Background

    Many children undergo various surgeries, which often lead to acute postoperative pain. This pain influences recovery and quality of life. Non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), specifically cyclo‐oxygenase (COX) inhibitors such as diclofenac, can be used to treat pain and reduce inflammation. There is uncertainty regarding diclofenac's benefits and harms compared to placebo or other drugs for postoperative pain.

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of diclofenac (any dose) for acute postoperative pain management in children compared with placebo, other active comparators, or diclofenac administered by different routes (e.g. oral, rectal, etc.) or strategies (e.g. 'as needed' versus 'as scheduled').

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, and trial registries on 11 April 2022.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in children under 18 years of age undergoing surgery that compared diclofenac (delivered in any dose and route) to placebo or any active pharmacological intervention. We included RCTs comparing different administration routes of diclofenac and different strategies.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane. Our primary outcomes were: pain relief (PR) reported by the child, defined as the proportion of children reporting 50% or better postoperative pain relief; pain intensity (PI) reported by the child; adverse events (AEs); and serious adverse events (SAEs). We presented results using risk ratios (RR), mean differences (MD), and standardised mean differences (SMD), with the associated confidence intervals (CI).

    Main results

    We included 32 RCTs with 2250 children. All surgeries were done using general anaesthesia. Most studies (27) included children above age three. Only two studies had an overall low risk of bias; 30 had an unclear or high risk of bias in one or several domains.

    Diclofenac versus placebo (three studies)

    None of the included studies reported on PR or PI. We are very uncertain about the benefits and harms of diclofenac versus placebo on nausea/vomiting (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.38 to 1.80; 2 studies, 100 children) and any reported bleeding (RR 3.00, 95% CI 0.34 to 26.45; 2 studies, 100 children), both very low‐certainty evidence. None of the included studies reported SAEs.

    Diclofenac versus opioids (seven studies)

    We are very uncertain if diclofenac reduces PI at 2 to 24 hours postoperatively compared to opioids (median pain intensity 0.3 (interquartile range (IQR) 0.0 to 2.5) for diclofenac versus median 0.7 (IQR 0.1 to 2.4) in the opioid group; 1 study, 50 children; very low‐certainty evidence). None of the included studies reported on PR or PI for other time points. Diclofenac probably results in less nausea/vomiting compared to opioids (41.0% in opioids, 31.0% in diclofenac; RR 0.75, 95% CI 0.58 to 0.96; 7 studies, 463 participants), and probably increases any reported bleeding (5.4% in opioids, 16.5% in diclofenac; RR 3.06, 95% CI 1.31 to 7.13; 2 studies, 222 participants), both moderate‐certainty evidence. None of the included studies reported SAEs.

    Diclofenac versus paracetamol (10 studies)

    None of the included studies assessed child‐reported PR. Compared to paracetamol, we are very uncertain if diclofenac: reduces PI at 0 to 2 hours postoperatively (SMD ‐0.45, 95% CI ‐0.74 to ‐0.15; 2 studies, 180 children); reduces PI at 2 to 24 hours postoperatively (SMD ‐0.64, 95% CI ‐0.89 to ‐0.39; 3 studies, 300 children); reduces nausea/vomiting (RR 0.47, 95% CI 0.25 to 0.87; 5 studies, 348 children); reduces bleeding events (RR 0.57, 95% CI 0.12 to 2.62; 5 studies, 332 participants); or reduces SAEs (RR 0.50, 95% CI 0.05 to 5.22; 1 study, 60 children).

    The evidence certainty was very low for all outcomes.

    Diclofenac versus bupivacaine (five studies)

    None of the included studies reported on PR or PI. Compared to bupivacaine, we are very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on nausea/vomiting (RR 1.28, 95% CI 0.58 to 2.78; 3 studies, 128 children) and SAEs (RR 4.52, 95% CI 0.23 to 88.38; 1 study, 38 children), both very low‐certainty evidence.

    Diclofenac versus active pharmacological comparator (10 studies)

    We are very uncertain about the benefits and harms of diclofenac versus any other active pharmacological comparator (dexamethasone, pranoprofen, fluorometholone, oxybuprocaine, flurbiprofen, lignocaine), and for different routes and delivery of diclofenac, due to few and small studies, no reporting of key outcomes, and very low‐certainty evidence for the reported outcomes. We are unable to draw any meaningful conclusions from the numerical results.

    Authors' conclusions

    We remain uncertain about the efficacy of diclofenac compared to placebo, active comparators, or by different routes of administration, for postoperative pain management in children. This is largely due to authors not reporting on clinically important outcomes; unclear reporting of the trials; or poor trial conduct reducing our confidence in the results.

    We remain uncertain about diclofenac's safety compared to placebo or active comparators, except for the comparison of diclofenac with opioids: diclofenac probably results in less nausea and vomiting compared with opioids, but more bleeding events.

    For healthcare providers managing postoperative pain, diclofenac is a COX inhibitor option, along with other pharmacological and non‐pharmacological approaches. Healthcare providers should weigh the benefits and risks based on what is known of their respective pharmacological effects, rather than known efficacy. For surgical interventions in which bleeding or nausea and vomiting are a concern postoperatively, the risks of adverse events using opioids or diclofenac for managing pain should be considered.

    • What are the benefits and harms of diclofenac for managing children's pain after they have had an operation?

    Key messages

    • Due to a lack of robust evidence, the benefits and harms of diclofenac for managing children's pain after they've had an operation are unclear.

    • Diclofenac probably reduces nausea and vomiting, but probably increases the risk of bleeding, compared to opioids (such as morphine).

    • Well‐designed and comprehensively‐reported studies are needed to shed light on the benefits and harms of diclofenac compared to opioids or other medicines, or for different ways of giving diclofenac to children.

    Postoperative pain management

    Pain after surgery or a procedure is common and can influence recovery and return to normal activities. There are many ways to reduce pain after an operation, including medicines such as diclofenac. Diclofenac is part of a class of medicines called non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) that can reduce inflammation and pain. They can be given by different routes, such as by mouth or through the anus into the rectum (back passage), and before, during, and after an operation.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out the benefits and harms of diclofenac compared to other treatments, as well as the most effective way to deliver diclofenac to children.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that compared (a) diclofenac to other treatments for postoperative pain; or (b) different ways of delivering diclofenac to children. We summarised the results and rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and size.

    What did we find?

    We found 32 studies that included 2250 children with postoperative pain. The largest study included 183 children and the smallest, 31 children. The studies were conducted in many countries around the world; the most common location was the United Kingdom (10 studies). The average age of the children was between 2 and 14 years. Diclofenac was used to manage postoperative pain in 12 different types of surgery; most commonly, ear, nose, and throat surgery (9 studies) or eye surgery (6 studies).

    Main results

    It is unclear if diclofenac given before, during, or after surgery has an effect on pain relief, pain intensity, or minor and serious harms compared to a placebo (a 'dummy' or sham treatment that does not contain any medicine but looks or tastes identical to the medicine being tested).

    It is unclear if diclofenac given before, during, or after surgery has an effect on pain relief or pain intensity compared to other medicines, such as opioids, paracetamol, or bupivacaine. Diclofenac probably results in less nausea and vomiting compared to opioids (7 studies involving 463 children), but probably increases the risk of any bleeding (2 studies involving 222 children), but we are very unsure about serious harms (death, long‐term disability, or hospitalisation).

    It is thus unclear if diclofenac given before, during, or after surgery has an effect on pain relief, pain intensity, or minor or serious harms compared to placebo or other medicines other than opioids. It is also unclear which route (by mouth or rectal delivery) is the most effective way to manage pain after surgery in children.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    We are not confident in the evidence because of study design limitations, and none of the studies measured some of our main outcomes of interest. There were also not enough large studies to be certain about the results.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is current to April 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    For children with postoperative pain and their families

    Pain management is complex, with various causes and clinical presentations of pain, and with the different medicines and non‐pharmacological treatments available. Pain management is even more complex in children, in whom assessing pain can be challenging. Our review found that, despite the volume of research conducted and reported, the overall evidence remains uncertain about the efficacy of diclofenac compared to placebo or an active comparator, or its efficacy by different routes of administration, for postoperative pain management in children.

    For clinicians

    For most comparisons, we remain uncertain about the safety of diclofenac compared to placebo or active comparators, except for the comparison of diclofenac with opioids. For the latter comparison, we found that diclofenac probably results in less nausea and vomiting, but more bleeding events, whose nature was not clearly defined in the original studies. For surgical interventions in which bleeding or nausea and vomiting are a concern postoperatively, the risks of adverse events using opioids or diclofenac for managing pain should be considered.

    For policy‐makers

    The evidence base remains too uncertain about whether diclofenac is the appropriate choice of analgesic to reduce pain reported by children after surgery, or about the extent of any potential harms. The choice of analgesic is guided by the treating healthcare provider, who must weigh up the benefits and risks based on what is known of the pharmacological effects or indirect measures of reduced pain – for example, differences in use of rescue medication – rather than known efficacy compared to other options.

    For funders of the intervention

    Many underpowered, poorly‐conducted and poorly‐reported trials have been published in the field. We remain unable to confidently answer the clinical question regarding the clinical efficacy and safety of diclofenac compared to placebo and other active comparators. Well‐conducted trials are possible, and are needed to guide the use of diclofenac for management of postoperative pain in children. The unethical conduct of underpowered trials needs close monitoring by research ethics committees.

    Implications for research

    General implications

    Large and well‐reported studies comparing diclofenac to placebo or to other commonly‐used pharmacological alternatives are needed in order to assess if diclofenac is better, similar, or worse than any other option to treat postoperative pain in children. Future research might compare different routes, schemes, or doses of delivering diclofenac, which have been assessed in very few studies.

    Design

    Most trials of diclofenac are small and not adequately powered to accurately detect any benefits or harms of diclofenac compared to placebo, to other pharmacological comparisons, or via different routes of administration. Larger trials powered to detect differences for both benefits and harms are needed.

    To limit any risk of bias, and subsequently increase our confidence in the evidence, implementing and accurately reporting any blinding of participants, personnel, or outcome assessors would increase the overall certainty of the evidence and support clinical decision‐making. In addition, accurate and transparent reporting of randomisation and concealment procedures, as well as publishing clearly‐defined protocols before enrolment of participants, would further reduce risks of bias.

    Trial authors should reflect on the use of rescue medication in the design of the study. They should consider the potential impact of unbalanced use of rescue medication between intervention groups and the influence that could have on the main measurements of pain relief and pain intensity between groups.

    Measurement (endpoints)

    Future studies should aim to measure baseline and post‐randomisation pain intensity using validated pain scale measurements suitable for the age of the child. This would give more reliable and valid outcome data on the reduction of pain – arguably the main reason for delivering diclofenac. In addition, authors should plan for and design studies to collect and transparently report any adverse events, both major and minor, occurring within a trial. These should be comprehensively and accurately reported to facilitate comparisons between interventions.

    It is also important to measure and report additional outcomes important for children. These include time to return to activities, such as eating, walking, or school, or major adverse events, such as a prolonged hospital stay or severe harms. This would add to the body of knowledge regarding outcomes outside the clinical scenario and the acute stage of postoperative pain. The development and use of core outcome sets by researchers investigating treatments for postoperative pain would greatly enhance future evidence syntheses, by allowing for easier synthesis of trial data and better comparisons between trials in diclofenac versus other pharmacological alternatives.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Surgery is common in children. Many hospitalised children experience pain, and children who undergo surgery frequently report more pain than children going through other medical treatments (Kozlowski 2014). In the USA alone, approximately 5% of children below the age of 18 years have a surgical procedure each year, accounting for approximately 3.9 million surgeries annually (Rabbitts 2020). Surgical procedures might be required for musculoskeletal trauma, wounds, injuries (e.g. burns), congenital anomalies (e.g. cleft palate), or diseases related to the throat (e.g. infection of the tonsils), abdomen (e.g. hernia), eye (e.g. strabismus), ear (e.g. otitis media), or teeth (e.g. dental implant) (Ostlie 2010). One study found all children reported pain after tonsillectomy one day after the procedure, and over 50% of the children reported pain at some point in the seven days after surgery (Stewart 2012). In addition, most children's return to and performance of usual activities was limited during the seven days after surgery (Stewart 2012). Two other studies measuring postoperative pain in children across various surgical procedures reported moderate to severe pain in 25% and 40.5% (95% confidence interval (CI) 32.7% to 48.4%) of the children within the first day after surgery (Avian 2016 and Mekonnen 2021, respectively).

    The pain reported after surgery may be related to the presurgical condition, perioperative practice (e.g. physical position and anaesthesia), or both. Tissue damage resulting from surgery may cause pain, which can be acute or develop into long‐term pain. An acute pain taxonomy developed by an expert group (involving Analgesic, Anesthetic, and Addiction Clinical Trial Translations, Innovations, Opportunities, and Networks (ACTTION), the American Pain Society (APS), the American Academy of Pain Medicine (AAPM), and the US Food and Drug Administration) has defined acute pain as pain lasting up to seven days (Kent 2017). The taxonomy further specifies that: (1) the duration of acute pain reflects the mechanisms and severity of the underlying event; (2) the prolongation of acute pain to 30 days is common; (3) pain extending past 90 days should not be considered acute pain; and (4) our understanding of pain mechanisms is currently insufficient to link varying pain durations to specific physiological mechanisms (Kent 2017). Acute postoperative pain can occur due to inflammation, tissue damage, nerve injury, or a combination of these as a result of the surgery. Acute pain after a surgical procedure can influence recovery and quality of life. Uncontrolled acute pain could also lead to sensitisation of pain and increase the risk of developing long‐term pain (Fletcher 2015).

    Acute postoperative pain management is a critical part of patient care to reduce pain and ensure optimal recovery. To manage postoperative pain effectively and diminish the consequences of pain following surgery, it is essential to be able to measure pain. Self‐report of pain severity is considered the best available approach to measuring pain in children over six years of age (Stinson 2006). Children below six years of age often express pain in non‐verbal ways and, therefore, it is recommended that pain be assessed through behavioural pain scales (e.g. observer‐reported pain) in younger children (Stinson 2006).

    Postoperative pain management includes both pharmacological and non‐pharmacological approaches. Examples of non‐pharmacological interventions include rest, distraction, physical activity, heat‐pads, or cold compresses (Andersson 2022). Examples of recommended pharmacological agents include opioids (e.g. codeine, morphine, tramadol, and fentanyl), non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), specifically cyclo‐oxygenase (COX) inhibitors (e.g. naproxen, ibuprofen, and diclofenac), or paracetamol (Gai 2020). It is common to combine different pain management strategies, which allows a reduction of the dose, thus possibly reducing the risk of harm (Verghese 2010).

    Description of the intervention

    Non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), including COX inhibitors, are a well‐established class of medicines used since 1966 to treat symptoms such as pain and swelling (Sallmann 1986). Globally, diclofenac is one of the most widely‐used COX inhibitors (McGettigan 2013). Diclofenac is used as an analgesic and anti‐inflammatory treatment, and is recommended for both long‐ and short‐term use in a variety of conditions – for example, after tonsillectomy or hernia repair in children (Riad 2007; Warltier 2003).

    As COX inhibitors may cause gastric (i.e. stomach) irritation and can affect platelet aggregation (i.e. blood clotting), their use may increase the risk of bleeding, ulceration, nausea, and vomiting (Odom 2014). In adults, diclofenac has also been associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular events than non‐use or use of other COX inhibitors (Schmidt 2018), and is associated with kidney injury (Forrest 2002). These potential effects in children are not very well reported.

    The main routes of administration for diclofenac are orally, rectally, and by injection (Standing 2011). Another route of administration of diclofenac is by topical gels, lotions, or patches (Banning 2008). The recommended dose of diclofenac depends on the delivery mode and the age and weight of the child. For example, for children aged 12 to 16 years (45 kg to 55 kg), orally‐administered diclofenac in doses of 25 mg can be recommended up to three or four times a day, and for children aged nine to 11 years (35 kg to 44 kg), up to three times a day (FASS 2020a). In addition, for diclofenac administered rectally in children over six years of age, the recommended dose can be up to 25 mg three times a day (FASS 2020b). However, when administered in the form of an injection, approximately half the dose is usually recommended (Standing 2011). Bioavailability in children may also be higher when diclofenac is administered rectally compared to orally (Marel 2004). Administration to young children is complicated by the lack of an oral solution and of a licenced intravenous preparation in the paediatric population.

    Timing of administration of diclofenac can vary: it can be delivered preoperatively, during the operation (intraoperatively), or postoperatively to manage pain. Intraoperatively, diclofenac may be administered alone or together with local anaesthesia in circumstances when a child's pain may be detected through behaviour or talking with the child; or it may be delivered without assessing the presence of pain but when postoperative pain is anticipated. In addition, diclofenac can be administered 'as needed' (i.e. based on individual pain) and 'as scheduled' (e.g. every eight hours).

    How the intervention might work

    NSAIDs and COX inhibitors reduce fever and inflammation, prevent blood clotting, and relieve pain (Verghese 2010). They are considered clinically beneficial for minor postoperative pain control, but it is not yet clear which pharmacological agent is best for postoperative pain. Of note, as single‐drug treatment for postoperative pain in children might be insufficient, multimodal treatment has been suggested (Vittinghoff 2018; Yaster 2010). After major surgery, COX inhibitors may have an opioid‐sparing effect on pain (Grahame‐Smith 2002). One blinded randomised controlled trial (RCT) demonstrated that administering COX inhibitors postoperatively reduced adults' opioid requirements, thus potentially reducing the incidence and severity of opioid‐induced adverse events (Cepeda 2005); whether this applies to children needs further exploration.

    Pain may result from tissue damage or inflammatory mediators that act on nerve endings (e.g. bradykinin, 5‐hydroxytryptamine). The hormone prostaglandin sensitises the nerve endings to the inflammatory mediators, heightening pain (Kokki 2003). The analgesic effects of NSAIDs are indirect and largely mediated by reducing prostaglandin synthesis, thus reducing sensitisation by the inflammatory mediators (FitzGerald 2004). NSAIDs inhibit the cyclo‐oxygenase (COX) isoenzymes 1 and 2, whose role is to produce prostaglandins and thromboxane A2 (Fineschi 1997). Thromboxane A2 is responsible for platelet aggregation and vasoconstriction (narrowing of blood vessels) (Kokki 2003). Prostaglandins play a protective role in the vascular system, kidneys, and gastrointestinal tract. Consequently, inhibiting prostaglandins and thromboxane A2 by inhibiting COX may contribute to the adverse‐event profile of NSAIDs, particularly gastric ulceration and bleeding (McNicol 2018a).

    Overall, NSAIDs' inhibition of prostaglandins and thromboxane A2 production may be effective for pain management, but can result in adverse events involving the gastrointestinal, haematological, cardiovascular, and renal systems. In addition, postoperative patients with potential blood volume depletion depend on prostaglandins to support renal function and, therefore, are at increased risk of developing NSAID‐induced kidney injury (Patrono 2001). Older references suggest that NSAIDs, more than any other medicines, are mentioned in drug toxicity reports (Hawkey 2002). However, more recent overviews propose that the evidence base is uncertain, and that more trials to understand the benefits and harms are needed (Boric 2017). Toxicity risk factors are therapy dosage and duration, pre‐existing renal impairment, and the patient's age.

    Diclofenac is classified as a benzene acetic acid derivative (Fineschi 1997). Diclofenac is plasma‐bound; in particular, to albumin. It goes through considerable first‐pass metabolism (Todd 1988), predominantly through 4'‐hydroxylation by cytochrome P450 (CYP) 2C9. In addition, 5'‐hydroxylation by CYP3A4, CYP2C19, CYP2C8, and CYP2C18 occurs (Kirchheiner 2003). Furthermore, diclofenac is excreted in the bile and urine as sulphate and glucuronide conjugates (Todd 1988). Depending on the child's developmental stage, one may find differing capacities for metabolism, conjugation, and elimination of drugs, including diclofenac (Kearns 2003; Morselli 1980).

    Why it is important to do this review

    This review is important because, to the best of our knowledge, there are no recent systematic reviews on diclofenac for acute postoperative pain in children. Existing systematic reviews have focused on the general perioperative use of NSAIDs and COX inhibitors in children (Krishna 2003; Rømsing 1997), and are now dated. A more recent review by McNicol and colleagues focused on the efficacy and safety of a different NSAID (ketorolac) for postoperative pain management in children (McNicol 2018b). Particularly because diclofenac is used in children for acute pain (Conroy 2001; Turner 1998), a rigorous, up‐to‐date evidence synthesis of its benefits and harms is warranted.

    Although there is a paucity of current evidence on diclofenac use for postoperative pain in children, more is known about its efficacy and safety in adults. A Cochrane review evaluating acute postoperative pain management in adults reported that, compared to placebo, single‐dose intravenous diclofenac may be more than twice as effective in relieving pain over four hours (low‐certainty evidence) and nearly four times more effective in relieving pain over six hours (low‐certainty evidence) (McNicol 2018a). This review also assessed the incidence and severity of adverse events (including prolonged postoperative site bleeding, vomiting, impaired renal function, and bronchospasm), and reported little difference between diclofenac and placebo with low‐ to moderate‐certainty evidence (McNicol 2018a).

    In children, diclofenac may offer a low‐cost, accessible, effective analgesic alternative to opioids and may reduce opioid use. Although opioids are administered to newborn infants following surgery (Kinoshita 2021a; Kinoshita 2021b), NSAIDs are preferred to opioids due to the latter's potential adverse effects in children (including nausea, vomiting, skin itching, constipation, and respiratory depression). However, as with other NSAIDs, there are also concerns about adverse events associated with diclofenac, including prolonged postoperative site bleeding, vomiting, impaired renal function, and bronchospasm. These are factors that healthcare providers may consider when weighing up the balance of benefits and harms of diclofenac use.

    Ultimately, diclofenac's utility for postoperative pain management in children is currently not well reported. This Cochrane review aimed to assess the safety and efficacy of diclofenac specifically in children and address the current knowledge and evidence gaps comparing diclofenac to other options for pain management. This Cochrane review replaces the withdrawn Cochrane review published in 2015 (Standing 2015).

    Objectives

    To assess the efficacy and safety of diclofenac (any dose) for acute postoperative pain management in children compared with placebo, other active comparators, or diclofenac administered by different routes (e.g. oral, rectal, etc.) or strategies (e.g. 'as needed' versus 'as scheduled').

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We prespecified the methods for conducting this review in a published protocol (Ringsten 2022).

    We aimed to include randomised controlled trials (RCTs) with blinding of outcome assessment and blinding of participants and personnel to minimise detection bias and performance bias for comparisons 1 and 2 (see Types of interventions). RCTs are the best design to minimise bias when evaluating the effects of an intervention. When blinding was not clearly described (e.g. study reports simply stated that the trial was blinded), we included the study, and reflected the 'unclear' reporting of blinding in our risk of bias assessments. We included quasi‐randomised studies (i.e. experimental studies in which participants were prospectively allocated using a non‐random method such as alternation, date of birth, or case record number), and assessed these methods in the risk of bias judgements for randomisation.

    For comparisons 3 and 4, where blinding of children, parents, and personnel was difficult to attain (because of different routes or timing of delivery), we included all RCTs regardless of blinding.

    We planned to include individually‐randomised, cluster‐randomised, and cross‐over trials (see Unit of analysis issues). However, we found no cluster‐RCTs or cross‐over trials eligible for inclusion in this version of the review.

    We included peer‐reviewed publications, online clinical trial results, summaries of otherwise unpublished clinical trials, and abstracts. If there were insufficient data, we attempted to locate the full study by contacting the study authors. If data from the full study were unavailable, we listed the study as 'awaiting classification' (see Studies awaiting classification).

    We excluded studies where the child was its own control (e.g. diclofenac treatment to one eye and placebo to the other eye, or split‐mouth study designs). We excluded non‐randomised studies, experimental studies using pain induction, case reports, and clinical observations.

    Types of participants

    We included studies in children under 18 years of age, treated for acute postoperative pain.

    For studies including both children and adults, we contacted the study authors to try to obtain outcome data for children only. When we were unsuccessful in gaining these data, we excluded the study if the mean age of the included participants was above 17 years of age. We included any type of procedure or surgery that resulted in pain in the postoperative period.

    We excluded studies on chronic pain, where diclofenac was primarily used for treating pain persisting beyond seven days, and when pain was not primarily associated with surgery.

    Types of interventions

    The following comparisons were eligible for inclusion.

    • Comparison 1: diclofenac versus placebo.

    • Comparison 2: diclofenac versus an active pharmacological comparator; that is, opioids (e.g. codeine, morphine, papaveretum, tramadol, nalbuphine, pethidine), dexamethasone, betamethasone, fluorometholone, naproxen, ibuprofen, paracetamol, bupivacaine, lignocaine, pranoprofen, ketorolac, flurbiprofen, clonidine, dexmedetomidine, and tenoxicam. We did not pool RCTs that compared diclofenac to different drugs (e.g. diclofenac versus tramadol with diclofenac versus ibuprofen) in the same analysis.

    • Comparison 3: different routes of diclofenac administration (e.g. oral versus rectal).

    • Comparison 4: 'as needed' diclofenac administration (e.g. based on pain scales) versus 'as scheduled' (e.g. a predefined time interval). Route and dose had to be the same in both arms.

    We included any dose, timing, and route of administration: both systemic (e.g. oral, rectal, intramuscular, intravenous) and topical (e.g. gel or drops).

    We included studies where the same co‐interventions were administered to both arms (e.g. diclofenac plus paracetamol versus placebo plus paracetamol). We excluded studies where co‐interventions were administered to one arm only (e.g. diclofenac plus paracetamol versus placebo without paracetamol).

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Pain relief, as reported by the child, and defined as the proportion of children in each treatment arm reporting 50% or better postoperative pain relief, as assessed in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively (dichotomous outcome).

    • Pain intensity, as reported by the child, using a validated pain scale (such as a visual analogue scale) (continuous outcome). We reported mean postoperative pain intensity and corresponding standard deviation (SD) for each treatment arm, as assessed in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively.

    • Adverse events: number of children experiencing any adverse events or harms, as reported by study authors (dichotomous outcome).

    • Serious adverse events, defined as events requiring a blood transfusion, hospitalisation, or resulting in permanent damage (FDA 2016) (dichotomous outcome).

    Secondary outcomes

    • Pain relief, as assessed by a third party (e.g. parents and carers) using a validated pain scale, in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively (continuous outcome).

    • Pain intensity, as assessed by a third party (e.g. parents and carers) using a validated pain scale (such as a visual analogue scale), in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively (continuous outcome).

    • Time to onset of meaningful (50%) pain relief. We extracted the mean time to achieve this degree of relief in each treatment arm and the corresponding SD (continuous outcome).

    • Number of children who required rescue medication. We extracted the proportion of participants in each treatment arm who received additional analgesic medication after having received the study drugs, in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively (dichotomous outcome).

    • Opioid consumption. In studies that allowed co‐administration of opioids, including patient‐controlled analgesia (PCA), we extracted the mean opioid consumption (in milligrams) in each treatment arm and the corresponding SD, in three time frames: within the first two hours postoperatively; from two to 24 hours postoperatively; and from one to seven days postoperatively. If opioid consumption was reported as milligrams per kilogram, we converted this to milligrams by multiplying reported values by the mean weight of participants in each study arm. We converted opioid requirements into intravenous morphine‐equivalents, using commonly‐used and widely‐accepted opioid conversion tables (continuous outcome).

    • Time to rescue medication, as reported by the child. We extracted the mean time to requiring rescue medication in each treatment arm and the corresponding SD (continuous outcome).

    • Time to rescue medication, as reported by a third party (e.g. parents and carers). We extracted the mean time to requiring rescue medication in each treatment arm and the corresponding SD (continuous outcome).

    • Global judgement of satisfaction with treatment (either participant, using the Patients' Global Impression of Change (PGIC), or carer): dichotomous information from categorical global evaluations (number of participants who report the top two categories, e.g. good/satisfied, excellent/very satisfied). For VAS ratings, we extracted the mean values of each intervention.

    • Return to activities of daily living (e.g. return to school and sports activities). We extracted the mean time to the activity in each treatment arm and the corresponding SD (continuous outcome).

    • Nausea, vomiting, or both (dichotomous outcome).

    • Any bleeding (dichotomous outcome), including gastrointestinal bleeding and oral bleeding.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases from inception and without any language restrictions on 11 April 2022.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via the Cochrane Library, Issue 2 of 12, 2022.

    • MEDLINE (via Ovid), including records from 1946 to 2 March 2022.

    • Embase (via Ovid), including records from 1974 to 2 March 2022.

    We tailored the searches to individual databases. The search strategies are available in Appendix 1. Search strategies for CENTRAL and Embase were based on the MEDLINE strategy.

    The Cochrane Pain, Palliative and Supportive Care (PaPaS) Review Group's Information Specialist developed the search strategies, which were independently peer‐reviewed. The Cochrane PaPaS Information Specialist also performed the searches.

    Searching other resources

    We searched ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov) and the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/) for ongoing trials. In addition, we checked the reference lists of reviews included in the abstract screening. We contacted study authors for additional information, when needed, to determine inclusion in the review or to clarify or complement information about methodological choices or outcome data.

    Data collection and analysis

    We collected information regarding the method of randomisation, blinding, intervention, stratification, and whether the trial was single‐centre or multicentre for each included study. We extracted data regarding trial participants (including age), number of participants, modality of administration, and dose of diclofenac. We analysed the clinical outcomes noted above in Types of outcome measures.

    Selection of studies

    We used Cochrane's Screen4Me workflow to help assess the search results. Screen4Me comprises three components: (1) known assessments – a service that matches records in the search results to records that have already been screened in Cochrane Crowd and been labelled as an RCT or as Not an RCT; (2) RCT classifier – a machine learning model that distinguishes RCTs from non‐RCTs; and (3) if appropriate, Cochrane Crowd (crowd.cochrane.org) – Cochrane's citizen science platform where the Crowd help to identify and describe health evidence.

    For this review, 785 records were left after component 1 and 2 in the Screen4Me workflow. Due to this low number of records, we did not use component 3.

    For more information about Screen4Me, see: community.cochrane.org/organizational-info/resources/resources-groups/information-specialists-portal/crs-videos-and-quick-reference-guides#Screen4Me. Detailed information regarding evaluations of the Screen4Me components can be found in the following publications: Marshall 2018; Noel‐Storr 2020; Noel‐Storr 2021; Thomas 2020.

    Two review authors (MR, AH), working in duplicate, independently determined the eligibility of each study identified by the search. We independently excluded studies that clearly did not satisfy our inclusion criteria and obtained full‐text copies of the remaining studies. Two review authors (MR, AH), working in duplicate, independently read these studies in full‐text and selected relevant studies. We resolved disagreements by consensus. We did not anonymise the studies before assessment. We included a PRISMA flowchart in the full review (Moher 2009). We included studies in the review irrespective of whether measured outcome data were reported in a 'usable' way.

    Data extraction and management

    Three review authors (MR, AH, SE), working in duplicate, independently extracted study and outcome data using a piloted template and checked for any discrepancies before entry into Review Manager Web (Review Manager Web 2019). Any disagreements were resolved by a third review author and by discussion within the author team. We combined multiple reports of the same study so that each study, rather than each report, was the unit of interest in the review. We collected the characteristics of the included studies to populate the Characteristics of included studies table in the full review.

    We extracted the following information:

    • study design;

    • setting;

    • participants;

    • intervention(s);

    • comparator(s);

    • outcomes (including our predefined outcomes of interest as well as all that were reported in the included study);

    • numerical data for outcomes of interest;

    • type of analysis/analyses presented.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (AH, SE) independently assessed the risk of bias for each study, using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011), with any disagreements resolved by a third author (MR) and through discussions within the author team.

    We assessed the following biases for each included study.

    • Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias). We assessed the method used to generate the allocation sequence as:

      • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

      • unclear risk of bias (insufficient detail about the method of randomisation to be able to judge the sequence generation as 'low' or 'high' risk of bias);

      • high risk of bias (studies that used a non‐random process (e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number)).

    • Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias). The method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment determines whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment. We assessed the methods as:

      • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered, sealed, opaque envelopes);

      • unclear risk of bias (insufficient detail about the method of allocation concealment to be able to judge it as 'low' or 'high' risk of bias);

      • high risk of bias (studies that did not conceal allocation (e.g. open list)).

    • Blinding of participants and personnel (checking for possible performance bias). We assessed the methods used to blind study participants and personnel from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We assessed methods as:

      • low risk of bias (study stated that it was blinded and described the method used to achieve blinding, such as identical tablets matched in appearance or smell, or a double‐dummy technique);

      • unclear risk of bias (study stated that it was blinded but did not provide an adequate description of how blinding was achieved);

      • we considered studies that did not have blinded participants and personnel to have a high risk of bias. We excluded studies rated at high risk of bias in this domain from the final analysis for comparisons 1 and 2.

    • Blinding of outcome assessment (checking for possible detection bias). We assessed the methods used to blind study participants and outcome assessors from knowing which intervention a participant received. We assessed the methods as:

      • low risk of bias (study had a clear statement that outcome assessors were unaware of treatment allocation, and ideally described how this was achieved);

      • unclear risk of bias (study stated that outcome assessors were blind to treatment allocation but lacked a clear statement on how blinding was achieved);

      • we excluded studies at high risk of bias (where outcome assessment was not blinded).

    • Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias due to the amount, nature, and handling of incomplete outcome data). We assessed the methods used to deal with incomplete data as:

      • low risk (no missing outcome data; reasons for missing outcome data unlikely to be related to true outcome (for survival data, censoring unlikely to be introducing bias); missing outcome data balanced in numbers across intervention groups, with similar reasons for missing data across groups; missing data have been imputed using 'baseline observation carried forward' analysis);

      • unclear risk of bias (insufficient reporting of attrition/exclusions to permit a judgement of 'low risk' or 'high risk' (e.g. number randomised not stated, no reasons for missing data provided, or the study did not address this outcome));

      • high risk of bias (reason for missing outcome data is likely to be related to true outcome, with either imbalance in numbers or reasons for missing data across intervention groups; 'as‐treated' analysis done with substantial departure of the intervention received from that assigned at randomisation; potentially inappropriate application of simple imputation).

    • Selective reporting (checking for reporting bias). We assessed reporting biases due to selective outcome reporting. We judged studies as:

      • low risk of bias (the study protocol was available, and all the study's prespecified (primary and secondary) outcomes that are of interest in the review were reported in a prespecified way);

      • unclear risk of bias (insufficient information available to permit a judgement of 'low risk' or 'high risk');

      • high risk of bias (not all of the study's prespecified primary outcomes were reported; one or more primary outcomes were reported using measurements, analysis methods, or subsets of the data (e.g. subscales) that were not prespecified; one or more reported primary outcomes were not prespecified (unless clear justification for their reporting is provided, such as an unexpected adverse event); one or more outcomes of interest in the review was reported incompletely so that they could not be entered in a meta‐analysis; the study report failed to include results for a key outcome that would be expected in such a study).

    Measures of treatment effect

    We performed statistical analyses using Review Manager Web (Review Manager Web 2019). We summarised reported outcome data in a meta‐analysis when we deemed the data to be sufficiently homogeneous, both clinically and statistically.

    When outcome data were reported in other formats (e.g. with medians deemed not possible to transform) or when some aspects of outcome data were still missing after attempts to contact the study authors (e.g. missing standard deviations, data reported only as statistically significant or not), we reported this narratively.

    Dichotomous data

    We presented the results for dichotomous data using risk ratios (RR) and risk differences (RD) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We calculated the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs if there was a statistically significant reduction (or increase) in RD.

    Continuous data

    We used the mean difference (MD) for continuous data when the included trials measured outcomes in the same way (e.g. time to rescue medication). We used the standardised mean difference (SMD) to combine trials that measured the same outcome but used different methods (e.g. validated scales aimed to assess pain intensity in children). If trials reported continuous data as medians and interquartile ranges (IQRs), we planned to convert median to mean and estimate the SD as interquartile range (IQR)/1.35, if the data passed the skewness test.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the participating child in individually randomised trials, and a child was considered only once in the analysis. We excluded studies where the child was its own control (e.g. diclofenac treatment to one eye and placebo to the other eye). As noted in Types of studies, we did not find any cluster‐randomised or cross‐over studies eligible for inclusion in the review (see Differences between protocol and review for methods we will use to synthesise and analyse cluster‐RCTs and cross‐over studies if they are included in future review updates).

    When multi‐arm trials were identified and included, and where two or more active treatment arms were compared with a placebo or control arm, we avoided double‐counting of participants in the placebo/control arm by splitting the total number between the active arms.

    If studies reported the same outcome at multiple time points within our predefined time intervals, we used data for the most commonly‐used time point only within our prespecified intervals (i.e. within the first two hours postoperatively; two to 24 hours postoperatively; one to seven days postoperatively).

    We planned to acknowledge any possible heterogeneity in the randomisation unit and perform a sensitivity analysis to investigate the possible effects of the randomisation unit.

    Dealing with missing data

    Where feasible, we conducted analyses on an intention‐to‐treat basis for all outcomes. Whenever possible, we analysed all participants in the treatment group to which they were randomised, regardless of the actual treatment received. When we identified important missing data (in the outcomes) or unclear data, we requested the missing data by contacting the original investigators. We were explicit about any assumptions we made whilst employing methods to deal with missing data. We planned to perform sensitivity analyses to assess how sensitive results were to reasonable changes in the undertaken assumptions. We addressed the potential impact of missing data on the review findings in the Discussion.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We estimated the treatment effects and examined the heterogeneity amongst trials by inspecting the forest plots and quantifying the impact of heterogeneity using the I² statistic. We used four thresholds to grade the degree of heterogeneity:

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important;

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity;

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity;

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    When we noted statistical heterogeneity (Deeks 2021), we explored the possible causes (e.g. differences in risk of bias, participants, interventions, or outcome assessments).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to explore publication bias in a funnel plot if we included 10 or more trials reporting the same outcome in a meta‐analysis. However, an insufficient number of trials was pooled for all outcomes in all comparisons to test for funnel plot asymmetry.

    Data synthesis

    For categorical outcomes, we calculated the typical estimates of RR and RD, each with its 95% CI. For continuous outcomes, we calculated the MD or the SMD, each with its 95% CI.

    We used a fixed‐effect model for all initial meta‐analyses. When meta‐analysis was inappropriate, we analysed and interpreted individual trials separately. When there was evidence of clinical heterogeneity, we explored this based on the different study characteristics and subgroup analyses specified in our protocol (Ringsten 2022).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We explored high statistical heterogeneity in the outcomes by visually inspecting the forest plots (Higgins 2020). Where statistical heterogeneity was significant, we planned to interpret the meta‐analysis results accordingly, and to downgrade the certainty of evidence in the summary of findings tables according to the GRADE recommendations.

    We did not conduct any of our prespecified subgroup analyses. The small number of included studies and the underlying very low‐certainty evidence meant that subgroup analyses were unlikely to detect any true differences between subgroups (Deeks 2021; Schandelmaier 2020). If sufficient studies are included in future updates of the review, we will consider performing subgroup analyses based on: dose of diclofenac; route of administration of diclofenac; children's age; and type of surgery. See Differences between protocol and review for further details.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to conduct sensitivity analyses to determine if the findings would be affected by restricting inclusion to studies:

    • that used adequate methods and were deemed to have an overall low risk of bias;

    • in which children's baseline pain levels were considered to be moderate or high;

    • with more than 49 children in each treatment arm.

    However, we did not conduct any of our prespecified sensitivity analyses. Only two of 30 studies had a low risk of bias in all domains, and hence a low risk of bias at study level; we rated the remainder as having an unclear or high risk of bias in at least one domain. It was not possible to establish children's baseline pain levels due to sparse reporting of these data in the included studies, as well as their use of different instruments without cut‐offs for different levels of pain. Furthermore, we did not consider it informative to restrict analyses to the five included studies, out of 32, with more than 49 children in each arm to check robustness. We planned to conduct sensitivity analysis for the review's primary outcomes only.

    We conducted a post hoc sensitivity analysis including studies using non‐validated instruments to measure pain intensity. We also conducted a post hoc sensitivity analysis using a random‐effects model when initial meta‐analyses had an I2 statistic of greater than 50%, to check the robustness of the choice of meta‐analysis model.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    Two review authors independently rated the certainty of the body of evidence for the outcomes. All members of the author team discussed the final judgements and any discrepancies in rating evidence certainty. We used the GRADE system to rank the certainty of the evidence, using GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT), and the guidelines provided in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Schünemann 2020) and GRADEpro handbook (Schünemann 2013).

    The GRADE approach uses five considerations (study limitations (risk of bias), unexplained heterogeneity and inconsistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the body of evidence for each outcome. The GRADE system uses the following criteria for assigning evidence grades.

    • High: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited; the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    The GRADE system considers study design as a marker of quality. RCTs are considered to produce high‐certainty evidence and can be downgraded for important limitations.

    Factors that may decrease the certainty level of a body of evidence are as follows.

    • Serious or very serious study limitations (risk of bias).

    • Important or serious inconsistency of results.

    • Some or major indirectness of evidence.

    • Serious or very serious imprecision.

    • Probability of publication bias.

    We planned to prepare summary of finding tables for two comparisons: diclofenac versus placebo (see summary of findings Table 1); and diclofenac versus active pharmacological comparator. However, we decided to disaggregate the second comparison into three separate comparisons, with corresponding summary of findings tables: diclofenac versus opioids, versus paracetamol, and versus bupivacaine (see summary of findings Table 2, summary of findings Table 3, and summary of findings Table 4, respectively). We did this because these were the most often‐used comparator interventions in the included studies, with a minimum of five studies in each comparison.

    We included key information concerning the certainty of the evidence, the magnitude of the effect of the interventions examined, and the sum of available data on the following outcomes.

    • Pain relief, as reported by the child, and defined as the proportion of children in each treatment arm reporting 50% or better postoperative pain relief, within the first two hours postoperatively (dichotomous outcome).

    • Pain relief, as reported by the child, and defined as the proportion of children in each treatment arm reporting 50% or better postoperative pain relief, within two to 24 hours postoperatively (dichotomous outcome).

    • Pain intensity, as reported by the child, using a validated pain scale (such as a visual analogue scale), within the first two hours postoperatively (continuous outcome). We reported mean postoperative pain intensity in each treatment arm and their corresponding SDs.

    • Pain intensity, as reported by the child, using a validated pain scale (such as a visual analogue scale), within two to 24 hours postoperatively (continuous outcome). We reported mean postoperative pain intensity in each treatment arm and their corresponding SDs.

    • Any bleeding (dichotomous outcome).

    • Nausea, vomiting, or both (dichotomous outcome).

    • Serious adverse events, defined as events requiring a blood transfusion, hospitalisation, or resulting in permanent damage (FDA 2016) (dichotomous outcome).

    Diclofenac was tested in 12 types of surgeries and procedures: nine were tonsillectomies with or without adenoidectomy (Bone 1988; Hardy 2010; Jafari 2016; Kang 1999; Rømsing 2000; Swanepoel 1999; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016; Watters 1988), six were strabismus surgeries (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Attarzadeh 2006; Kim 2018; Morton 1997; Snir 2000; Wennstrom 2002), four were inguinal herniotomies (Moores 1990; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Ryhanen 1994), four appendicectomies (Baer 1992; Morton 1999; Nze 2006; Willey 2005), and two dental surgeries (El Batawi 2015; Littlejohn 1996). Further details are found in Table 1.

    All studies included surgeries and procedures that were completed under general anaesthesia (i.e. an induced state of unconsciousness accompanied by partial or complete loss of protective reflexes), necessitating at least a one‐night stay in a hospital or clinic.

    Interventions

    Diclofenac was delivered preoperatively in 21 trials. Of these, 14 trials delivered diclofenac rectally (Baer 1992; Borkar 2005; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Littlejohn 1996; McGowan 1998; Menezes 2002; Moores 1990; Nishina 2000; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Swanepoel 1999; Ubale 2016; Wennstrom 2002), four delivered diclofenac by injection (Kang 1999; Ryhanen 1994; Thiagarajan 1993; Watters 1988), two ophthalmically (Kim 2018; Morton 1997), and one orally (Tay 2002). Diclofenac was delivered intraoperatively in two trials: one trial delivered it rectally (Bone 1988), and one by injection (Nze 2006). Diclofenac was delivered postoperatively in nine trials: five trials delivered it rectally (El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016; Mireskandari 2011; Morton 1999; Willey 2005), two trials ophthalmically (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Attarzadeh 2006), one trial orally (Rømsing 2000), and another topically (Snir 2000). For further details, see Table 1.

Twenty studies used the same route of administration of comparators as diclofenac, and 10 studies used a different route.     Excluded studies

    As noted above, we excluded 14 studies (14 records) with reasons (see Characteristics of excluded studies). Seven studies had ineligible comparisons (Adarsh 2012; Filatov 2000; Gadiyar 1995; Mak 2001; Rawlinson 2011; Sun 2009; Yallapragada 2016). Four studies did not attempt to blind participants, personnel, or outcome assessors, even though blinding would not have been difficult to attain for comparisons 1 and 2 (i.e. diclofenac versus placebo; diclofenac versus an active pharmacological comparator) (Bhattacharya 2005; Courtney 2001; Majeed 2020; Majeed 2021). In two studies, the children were their own controls (i.e. the studies used a split‐mouth design: Bhargava 2019; Lima 2017), and the final study had an otherwise ineligible design (Hannam 2014).

    Studies awaiting classification

    We listed 23 studies as awaiting classification, either because we could not obtain full‐text publications, or results were not reported in a usable way and study authors' clarification is still needed. Of these, 17 studies were published as abstracts (Amodeo 1992; Aziz 2003; Bhattacharya 2003; Dommerby 1984; Gurkan 1999; Henderson 1994; Hernandez 1997a; Mayer 1980; Mehlisch 1994; Nelson 1994; Niemi 1995; Owolabi 2005; Oztekin 1996; Ozyuvaci 2001; Richardson 1994; Samarkandi 2005; Sher 1993), and we had only the title for the remaining six studies (Azuma 1982; Berry 1992; Bossi 1984; Leontev 2004; Nakayama 1982; Sierra 1991). All studies mentioned the use of diclofenac as an intervention; two trials potentially meet our eligibility criteria (Oztekin 1996; Samarkandi 2005). The remaining 11 studies were either unclear on the methods used (Amodeo 1992; Aziz 2003; Azuma 1982; Berry 1992; Bhattacharya 2003; Bossi 1984; Leontev 2004; Mayer 1980; Nakayama 1982; Ozyuvaci 2001; Sierra 1991), or the participants' ages were not stated (Amodeo 1992; Bossi 1984; Gurkan 1999; Henderson 1994; Hernandez 1997a; Mayer 1980; Mehlisch 1994; Nelson 1994; Niemi 1995; Owolabi 2005; Ozyuvaci 2001; Richardson 1994; Sher 1993).

    Allocation

    Random sequence generation

    We judged 10 studies to have a low risk of bias for randomisation sequence generation (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Mireskandari 2011; Morton 1999; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Ryhanen 1994; Rømsing 2000; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016; Willey 2005).

    We judged 18 studies to have an unclear risk of bias in the randomisation procedure, as they provided insufficient detail about how the randomisation list was generated or ensured (Attarzadeh 2006; Baer 1992; Bone 1988; Borkar 2005; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Kang 1999; Kim 2018; Littlejohn 1996; McGowan 1998; Moores 1990; Morton 1997; Nishina 2000; Nze 2006; Swanepoel 1999; Tay 2002; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002).

    We judged four studies to have a high risk of bias in this domain (El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016; Menezes 2002; Snir 2000). In El Batawi 2015, the children were randomised based on the previous drug of choice used prior to the operation. In Jafari 2016, the children were randomised based on the hospital file number. In Menezes 2002, the children were allocated in pairs to different comparisons based on the timing of enrolment in the study. In Snir 2000, the children were randomised by even or odd numbers from unspecified numbered lists.

    Allocation concealment

    We judged four studies to have a low risk of bias for allocation concealment (Nnaji 2017; Rømsing 2000; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016).

    We judged 25 studies to have an unclear risk of bias in allocation concealment, as they provided insufficient information about how or if the allocation of participants was concealed (Attarzadeh 2006; Baer 1992; Bone 1988; Borkar 2005; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Kang 1999; Kim 2018; Littlejohn 1996; McGowan 1998; Menezes 2002; Mireskandari 2011; Moores 1990; Morton 1997; Morton 1999Nishina 2000; Nze 2006; Riad 2007; Ryhanen 1994; Snir 2000Swanepoel 1999; Tay 2002; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002; Willey 2005).

    We judged three studies to have a high risk of bias in this domain (Akyol‐Salman 2007; El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016). In Akyol‐Salman 2007, randomisation into groups was based on even and odd numbers in the random sequence, which was not concealed. In El Batawi 2015, the participants were allocated based on the previous drug of choice, which was not possible to conceal. In Jafari 2016, the children were allocated based on hospital file number, which was not concealed.

    Blinding

    Blinding of participants and personnel

    We judged blinding of children and personnel to limit performance bias to low risk for 11 studies (Baer 1992; El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016; Littlejohn 1996; Mireskandari 2011; Moores 1990; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Rømsing 2000; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016).

    We judged 17 studies to have an unclear risk of bias for blinding of participants and personnel because they gave insufficient details about blinding (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Attarzadeh 2006; Bone 1988; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Kang 1999; Kim 2018; McGowan 1998; Menezes 2002; Morton 1997; Morton 1999; Nze 2006; Ryhanen 1994; Tay 2002; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002; Willey 2005).

    We judged four studies to have a high risk of bias in this domain. Three studies provided no or few details of attempts to blind personnel, and used different routes of administration of the interventions and comparator(s) (Borkar 2005; Nishina 2000; Swanepoel 1999). One study transparently reported that investigators did not remove bottle labels due to a lack of consent from parents to do this (Snir 2000).

    Blinding of outcome assessment

    We judged that blinding of the outcome assessors was adequate to limit detection bias in 22 studies (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Baer 1992; Bone 1988; Borkar 2005; El Batawi 2015; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Jafari 2016; Littlejohn 1996; Mireskandari 2011; Moores 1990; Morton 1997; Nishina 2000; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Ryhanen 1994; Rømsing 2000; Snir 2000; Swanepoel 1999; Tay 2002; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016).

    We judged 10 studies to have an unclear risk of bias in this domain because they gave insufficient information about blinding of outcome assessors (Attarzadeh 2006; Kang 1999; Kim 2018; McGowan 1998; Menezes 2002; Morton 1999; Nze 2006; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002; Willey 2005).

    Incomplete outcome data

    Due to the short length of follow‐up in studies of acute postoperative pain, we judged the risk of missing outcome data leading to attrition bias to be low for 18 studies (Baer 1992; Bone 1988; El Batawi 2015; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Jafari 2016; Kang 1999; Littlejohn 1996; Mireskandari 2011; Morton 1999; Nishina 2000; Nze 2006; Riad 2007; Snir 2000; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002).

    We judged 11 studies to have an unclear risk of bias, either because they did not clearly report post‐randomisation exclusions in the overall small study samples, or they provided unclear reasons for attrition and participant exclusions (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Attarzadeh 2006; Menezes 2002; Moores 1990; Morton 1997; Nnaji 2017; Ryhanen 1994; Rømsing 2000; Swanepoel 1999; Tay 2002; Willey 2005).

    We judged three studies to have a high risk of bias due to incomplete outcome data (Borkar 2005; Kim 2018; McGowan 1998). Borkar 2005 and McGowan 1998 excluded children in one group due to difficulties and adverse events while administering the interventions, without reporting or accounting for this in the analyses. Kim 2018 excluded children due to poor compliance, incomplete regular follow‐up, and missing data, with potential differences between groups.

    Selective reporting

    We judged 26 studies to have a low risk of bias due to selective reporting (Akyol‐Salman 2007; Bone 1988; El Batawi 2015; Gupta 2008; Hardy 2010; Jafari 2016; Kim 2018; Littlejohn 1996; McGowan 1998; Menezes 2002; Mireskandari 2011; Moores 1990; Morton 1997; Morton 1999; Nishina 2000; Nnaji 2017; Nze 2006; Riad 2007; Ryhanen 1994; Rømsing 2000; Tay 2002; Thiagarajan 1993; Ubale 2016; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002; Willey 2005).

    We judged four studies to have an unclear risk of bias due to selective reporting (Attarzadeh 2006; Kang 1999; Snir 2000; Swanepoel 1999). Attarzadeh 2006 did not report any protocol and made methodological choices that had an important impact on the results. Kang 1999 only reported three out of five specified subscales of a pain intensity instrument, with unclear reasons why, and an unclear impact on the reported results. Snir 2000 did not report pain intensity for the most relevant time point for acute postoperative pain that they had prespecified (i.e. postoperative day 1) for unclear reasons, but reported this outcome for other time periods (weeks 1, 2, and 4). Swanepoel 1999 reported the methods and results in a conference abstract (e.g. very short description of methods and a limited number of outcomes due to the short format); we thus judged this study as having an unclear risk of selective reporting.

    We judged two studies to have a high risk of bias for selective reporting (Baer 1992; Borkar 2005). Baer 1992 transparently reported that bleeding events and overall adverse events were recorded for the interventions, but these were not reported in the publication. Borkar 2005 also transparently reported that requirements for additional analgesia were recorded, but these data were not reported in the publication.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We did not identify any other sources of bias that we judged could introduce a high or unclear risk of bias in any of the included studies.

    Comparison 1. Diclofenac versus placebo

    Three studies evaluated the safety and efficacy of diclofenac versus placebo (Kang 1999; Mireskandari 2011; Nze 2006). Only Kang 1999 and Mireskandari 2011 provided outcome data that could be used in our analysis for the outcomes described below. The Nze 2006 study used a non‐validated pain scale, meaning we could not report on outcomes for this study.

    Primary outcomes
    Pain relief reported by the child

    No studies reported this outcome for any time frame.

    Pain intensity reported by the child

    No studies reported this outcome for any time frame.

    Adverse events

    Two studies reported this outcome (Kang 1999; Mireskandari 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on adverse events compared to placebo (risk ratio (RR) 0.55, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.15 to 1.94; I2 = 2%; 2 studies, 100 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Serious adverse events

    No studies reported this outcome.

    Secondary outcomes
    Pain intensity reported by a third party (0 to 2 hours)

    One study reported this outcome using the Children's Hospital of Eastern Ontario Pain Scale (CHEOPS) (Mireskandari 2011). Diclofenac may reduce pain intensity compared to placebo (mean difference (MD) ‐0.80, 95% CI ‐1.57 to ‐0.03; 1 study, 60 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision.

    Pain intensity reported by a third party (2 to 24 hours)

    One study reported this outcome using CHEOPS (Mireskandari 2011). Diclofenac may reduce pain intensity compared to placebo (MD ‐0.80, 95% CI ‐1.37 to ‐0.23; 1 study, 60 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision.

    Pain intensity reported by a third party (1 to 7 days)

    One study reported this outcome using CHEOPS (Mireskandari 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on pain intensity compared to placebo (MD ‐0.30, 95% CI ‐0.69 to 0.09; 1 study, 60 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Opioid consumption (2 to 24 hours)

    One study reported this outcome (Mireskandari 2011). Diclofenac probably reduces opioid consumption compared to placebo (MD ‐1.40, 95% CI ‐1.70 to ‐1.10; 1 study, 60 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.5). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for risk of bias.

    Opioid consumption (1 to 7 days)

    One study reported this outcome (Mireskandari 2011). Diclofenac probably reduces opioid consumption compared to placebo (MD ‐1.90, 95% CI ‐2.31 to ‐1.49; 1 study, 60 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for risk of bias.

    Nausea, vomiting, or both

    Two studies reported this outcome (Kang 1999; Mireskandari 2011). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on nausea and vomiting compared to placebo (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.38 to 1.80; I2 = 31%; 2 studies, 100 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.7). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Any bleeding

    Two studies reported this outcome (Kang 1999; Mireskandari 2011). There were no bleeding events reported in the Mireskandari 2011 study. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on bleeding compared to placebo (RR 3.00, 95% CI 0.34 to 26.45; I2 = NA; 2 studies, 100 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.8). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Outcomes without data

    None of the studies for this comparison reported the following outcomes: number of children who required rescue medication (for any of the three time frames); opioid consumption (at 0 to 2 hours); time to rescue medication, as reported by a third party; and return to activities of daily living.

    Comparison 2. Diclofenac versus opioids

    Seven studies evaluated the safety and efficacy of diclofenac versus opioids (Bone 1988; Kang 1999; Littlejohn 1996; Menezes 2002; Thiagarajan 1993; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002).

    Primary outcomes
    Pain relief reported by the child

    No studies reported this outcome for any time frame.

    Pain intensity reported by the child (2 to 24 hours)

    One study (50 participants) reported this outcome as median and interquartile range (IQR), using a visual analogue toy pain intensity scale of 0.0 to 10.0 inches (Watters 1988). We did not meta‐analyse these data (as they did not pass a skewness test). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on pain intensity compared to opioids. In the diclofenac group, the median pain score was 4.0 inches (IQR 2.0 to 8.0 inches) and in the opioid group, the median pain score was 7.0 inches (IQR 2.7 to 9.6 inches), with no statistically significant difference as reported by the study authors. We graded the evidence certainty as very low, downgrading one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Adverse events

    Four studies reported this outcome (Bone 1988; Kang 1999; Littlejohn 1996; Menezes 2002). Diclofenac may reduce adverse events compared to opioids (RR 0.15, 95% CI 0.03 to 0.77; I2 = 0%; 4 studies, 180 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.1). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision.

    Serious adverse events

    No studies reported this outcome.

    Secondary outcomes
    Pain intensity reported by a third party (0 to 2 hours)

    Two studies reported this outcome (Littlejohn 1996; Watters 1988). We did not meta‐analyse these data, as the studies used the median and IQR to report pain intensity at zero to two hours postoperatively (the data did not pass a skewness test). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on pain intensity compared to opioids. In Littlejohn 1996, the median pain score was 0.0 (range 0.0 to 6.0) in the diclofenac group and 2.0 (range 0.0 to 7.0) in the opioid group, on an objective pain scale (40 participants). Watters 1988 reported a median pain score of 0.3 inches (IQR 0.0 to 2.5 inches) in the diclofenac group and a median pain score of 0.7 inches (IQR 0.1 to 2.4 inches) in the opioid group, on a visual analogue toy pain intensity scale of 0.0 to 10.0 inches (50 participants). We graded the evidence certainty as very low, downgrading one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Number of participants who required rescue medication (0 to 2 hours)

    One study reported this outcome (Thiagarajan 1993). Diclofenac may increase the number of participants who require rescue medication compared to opioids (RR 1.48, 95% CI 0.82 to 2.67; 1 study, 183 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.2). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Number of participants who required rescue medication (2 to 24 hours)

    Five studies reported this outcome (Bone 1988; Littlejohn 1996; Menezes 2002; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on number of participants who require rescue medication compared to opioids (RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.43 to 1.92; I2 = 71%; 5 studies, 240 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.3). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias, one level for serious inconsistency, and one level for serious imprecision.

    Time to rescue medication, as reported by a third party (e.g. parents and carers)

    Two studies reported this outcome (Bone 1988; Watters 1988). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on participants who require rescue medication compared to opioids. Bone 1988 reported that the mean difference in time to rescue medication (paracetamol), as reported by a third party, when diclofenac was used compared to opioids was 0.40 hours (95% CI ‐0.46 to 1.26; 1 study, 40 participants; Analysis 2.4). The Watters 1988 study (50 participants) reported this outcome as median and IQR; we did not meta‐analyse the data (did not pass a skewness test). In the diclofenac group, the median time to rescue medication (paracetamol) was 1.08 hours (IQR 0.15 to 5.55) and in the opioid group, a median of 1.58 hours (IQR 0.33 to 5.23) was reported. We rated the evidence certainty as very low, downgrading one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Nausea, vomiting, or both

    Seven studies reported this outcome (Bone 1988; Kang 1999; Littlejohn 1996; Menezes 2002; Thiagarajan 1993; Watters 1988; Wennstrom 2002). Diclofenac probably results in less nausea and vomiting compared to opioids (RR 0.75, 95% CI 0.58 to 0.96; I2 = 38%; risk difference (RD) ‐0.11, 95% CI ‐0.18 to ‐0.04; I2 = 75%; 7 studies, 463 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.5 and Analysis 2.6). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious risk of bias.

    Any bleeding

    Two studies reported this outcome (Kang 1999; Thiagarajan 1993). Diclofenac probably increases bleeding events compared to opioids (RR 3.06, 95% CI 1.31 to 7.13; I2 = 0%; RD 0.13, 95% CI 0.04 to 0.21; I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 222 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.7 and Analysis 2.8). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for serious imprecision.

    Outcomes without data

    None of the studies reported the following outcomes: number of participants who required rescue medication (from one to seven days); opioid consumption within any of the three prespecified time frames; and return to activities of daily living.

    Comparison 3. Diclofenac versus paracetamol

    Ten studies evaluated the safety and efficacy of diclofenac versus paracetamol (Baer 1992; El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016; Mireskandari 2011; Morton 1999; Nnaji 2017; Riad 2007; Rømsing 2000; Tay 2002; Ubale 2016).

    Primary outcomes
    Pain relief reported by the child

    No studies reported this outcome for any time frame.

    Pain intensity reported by the child (0 to 2 hours)

    Two studies reported this outcome (Jafari 2016; Ubale 2016). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on pain intensity compared to paracetamol (standardised mean difference (SMD) ‐0.45, 95% CI ‐0.74 to ‐0.15; I2 = 12%; 2 studies, 180 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.1). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for very serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision.

    Pain intensity reported by the child (2 to 24 hours)

    Three studies reported this outcome (El Batawi 2015; Jafari 2016; Ubale 2016). The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of diclofenac on pain intensity compared to paracetamol (SMD ‐0.64, 95% CI ‐0.89 to ‐0.39; I2 = 98%; 3 studies, 300 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.2). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for very serious risk of bias and one level for serious inconsistency.

    Adverse events

    Although four studies reported this outcome (Jafari 2016; Mireskandari 2011; Morton 1999; Nnaji 2017), we only pooled the results of three studies in a meta‐analysis (Jafari 2016; Mireskandari 2011; Nnaji 2017), as the fourth study reported the data as median and range and did not pass a skewness test (Morton 1999).     Imputations

    Diclofenac versus placebo

    For Kang 1999, we extracted pain intensity data from individual pain ratings presented in the report and recalculated these as group means and standard deviation.

    Diclofenac versus paracetamol

    For El Batawi 2015, we used the reported P values to estimate standard deviations for each intervention arm, following guidance in Chapter 6 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2023), assuming equal standard deviations in both groups.

    For Nnaji 2017, we estimated, extracted, and recalculated the mean and standard deviation for 'time to rescue medication' data from the published graphs.

    Diclofenac: oral versus rectal

    For Swanepoel 1999, we imputed the group sizes by assuming that there was an equal distribution of participants to the two groups from the full sample size.

    For Willey 2005, we imputed the group sizes for the outcomes of 'number of participants requiring rescue medication' and 'nausea/vomiting' based on the assumption that the group sizes were the same as reported for other outcomes.

    Sensitivity analysis using a random‐effects model

    We conducted a post hoc sensitivity analysis using a random‐effects model for those analyses with substantial heterogeneity (Analysis 2.6; Analysis 3.2; Analysis 3.6; Analysis 3.13; Analysis 3.15; Analysis 4.6; Analysis 4.8). A random‐effects model only slightly changed the point estimate for these analyses and led to the same GRADE judgements, indicating that, at least for these outcomes, the choice of model did not change our findings.

    Sensitivity analysis including non‐validated pain scales

    We conducted a post hoc sensitivity analysis including studies reporting on pain intensity outcomes using non‐validated scales, to explore the robustness of our results.

    Diclofenac versus placebo

    Nze 2006 reported pain intensity using the 'Maunuksela score' at swallowing, for the outcome 'pain intensity at two to 24 hours as reported by a third party'. The inclusion of Nze 2006 increased precision and slightly reduced the point estimate from SMD ‐0.71 (SD ‐1.23 to ‐0.18) to SMD ‐0.59 (SD ‐0.86 to ‐0.31).

    Diclofenac versus paracetamol

    Nnaji 2017 reported pain intensity on a five‐level observational behavioural scale, from laughing to crying/screaming, for the outcome 'pain intensity as reported by a third party' for the time frames of two to 24 hours and one to seven days postoperatively. The inclusion of Nnaji 2017 for pain intensity at two to 24 hours slightly increased precision and moderately increased the point estimate from SMD ‐0.29 (SD ‐0.80 to 0.21) to SMD ‐0.58 (SD 0.96 to ‐0.21). For pain intensity at one to seven days, the inclusion of Nnaji 2017 slightly improved precision but did not influence the point estimate from SMD ‐0.33 (SD ‐0.84 to 0.18) to SMD ‐0.32 (SD ‐0.69 to 0.04).

    Diclofenac versus bupivacaine

    Gupta 2008 reported pain intensity on a five‐level observational behavioural scale, from laughing to crying/screaming, for the outcome 'pain intensity as reported by a third party' for the time frames of zero to two hours and two to 24 hours postoperatively. The inclusion of Gupta 2008 for pain intensity at zero to two hours slightly increased the precision and slightly reduced the point estimate from SMD 0.39 (SD ‐0.04 to 0.81) to SMD 0.23 (SD: ‐0.12 to 0.58). In addition, some heterogeneity was introduced, as indicated by the I2 statistic increasing from 0% to 25%. For pain intensity at two to 24 hours, Gupta 2008 did not influence the precision but moderately reduced the point estimate from SMD 0.39 (SD ‐0.04 to 0.81) to SMD 0.03 (SD ‐0.32 to 0.38). In addition, a high level of heterogeneity was introduced, as indicated by the I2 statistic increasing from 0% to 78%.

    Diclofenac versus dexamethasone

    Attarzadeh 2006 reported pain intensity on a four‐level scale, from no discomfort to severe discomfort, for the outcome 'pain intensity at one to seven days', but the study was unclear about whether this outcome was reported by the child or a third party. The inclusion of Attarzadeh 2006 slightly increased precision and slightly increased the point estimate from SMD 0.03 (SD ‐0.49 to 0.56) to SMD 0.11 (SD ‐0.27 to 0.49).

    Snir 2000 reported pain intensity on a four‐level scale, from total comfort to severe discomfort, for the outcome 'pain intensity at one to seven days', but without clearly indicating if this outcome was reported by the child or a third party. Pooling Snir 2000 with Attarzadeh 2006 slightly improved precision and slightly reduced the point estimate from SMD 0.11 (SD ‐0.27 to 0.49) to SMD ‐0.02 (SD ‐0.34 to 0.31).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Despite identifying 32 trials, we remain uncertain about the efficacy of diclofenac compared to placebo, to an active comparator, or by different routes of administration for postoperative pain management in children. This is largely due to: authors not reporting on clinically important outcomes; unclear reporting of the trials; poor trial conduct; and small study sizes, all of which reduced our confidence in the results. We also remain uncertain about the safety of diclofenac compared to placebo or other active comparators, except for diclofenac versus opioids, where diclofenac probably results in less nausea and vomiting, but probably results in more bleeding events of any nature.

    None of the studies comparing diclofenac to placebo reported on pain relief, pain intensity as reported by the child (at less than two hours or from two to 24 hours postintervention), or serious adverse events. The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of diclofenac on any bleeding and nausea/vomiting compared to placebo.

    None of the studies comparing diclofenac to opioids reported on pain relief or pain intensity as reported by the child (within the first two hours or from two to 24 hours postoperatively). Diclofenac probably results in less nausea/vomiting but probably increases bleeding events compared to opioids. No studies in this comparison reported serious adverse events.

    None of the studies comparing diclofenac to paracetamol reported on pain relief as reported by the child (within the first two hours or from two to 24 hours postoperatively). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of diclofenac on pain intensity reported by the child (within the first two hours or from two to 24 hours postoperatively), any bleeding, nausea/vomiting, and serious adverse events compared to paracetamol.

    None of the studies comparing diclofenac to bupivacaine reported on pain relief or pain intensity as reported by the child (within the first two hours or from two to 24 hours postoperatively). The evidence is very uncertain about the effects of diclofenac on any bleeding, nausea/vomiting, and serious adverse events compared to bupivacaine.

    We are very uncertain about the benefits and harms of diclofenac versus any other active pharmacological comparator (dexamethasone, pranoprofen, fluorometholone, oxybuprocaine, flurbiprofen, lignocaine), and for different routes and delivery of diclofenac, due to the few, small studies, no reporting of key outcomes, and very low certainty of evidence for the reported outcomes. We are unable to draw any meaningful conclusions from the numerical results.

    We did not identify any ongoing studies.

    We conducted a post hoc sensitivity analysis including studies using non‐validated instruments to measure pain intensity, in addition to studies using validated instruments. These analyses did not have any significant impact on the certainty of evidence or the conclusions we could draw from our results.

    We planned to conduct subgroup analyses to explore heterogeneity based on dose, route of administration, age of the children, and type of surgery. Due to few reported outcomes in each subgroup analysis, we have very low confidence in any potential subgroup effects as explanations for any heterogeneity.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Pharmacological comparisons and outcomes

    We did not identify any studies comparing diclofenac to betamethasone, naproxen, ibuprofen, ketorolac, dexmedetomidine, or tenoxicam that fit our inclusion criteria. This indicates a knowledge gap about the benefits and harms of these agents, as investigated in randomised controlled trials. In addition, many of our primary and secondary outcomes were not reported in the different comparisons, indicating specific knowledge gaps within outcome domains (see Effects of interventions).

    Route of administration

    Of the 32 included studies, 20 used a rectal route of administration, five used injection as an administration route, four used an ophthalmic route, two studies used an oral route, and one study used a topical route (see the overview of study characteristics in Table 1). As only two studies explored specific comparisons between different routes of diclofenac administration (Swanepoel 1999; Willey 2005: oral versus rectal delivery), there is uncertainty if any route has more benefits and fewer harms.

    Surgeries and pain intensity

    All included studies administered diclofenac for acute postoperative pain. The types of surgery were diverse, ranging from ophthalmology, dentistry, ear, nose and throat (ENT), cleft palate repair, general surgery (appendicectomy, laparoscopy, inguinal hernia repair, etc.), circumcision, and lower limb orthopaedic surgery. All surgery was performed under general anaesthesia. However, the surgeries varied in terms of the anatomical area, duration, complexity, and ultimately, the extent of tissue injury (see Table 1). There were too few studies per subgroup of type of surgery to permit analyses and meaningful interpretation of results.

    Ages of children

    Of 32 included studies, 26 studies included participants with a mean age above four years in all groups, while only six studies included children with a mean age below four years (Baer 1992; Gupta 2008; Mireskandari 2011; Nnaji 2017; Nze 2006; Ryhanen 1994; see Table 1). Thus, there is limited evidence for younger children regarding the benefits and harms of diclofenac.

    Pain intensity measurements

    Different studies used different instruments to assess pain intensity. Many of the pain measurement instruments used in the included studies had a limited range of options (e.g. having two or three levels of pain) or were of questionable validity (e.g. measuring 'stress' behaviours such as movement and crying). As defined in our protocol, we aimed to include only validated pain measurement instruments in children. The validity of available pain intensity measures is discussed by Birnie and colleagues (Birnie 2019), highlighting that only a few of the instruments currently used to assess pain intensity in children can be recommended. Our decision to restrict inclusion to studies using validated pain instruments led to only a few studies being included in our outcomes of pain intensity at different time points. To explore our choice of pain intensity measurements, we conducted a sensitivity analysis including pain intensity data from studies using non‐validated instruments (see Effects of interventions). Including these measures made little or no impact on our results and had no impact on our conclusions.

    Pain relief measurements

    The dichotomous outcome "pain relief defined as the proportion of participants with 50% or greater postoperative pain relief" was not reported or possible to calculate in any of the studies included in our review, indicating that authors of studies in postoperative pain in children have not used this definition to report pain reduction to date. We selected this outcome based on previous guidance on important outcomes based on Cochrane Pain, Palliative and Supportive Care (PaPaS) Group's guiding resource, Optimal methods for the use of ‘pain’ as an outcome in systematic reviews of postoperative pain management. In contrast, a previous Cochrane review assessing diclofenac in adults found four studies reporting data for this outcome that could be used in a meta‐analysis (McNicol 2018a), indicating differences in reporting between study cohorts. This could be due to the inherent challenges in assessing pain in children compared to adults, as described in Description of the condition.

    Adverse event measurements

    The collection and reporting of adverse events and serious adverse events data in many of the included studies was probably not done in a consistent and structured way. Ideally, predefined adverse events categories would transparently report cut‐offs for when an event is considered adverse, and descriptions of a structured collection of adverse events in trials would be included in the study publications or protocols – but these seldom were. In addition, many of the studies did not include enough participants to detect any differences in serious adverse events that occurred less frequently, and very few transparently reported if any serious adverse events happened (e.g. our definition of 'serious adverse events' in Types of outcome measures was only transparently reported in two studies (McGowan 1998; Nnaji 2017)). We did not include non‐randomised studies in this review. Including non‐randomised studies of diclofenac could increase the amount of reported outcome data for adverse and serious adverse events and possibly lead to higher certainty of evidence for these outcomes.

    Interaction of pain intensity and use of rescue medication

    The reported outcome data for pain intensity in our included studies could have been influenced by any rescue medication given. That is, we can assume that individuals or groups given rescue medication would have a greater reduction in pain intensity than those not given rescue medication. Some studies excluded participants from the analysis once rescue medication was given (e.g. Ubale 2016), while most studies either kept the children in the analysis or did not indicate that they excluded those children in their description of the methods used. These differences could introduce heterogeneity and influence the analysis of pain intensity outcomes.

    One approach for future trials, apart from excluding children from the analysis when rescue medication has been given, could be to consider pain intensity ratings and rescue medication used together. For example, a higher pain intensity score but also a higher rescue medication use in the diclofenac group compared to the control group could indicate that rescue medication is working the way it is intended (e.g. reducing pain intensity), and that pain intensity should not be considered to be one of the main outcomes to evaluate effectiveness.

    Diclofenac compared to no treatment

    We did not include a comparison of diclofenac to 'no treatment' in the review. This decision limits evidence for decision‐makers to either strict placebo comparisons or diclofenac versus other pharmacological agent comparisons. We decided not to include 'no treatment' as a comparison to diclofenac in order to limit bias due to placebo effects or other unspecific effects which could occur when no alternative treatment is given.

    Certainty of the evidence

    According to the GRADE approach, the overall certainty of the evidence for critical outcomes for diclofenac administration ranged from moderate, in the comparison of diclofenac to opioids, to very low in the other comparisons (see summary of findings Table 1; summary of findings Table 2; summary of findings Table 3; summary of findings Table 4).

    The certainty of the evidence was moderate for two outcomes in the diclofenac versus opioids comparison: we downgraded the nausea/vomiting outcome due to several studies having an unclear risk of bias, whereas we downgraded the evidence for any bleeding due to the imprecision of the estimates (i.e. two studies with few events) (summary of findings Table 2).

    The same outcomes were also reported in the diclofenac versus placebo comparison, but the certainty of the evidence was very low, due to serious concerns for risk of bias and very serious imprecision (summary of findings Table 1).

    In the diclofenac versus paracetamol comparison, we downgraded pain intensity as reported by the child within the first two hours postoperatively by two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision, and we downgraded pain intensity as reported by the child at two to 24 hours postoperatively by two levels for risk of bias and one level for inconsistency (I2 = 98%). We downgraded the nausea/vomiting and any bleeding outcomes to very low certainty due to serious concerns for risk of bias and very serious imprecision. We downgraded the serious adverse events outcome by three levels for extremely serious imprecision, due to few events and wide confidence intervals including both severe harms and large benefits (summary of findings Table 3).

    The certainty of the evidence was very low for all three outcomes reported in the diclofenac compared to bupivacaine comparison; namely, nausea/vomiting, any bleeding, and serious adverse events, due to serious concerns for risk of bias and very serious imprecision (summary of findings Table 4).

    We did not explore publication bias using funnel plots because fewer than 10 studies reported the same outcomes within the same comparison.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We were unable to obtain full‐text publications for 23 studies (see Characteristics of studies awaiting classification), despite extensive searches by an experienced information specialist and attempts to contact study authors by email. Sixteen of these 23 studies were published in the 1980s and 1990s. Based on information in the available abstracts, most had relatively small study samples.

    As for the studies included in the review, most were relatively dated and small. Only five of 32 were published in 2015 or later. We received few responses to our requests to study authors for additional data or information to clarify study methods. Having access to complete study data from the included studies – including outcome data in appropriate formats and information that would shed light on study methods – would mitigate our concerns about potential bias.

    As planned, we used standardised mean differences to synthesise the pain intensity outcomes where studies used different scales. This approach reduces variability and provides more precise effect estimates when outcome data are combined in a meta‐analysis. The potential downside is that it can lead to heterogeneity, due to different instruments and scales not measuring the same concept (in our instance, pain intensity). We tried to limit the potential heterogeneity by only including validated instruments in the meta‐analysis (see Overall completeness and applicability of evidence), and by performing a sensitivity analysis that included other pain intensity instruments to check the robustness of the results to this decision (see Effects of interventions). This did not result in any substantial differences, and we rated all pain intensity outcomes as having very low‐certainty evidence. We also planned to separate pain intensity outcomes based on who judged the pain (i.e. the child or a third party), due to pain being a subjective sensation that most reliably can be judged by the child experiencing pain and not by hospital staff or parents. Even though we took the measures described above to limit and explore the impact of our choice, it is possible that some heterogeneity was introduced due to differences between instruments and pain scales, which could influence the effect estimate and the variability of the pain intensity we have reported.

    Overall, we imputed data for five of the 32 studies. These steps are described in detail in the Effects of interventions section. Although we made assumptions to impute these data (i.e. standard deviations from P values; assuming group sizes based on full study sizes; or assuming the same group sizes between different outcomes), we judged that the imputations would have little to no impact on our results and conclusions. In addition, we decided not to transform outcome data presented as medians to means using commonly used transformation methods, due to the clearly skewed data used to calculate medians by the primary study authors. Instead, we reported these outcomes narratively.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    As noted in Why it is important to do this review, as far as we know, the only previous systematic review exploring diclofenac for acute pain in children – a Cochrane review by Standing and colleagues – was withdrawn as it was out of date (Standing 2015). Notably, that review did not focus specifically or exclusively on postoperative pain, as this review does. The latest searches for the Standing 2015 review were performed in 2008, whereas ours were performed in April 2022, allowing for the inclusion of more recent studies.

    Standing and colleagues did not conduct an analysis of pain intensity outcomes, as the included studies measured pain intensity using different instruments. We used standardised mean difference to combine different pain intensity instruments. We also distinguished between self‐reported and third‐party‐reported pain. We included outcomes assessed with pain intensity instruments validated for younger children, for whom communicating about or rating of pain is difficult (Suraseranivongse 2001; Suraseranivongse 2005), as well as instruments for children where verbal communication and rating of pain is possible (Birnie 2019).

    The need for rescue medication constituted the only outcome for evaluating the efficacy of diclofenac in the Standing 2015 review, whereas we included several measures of efficacy (see Primary outcomes and Secondary outcomes). For adverse events and harms, Standing 2015 included randomised controlled trials as well as observational studies, meaning they included an additional 56 reports of non‐randomised studies monitoring adverse events. Our review included only adverse events and harms reported in RCTs.

    Our approach to comparisons also differed from that adopted by Standing and colleagues. Whereas we compared diclofenac to placebo only, Standing 2015 included 'no treatment' in the same comparison as placebo. Pooling placebo‐controlled trials and trials using 'no treatment' as the control group within the same comparison could, particularly for a subjective outcome such as pain, have a large impact on the overall results and conclusions and create a more heterogenous result. In addition, we compared diclofenac to all possible pharmacological comparisons, whereas Standing 2015 only reported on the diclofenac versus paracetamol comparison.

    As mentioned in our Background, McNicol and colleagues evaluated the benefits and harms of a single dose of intravenous diclofenac in adults in a Cochrane review (McNicol 2018a). Thus, this review differs from ours in that it was limited to the intravenous route of delivery (whereas all routes of delivery were eligible for inclusion in this review), and, of course, it looked at a different population. In McNicol 2018a, the certainty of the evidence was low or very low overall for outcomes related to benefits and harms, similar to our review. Notably, McNicol 2018a was able to include three studies in the placebo comparison for the outcome of 'number of participants having 50% pain relief', as these studies used validated and self‐reported pain scales in the adult population. This contrasts with our review of studies in children, none of which assessed this outcome.
