Surgical versus conservative interventions for treating acromioclavicular dislocation of the shoulder in adults

    • Background

    Dislocation of the acromioclavicular joint is one of the most common shoulder injuries in a sport‐active population. The question of whether surgery should be used remains controversial. This is an update of a Cochrane Review first published in 2010.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects (benefits and harms) of surgical versus conservative (non‐surgical) interventions for treating acromioclavicular dislocations in adults.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Bone, Joint and Muscle Trauma Group Specialised Register (to June 2019), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (the Cochrane Library 2019, Issue 6), MEDLINE (1946 to June 2019), Embase (1980 to June 2019), and LILACS (1982 to June 2019), trial registries, and reference lists of articles. There were no restrictions based on language or publication status.

    Selection criteria

    We included all randomised and quasi‐randomised trials that compared surgical with conservative treatment of acromioclavicular dislocation in adults.

    Data collection and analysis

    At least two review authors independently performed study screening and selection, 'Risk of bias' assessment, and data extraction. We pooled data where appropriate and used GRADE to assess the quality of evidence for each outcome.

    Main results

    We included five randomised trials and one quasi‐randomised trial. The included trials involved 357 mainly young adults, the majority of whom were male, with acute acromioclavicular dislocation. The strength of the findings in all studies was limited due to design features, invariably lack of blinding, that carry a high risk of bias. Fixation of the acromioclavicular joint using hook plates, tunnelled suspension devices, coracoclavicular screws, acromioclavicular pins, or (usually threaded) wires was compared with supporting the arm in a sling or similar device. After surgery, the arm was also supported in a sling or similar device in all trials. Where described in the trials, both groups had exercise‐based rehabilitation. We downgraded the evidence for all outcomes at least two levels, invariably for serious risk of bias and serious imprecision.

    Low‐quality evidence from two studies showed no evidence of a difference between groups in shoulder function at one year, assessed using the Disability of the Arm, Shoulder, and Hand questionnaire (DASH) (0 (best function) to 100 (worst function)): mean difference (MD) 0.73 points, 95% confidence interval (CI) −2.70 to 4.16; 112 participants. These results were consistent with other measures of function at one‐year or longer follow‐up, including non‐validated outcome scores reported by three studies. There is low‐quality evidence that function at six weeks may be better after conservative treatment, indicating an earlier recovery. Very low‐quality evidence from one trial found no difference between groups in participants reporting pain at one year: risk ratio (RR) 1.32, 95% CI 0.54 to 3.19; 79 participants. There is very low‐quality evidence that surgery may not reduce the risk of treatment failure, usually resulting in non‐routine secondary surgery: 14/168 versus 15/174; RR 0.99, 95% CI 0.51 to 1.94; 342 participants, 6 studies. The main source of treatment failure was complications related to surgical implants in the surgery group and persistent symptoms, mainly discomfort, due to the acromioclavicular dislocation in the conservatively treated group.

    There is low‐quality evidence from two studies that there may be little or no difference between groups in the return to former activities (sports or work) at one year: 57/67 versus 62/70; RR 0.96, 95% CI 0.85 to 1.10; 137 participants, 2 studies. Low‐quality but consistent evidence from four studies indicated an earlier recovery in conservatively treated participants compared with those treated with surgery. There is low‐quality evidence of no clinically important difference between groups at one year in quality of life scores, measured using the 36‐item or 12‐item Short Form Health Survey (SF‐36 or SF‐12) (0‐to‐100 scale, where 100 is best score), in either the physical component (MD −0.63, 95% CI −2.63 to 1.37; 122 participants, 2 studies) or mental component (MD 0.47 points, 95% CI −1.51 to 2.44; 122 participants). There is very low‐quality and clinically heterogenous evidence of a greater risk of an adverse event after surgery: 45/168 versus 16/174; RR 2.82, 95% CI 1.65 to 4.82; 342 participants, 6 studies; I2 = 48%. Common adverse outcomes were hardware complications or discomfort (18.5%) and infection (8.7%) in the surgery group and persistent symptoms (7.1%), mainly discomfort, in the conservatively treated group. The majority of surgical complications occurred in older studies testing now‐outdated devices known for their high risk of complications. The very low‐quality evidence from one study (70 participants) means that we are uncertain whether there is a between‐group difference in patient dissatisfaction with cosmetic results.

    It is notable that the evidence for function, return to former activities, and quality of life came from the two most recently conducted studies, which tested currently used devices and interventions in clearly defined participant populations that represented the commonly perceived population for which there is uncertainty over the use of surgery. There were insufficient data to conduct subgroup analysis relating to type of injury and whether surgery involved ligament reconstruction or not.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is low‐quality evidence that surgical treatment has no additional benefits in terms of function, return to former activities, and quality of life at one year compared with conservative treatment. There is, however, low‐quality evidence that people treated conservatively had improved function at six weeks compared with surgical management. There is very low‐quality evidence of little difference between the two treatments in pain at one year, treatment failure usually resulting in secondary surgery, or patient satisfaction with cosmetic result. Although surgery may result in more people sustaining adverse events, this varied between the trials, being more common in techniques such as K‐wire fixation that are rarely used today. There remains a need to consider the balance of risks between the individual outcomes: for example, surgical adverse events, including wound infection or dehiscence and hardware complication, against risk of adverse events that may be more commonly associated with conservative treatment such as persistent symptoms or discomfort, or both.

    There is a need for sufficiently powered, good‐quality, well‐reported randomised trials of currently used surgical interventions versus conservative treatment for well‐defined injuries.

    • Surgical versus conservative for treatment for acromioclavicular dislocations of the shoulder in adults

    This is a summary of research on the effects of surgery compared with non‐surgical (conservative) treatment methods to treat a dislocated acromioclavicular joint.

    Background

    The acromioclavicular joint is located at the top of the shoulder. It is the joint between the collarbone and the acromion (a projection from the shoulder blade that is located at the point of the shoulder). Acromioclavicular joint dislocation is one of the most common shoulder injuries treated in a sport‐active population. It is a common shoulder injury in cyclists and contact sports players such as footballers, boxers, and martial arts practitioners. The dislocation typically occurs in young men who fall and suffer a direct impact to the top of the shoulder. Most acromioclavicular dislocations are treated without surgery, especially those with less severe separation between the bones. Non‐surgical or conservative treatment involves immobilisation of the arm. However, the outcome can be less satisfactory when a more serious dislocation occurs. Surgical treatments involve repositioning the joint parts and repairing or reconstructing the ruptured ligaments.

    Results of the search

    We searched medical databases up to June 2019 and included six trials involving 357 participants who were mainly young male adults. All studies compared surgical fixation (using either screws, plates, pins, or other devices to stabilise the dislocated joint) with a conservative method (immobilisation with one of three types of slings or a specially designed splint). In all trials the arm was also supported in a sling or similar device after surgery. Where described, both groups had exercise‐based rehabilitation.

    Key results

    We found that surgery compared with conservative treatment may not improve shoulder function, return to former activities (sport and work), or quality of life at one year. We found evidence that at six weeks, these outcomes may be better after conservative treatment, indicating an earlier recovery. It is unclear whether there is a difference between surgery and conservative treatment in pain at one year, treatment failure usually resulting in secondary surgery, or patient unhappiness with their shoulder appearance. The review found more complications in the surgery group, which were mainly related to the surgical hardware or infection from the surgery. In contrast, complications in the conservatively treated group were mainly discomfort. The risk of such complications varied amongst the six studies and is likely to depend on the type of surgery used. Surgical complications were more common in the older trials, which used types of surgery rarely used today.

    Quality of the evidence

    All six studies had weaknesses that could affect the reliability of their results. We considered the evidence for all outcomes to be either of low or very low quality.

    Conclusions

    Low‐quality evidence indicates that surgery may not provide benefits at one year over conservative treatment for acromioclavicular dislocations in adults. Further good‐quality studies may help resolve this uncertainty and change these conclusions.

    Implications for practice

    There is at best low‐quality evidence from six small trials on the relative effectiveness of surgical versus conservative treatment for acute acromioclavicular dislocation. Based on low‐quality evidence, surgical intervention may have no additional benefits in terms of function, return to former activities, and quality of life at one year. The evidence for these three outcomes came from the two most recently conducted studies, which tested currently used devices and interventions. However, there is low‐quality evidence that people treated conservatively had improved function at six weeks compared with those who were treated surgically. There is very low‐quality evidence of little difference between the two treatments in pain at one year, treatment failure usually resulting in secondary surgery, or patient satisfaction with the cosmetic result. There is very low‐quality evidence based on clinically heterogeneous data that surgery may result in more adverse events. Most complications were in the surgical group, and the most common adverse events were complications related to surgical implants. The majority of surgical complications were in studies testing now‐outdated devices known for their high risk of complications. There were insufficient data to conduct subgroup analysis relating to type of injury and whether surgery involved ligament reconstruction or not. Consequently, whilst these results favour a non‐operative approach, possible benefits of surgery cannot be ruled out including for more complex, high‐grade injuries.

    Implications for research

    Further research exploring the comparison between surgical and conservative interventions for the treatment of acromioclavicular dislocation is justified because acromioclavicular dislocation is a common shoulder injury in an active population. Although awareness of the three ongoing single‐centre trials is important, we suggest that it is not necessary to wait for the completion of these trials with regard to the following suggestions for future research.

    • Consensus on indications for surgical and conservative treatment of acromioclavicular dislocation should be developed to determine the priorities for inclusion criteria for future comparative studies. A high‐quality, multicentre randomised controlled trial could then be developed to compare current surgical methods versus conservative management for the treatment of acute grade III acromioclavicular dislocation.

    • Consensus is needed on what comprises good standard conservative treatment, such as type of sling and duration of immobilisation, and rehabilitation, including for testing in the future trials.

    • Future trials should be developed using standard and validated outcome measures, including pain, and assess resource implications and costs. As proposed by an external referee, consideration should be given to anxiety and depression scores. We also recommend that future randomised controlled trials should adhere to reporting standards following the CONSORT statements (Schulz 2010).

    • Future trials should also determine the differences between early and delayed surgical stabilisation of grade III acromioclavicular dislocations.

    • Comparisons of surgical techniques that do not require a routinely subsequent surgical removal of implant, such as anatomic reconstructions and arthroscopic reconstruction, amongst others, should be tested in randomised controlled clinical trials.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    The acromioclavicular joint is located at the top of the shoulder. It is the joint between the acromion (a process at the top of the scapula or shoulder blade) and the lateral (outer) end of the clavicle (collarbone). The joint is stabilised by a combination of dynamic musculature (the deltoid and trapezius muscles) and static ligamentous (tough fibrous) structures: the latter comprise the joint’s fibrous capsule, the acromioclavicular ligaments (thickenings in the fibrous capsule), and the coracoclavicular ligaments, which connect the coracoid process (another projection from the scapula) to the clavicle (Post 1985).

    Acromioclavicular joint dislocation is one of the most common shoulder injuries treated in a sport‐active population. Based on data collected in Italy, Chillemi 2013 estimated the incidence of this injury is 1.8 per 10,000 people per year in urban areas. It is the most prevalent shoulder injury in participants in contact sports (any sport in which the impact of one person against another is an inherent part of the sport, such as boxing, football, ice hockey, and martial arts) (Bishop 2006). Data reveal that acromioclavicular dislocations occur in 41% of collegiate football players and in 40% of (US) National Football League quarterbacks (Kaplan 2005; Kelly 2004). These dislocations are more common in men than in women: Jacobs 1966 reported a 5 to 1 ratio, and Chillemi 2013 an 8.5 to 1 ratio. Whilst this partly reflects that men are more likely to practise contact sports than women, Chillemi 2013 found that cycling was the most common sports activity resulting in this injury.

    The most common mechanism of injury is a direct blow to the top/point of the shoulder, usually from a fall. Less commonly, an indirect trauma may be transmitted up the arm because of a fall onto an outstretched hand.

    The clinical features of acute acromioclavicular dislocations include pain at the acromioclavicular joint. This does not usually radiate, and its severity is often related to the degree of injury. The arm is voluntarily held by the side of the body, and some movements are restricted due to pain. Physical examination may show swelling and bruising, an abrasion or a typical deformity, in which the clavicle becomes prominent and there is a depression below it (Bowers 1935).

    The injury is more often an incomplete separation rather than a complete separation, in the ratio of 2 to 1 (Galatz 2005). Acromioclavicular separations have been described as subluxations when they are partial, and dislocations when complete (Imatani 1975a). Allman 1967 classified sprains of acromioclavicular joint into grades I, II, and III, as follows.

    • Grade I: results from a mild force that causes tearing of only a few fibres of the acromioclavicular ligament and capsule. There is no instability of the joint.

    • Grade II: is caused by moderate force with rupture of the capsule and acromioclavicular ligament. This injury often causes a subluxation (partial or incomplete dislocation). The coracoclavicular ligaments are not ruptured.

    • Grade III: results from a severe force that ruptures both the acromioclavicular and coracoclavicular ligaments, producing dislocation of the acromioclavicular joint.

    This classification was widely accepted (Taft 1987), but has largely been superceded by the Rockwood classification, which identified six types of injury (see Table 1). The first three types are the same as Allman's grades (Galatz 2005). The other three types (IV, V, and VI) are all subtypes of Allman's grade III injuries, varying only in the degree and direction of the clavicle displacement (see Table 1).

    Description of the intervention

    There is a general consensus in the current literature regarding the treatment of acromioclavicular dislocation. Typically, conservative treatment is recommended for low‐grade injuries (Rockwood grades I and II), and surgical ligament reconstruction to high‐grade injuries, usually grades IV to VI. However, there remains disagreement regarding the management of grade III injuries. Treatment decisions for these injuries are frequently based on the patient’s occupation and sport activity, after thorough attention of the putative relative benefits and harms of each intervention (Borbas 2019; Moatshe 2018; Phadke 2019).

    Non‐surgical, or conservative treatment generally involves immobilisation of the arm. Immobilisation can be with a sling: a broad‐arm sling, which is commonly used in current practice, or braces and strapping, which were more typically used in the past. After a short period of immobilisation lasting around two weeks, gradual mobilisation is started; however, the variation in the immobilisation time frame and the lack of consensus on this is topical at present (Chaudhury 2018; Phadke 2019; Stucken 2015).

    Surgical procedures include repositioning of the joint parts and the repair or reconstruction using ligament tissue from other parts of the body (e.g. coracoacromial ligament and semitendinosus, anterior tibialis tendons, or a combination of these) of the various ruptured ligaments. Devices such as the coracoclavicular screw, hook plate, fixation pins, and wires may be used. Newer surgical interventions now exist that are currently being adopted, such as new anatomic techniques using allograft and biological grafts, and arthroscopic‐assisted coracoclavicular ligament reconstruction (Fauci 2013; Le Hanneur 2018; Shin 2017; Yin 2018). In addition, some techniques have been tested in randomised clinical trials (Fauci 2013; Yin 2018). Implants such as coracoclavicular screw, hook plate, and Kirschner wires require routine removal by further surgery. The postoperative rehabilitation protocol is also controversial in the current practice. Most surgeons prescribe that the arm should be immobilised in a sling to allow healing to take place followed by gradual mobilisation; however, other surgeons suggest that the arm should be immobilised in a shoulder abduction orthosis at 30° for four weeks. Active range of motion on all ranges is typically allowed after four or six weeks. Resisted exercises may be started after six weeks, and after six months, patients can return to sports without restriction (Cote 2010; Müller 2018; Porschke 2019).

    How the intervention might work

    Grade I and II injuries, or acromioclavicular joint subluxations, are almost invariably treated conservatively. This review focused on more severe injuries, where the joint is dislocated.

    Surgical management for acromioclavicular dislocation has been advocated as it enables the restoration of the joint anatomy, thus avoiding the obvious deformity and a potentially unsatisfactory outcome (Rawes 1996). However, potential disadvantages of surgery include wound infection, migration of pins used for fixation, erosion of the bone by fixation devices, failure of metallic fixation devices, recurrence of deformity, a painful or unsightly scar, late development of acromioclavicular arthralgia, and the necessity of a second operation to remove fixation devices (Galatz 2005; Weaver 1972).

    The advantages of conservative treatment are a shorter period of rehabilitation and avoidance of hospitalisation (Bannister 1989a; Larsen 1986; Rawes 1996). Reported disadvantages are that about 20% of patients treated conservatively have unsatisfactory results due to pain, instability, and limitation of motion (Kennedy 1954). Moreover, there is an uncertainty regarding healing and the potential need for reconstructive surgery, which is more difficult to perform if the displacement has persisted for a long period (Larsen 1986; Roper 1982).

    Why it is important to do this review

    This is an update of a Cochrane Review first published in 2010 (Tamaoki 2010). Based on evidence from three trials at high risk of bias, Tamaoki 2010 concluded that "there is insufficient evidence from randomised controlled trials to determine when surgical treatment is indicated for acromioclavicular dislocation in adults in current practice".

    Over 35 conservative methods (e.g. sling, Velpeau bandage, strapping) and around 150 surgical methods of correction have been identified and advocated for acromioclavicular dislocation (Beitzel 2013). The question of whether surgery should be used remains controversial (Beitzel 2013; Phillips 1998; Spencer 2007). In addition, the development and use of new surgical techniques in current practice should be evaluated.

    We identified five recent systematic reviews comparing surgical versus conservative interventions focusing on these injuries (Chang 2018; Korsten 2014; Longo 2017; Smith 2011; Tang 2018). All of these reviews, which included evidence from studies other than from randomised controlled trials, provisionally concluded against the use of surgery for most dislocations but also emphasised the poor evidence base. Our review tested this basic treatment question by applying more rigorous methodology, including restricting the included studies to randomised or quasi‐randomised trials and performing a broader literature search that included non‐English literature, and through enabling future updates in the light of new evidence.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects (benefits and harms) of surgical versus conservative (non‐surgical) interventions for treating acromioclavicular dislocations in adults.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered any randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (studies in which the allocation is not strictly random, e.g. by date of birth, hospital record number, alternation) comparing surgical with conservative interventions for treating acromioclavicular dislocation.

    Types of participants

    We included trials with participants who met the following criteria.

    • Skeletally mature (adults greater than 18 years of age)  

    • History of acute (less than three weeks) acromioclavicular dislocation, irrespective of method of diagnosis (usually clinical and imaging evaluations)

    • No other symptomatic shoulder disorders (e.g. osteoarthritis, shoulder instability, fracture)

    Types of interventions

    We considered all surgical and conservative interventions used for treating acromioclavicular dislocation.

    • Surgical interventions included the coracoclavicular screw, reconstruction of coracoclavicular ligaments, acromioclavicular wires and hook plates.

    • Conservative interventions included immobilisation of the arm in a sling, use of braces, and adhesive strapping.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Shoulder function (preferably patient reported): an example of a validated patient‐reported measure of upper limb function is the Disability of the Arm, Shoulder, and Hand questionnaire (DASH) (Hudak 1996). A commonly used instrument for assessing shoulder function is the Constant score (Constant 1987), which is a composite score for shoulder function that includes subjectively rated pain and activities of daily living, as well as objectively rated range of movement and strength.

    • Pain: measured by validated pain scales (visual analogue scale (VAS) or numerical rating scale (NRS)) (Revill 1976).

    • Treatment failure: measured by the number of symptomatic participants who have undergone or are being considered for a non‐routine secondary surgical intervention for

      1. mechanical failure, defined as a condition in which an implant loses its capacity to carry a load (e.g. fixation failure, migration of wires, broken screw or surgical implant);

      2. loss of reduction;

      3. distal clavicle fractures.

    Timing of primary outcomes measurement

    We extracted outcome data at the following time periods: short‐term follow‐up (up to six weeks following treatment); intermediate follow‐up (more than six weeks and up to six months following treatment); and long‐term follow‐up (longer than six months and up to 12 months following treatment). We also assessed and reported outcomes with follow‐up more than one year after treatment.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Return to previous activities: this includes return to work, sport, and activities of daily living; and time to return.

    • Health‐related quality of life measures: e.g. the 36‐item Short Form Health Survey (SF‐36) (Ware 1992).

    • Adverse events (complications of treatment).

      • We set out to collect the overall numbers of participants with any early or late complication in the following categories.

        • Early: local infection (e.g. wound or skin breakdown or skin necrosis under strapping) or dehiscence, or both

        • Late: traumatic arthritis, calcification of coracoclavicular ligament, requirement for reoperation, stiffness (restricted range of shoulder movement), and deformity

      • However, partly based on data availability, we decided to present the following four categories.

        • Local infection or dehiscence, or both

        • Hardware irritation and/or prominence and/or complication

        • Restriction of range of movement

        • Other complications reported in the trials

    • Patient satisfaction with the cosmetic result

    • Resource use: including hospital admission and length of stay, number of outpatient attendances, and costs of treatment.

    • Radiological outcomes

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Bone, Joint and Muscle Trauma Group Specialised Register (17 June 2019), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (17 June 2019, Issue 6), MEDLINE (1946 to 18 June 2019), Embase (1980 to 17 June 2019), and LILACS (Latin American and Caribbean Literature on the Health Sciences) (lilacs.bvsalud.org/en/) (1982 to 17 June 2019). We also searched the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/) (17 June 2019) and US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov/) (17 June 2019) for ongoing and recently completed trials. We limited the searches for this update to 2009 onwards. Details of the search strategies used for the previous version of the review are shown in Tamaoki 2010. We did not place any restrictions on language or publication status.

    The subject‐specific search was combined with the Cochrane Highly Sensitive Search Strategy for identifying randomised trials in MEDLINE: sensitivity‐ and precision‐maximising version (Lefebvre 2011). Search strategies for all databases are shown in Appendix 1.

    Searching other resources

    We checked the reference lists of articles, reviews, and textbooks for potentially relevant studies. We included the findings from handsearches of abstracts of the British Elbow and Shoulder Society annual meetings (www.bess.org.uk/) (2001 to February 2009), the Orthopaedic Trauma Association annual meetings (ota.org/) (1996 to February 2009), and the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons annual meetings (www.aaos.org/) (1996 to February 2009).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (MT and ML) independently selected and assessed potentially eligible studies for inclusion using a piloted form. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion or through adjudication by a third review author (FTM) when necessary.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (MT and ML) independently collected data from the included studies using a piloted data extraction form. Any disagreements were resolved by a third review author (FTM). Two review authors (MT and ML) entered data into Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2014). When necessary, we sent requests to trial authors for additional information or data.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MT and ML) independently assessed individual trials for risk of bias using Cochrane's 'Risk of bias' tool (Higgins 2011). The review authors were not masked to the source or authorship of trial reports. We assessed the following domains.

    • Random sequence generation (the method used to generate the allocation sequence)

    • Allocation concealment

    • Blinding of participants and personnel

    • Blinding of outcome assessment. We considered this separately for subjective and objective outcomes.

    • Incomplete outcome data

    • Selective reporting

    • Other bias (e.g. major baseline imbalance; inappropriate influence of funders; risk of bias associated with inexperience of surgeons and other care providers with the interventions, differences in rehabilitation)

    We assessed each of these domains as low risk of bias, high risk of bias, and unclear risk of bias (either lack of information or uncertainty over the potential for bias). Any disagreements between authors regarding assessment of risk of bias were resolved by consensus.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed dichotomous outcome data as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We planned that when we observed a statistically significant difference between surgery and conservatively treated groups, we would report the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) with 95% CIs and the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs.

    We expressed continuous outcome data as mean differences (MD) with 95% CI. We intended to pool the data as an MD if two or more studies presented data derived from the same validated instrument of evaluation (with the same units of measure). If primary studies measured the same variables through different instruments (and different units of measure), we would use the standardised mean difference (SMD).

    Unit of analysis issues

    We considered that the unit of randomisation in the included trials was likely to be the individual participant, which we have found to be the case so far. Where trials included people with bilateral acromioclavicular dislocations, data may be evaluated for shoulder instead of for individual participants. Should this occur in a future update, we will consider reporting data for such trials only where the disparity between the units of analysis and randomisation is small. Where data are pooled, we will perform a sensitivity analysis to examine the effects of pooling these incorrectly analysed trials with the other correctly analysed trials.

    We were alert to, and took appropriate action to avoid, unit of analysis issues relating to outcome reporting at different follow‐up times, and the presentation of outcomes, such as total complications, by the number of outcomes rather than participants with these outcomes.

    Dealing with missing data

    We performed intention‐to‐treat analysis with the goal of including all participants randomised to any intervention. When there was insufficient information relative to estimate effects, such as number of participants, means, measures of uncertainty (standard deviation or error), or number of events and participants, we contacted the main authors of the included trials.

    For continuous outcomes with no standard deviations reported, we calculated standard deviations from standard errors, P values, or CIs, or extrapolated data from graphs, according to the methods outlined in Higgins 2011.

    When it was impossible to acquire adequate data for the forest plot (e.g. means and standard deviations), we have presented data in the text or appendices, or both.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed the heterogeneity of estimate effects between the included studies by visual inspection of the forest plot (analysis) along with consideration of the Chi2 test for heterogeneity and the I2 statistic. We quantified the possible magnitude of inconsistency (i.e. heterogeneity) across studies, using the I2 statistic with a rough guide for interpretation as follows: 0% to 40% might not be important; 30% to 60% may represent moderate heterogeneity; 50% to 90% may represent substantial heterogeneity; and 75% to 100% considerable heterogeneity (Deeks 2011).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to generate funnel plots of primary outcomes to assess the potential for publication bias (small‐study effects). However, the small number of included studies precluded this form of analysis (Sterne 2011). We also assessed outcome reporting bias by comparing results extracted from published journal reports with results from other sources (e.g. correspondence) and by checking study registrations or published protocols.

    Data synthesis

    When considered appropriate, we pooled the results of comparable groups of trials using the fixed‐effect model and 95% CIs. Although we intended to consider using the random‐effects model where there was clear diversity in clinical or methodological characteristics, the need for this did not arise.

    Assessment of the quality of the evidence and 'Summary of findings' table

    We used the GRADE approach to assess the quality of evidence related to all outcomes listed in the Types of outcome measures section (Schünemann 2011). We assessed the quality of the evidence as ‘high’, ‘moderate’, ‘low’, or ‘very low’ depending on the presence and extent of five factors: risk of bias, inconsistency of effect, indirectness, imprecision, and publication bias.

    We presented the main results of the surgical versus conservative treatment comparison in a 'Summary of findings' table, which provides key information concerning the quality of the evidence, the magnitude of effect of the interventions examined, and the sum of available data on the main outcomes.

    Outcomes for the 'Summary of findings' table

    We included the following outcomes in our 'Summary of findings' table at long‐term follow‐up (set at 12 months): shoulder function, pain, treatment failure, return to previous activities, health‐related quality of life measures, adverse events, and patient satisfaction with the cosmetic result. (For the last outcome, we presented dissatisfaction data as these were the only one reported.)

    For the purpose of pooling data where studies included more than one measure of function, we preferentially included one measure according to the following hierarchy: DASH score (Hudak 1996), Constant score (Constant 1987), University of California, Los Angeles (UCLA) score (Amstutz 1981), Oxford Shoulder Score (Dawson 1996; Dawson 2009). We restricted follow‐up for function to at least 12 months because the results are more consistent during this period; after 12 months there is typically no modification in function of individuals with acromioclavicular dislocation.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    There were insufficient data to perform our planned subgroup analyses, which aimed to explore effect size differences in relation to the type of injury (Rockwood classification types) and whether the surgery involved ligament reconstruction or not.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to perform sensitivity analyses investigating the effects of allocation concealment, the inclusion of studies with a high risk of bias, and of missing data. We also planned to assess the presence of small‐study bias (i.e. intervention effect is more beneficial in smaller studies) in the meta‐analysis by comparing the fixed‐effect estimate with the random‐effects estimate for primary outcomes. We explored the effects of excluding ‘historical’ studies that did not represent current practice.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    For this update (2009 to June 2019), we screened a total of 921 records from the following databases: Cochrane Bone, Joint and Muscle Trauma Group Specialised Register (3 records); CENTRAL (161), MEDLINE (99), Embase (315), LILACS (159), the WHO ICTRP (54), and ClinicalTrials.gov (130). We also identified two potentially eligible studies from the references of other studies, and one study from the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons Annual Meeting abstracts.

    After removal of duplicates, a total of 739 records remained. We excluded 724 records based on titles and abstracts. We obtained and evaluated the full texts of the remaining 15 records and linked any references pertaining to the same study under a single study ID. We included three studies, reported in 10 articles (COTS 2015; Joukainen 2014; Murray 2018); COTS 2015 and Joukainen 2014 were classified as ongoing trials in the previous review. We also found another report for a previously included study (Bannister 1992). We excluded one study (NCT02195219). We identified three ongoing studies (JPRN‐UMIN000013901; NCT01725997; NCT02677441). We added no records to studies awaiting classification.

    There were a total of six included studies, four excluded studies, and three ongoing studies.

    Included studies

    Details of the six included studies are shown in the Characteristics of included studies tables. All studies were reported in English.

    No study published its study protocol before reporting its results. Three studies registered their protocol before publication in national or international trials registers (COTS 2015; Joukainen 2014; Murray 2018).

    Bannister 1989 presented different results (different time points) across one separate full publication (Bannister 1992). COTS 2015 reported its results in one conference abstract (McKee 2012), and presented different results (different outcomes) in different publications, publishing quality of life results in Mah 2017. Each of the remaining four trials reported results in a single publication (Imatani 1975; Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986; Murray 2018). Where a full report was available, we did not use conference abstract data in our analyses.

    We sent requests seeking additional information or data to authors of all studies. We received an author's response from Larsen 1986 only; however, the missing data were no longer available.

    Study designs

    All studies were parallel‐group controlled studies with two intervention groups. Five studies were RCTs (Bannister 1989; COTS 2015; Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986; Murray 2018), and one was a quasi‐RCT (Imatani 1975). Bannister 1989 was conducted in four centres in the UK, COTS 2015 in 11 centres in Canada, and Larsen 1986 in three centres in Denmark. The other studies were single‐centre studies: Imatani 1975 (USA), Joukainen 2014 (Finland), and Murray 2018 (UK).

    Sample sizes

    The six included studies enrolled a total of 357 participants, of whom 171 were allocated surgery and 186 to conservative treatment. Whilst not pooled, data from five trials for function at one year were available for 272 participants, thus 76% of those enrolled, comprising 135 allocated surgery and 137 allocated conservative treatment. The maximum pooled data were available for treatment failure: 342 participants (168 versus 174).

    Participants
    Age and gender

    All included studies assessed adult patients. The mean age of participants in Bannister 1989 was 32.5 years; in COTS 2015 37.6 years (range 16 to 60 years); in Imatani 1975 23.6 years (range 17 to 34 years); in Joukainen 2014 34.5 years; and in Murray 2018 30.5 years (range 17 to 35 years). The median age of participants in Larsen 1986 was 36 years (range 19 to 78 years). Overall, the review population was young adults, with older people generally excluded.

    Gender data provided for assigned or assessed participants showed there were more male participants overall (312/335; 93.1%), with the proportion of men in individual studies ranging from 88.1% in Larsen 1986 to 100% in Imatani 1975.

    Types/classification of dislocation

    Bannister 1989, Imatani 1975, and Larsen 1986 did not specify the classification of dislocation. In Bannister 1989, the dislocation was divided by displacement of < 2 cm and ≥ 2 cm. Imatani 1975 included only participants who had displacement of 50% or more of the clavicle width. Larsen 1986 included only participants who had displacement of 75% or more of the clavicle width. Injuries were classified according to the Rockwood classification in COTS 2015 (type III, IV, or V) and Murray 2018 (type III or IV) (Rockwood 2004). Dislocations were classified according to the Tossy classification in Joukainen 2014 (type III) (Tossy 1963).

    Interventions

    Bannister 1989 compared surgery involving reduction of the acromioclavicular joint and fixation of the clavicle to the coracoid process with an AO cancellous or malleolar screw and washer (Bosworth screw) versus conservative treatment, where arm was rested in a broad‐arm sling for two weeks. Postsurgical care began with the use of a broad‐arm sling for two weeks. The rehabilitation of the two groups was identical, with increasing movement and weight training supervised by physiotherapist.

    COTS 2015 compared surgery with open reduction and definitive fixation with a hook plate and screws versus conservative treatment using a sling for four weeks. Postsurgical care began with the use of a sling for two weeks. The rehabilitation of the two groups differed. In the surgical group, rehabilitation began two weeks after the intervention with pendulum exercises, followed by active and passive exercises, and use of the sling was gradually discontinued. After six weeks, resisted exercises were implemented with return to full activity at three months. In the conservative group, rehabilitation began four weeks after injury with active and passive exercise, followed by resisted exercises at six weeks.

    Imatani 1975 compared surgery involving open reduction and internal fixation either by pins transfixing the acromioclavicular joint (Phemister procedure) or a screw from the clavicle to the coracoid process (Bosworth screw) versus conservative treatment involving immobilisation in a Velpeau bandage or arm sling for three weeks. Postsurgical immobilisation was maintained for a minimum of four weeks. The study authors did not report rehabilitation of the two groups.

    Joukainen 2014 compared surgery using open reduction and fixation of the acromioclavicular joint with two Kirschner wires (Phemister procedure) versus conservative intervention with Kenny‐Howard splint (a sling designed to put downward pressure on superiorly displaced clavicle and upward pressure on the arm) for four weeks. The shoulder was immobilised with a sling for four weeks after surgery. The study authors did not report rehabilitation of the two groups.

    Larsen 1986 compared surgery involving open reduction and internal fixation by pins transfixing the acromioclavicular joint (Phemister procedure) versus conservative treatment involving a sling and a swathe binding the arm to the body for two weeks followed by a simple sling for another two weeks. Postsurgical immobilisation was the same as described for the conservative treatment. The rehabilitation of the two groups was identical, with free movement allowed after one month with no loading on the limb and no restrictions after six weeks.

    Murray 2018 compared surgery using open reduction and fixation of the acromioclavicular joint with two tunnelled suspension devices (TightRope; Arthrex) versus conservative treatment using a collar and cuff for three weeks. The two devices were placed through two drill‐holes and tensioned with the acromioclavicular joint reduced; in addition, the acromioclavicular ligament and capsule were repaired, and the fascia and deep tissues were closed with 2‐0 Vicryl suture. Postsurgical care also began with a sling for three weeks. The rehabilitation of the two groups was identical: a sling was used for three weeks with encouragement to perform passive circumduction exercises without the sling four times per day. After discarding the sling, participants underwent a physiotherapist‐led programme beginning with active shoulder motion exercises; unrestricted range of motion was allowed beginning at six weeks.

    The surgical interventions used in Bannister 1989, Imatani 1975, Joukainen 2014, and Larsen 1986 are generally not used in current practice due to high complication rates. The use of the Velpeau bandage in Imatani 1975 and the Kenny‐Howard splint in Joukainen 2014 are also uncommon.

    Surgery was performed within 4 days of injury in Larsen 1986, within 10 days in Bannister 1989, and within 14 days in Imatani 1975 and Murray 2018. Timing of surgery data were not provided for COTS 2015 and Joukainen 2014; however, it is notable that dislocations greater than 28 and 21 days from injury, respectively, were excluded in these studies.

    Outcome measures

    The included studies varied in timing of follow‐up. Bannister 1989 reported follow‐up at one and four years. COTS 2015 conducted follow‐up to two years. Imatani 1975 and Murray 2018 specified a follow‐up time point of one year. Joukainen 2014 presented a mean follow‐up of 18 to 20 years. Larsen 1986 reported a mean follow‐up of 13 months.

    Primary outcomes

    Function or disability

    Bannister 1989 and Larsen 1986 evaluated shoulder function or disability by a non‐validated score system. COTS 2015 evaluated shoulder function or disability by the DASH questionnaire, Beaton 2001; Hudak 1996, and the Constant score (Constant 1987). Joukainen 2014 used the Constant score (Constant 1987; Constant 2008), the UCLA score (Amstutz 1981), the Simple Shoulder Test (Lippitt 1992), and a non‐validated score system. Murray 2018 used DASH, Gummesson 2003, and the Oxford Shoulder Score (Dawson 1996; Dawson 2009). Imatani 1975 did not evaluate this primary endpoint.

    Pain

    Three studies evaluated pain as part of a non‐validated scoring system, but the results of this outcome were not reported separately (Bannister 1989; Imatani 1975; Larsen 1986). However, Larsen 1986 also assessed pain as present or absent.

    Treatment failure

    All the studies measured treatment failure that generally required a subsequent operation.

    Secondary outcomes

    Return to previous activities

    Bannister 1989, COTS 2015, and Larsen 1986 described endpoints of return to previous activities (work and activities of daily living), and Murray 2018 reported return to pre‐injury sporting activity.

    Health‐related quality of life measures

    Only two studies assessed health‐related quality of life: COTS 2015 by the SF‐36 version 2 and Murray 2018 by the 12‐item Short Form Health Survey (SF‐12) physical and mental components.

    Adverse events

    Although all six studies reported adverse events, these did not conform to our plans to present the numbers of participants with one or more early complications and those with one or more later complications. Additionally, Bannister 1989 and Imatani 1975 confined their report to cases of treatment failure, all of which resulted in surgery in Bannister 1989. Details of the complications are provided in the Effects of interventions section.

    Patient satisfaction with the cosmetic result

    Only COTS 2015 measured participant satisfaction with the appearance of their shoulder.

    Resource use

    Only one trial evaluated economic assessment. Murray 2018 presented the mean expenditure for the surgery and conservative treatment.

    Radiological outcomes

    All included trials performed radiographic assessment. This included recording of soft‐tissue calcification and radiographic residual deformity (distance between coracoid and clavicle or acromion and clavicle).

    The authors of three trials reported no potential conflict of interest and no funding sources (Bannister 1989; Imatani 1975; Larsen 1986).

    COTS 2015 was funded jointly by the Orthopaedic Trauma Association, the Osteosynthesis and Trauma Care Foundation, and Synthes Inc. One author in Joukainen 2014 was supported by a grant from the Research Fund of Kuopio University Hospital District. Murray 2018 was funded by Arthrex (Naples, Florida, USA).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded four studies following further assessment. For details see Characteristics of excluded studies.

    We excluded three of the four studies due to irrelevant study design (not an RCT or quasi‐RCT). We excluded NCT02195219 because the registration for this study was withdrawn in June 2018, with the actual recruitment being listed as zero.

    Ongoing studies

    Our search for ongoing studies found three studies (JPRN‐UMIN000013901; NCT01725997; NCT02677441); see Characteristics of ongoing studies. The three ongoing studies are parallel RCTs comparing surgical versus conservative interventions to treat acromioclavicular dislocation. They appear to be single‐centre studies, taking place in Poland (JPRN‐UMIN000013901), Sweden (NCT01725997), and Switzerland (NCT02677441), and should enrol a total of 496 participants.

    Allocation

    A random number was drawn and the participant allocated to operative or conservative management in Bannister 1989, and random sequence generation was performed using a computer random number generator in COTS 2015 and a table of random numbers in Larsen 1986; we thus judged these three studies to be at low risk of selection bias. Imatani 1975 was quasi‐randomised based on alternation and thus at high risk of bias for this item. The two remaining studies provided insufficient information about the sequence generation process to permit a judgement for this domain (Joukainen 2014; Murray 2018), thus we assessed them as at unclear risk of selection bias.

    Concealment of allocation before assignment was adequate for COTS 2015 and Murray 2018, which used sequentially numbered, opaque, sealed envelopes, thus we judged these studies as at low risk of selection bias. There was no concealment of allocation in Bannister 1989 and Imatani 1975, therefore we assessed these studies as at high risk of selection bias. The two remaining studies provided insufficient information to confirm that allocation was concealed, thus we judged them as at unclear risk of bias for this domain (Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986).

    Blinding

    We judged all studies to be at high risk of performance bias. As they all compared surgery with conservative treatment, it was not possible to blind treatment providers. No studies included sham surgery, therefore participants were not blinded.

    It may have been possible to blind outcome assessors; however, only two studies mentioned correctly blinding assessors (Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986). For self‐reported outcomes (e.g. function, pain, and quality of life), we considered these two studies to be at low risk of detection bias. We assessed the remaining four studies as at high risk of detection bias for self‐reported outcomes (Bannister 1989; COTS 2015; Imatani 1975; Murray 2018). However, lack of blinded outcome assessors of the more objective outcomes (treatment failure as indicated by non‐routine secondary surgery; infection or dehiscence; hardware irritation leading to removal) would be less likely to lead to detection bias. Hence, for these outcomes we judged the remaining four studies as at unclear risk of bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    We considered studies to be at low risk of attrition bias if more than 80% of participants completed the follow‐up; missing outcomes data were balanced in number across intervention groups; and an intention‐to‐treat analysis was reported for the primary outcomes. As a result, three trials were at low risk of attrition bias (Bannister 1989; Larsen 1986; Murray 2018), and three trials were at high risk of attrition bias (COTS 2015; Imatani 1975; Joukainen 2014).

    We judged Bannister 1989, Larsen 1986, and Murray 2018 as at low risk of bias because more than 80% of participants completed the one‐year follow‐up; missing outcomes data were balanced in number across intervention groups; and an intention‐to‐treat analysis was likely performed; however, outcome data for participants who had withdrawn from the trial or who were lost to follow‐up were not presented.

    We classified COTS 2015 as at high risk of bias because although 84.3% and 67.5% of participants were assessed at one‐year follow‐up for Constant score and DASH questionnaire, respectively, the missing outcome data were not balanced in numbers across intervention groups: more participants in the conservatively treated group were lost to follow‐up at 12 months. For Constant score: 2/40 (5%) surgical versus 11/43 (25.6%) conservative at 12 months; for DASH questionnaire: 10/40 (25%) surgical versus 17/43 (39.5%) conservative at 12 months.

    We judged Imatani 1975 and Joukainen 2014 to be at high risk of bias because less than 80% of participants completed the end of the follow‐up period.

    Selective reporting

    We classified all included trials as at high risk of reporting bias. The reasons for this assessment are as follows. In Bannister 1989, Imatani 1975, and Larsen 1986, the trial registration and the study protocol were not available, and function or disability, or both, were not evaluated using a validated score. In COTS 2015, Joukainen 2014, and Murray 2018, the endpoint pain was measured by non‐standard validated participant‐reported measures; in addition, the authors of two studies did not describe some of the reported outcomes at the trial registration: Shoulder Pain and Disability Index in COTS 2015, and pain measured by VAS in Murray 2018. Furthermore, Imatani 1975 and Joukainen 2014 did not specify time points of outcomes.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We classified four trials as at low risk of other bias (COTS 2015; Imatani 1975; Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986), and two trials as at unclear risk of other potential threats to validity (Bannister 1989; Murray 2018). In Bannister 1989, there was insufficient information on baseline characteristics to permit a judgement, and in Murray 2018, information was insufficient to determine whether there was any bias relating to the funding of the trial by the maker of the suspensory device used.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings for the main comparison Surgical intervention compared to conservative intervention for treating acromioclavicular dislocation of the shoulder in adults

    The primary comparison in this review was any surgical intervention versus any conservative intervention. Subgroup analyses were precluded due to insufficient data.

    Surgical versus conservative interventions for treating acromioclavicular dislocation

    Function or disability (primary outcome)
    Upper limb functional outcome measures

    DASH questionnaire (0 (best function) to 100 (worst function))

    Data for this outcome reported at several time points from COTS 2015 and Murray 2018 are presented in Analysis 1.1. There is low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision, of little or no difference between surgery and conservative treatment in function measured using the DASH questionnaire at one year: mean difference (MD) 0.73 points, 95% confidence interval (CI) −2.70 to 4.16; 112 participants, 2 studies. A similar finding applied at six months (MD 1.70 points, 95% CI −2.98 to 6.39; 116 participants) and two years (MD −1.61 points, 95% CI −5.76 to 2.45; 55 participants, 1 study). Conversely, there is low‐quality evidence that DASH scores were lower in the conservative group at six weeks (MD 15.10 points, 95% CI 7.87 to 22.33; 115 participants) and three months (MD 8.38 points, 95% CI 2.62 to 14.14; 112 participants). At both these time points, the 95% CI overlaps the minimal clinically important difference (MCID) of 10 points (Gummesson 2003; Hudak 1996). There is thus evidence of a small but clinically important difference in function in favour of conservative treatment at six weeks, but not subsequently.

    Constant score (0 (worst function) to 100 (best function))

    Data for this outcome, which were reported at several time points up to two years from COTS 2015 and at 18‐ to 20‐year follow‐up for Joukainen 2014, are presented in Analysis 1.2.

    There is very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision, in particular reflecting small numbers from one study, of little or no difference between surgery and conservative treatment in function measured via the Constant score at one year: MD −0.61 points, 95% CI −6.20 to 4.98; 1 study, 70 participants. A similar finding applied to the results at two years and at over 18 years' follow‐up (see Analysis 1.2). An MCID for the Constant score for this condition and usual population is not available. Estimates for the MCID of the Constant score for other shoulder conditions range from 5.7 to 10.4 points (Kukkonen 2013; Simovitch 2018; Torrens 2016). Estimates for minimal detectable changes are, however, larger than these: for example, 13.6 points for individuals with clavicle fractures and 23 points for impingement and rotator cuff tears (Ban 2016; Henseler 2015). In COTS 2015, Constant shoulder scores were statistically significantly better in the conservative group at six weeks (MD −24.76 points, 95% CI −33.63 to −15.89; 82 participants); three months (MD −16.91 points, 95% CI −24.27 to −9.55; 76 participants); and six months (MD −11.31 points, 95% CI −17.19 to −5.43; 79 participants). The clinical importance of these results is less certain, but if we adopt the minimal detectable change data from Ban 2016 as a benchmark, these results are consistent with the findings of a small but clinically important difference in function in favour of conservative treatment at six weeks that dismisses over time.

    UCLA score (2 (worst function) to 35 (best function))

    Joukainen 2014 found no evidence of a difference in UCLA scores between the two intervention groups at 18‐ to 20‐year follow‐up: MD −2.00 points, 95% CI −5.11 to 1.11; 25 participants; very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision; Analysis 1.3.

    Oxford Shoulder Score (0 (worst function) to 48 (best function))

    Murray 2018 found no evidence of a difference in the Oxford Shoulder Score between the two intervention groups at one year: MD −0.09 points, 95% CI −1.74 to 1.56; 56 participants; low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision; Analysis 1.4.

    Other scores

    Three studies recorded patient‐reported outcome measures of shoulder function using a non‐validated score (Bannister 1989; Imatani 1975; Larsen 1986). Shoulder function in terms of pain, motion, and function or strength was assessed in all three trials; however, non‐validated scores and categorisation were used. Bannister 1989, which used a non‐validated score system based on a 100‐point scale (40 points assigned for pain, 30 for function, and 30 for motion), reported that 77% of surgical‐group participants versus 88% of conservative‐group participants had either good or excellent results after one year. At four years, 74% of surgical‐group participants and all conservative‐group participants had good or excellent results. Imatani 1975, using the same scoring system but with a slightly different categorisation, found that 45% of participants in the surgical group versus 58% in the conservative group had good or excellent results after a minimum of one‐year follow‐up. Larsen 1986, which used another non‐validated score based on a 12‐point scale (4 points assigned for pain, 4 for motion, and 4 for strength), found that only one participant in each group did not have a good or excellent results after 13 months. There is very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias, one level for serious imprecision, and one level for serious indirectness reflecting the inadequate outcome measure, of no or little between‐group differences in the number of participants with an unsatisfactory outcome (fair or poor) at one year: risk ratio (RR) 1.49, 95% CI 0.75 to 2.95; 3 studies, 160 participants; Analysis 1.5.

    There was very low‐quality evidence from Joukainen 2014 (25 participants) of no between‐group difference at 18 to 20 years' follow‐up in the Larsen score (1 to 12; best outcome: MD 0.00 points, 95% CI −0.72 to 0.72). Although there was a statistically significant difference in favour of the conservative group when assessed using the Simple Shoulder Test score (0 to 12; best outcome: MD −2.00 points, 95% CI −3.45 to −0.55), the clinical importance of this is not established, and the results are inconsistent with the other results for this trial.

    Pain (primary outcome)

    No data were available for pain measured using a VAS. Whilst all three studies using a non‐validated score that included pain, the pain component was not reported separately (Bannister 1989; Imatani 1975; Larsen 1986). However, Larsen 1986 assessed pain dichotomously (presence or absence) at one year and found no evidence of a difference between groups in participants with pain (9/39 versus 7/40; RR 1.32, 95% CI 0.54 to 3.19; 79 participants; very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias, one level for serious imprecision, and one level for serious indirectness, reflecting the poor definition of this outcome; Analysis 1.6.

    Treatment failure (primary outcome)

    There was very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision, of little or no difference between groups for treatment failure, usually resulting in surgery (14/168 versus 15/174; RR 0.99, 95% CI 0.51 to 1.94; 342 participants, 6 studies; Analysis 1.7).

    Treatment failures were usually excluded from follow‐up analysis in two trials (Imatani 1975; Larsen 1986). In Bannister 1989, reasons for failure of surgical treatment were screw cut‐out (2 cases), broken screw (1 case), and painful subluxation (2 cases); reasons for failure of conservative treatment were painful subluxation (2 cases), unacceptable shoulder weakness (1 case), and unacceptable appearance (1 case). In COTS 2015, reasons for failure of surgical treatment were infection (1 case) and acromial erosion (1 case); reasons for failure of conservative treatment were distal clavicle protrusion (2 cases). Imatani 1975 reported failure in two cases in the surgical group in which the Bosworth screw pulled out of the coracoid process and the position of reduction was lost. In Joukainen 2014, one participant in the surgical group needed a reoperation to remove deeply positioned wire, and one participant in the conservative group was operated on for an unsatisfactory treatment result. Two participants in each group in Larsen 1986 had a subsequent operation due to residual pain; one other surgically treated participant had a reoperation to remove broken wires after three weeks; and another surgically treated participant had a reoperation after four weeks because of lateral migration of the wires. Finally, one conservatively treated participant had an operation because of skin problems resulting from a major prominence of the clavicle. In Murray 2018, no participant had a reoperation in the surgical group, whilst five participants in the conservatively treated group required a surgical procedure for persistent symptoms, mainly discomfort.

    Return to previous activities

    Two studies reported, respectively, on the number of participants who returned to work, COTS 2015, and sports, Murray 2018. There is low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision, of little or no difference between groups in return to former activities at one year (57/67 versus 62/70; RR 0.96, 95% CI 0.85 to 1.10; 137 participants, 2 studies; Analysis 1.8). There was low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and one level for very serious imprecision, of a lower return to former activities in the surgery group at six weeks (15/67 versus 25/70; RR 0.63, 95% CI 0.37 to 1.08; 137 participants, 2 studies). This is consistent with the more favourable function results at six weeks in the conservatively treated groups of both studies and with evidence from two other studies of an earlier recovery in conservatively treated participants compared with those treated with surgery.

    In Bannister 1989, surgically treated participants consistently returned to their previous activities later than those managed conservatively. Manual workers treated surgically returned to work after an average of 11 weeks, compared with four weeks in those treated conservatively. Clerical workers in the surgery group returned to work in four weeks compared with one week for those in the conservatively treated group (reported P < 0.01). The mean time for return to practicing sport was 11 weeks: 16 weeks after surgery and seven weeks after conservative treatment (reported P < 0.05).

    Larsen 1986 reported that participants treated surgically were off work longer than those treated conservatively (median (range): 8 weeks (2 to 22 weeks) versus 6 weeks (0 to 12 weeks); reported P = 0.067.

    Imatani 1975 and Joukainen 2014 did not assess time to return to previous activities.

    Health‐related quality of life measures

    Data for the physical and mental component scores for the SF‐36 version 2 survey, reported at several time points up to two years in COTS 2015, and the SF‐12, reported at one year in Murray 2018, are presented in Analysis 1.9.

    There is low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and one level for serious imprecision, of no clinically important difference between groups at one year in quality of life scores (0‐to‐100 scale, where 100 is best score) relating to either physical quality of life (MD −0.63, 95% CI −2.63 to 1.37; 122 participants, 2 studies) or mental quality of life (MD 0.47 points, 95% CI −1.51 to 2.44; 122 participants, 2 studies). Similar findings of a lack of difference between groups applied to all time points reported for mental quality of life and for the two‐year results for physical quality of life. The physical component scores were significantly higher and thus in favour of the conservative intervention at six weeks (MD −4.60 points, 95% CI −8.58 to −0.62); three months (MD −6.38 points, 95% CI −9.15 to −3.61); and six months (MD −2.89 points, 95% CI −5.23 to −0.55). The MCID of the SF‐36 ranges from 2.0 to 7.8 points (scale 0 to 100; Andresen 2018; Angst 2001; Laucis 2015). All these differences in favour of conservative treatment were thus potentially clinically important but to a limited extent for the two early time points and marginally if at all at six months; very low‐quality evidence downgraded one level for serious bias and two levels for very serious imprecision.

    Adverse events

    All studies described adverse events, but these were limited to those linked with treatment failure in Bannister 1989 and Imatani 1975. As shown in Analysis 1.10, the majority of all complications were in the surgical group, and the most common adverse events were complications related to surgical implants. The majority of surgical complications were in studies testing now‐outdated devices, such as wiring, known for their high risk of complications. These are reported below.

    Local infection or dehiscence, or both

    As expected, all 11 cases (8.7% of surgical treatment group) of mainly superficial infection occurred in the surgical treatment group (11/126 versus 0/126; RR 7.76, 95% CI 1.54 to 39.00; 252 participants, 4 studies).

    Hardware irritation and/or prominence and/or complication

    Also as expected, all 31 hardware‐related adverse events (18.5% of surgical treatment group) occurred in the surgical treatment group (31/168 versus 0/174; RR 12.93, 95% CI 3.70 to 45.21; 342 participants, 6 studies).

    Only Murray 2018 reported no adverse events related to surgical implants. In Bannister 1989, reasons for hardware complications were screw cut‐out (2 cases) and broken screw (1 case). In COTS 2015, reasons for hardware complications were plate loosening (2 cases), acromial erosion (2 cases), and clavicle fracture at the end of the plate (1 case). In Imatani 1975, two participants in the surgery group had a surgical complication (screw pull‐out) within the first 48 hours of the operation; neither participant received a reoperation. Joukainen 2014 reported loss of the optimal position of the K‐wire (4 cases) and broken K‐wires (1 case). There were problems related to the wires in Larsen 1986; when wires broke, the site of breakage was always the acromioclavicular joint; 16 participants had wire breakage.

    Restriction of range of movement

    Bannister 1989 reported that 40% of participants in the surgically treated group versus 88% of those in the conservatively treated group had gained full range of movement after four months, and around 90% of all participants had recovered full power. Imatani 1975 reported on range of movement or strength only as part of a non‐validated total score.

    All four participants with restricted range of movement documented in COTS 2015 and Larsen 1986 were in the surgery group: 4/81 versus 0/86; RR 5.29, 95% CI 0.63 to 44.45; 167 participants, 2 studies).

    We could not include data from Joukainen 2014 in the analysis, which also reported no statistically significant differences between groups in restriction of range of movement (active flexion and abduction). In addition, Murray 2018 did not report on their planned outcome of range of shoulder motion (flexion, extension, abduction, internal and external rotation).

    Other adverse events

    Other adverse events included a mixture of other major and minor complications. There is very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision, of no or little difference between groups in other adverse events (7/126 versus 10/126; RR 0.73, 95% CI 0.31 to 1.74; 252 participants, 4 studies).

    In COTS 2015, minor complications in the operative group included four reports of peri‐incisional numbness and heterotopic ossification in two participants; in the conservatively treated group, two participants had distal clavicle protrusion, and one participant developed heterotopic ossification. In Joukainen 2014, one participant in the conservatively treated group had persistent shoulder pain needing acromioclavicular joint resection. One participant in Larsen 1986 presented skin problems resulting from a major prominence of the clavicle after the conservative intervention. One participant in Murray 2018 had a basilic vein thrombosis three months after surgery that was treated with a three‐month course of warfarin; five participants in the conservatively treated group in this trial required a surgical procedure for persistent symptoms following treatment, mainly discomfort. Overall, nine participants (7.1%) in the conservatively treated group had persistent symptoms or discomfort, or both.

    Total participants with adverse event outcome

    This outcome included participants with any complication, such as wound infection and dehiscence, hardware problems, and complications such as shoulder stiffness and treatment failure. We established a rule to avoid the risk of unit of analysis problems where individual complications could have occurred together in one person. There is very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias, one level for serious imprecision, and one level for serious inconsistency, of a greater risk of adverse events with surgical treatment: 45/168 versus 16/174; RR 2.82, 95% CI 1.65 to 4.82; 342 participants, 6 studies; I2 = 48%; Analysis 1.11).

    Patient satisfaction with the cosmetic result

    Only COTS 2015 measured participant satisfaction with the appearance of their shoulder. However, only patient dissatisfaction data were presented. There is very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias and two levels for very serious imprecision, of little or no difference in participant unhappiness with their shoulder appearance at one year: 2/38 versus 5/32; RR 0.34, 95% CI 0.07 to 1.62; 70 participants; Analysis 1.12. Of note is that one of the treatment failures in the conservatively treated groups of Bannister 1989 and Murray 2018 was for cosmetic reasons.

    Resource use

    Only Murray 2018 assessed resource use, finding that the mean expenditure for the surgical group (GBP 3359.73) was greater than that for the conservatively treated group (GBP 796.22) over the first postoperative year (reported P < 0.0001).

    Radiological outcomes

    Bannister 1989 reported that in the surgical group, all cases were initially fixed anatomically, but that reduction was lost in 35% of participants when the coracoclavicular screw was removed. In conservatively treated participants, both acromioclavicular and coracoclavicular separations reduced by 5 mm over the first year. The mean reduction of acromioclavicular separation achieved by surgery was 12 mm, 4 mm higher than with conservative management.

    COTS 2015 found that the majority of participants in the surgical group had a reduced acromioclavicular joint compared with no participants in the conservatively treated group at all time points (6 weeks: 36/40 versus 0/42; 3 months: 35/39 versus 0/38; 6 months: 32/38 versus 0/39; 1 year: 30/37 versus 0/32; and 2 years: 17/22 versus 0/20).

    Imatani 1975 reported that all participants in the conservatively treated group demonstrated some displacement on the stressed view, and two participants (17%) had no displacement on the unstressed view. Four participants (36%) treated surgically had marked calcification; all of these participants had a Bosworth screw procedure.

    In Joukainen 2014, the radiographic analysis showed that the mean width of the acromioclavicular joint at long‐term follow‐up was statistically significantly greater after conservative treatment than in the surgical group (8.3 mm versus 3.4 mm, respectively; P = 0.004).

    Larsen 1986 reported that calcifications around ligaments were seen in 69% of participants in the surgical group and 63% of those in the conservatively treated group. In all but two of the surgically treated participants, the correct anatomical position of the clavicle was retained. At final follow‐up, all conservatively treated participants had a displacement of the clavicle between 125% and 150% of its width, but in only four participants (10%) did an application of load cause any increase of this displacement.

    Murray 2018 described that at one year, the mean displacement was lower in participants who underwent surgery (1.75 mm) than in those managed conservatively (10.61 mm, reported P < 0.0001).

    Sensitivity analyses

    The limitations of the available data meant that sensitivity analyses served mainly to inform on the GRADE ratings. For example, the effect on treatment failure of excluding trials with an unclear or high risk of selection bias relating to failure to conceal allocation resulted in the retention of data from two of the six trials, COTS 2015 and Murray 2018: 2/69 versus 7/74; RR 0.36, 95% CI 0.09 to 1.45; 143 participants; I2 = 52%. This was an endorsement of our judgement that the available evidence was of very low quality. Evidence for function, return to former activities, and quality of life came mainly or solely from the two most recently conducted studies (COTS 2015; Murray 2018), both of which included participants with high‐grade dislocations of acromioclavicular: COTS 2015 included Rockwood types III, IV, or V, and Murray 2018 included types III or IV. We performed sensitivity analyses including only studies that used current techniques to treat acromioclavicular dislocation for treatment failure and total participants with adverse events (COTS 2015; Murray 2018). We found very low‐quality evidence, downgraded one level for serious risk of bias, one level for serious imprecision, and one level for serious inconsistency, of little or no difference between groups for treatment failure (2/69 versus 7/74; RR 0.36, 95% CI 0.09 to 1.45; 143 participants, 2 studies) or total participants with adverse events (15/69 versus 8/74; RR 2.01, 95% CI 0.89 to 4.53; 143 participants, 2 studies); analyses not shown.

    Subgroup analyses

    Our plans to study the outcomes in different age groups and for different dislocation types were precluded by lack of data. Nevertheless, Bannister 1989, using a non‐validated score, reported that for the 12 participants with dislocations with acromioclavicular separation greater than 2 cm, surgery resulted in better functional results (good or excellent: 5/7 (71%) in the surgical group versus 1/5 (20%) in the conservatively treated group). As detailed above, only COTS 2015 and Murray 2018 classified dislocation in accordance with the Rockwood classification (Rockwood 2004).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included only six controlled trials (five randomised and one quasi‐randomised) that addressed the basic question of whether surgery is required for acromioclavicular dislocation. The included studies were small, involving a total of 357 participants, with data available for the primary outcomes limited to 272 participants (5 studies) for function at one year, 79 participants (1 study) for pain, and 342 participants (6 studies) for treatment failure. Different surgical procedures were performed, and none of the included studies provided evidence for all important outcomes. The quality of evidence for these outcomes is low or very low.

    It is noteworthy that the evidence for important outcomes (function, return to former activities, and quality of life) derive mainly from the two most recently conducted studies (COTS 2015; Murray 2018), whose interventions reflect current practice (surgical and conservative), and both of which included participants with high‐grade dislocations of acromioclavicular in accordance with the Rockwood classification.

    The main findings of the review are summarised in summary of findings Table for the main comparison. There is low‐quality evidence from two studies of no clinically important difference between surgery and conservative treatment in function at one year measured via the DASH questionnaire, a validated patient‐reported outcome measure of upper arm function. These results were consistent with other measures of function at one‐year or longer follow‐up, including non‐validated outcome scores reported by three studies. There is low‐quality evidence that function at six weeks may be better after conservative treatment, indicating an earlier recovery. The evidence from one study on participants with pain at one year, which showed no between‐group difference, was of very low quality, meaning that this finding is uncertain. Very low‐quality evidence from six studies showed no between‐group difference for treatment failure, which usually resulted in subsequent surgery. The main sources of treatment failure were problems related to hardware in the surgery group, and persistent symptoms, mainly discomfort, from acromioclavicular dislocation in the conservatively treated group.

    There is low‐quality evidence from two studies that there may be little or no between‐group difference in return to former activities (sports or work) at one year. However, low‐quality, but consistent, evidence from four studies indicated an earlier recovery in conservatively treated participants compared with those treated with surgery. There is low‐quality evidence from two studies of no clinically important difference between groups at one year in quality of life, either physical or mental. There is very low‐quality evidence of a greater risk of an adverse event after surgery; the majority of surgical complications came from older trials testing devices that are not typically used in current practice. Common adverse outcomes were hardware complications or discomfort (18.5%) and infection (8.7%) in the surgery group, and persistent symptoms (7.1%), mainly discomfort, in the conservatively treated group. Very low‐quality evidence from one study means that we are uncertain whether there is a between‐group difference in patient dissatisfaction with the cosmetic result.

    The only trial that assessed resource use found that the mean expenditure for the surgical group was greater than that for the conservatively treated group over the first postoperative year (Murray 2018).

    There were insufficient data to conduct subgroup analysis relating to type of injury and whether surgery involved ligament reconstruction or not.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The five RCTs and one quasi‐RCT included in this review recruited 357 participants with acute acromioclavicular dislocation, but there was often a substantial drop in the data available for pooling. For our primary outcomes, only 112 participants from two studies contributed data to function as measured using a validated patient‐reported outcome measure at one year, no studies reported data for a validated pain score, but 342 participants from six studies contributed data to treatment failure. All included trials reported the inclusion criteria used to assess acute acromioclavicular dislocation, which is usually defined as existing within three weeks from the injury (Flint 2014). This confirmation of acute injury was also shown by the timing of surgery, which occurred within 4, 10, or 14 days of the injury in four trials. The six included trials did not permit a comprehensive review of the relative effectiveness of surgical or conservative treatment for acromioclavicular dislocation.

    The sex and age profile of the review participants is representative of the expected population for these injuries. Whilst it was clear that all participants had an acromioclavicular dislocation, only two trials, COTS 2015 and Murray 2018, evaluated participants according to the Rockwood classification (Galatz 2005). COTS 2015 included Rockwood types III, IV, and V; Murray 2018 included types III and IV. Notably, Bannister 1989, Imatani 1975, and Larsen 1986 did not specify the classification of dislocation, which hampers interpretation of the applicability of the evidence. However, all included trials reported that participants had a complete acromioclavicular dislocation (i.e. not a subluxation).

    One major limitation of the review, which significantly affects the applicability of the findings, is that the majority of the current surgical techniques, such as anatomic reconstructions, hook plate and arthroscopic reconstruction, tunnelled suspension devices, among others, were covered in only two studies: hook plate in COTS 2015 and tunnelled suspension devices in Murray 2018. The techniques used in the other four included studies reflect that all of these studies were conducted over 30 years ago. Bannister 1989 and Imatani 1975 used a screw to fix the clavicle to the coracoid; this procedure is technically challenging, and loss of fixation due to screw cut‐out is a well‐recognised complication. Joukainen 2014 and Larsen 1986 used Kirschner wire fixation; given the high rate of migration and breakage of wires, we expect the use of Kirschner wire fixation to be uncommon in current practice.

    Consideration is also required of conservative treatment and rehabilitation in terms of current practice. The use of the Velpeau bandage in Imatani 1975 and the Kenny‐Howard splint in Joukainen 2014 are uncommon in current practice. Additionally, details of the rehabilitation protocols were not provided in these two trials.

    Another limitation of this review, which also affects the applicability of the findings, is that the all included studies failed to measure all the outcomes of importance; notably, function using a validated score was assessed in only three studies, and no study measured pain using a validated score.

    Quality of the evidence

    The evidence for the comparison of surgical versus conservative treatment for acromioclavicular dislocation was of low quality for function, return to former activities (work or sport), and heath‐related quality of life, and very low quality for pain, treatment failure, numbers of participants with one or more adverse events, and patient dissatisfaction with the cosmetic result; see summary of findings Table for the main comparison. We downgraded the quality of the evidence by one level for all outcomes due to high risk of bias from methodological flaws such as inadequate allocation concealment in several studies and lack of blinding, although this was mainly unavoidable given the nature of the two treatments, in all studies. Only two included studies, COTS 2015 and Murray 2018, reported according to the standards of the CONSORT statement (Schulz 2010). We downgraded the quality of the evidence for all outcomes at least one level for imprecision due to the small number of events or totals. We downgraded the quality of the evidence for indirectness due to poor definition of pain reported in Larsen 1986. We also downgraded the quality of the evidence for adverse events due to inconsistency, where there was evidence of statistical heterogeneity that also reflected clinical heterogeneity. We did not downgrade for publication bias, given that we found no evidence of missing trials nor was there enough evidence to test for this.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We conducted this review according to the criteria and methods set out in a published protocol. During the process of the review we tried to minimise publication bias; our search strategy was comprehensive and has been maintained properly and regularly updated by the contact author (ML). It included handsearching conference proceedings and the search for ongoing and recently completed trials. However, the possibility that some studies have been missed cannot be eliminated. We attempted to contact the authors of all included trials, but only the authors of one trial responded (Larsen 1986); however, for this trial the missing data were no longer available.

    As indicated in the footnotes of Analysis 1.1, Analysis 1.4, and Analysis 1.9, we derived missing data from Murray 2018 for DASH scores at several time points, and the Oxford Shoulder Score and SF‐12 physical and mental components at one year, either from the graphs or P values provided in the report. Our data were consistent with the author’s claims, and is thus not a source of bias.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    In the first version of the review, we described four published systematic reviews on the treatment of acromioclavicular dislocations (Bäthis 2000;Ceccarelli 2008;Hootman 2004;Phillips 1998; Spencer 2007), and discussed their findings in relation to ours (Tamaoki 2010). Our search update resulted in the identification of four new systematic reviews that assessed surgical versus conservative interventions to treat acromioclavicular dislocations: one review was published in 2011 (Smith 2011), one in 2014 (Korsten 2014), and two in 2018 (Chang 2018; Tang 2018). The results of our review, which demonstrated no clinical differences between the two interventions in terms of shoulder function, pain, and quality of life, were often in accordance with the findings of these four new systematic reviews, described below. None of these new systematic reviews or our review found a clear indication for surgery in terms of evidence of better long‐term function. Notably, our review also identified three ongoing RCTs that are likely to help inform practice in a future update.

    Smith 2011 assessed six retrospective case series in their review. None of the studies assessed in this review were appraised in our review. The authors concluded that there was no statistically significant difference in clinical or radiological outcomes between surgical and conservative interventions for participants with Rockwood grade III acromioclavicular dislocation. Although they found that surgery resulted in a better cosmetic outcome, it had a greater duration of sick leave compared with the conservative intervention.

    Korsten 2014 included the results of eight studies that compared surgical versus conservative interventions, amongst which were six retrospective cohort studies and two prospective cohort studies. This review found no conclusive evidence for the treatment of Rockwood grade III acromioclavicular dislocations. However, they considered that physically active young adults seemed to have a slight advantage in outcome when treated surgically.

    Chang 2018 included the results of 19 studies with a total of 954 participants in their review: 507 participants underwent surgery and 447 conservative management. There were four RCTs (Bannister 1989; COTS 2015; Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986), one quasi‐RCT (Imatani 1975), and 14 cohort studies. Chang 2018 concluded that there is no clinical between‐intervention difference in functional outcome scores in high‐grade acromioclavicular joint dislocations. They reported that participants who underwent conservative treatment had a more rapid return to work but a poorer cosmetic outcome.

    Tang 2018 included 10 studies involving 326 participants who underwent surgical treatment and 323 participants who were managed conservatively. Four of the 10 studies were RCTs (Bannister 1989; COTS 2015; Joukainen 2014; Larsen 1986); the other six were retrospective studies. The authors concluded there were no significant differences between surgical and conservative treatment in terms of function, pain, weakness, tenderness, post‐traumatic arthritis, and restriction of strength.

    Table 1. Rockwood classification of injury types

    Type

    Description

    Type I

    A mild injury involving spraining of the acromioclavicular ligaments while leaving the joint intact

    Type II

    Injury where the acromioclavicular ligaments are torn and acromioclavicular joint is disrupted, whilst the coracoclavicular ligaments are intact

    Type III

    Injury involves complete tearing of both the acromioclavicular and coracoclavicular ligaments with 100% dislocation of the joint.

    Type IV

    Injury is a complete acromioclavicular dislocation with posterior displacement of the clavicle through or into the trapezius fascia.

    Type V

    Injury is a complete acromioclavicular dislocation with 100% to 300% superior dislocation of the clavicle. It can involve significant disruption of the deltotrapezial fascia.

    Type VI

    Injury involves inferior displacement of the clavicle into a subacromial or subcoracoid position.
