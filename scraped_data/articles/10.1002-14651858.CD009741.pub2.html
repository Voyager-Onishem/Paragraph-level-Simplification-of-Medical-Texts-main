Hormonal contraception for women exposed to HIV infection

  • G Justus Hofmeyr
  • Mandisa Singata
  • Jennifer Sneden
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 20 May 2014 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009741.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی
  • Français

    • Background

    Concern about a possible association of hormonal contraception with HIV acquisition has been raised by three types of evidence. Firstly, high‐dose progestogen treatment greatly increases HIV acquisition in female non‐human primates. Secondly, biological plausibility for a link between hormonal contraception anf HIV acquisition is provided by evidence of a hypo‐oestrogenic state induced by progestogen contraception with vaginal mucosal thinning, and evidence of effects on the humoral and cellular immune systems. Thirdly, some but not other large observational studies have found an increase in HIV acquisition among women using hormonal contraception.

    Objectives

    To determine, from the best available evidence, the effect of hormonal contraception on HIV acquisition.

    Search methods

    We used the Cochrane Fertility Regulation Group trials search strategy.

    Selection criteria

    Published, unpublished and ongoing trials with random allocation, comparing hormonal with non‐hormonal methods, other hormonal methods or no contraception in women at risk of HIV acquisition.

    Data collection and analysis

    Data will be extracted from eligible trials onto a data extraction sheet and analysed using routine Cochrane Collaboration methodology,

    Main results

    One ongoing randomised trial was identified, with no data available to date.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is currently no robust evidence from randomized trials on the possible effect of hormonal contraception on HIV acquisition. High quality trials in this area are needed to inform counselling of individual woman and public health policy.

    • Insufficient evidence to confirm an association between hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition

    Modern contraception methods play an important role in global health, empowering women to avoid unintended pregnancy and thus reducing avoidable deaths and ill‐health of mothers and babies related to unintended pregnancies. Hormonal methods are those in which women receive female sex hormones (progestogens with or without oestrogens), by mouth, injection, vaginal rings, skin patches, progestogen‐containing intrauterine devices or devices implanted below the skin. It is known that monkeys treated with high doses of progestogen (higher than those used for contraception) are more likely to be infected with HIV. It is also known that hormonal contraception alters the systemic and local human immune system in ways which might increase susceptibility to HIV infection, and that progestogen contraception lowers the woman's own oestrogen levels causing thinning of the lining of the vagina. In some, but not all, observational studies, it was found that women using hormonal contraception (specifically DMPA) were more likely to acquire HIV. However, as these were not randomised trials, this effect might be related to the characteristics of women who choose to use hormonal contraception, rather than an effect of the contraceptive itself.

    This review found only one ongoing trial with no data available to date. There is thus no high quality evidence available as to whether or not hormonal contraception is associated with HIV acquisition. Because of the importance of hormonal contraception for women's health, no change in practice would be justified without such evidence. Appropriate randomised trials are needed to answer this important question.

    All women using non‐barrier contraception should be advised that such contraception does not protect against HIV infection, and that they should take such precautions as are appropriate to their personal circumstances.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The WHO has released a policy implications brief, ‘Programmatic and research considerations for hormonal contraception for women at risk of HIV and women living with HIV’(WHO 2012b). The brief included the following statements:

    Introduction: "This expert group agreed that access to both HIV prevention and to family planning are critically important for individual and public health. Family planning reduces the risk of unintended pregnancy, contributes to lower rates of maternal and infant mortality and morbidity, reduces the number of infants exposed to HIV perinatally, and empowers women to have more control over their reproductive health. HIV remains a significant public health threat worldwide and is a leading cause of death in women of reproductive age in some low‐ and middle‐income countries."

    1. "Based on current evidence, family planning programmes delivering services to women at risk of, or living with, HIV infection can continue to offer all methods of hormonal contraception. However, as none of these methods protects against HIV, the use of condoms or other HIV preventive measures should always be strongly recommended."

    2. "Ensure the availability of a wider variety of highly effective contraceptive methods, including hormonal methods (oral, injectables, patches, rings, and implants), intrauterine devices (IUDs, both copper‐bearing and levonorgestrel releasing), barrier methods (female and male condoms), and voluntary sterilization (for both men and women), to all medically eligible women and couples seeking family planning services, including women who are at risk of HIV infection and women living with HIV."

    Implications for research

    The WHO brief referred to above included recommendations to:

    3. "Conduct further high quality research employing strong research designs to gather more definitive evidence regarding the epidemiological association between various methods of hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition, transmission, and disease progression, including evaluating longer‐acting methods (such as implants, IUDs, and injectables) and newer methods that have not been included in prior studies (such as the combined contraceptive patch and the combined contraceptive vaginal ring). Injectable contraceptives that employ alternatives to medroxy‐progesterone acetate should be investigated."

    4. "Investigate basic science questions related to understanding the biological mechanisms of HIV acquisition, transmission, and progression in relation to effects of standard hormonal contraception doses."

    In terms of the remit of this review, priority needs to be given to adequately powered trials with random allocation comparing hormonal (particularly DMPA) with non‐hormonal family planning methods, such as the copper IUD, or alternative hormonal methods. Because seroconversion is a rare event, sample sizes will need to be large. Such trials should take into account the importance of choice in family planning, possible mechanisms of effect such as the effect of family planning method on condom use, and the medical eligibility of participants.

    Background

    Preventing unplanned pregnancies is critical to the health of women and children worldwide. The World Health Organization identified effective contraception as a key strategy in relieving the global burden of HIV through multiple outcomes (WHO 2010). These outcomes include reducing vertical HIV transmission, alleviating maternal morbidity due to HIV infection, and decreasing the number orphaned children due to parental HIV‐related deaths (Morrison 2011; WHO 2010).

    In 2009, 56.1% of all partnered women worldwide between the ages 15 and 49 used modern methods of contraception to prevent pregnancy (UN 2010). While sterilization is the most prevalent form of contraception, at least 150 million women use hormonal contraception for family planning. Hormonal contraceptives include oral contraceptives, injectable contraceptives, transdermal contraceptives, hormonal intrauterine systems and implants (Morrison 2009; UN 2010).

    In low‐resource settings, longer‐acting and highly effective methods are needed to offer women effective, convenient contraception. While the male condom acts as a physical barrier to sexually transmitted diseases, it is often ineffective in situations where women lack agency in sexual risk taking (Morrison 2011). Women must make difficult decisions regarding contraceptive choice due to lack of accessibility and affordability of modern technologies. In sub‐Saharan Africa, where the prevalence and risk of HIV acquisition is especially high, injectable contraceptives constitute a high proportion of all available modern contraceptive technology (43% in South Africa) (Morrison 2009).  

    Recent epidemiologic evidence heightens concern over the possible association between women receiving hormonal contraception and an increased risk of HIV‐1 acquisition. Of particular concern are depot medroxyprogesterone actate (DMPA) as well as other injectable progestogens.  Multiple mechanisms by which hormonal contraception might increase the risk of HIV have been reviewed in Blish 2011.

    A large‐scale observational study presented at the 2011 International Aids Society conference found the use of hormonal contraceptives to be associated with a two‐fold increased risk of HIV‐1 acquisition by women in serodiscordant relationships (Heffron 2011, Heffron 2012). The post hoc identification of this association and potential bias due to self reported contraceptive use may reduce the validity of these findings, as well as the potential for confounding factors inherent in observational studies (Wawer 2012); however, some previous observational data support this positive correlation. 

    To date, over 50 identified observational studies have addressed the question of whether hormonal contraception is associated with an increased susceptibility to HIV infection (Baeten 2007b; Heikinheimo 2009). Numerous detailed reviews of observational studies have been published and are ongoing. Results from the largest prospective cohort studies, most of which were conducted in regions with high HIV prevalence, are conflicting. In 1999, a meta‐analysis of 28 observational studies found a positive correlation between the use of oral contraceptives and the risk of HIV acquisition (Wang 1999). In the following years, three more large cohort studies found a significant positive correlation (Baeten 2007a; Kumwenda 2008; Morrison 2010) while others found no significant association (Kiddugavu 2003; Kleinschmidt 2007). Morrison 2009 conducted a systematic review using high‐quality studies that included a comparison group, a large sample size, detailed information on contraception compliance and appropriate adjustment for potential confounding. Of the 13 quality studies examining oral contraceptive use and HIV acquisition, two found a significantly increased risk of HIV. Three out of 10 studies evaluating depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA) use and HIV acquisition found a significant positive correlation. While the discrepancies between multiple observational studies are profound, evidence to date suggests that hormonal contraception may be a risk factor for HIV acquisition in high‐risk populations, such as female sex workers, but may not be generalizable to the entire population of female contraceptive users (Baeten 2007a; Morrison 2009). In a subsequent systematic review (Polis 2013), 8 observational studies met the inclusion criteria. Some (Baeten 2007a, Morrison 2010 and Heffron 2012) but not all studies found an association of DMPA or unspecified injectable contraception with HIV acquisition (risk ratio 1.6 to 2.2). No association was found with NET‐EN nor oral contraceptives.

    The available observational studies are subject to several methodological limitations that may decrease the reliability of their results. Determining temporality of HIV acquisition with precision poses a serious obstacle to cross‐sectional studies and is challenging to address when including women with multiple casual partners (Bulterys 1994). Inadequate selection of comparison groups in the study design, followed by limited numbers of HIV seroconversions, reduces the power of most large cohort studies (Kapiga 1998; Kilmarx 2000). Studies which were not originally designed to measure the association between hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition are limited by the lack of an a priori hypothesis and and an appropriate sample size calculation; the contraception use and possible confounding factors may not have been measured rigorously. Furthermore, the tendency for non‐randomised studies to be conducted in high prevalence settings or among women at high risk may impact the generalizability of the data (Baeten 2007a; Kilmarx 2000; Lavreys 2004).

    Important confounding factors impede accurate interpretation of observational data. These factors include sexual behavior and condom use, which often relate to choice of contraception and self report; a notoriously inaccurate instrument for obtaining reliable data. Studies have found that half to three‐quarters of women with biological evidence of vaginal semen report having no incidence of unprotected intercourse (Mensch 2011). Perhaps even more importantly, few of the available studies were originally designed with the intention to describe the relationship between hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition and are therefore subject to uncontrolled confounding and the statistical problem of multiple comparisons between variables.

    The heterogeneity of observational studies conducted to date indicates an unmet need for consistent evidence on hormonal contraception and risk of HIV. A review of randomised controlled trials specifically targeting hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition is needed to validate current observational data and to provide direction for policy‐makers. We will conduct a systematic review of all randomised controlled trials that assess hormonal contraception use by women exposed to HIV in order to characterize better and provide robust evidence of the relationship between hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition.

    Description of the condition

    Worldwide, 33.3 million adults and children live with HIV with 2.6 million being newly infected (UNAIDS 2010). The proportion of women living with HIV has remained at 52% of the global total, despite wide‐scale efforts to prevent new transmission. Currently, about two thirds of all adult women living with HIV reside in sub‐Saharan Africa (UNAIDS 2010). These are also regions where there are high levels of use of progestogen contraception, particularly DMPA. Most of these women acquire HIV through unprotected heterosexual intercourse. Women exposed to HIV during intercourse undergo seroconversion during the acute phase of the primary infection. Antibodies against HIV can be detected by immunological testing within three months postexposure (CDC 2011). Women, in particular, are vulnerable to HIV exposure and male condoms are often unavailable to act as barriers to sexually transmitted infections (Morrison 2011). Preventing transmission of HIV to uninfected women in serodiscordant relationships or women at a high risk of HIV exposure would not only reduce HIV prevalence in women worldwide, but would reduce vertical transmission in women who become pregnant.

    Description of the intervention

    Modern contraception is a highly effective method to prevent unplanned pregnancy. Modern methods of contraception include sterilization, oral contraceptives, injectables such as depot medroxyprogesterone acetate (DMPA), implants, intrauterine devices (IUDs), male condoms and vaginal barriers (UN 2010). Of these modern methods, hormonal contraceptive methods, including daily oral pills and long‐acting injectables, patches, implants and devices are used by more than 150 million women worldwide (Morrison 2009).

    Hormonal contraceptives prevent pregnancy through several mechanisms that manipulate the female menstrual cycle through exogenous oestrogen, progestogen or both. These mechanisms include blocking ovulation, altering the endometrial lining to inhibit implantation, and thickening vaginal secretions to decrease sperm motility (Shulman 2011). Combined oestrogen and progestogen contraceptives are available in both oral and non‐oral forms. Non‐oral technologies include hormone‐releasing rings and epidermal patches. While combined daily oral contraceptives remain the most commonly used hormonal method of reversible contraception, consistency and accuracy in self medication decrease the overall efficacy for many women (Urdl 2005). 

    Progestogen‐only contraceptives can assume a variety of forms including progestogen‐only pills (POPs), injectable three‐monthly (DMPA) and two‐monthly (norethisterone enanthate ‐ NET‐EN) contraceptives, etonogestrel single rod implants and intrauterine systems containing levonorgestrel (IUSs). Over 50 million women use progestin‐only injectables, mostly DMPA, for contraceptive purposes. In many sub‐Saharan countries, DMPA is the most utilized modern contraceptive (UN 2010). 

    While modern contraceptive methods have greatly reduced the number of unplanned pregnancies worldwide, obstacles still exist in some regions for women who seek contraceptive technologies. In comparison to the high rates of modern contraceptive usage globally, sub‐Saharan Africa lags far behind with a 15.7% prevalence of women using modern methods (Urdl 2005). Cost, availability and access can all pose serious barriers to women in low‐resource settings who seek family planning services. Without these reproductive technologies, unplanned pregnancy could lead to multiple negative health, social and economic outcomes.

    How the intervention might work

    Several epidemiological studies call attention to the possible association between hormonal contraceptive use and an increased risk of HIV acquisition in women (Baeten 2007a; Heffron 2011; Heffron 2012; Kumwenda 2008). The biological plausibility of this association is supported by a number of different mechanisms for increased HIV susceptibility. Hormonal contraceptive suppression of endogenous oestrogen levels (Lawrie 1998), leading to a thinning of the vaginal epithelium, may increase susceptibility to viral transmission (Blish 2011; Stringer 2010). DMPA, specifically, triggers vaginal epithelial thinning similar to that observed in the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle (Mauck 1999). Animal studies have shown a clear association between progestogen administration, vaginal thinning and an increased risk of infection (Marx 1996), all of which are effects that can be reversed by prophylactic treatment with oestrogen (Smith 2004). However, vaginal thinning in humans is less evident than in monkeys, and the clinical significance has been questioned. Hormonal contraceptive use has also been associated with cervical ectopy (Critchlow 1995), which correlates with an increased susceptibility to HIV acquisition (Moss 1991). More recently, it has been suggested that combined oral contraceptives (not DMPA) are associated with cervical ectopy (Bright 2011) and that cervical ectopy may not be associated with increased HIV susceptibility.

    Other models propose suppressive effects on the woman’s immune system through hormonal variation (Hel 2010). These changes have been demonstrated both for exogenous hormone administration and for physiological hormonal fluctuations related to the menstrual cycle. These effects include inhibition of both the cellular and humoral responses as well as increased shedding and viral load within the urogenital tract (Lavreys 2004; Stringer 2010). Other clinical trials suggest that hormonal contraception may decrease the normal vaginal colonization of Lactobacillus, which is protective against HIV (Martin 1999; Miller 2000). Given the availability of current biological data, there is evidence to propose a possible multifactorial biological mechanism for hormonal contraception that may lead to increased susceptibility to HIV.

    Another mechanism whereby hormonal contraceptive use may influence susceptibility to HIV infection is by provoking behavioral changes. For example, vaginal dryness often observed with the use of progestogen contraception may make condom use more uncomfortable. Furthermore, confidence in the effectiveness of the method to prevent pregnancy may influence both sexual behavior and condom use. Fear of infection has been found to be a less effective motivation for condom use than fear of pregnancy (Fleisher 1994).

    The dosage and effect size reported in biological studies using animal models should be recognized. It is common to use a high‐dose exposure to DMPA in HIV prevention studies in non‐human primates in order to increase the risk of infection and the magnitude of the exposure effect on the HIV outcome (Mauck 1999). The effect of hormonal contraception doses in animals analogous to those used for family planning has not, to our knowledge, been tested. 

    Why it is important to do this review

    It is critical to provide safe and effective contraception to women exposed to HIV in order to reduce unintended pregnancies. New and conflicting evidence regarding hormonal contraceptive use and risk for HIV infection creates a global dilemma regarding appropriate advice for women seeking family planning. An international consultation held in Washington DC on 13‐14 December 2011 considered the need for further research on the possible link between hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition, including the need for robust randomised clinical trials.

    Objectives

    To determine, from the best available evidence, the effect of hormonal contraception on HIV acquisition.

    Methods

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We located reports using the Cochrane Fertility Regulation Group trials search strategy.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Fertility Regulation Group Trials Register, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials, MEDLINE/PubMed, EMBASE, LILACS and two registers of ongoing controlled trials (www.clinicaltrials.gov and the Current Controlled Trials MetaRegister). We searched on the following terms: HIV AND (contraception OR hormone*) AND random.

    Searching other resources

    We searched the reference lists of studies located.

    Data collection and analysis

    We took the following references into account: Egger 1997 and Harbord 2006.

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors independently assessed for inclusion all the potential studies we identified as a result of the search strategy. We resolved any disagreement through discussion or, if required, we would consult a third review author or review group editor.

    Data extraction and management

    We designed a form to extract data. For eligible studies, two review authors extracted the data using the agreed form. We would resolve discrepancies through discussion or, if required, we would consult a third review author or review group editor. We entered data into Review Manager 5 software (RevMan 2011) and checked for accuracy.

    When information regarding any of the above is unclear, we would attempt to contact authors of the original reports to provide further details.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors independently assessed risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). We would resolve any disagreement by discussion or by involving a third review author or editor of the review group.

    (1) Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups.

    We would assess the method as:

    • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

    • high risk of bias (any non‐random process, e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number); or

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (2) Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias)

    We would describe for each included study the method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment and will assess whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment.

    We would assess the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered sealed opaque envelopes);

    • high risk of bias (open random allocation; unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes; alternation; date of birth);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (3.1) Blinding of participants and personnel (checking for possible performance bias)

    We would describe for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind study participants and personnel from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We would consider that studies are at low risk of bias if they were blinded, or if we judged that the lack of blinding would be unlikely to affect results. We would assess blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We would assess the methods as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for participants;

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for personnel.

    (3.2) Blinding of outcome assessment (checking for possible detection bias)

    We would describe for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind outcome assessors from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We would assess blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We would assess methods used to blind outcome assessment as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias.

    (4) Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias due to the amount, nature and handling of incomplete outcome data)

    We would describe for each included study, and for each outcome or class of outcomes, the completeness of data including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We would state whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. Where sufficient information was reported, or could be supplied by the trial authors, we would re‐include missing data in the analyses which we undertook.

    We would assess methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data; missing outcome data < 20% and balanced across groups);

    • high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups; ‘as treated’ analysis done with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (5) Selective reporting (checking for reporting bias)

    We would describe for each included study how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found.

    We would assess the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (where it is clear that all of the study’s pre‐specified outcomes and all expected outcomes of interest to the review have been reported);

    • high risk of bias (where not all the study’s pre‐specified outcomes have been reported; one or more reported primary outcomes were not pre‐specified; outcomes of interest are reported incompletely and so cannot be used; study fails to include results of a key outcome that would have been expected to have been reported);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (6) Other bias (checking for bias due to problems not covered by (1) to (5) above)

    We would describe for each included study any important concerns we have about other possible sources of bias.

    We would assess whether each study was free of other problems that could put it at risk of bias:

    • low risk of other bias;

    • high risk of other bias;

    • unclear whether there is risk of other bias.

    (7) Overall risk of bias

    (See Table 8.5c in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011)). We would make explicit judgments about whether studies were at high risk of bias, according to the criteria given in Higgins 2011. With reference to (1) to (6) above, we would assess the likely magnitude and direction of the bias and whether we considered it was likely to impact on the findings. We would explore the impact of the level of bias through undertaking sensitivity analyses (see Sensitivity analysis).

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we would present results as summary risk ratio with 95% confidence intervals.

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we would use the mean difference if outcomes were measured in the same way between trials. We would use the standardized mean difference to combine trials that measured the same outcome, but used different methods.

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cluster‐randomized trials

    We would include cluster‐randomised trials in the analyses along with individually randomised trials. We would adjust theirsample sizes using the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Section 16.3.4 or 16.3.6) using an estimate of the intra cluster correlation co‐efficient (ICC) derived from the trial (if possible), from a similar trial or from a study of a similar population. If we used ICCs from other sources, we would report this and conduct sensitivity analyses to investigate the effect of variation in the ICC. If we identified both cluster‐randomised trials and individually randomised trials, we plan to synthesize the relevant information. We would consider it reasonable to combine the results from both if there was little heterogeneity between the study designs and the interaction between the effect of intervention and the choice of randomisation unit was considered to be unlikely.

    We would also acknowledge heterogeneity in the randomisation unit and perform a sensitivity analysis to investigate the effects of the randomisation unit.

    Cross‐over trials

    In the unlikely event of identifying cross‐over trials, we would use the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions section 16.4 for 'Risk of bias' assessment and analysis.

    Dealing with missing data

    For included studies, we would note levels of attrition. We would explore the impact of including studies with high levels of missing data in the overall assessment of treatment effect by using sensitivity analysis.

    For all outcomes, we would carry out analyses, as far as possible, on an intention‐to‐treat basis, i.e. we would attempt to include all participants randomised to each group in the analyses, and all participants would be analysed in the group to which they were allocated, regardless of whether or not they received the allocated intervention. The denominator for each outcome in each trial would be the number randomised minus any participants whose outcomes were known to be missing.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We would assess statistical heterogeneity in each meta‐analysis using the T², I² and Chi² statistics. We would regard heterogeneity as substantial if the I² statistic was greater than 50% and either T² was greater than zero, or there was a low P value (less than 0.10) in the Chi² test for heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If there were 10 or more studies in the meta‐analysis we would investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We would assess funnel plot asymmetry visually, and use formal tests for funnel plot asymmetry. For continuous outcomes we would use the test proposed by Egger 1997, and for dichotomous outcomes we would use the test proposed by Harbord 2006. If asymmetry was detected in any of these tests or was suggested by a visual assessment, we would perform exploratory analyses to investigate it.

    Data synthesis

    We would carry out statistical analysis using the Review Manager 5 software (RevMan 2011). We would use fixed‐effect meta‐analysis for combining data where it was reasonable to assume that studies were estimating the same underlying treatment effect, i.e. where trials were examining the same intervention and the trials’ populations and methods are judged sufficiently similar. If there was clinical heterogeneity sufficient to expect that the underlying treatment effects differed between trials, or if substantial statistical heterogeneity was detected, we would use random‐effects meta‐analysis to produce an overall summary if an average treatment effect across trials was considered clinically meaningful. We would treat the random‐effects summary as the average range of possible treatment effects and we would discuss the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. If the average treatment effect was not clinically meaningful we would not combine trials.

    If we used random‐effects analyses, we would present the results as the average treatment effect with 95% confidence intervals, and the estimates of T² and the I² statistic.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    If we identified substantial heterogeneity, we would investigate it using subgroup analyses and sensitivity analyses. We would consider whether an overall summary was meaningful, and if it was, use random‐effects analysis to produce it.

    We plan to carry out the following subgroup analyses.

    1. Category of hormonal contraception compared with non‐hormonal/no contraception (e.g. combined, progestogen only, mixed or unclear category).

    2. Selection of participants (e.g. settings with high HIV prevalence; known HIV positive partner; selection mixed or unclear).

    We would use all outcomes in subgroup analysis.

    For fixed‐effect inverse variance meta‐analyses we would assess differences between subgroups by interaction tests. For random‐effects and fixed‐effect meta‐analyses using methods other than inverse variance, we would assess differences between subgroups by inspection of the subgroups’ confidence intervals; non‐overlapping confidence intervals indicate a statistically significant difference in treatment effect between the subgroups.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We would perform sensitivity analysis based on risk of bias by excluding trials without low risk of bias.

    Discussion

    The importance of the question addressed by this review is highlighted by the fact that the WHO summoned a consultation from 31 January to 2 February 2012 to assess the validity of existing evidence on hormonal contraception so that recommendations could be made regarding contraceptive usage for women exposed to HIV. This lead to the publication of a technical statement "Hormonal contraception and HIV"(WHO 2012a) including the following statements:

    "Several biological mechanisms by which individual methods of hormonal contraception could theoretically increase the risk of HIV acquisition, progression, or transmission have been postulated, but it is unclear which (if any) are clinically relevant. Potential mechanisms include alteration of the systemic and local immune response or changes in the genital tract environment. It was noted that different forms of hormonal contraception may change these factors in different ways. ..... Some findings suggest a harmful effect of progestogen, and others suggest no effect, leading to inconsistency in findings. The extent to which data from animal and laboratory studies, including doses used, can be applied to clinical outcomes in humans remains uncertain.

    "In general, most available epidemiological evidence has assessed COCs or progestogen‐only injectable contraceptives (including DMPA and NET‐EN); ...... In total, 20 prospective studies assessed the risk of HIV acquisition among HIV‐negative women using different hormonal contraceptives; ..... Most higher‐quality studies found no statistically significant association between oral contraceptive pill use and HIV acquisition, although point estimates varied and several had limited statistical power (indicated by wide 95% confidence intervals). No currently available studies report a statistically significant association between use of NET‐EN and HIV‐acquisition risk. Evidence on injectables was mixed; some higher quality observational studies reported a significant increase in risk (ranging from a 48% to 100%) of HIV acquisition, other higher‐quality observational studies did not report such an association. All studies had limitations that affected data interpretation. Inconsistencies between point estimates related to injectable contraception were not explained by differences in overall HIV incidence in the study population, primary study objective, study size, number of seroconverters, or the statistical methods used. Other methodological factors, including manner of controlling for potential differences in condom use, length of time between study visits, and analysis of serodiscordant couples could explain part, if not all, of the differences in results from the various studies. These factors merit additional consideration in future analyses. Owing to serious limitations and inconsistency in the data, the quality of the body of evidence on hormonal contraception and HIV acquisition in women was given a GRADE rating of “low”.

    "After detailed, prolonged deliberation, informed by systematic reviews of the available evidence and presentations on biological and animal data, GRADE profile summaries on the strength of the epidemiological evidence, and analysis of risks and benefits to country programmes, the group concluded that the World Health Organization should continue to recommend that there are no restrictions (Medical Eligibility Criteria (MEC) Category 1) on the use of any hormonal contraceptive method for women living with HIV or at high risk of HIV. However, the group recommended that a new clarification (under Category 1) be added to the MEC for women using progestogen‐only injectable contraception at high risk of HIV as follows: Some studies suggest that women using progestogen‐only injectable contraception may be at increased risk of HIV acquisition, other studies do not show this association. A WHO expert group reviewed all the available evidence and agreed that the data were not sufficiently conclusive to change current guidance. However, because of the inconclusive nature of the body of evidence on possible increased risk of HIV acquisition, women using progestogen‐only injectable contraception should be strongly advised to also always use condoms, male or female, and other HIV preventive measures. Expansion of contraceptive method mix and further research on the relationship between hormonal contraception and HIV infection is essential. These recommendations will be continually reviewed in light of new evidence."

    Summary of main results

    No completed randomised trials were identified.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    In the background and discussion we have referred to the evidence from research other than randomised clinical trials. This falls into three categories:

    1. Basic science research: There is compelling evidence that progestogens induce a hypo‐oestrogenic state with thinning of the vaginal mucosa, and that hormonal contraception inhibits the humoral and cell‐mediated immune responses. This provides a plausible biological basis for an effect of hormonal contraception on HIV acquisition. However, whether or not these biological processes translate to clinical effects is not known,

    2. Animal research: It is well known that high‐dose progestogens greatly increase susceptibility to HIV infection in female non‐human primates. The effect of dosages similar to those used for contraception, and the applicability of such effects to human clinical disease, are not known.

    3. Epidemiological research: Data are available from several large observational studies. Such studies are inherently subject to confounding because factors which determine a woman's use of and choice of contraception are likely to be associated with her risk of HIV acquisition. The nature of such interactions are complex and cannot be fully understood, and therefore cannot be adjusted for accurately. These uncertainties are likely to account for the conflicting results from various studies.

    In the absence of evidence from randomized trials, the best evidence available to date is the detailed reviews of observational and other data, reviewed by the WHO consultation referred to under discussion above. The consensus of the panel was that the evidence is inconclusive.

    Quality of the evidence

    None of the evidence available to date is sufficiently reliable to determine whether or not hormonal contraception affects HIV susceptibility.

    N/A

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Recent large and detailed reviews of the available evidence have been conducted (eg Morrison 2009; Curtis 2009; Polis 2013; ), and are in agreement that the evidence to date on DMPA is inconclusive, and on other hormonal contraception is reassuring.
