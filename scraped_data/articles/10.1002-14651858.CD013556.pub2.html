Effects of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease

    • Background

    Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are a major cause of disability and the leading cause of death worldwide. To reduce mortality and morbidity, prevention strategies such as following an optimal diet are crucial. In recent years, low‐gluten and gluten‐free diets have gained strong popularity in the general population. However, study results on the benefits of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet are conflicting, and it is unclear whether a gluten‐reduced diet has an effect on the primary prevention of CVD.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the primary prevention of CVD in the general population.

    Search methods

    We systematically searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL and Web of Science up to June 2021 without language restrictions or restrictions regarding publication status. Additionally, we searched ClinicalTrials.gov for ongoing or unpublished trials and checked reference lists of included studies as well as relevant systematic reviews for additional studies.

    Selection criteria

    We planned to include randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and non‐randomised studies of interventions (NRSIs), such as prospective cohort studies, comparing a low‐gluten or gluten‐free diet or providing advice to decrease gluten consumption with no intervention, diet as usual, or a reference gluten‐intake category. The population of interest comprised adults from the general population, including those at increased risk for CVD (primary prevention). We excluded cluster‐RCTs, case‐control studies, studies focusing on participants with a previous myocardial infarction and/or stroke, participants who have undergone a revascularisation procedure as well as participants with angina or angiographically‐defined coronary heart disease, with a confirmed diagnosis of coeliac disease or with type 1 diabetes.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed eligibility of studies in a two‐step procedure following Cochrane methods. Risk of bias (RoB) was assessed using the Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB2) and the 'Risk Of Bias In Non‐randomised Studies — of Interventions' (ROBINS‐I) tool, and the certainty of evidence was rated using the GRADE approach.

    Main results

    One RCT and three NRSIs (with an observational design reporting data on four cohorts: Health Professionals Follow‐up Study (HPFS), Nurses' Health Study (NHS‐I), NHS‐II, UK Biobank) met the inclusion criteria. The RCT was conducted in Italy (60 participants, mean age 41 ± 12.1 years), two NRSIs (three cohorts, HPFS, NHS‐I, NHS II) were conducted across the USA (269,282 health professionals aged 24 to 75 years) and one NRSI (Biobank cohort) was conducted across the UK (159,265 participants aged 49 to 62 years). Two NRSIs reported that the lowest gluten intake ranged between 0.0 g/day and 3.4 g/day and the highest gluten intake between 6.2 g/day and 38.4 g/day. The NRSI reporting data from the UK Biobank referred to a median gluten intake of 8.5 g/day with an interquartile range from 5.1 g/day to 12.4 g/day without providing low‐ and high‐intake categories.

    Cardiovascular mortality

    From a total of 269,282 participants, 3364 (1.3%) died due to cardiovascular events during 26 years of follow‐up. Low‐certainty evidence may show no association between gluten intake and cardiovascular mortality (adjusted hazard ratio (HR) for low‐ versus high‐gluten intake 1.00, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.95 to 1.06; 2 NRSIs (3 cohorts)).

    All‐cause mortality

    From a total of 159,265 participants, 6259 (3.9%) died during 11.1 years of follow‐up. Very low‐certainty evidence suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake is associated with all‐cause mortality (adjusted HR for low vs high gluten intake 1.00, 95% CI 0.99 to 1.01; 1 NRSI (1 cohort)).

    Myocardial infarction 

    From a total of 110,017 participants, 4243 (3.9%) participants developed non‐fatal myocardial infarction within 26 years. Low‐certainty evidence suggested that gluten intake may not be associated with the development of non‐fatal myocardial infarction (adjusted HR for low versus high gluten intake 0.99, 95% CI 0.89 to 1.10; 1 NRSI (2 cohorts)). Lowering gluten intake by 5 g/day also showed no association on the primary prevention of non‐fatal and fatal myocardial infarction (composite endpoint) in linear dose‐response meta‐analyses (adjusted HR 1.02, 95% CI 0.98 to 1.06; 1 NRSI (2 cohorts)).

    Coronary risk factors 

    Type 2 diabetes

    From a total of 202,114 participants, 15,947 (8.0%) developed type 2 diabetes after a follow‐up between 22 and 28 years. There was low‐certainty evidence that a lower compared with a higher gluten intake may be associated with a slightly increased risk to develop type 2 diabetes (adjusted HR 1.14, 95% CI 1.07 to 1.22; 1 NRSI (3 cohorts)). Furthermore, lowering gluten intake by 5 g/day may be associated with a slightly increased risk to develop type 2 diabetes in linear dose‐response meta‐analyses (adjusted HR 1.12, 95% CI 1.08 to 1.16; 1 NRSI (3 cohorts)).

    Blood pressure, low‐density lipoprotein level, body mass index (BMI)

    After six months of follow‐up, very low‐certainty evidence suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake affects systolic blood pressure (mean difference (MD) ‐6.9, 95% CI ‐17.1 to 3.3 mmHg). There was also no difference between the interventions for diastolic blood pressure (MD ‐0.8, 95% CI ‐5.9 to 4.3 mmHg), low‐density lipoprotein levels (MD ‐0.1, 95% CI ‐0.5 to 0.3 mmol/L) and BMI (MD ‐0.1, 95% CI ‐3.3 to 3.1 kg/m²). 

    No study reported data on adverse events or on other outcomes. Funding sources did not appear to have distorted the results in any of the studies.

    Authors' conclusions

    Very low‐certainty evidence suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake is associated with all‐cause mortality. Our findings also indicate that low‐certainty evidence may show little or no association between gluten intake and cardiovascular mortality and non‐fatal myocardial infarction. Low‐certainty evidence suggested that a lower compared with a higher gluten intake may be associated with a slightly increased risk to develop type 2 diabetes — a major cardiovascular risk factor. For other cardiovascular risk factors it is unclear whether there is a difference between a gluten‐free and normal diet. Given the limited findings from this review predominantly based on observational studies, no recommendations for practice can be made.

    • Effects of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease

    Background

    Cardiovascular diseases are disorders of the heart and blood vessels, including acute coronary and cerebrovascular events (such as myocardial infarction and stroke). Approximately 50% of disabilities resulting from cardiovascular disease are associated with a suboptimal diet. In particular, dietary gluten (a protein found in certain cereal grain) has been linked to a wide range of adverse health outcomes. For example, different gastrointestinal symptoms (such as malabsorption or diarrhoea) often occur in people affected by gluten‐related diseases and the only effective treatment for these populations is an often life‐long gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet. Gluten‐reduced and gluten‐free diets have also gained a strong popularity in the general population. The evidence on the benefits and harms of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet in the general population is, however, contradictory: While gluten avoidance may be associated with disease prevention, there are also concerns that a gluten‐free or gluten‐restricted diet may be suboptimal (due to a reduced consumption of whole‐grain, a dietary key component).

    Review question

    We reviewed the available studies to determine the effects (i.e. health‐related benefits and risks) of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease in the general population.

    Study characteristics

    The evidence is current to June 2021. We identified one randomised controlled trial (RCT) and three non‐randomised studies of interventions (NRSIs) with an observational design. The RCT was conducted in Italy and included 60 healthy adults with a follow‐up of six months. The NRSIs included more than 450,000 participants (either health professionals from the USA or adults (volunteers) from the general population from the UK) and reported a maximum follow‐up of more than 25 years. In the NRSIs, the lowest median gluten intake was 2.6 g/day and the highest was 9.4 g/day (thereby, 1 g/day of gluten intake is equivalent to half a slice of white bread). The RCT compared a gluten‐free with a usual diet.

    Key results

    Our results suggest that it is unclear whether gluten intake is associated with all‐cause mortality. Furthermore, our findings suggest no association between gluten intake and both cardiovascular mortality and non‐fatal myocardial infarction. A lower compared with a higher gluten intake may be associated with a slightly increased risk to develop type 2 diabetes — a major cardiovascular risk factor. These findings were independent of age, ethnicity, body mass index (BMI), family history of diabetes, smoking status, alcohol intake, physical activity, menopausal status and postmenopausal hormone use, oral contraceptive use, multivitamin use, total energy intake, and intakes of magnesium, folic acid and cereal fibre.

    The results from the only published RCT suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake affects systolic blood pressure. Moreover, in this RCT no differences between a gluten‐free and usual diet were found for other cardiovascular risk factors including diastolic blood pressure, low‐density lipoprotein levels and BMI.

    No study reported data on adverse events, quality of life or on other health‐relevant outcomes.

    Certainty of evidence

    The certainty of evidence in this review was low to very low. The studies had several methodological flaws. Taking into account that the majority of the participants from the NRSIs were health professionals, the applicability to the general population is also questionable. Given the limited findings from this review predominantly based on observational studies, no recommendations for practice can be made.

    Implications for practice

    Given the limited findings from this review predominantly based on observational studies, no recommendations for practice can be made.

    Implications for research

    It is highly important to further investigate whether gluten intake is associated with harmful effects. We suggest a randomised study comparing a gluten‐reduced diet with a usual diet considering a wide range of non‐communicable disease outcomes in the general population.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Cardiovascular diseases are disorders of the heart and blood vessels, including (but not limited to) acute coronary and cerebrovascular events such as myocardial infarction and stroke. Cardiovascular diseases are a major cause of disability and the leading cause of death worldwide (GBD 2018). Although there is a large geographical variation, it was estimated that 31% of all global deaths were due to cardiovascular disease in 2016 (WHO 2019). Considering that the world's population increases and becomes older, the prevalence of CVD is expected to increase further in the future (Roth 2015).

    Modifiable and non‐modifiable risk factors for the development of cardiovascular disease have been identified. The most established modifiable risk factors are physical inactivity, obesity, tobacco use, high blood pressure, diabetes, abnormal blood lipids and suboptimal diet (GBD 2018; Hardoon 2008; Mendis 2011). Non‐modifiable factors that can affect the risk for cardiovascular events include higher age, male sex, ethnic background and genetic predisposition (Mendis 2011).

    To decrease mortality and morbidity resulting from cardiovascular disease, early detection and different prevention strategies particularly focusing on modifiable risk factors are crucial. According to the Global Burden of Disease study, a suboptimal diet is the leading risk factor for approximately 50% of disabilities resulting from cardiovascular disease (GBD 2019), and may also explain the varying geographical dispersal of cardiovascular disease (Scarborough 2011; Yusuf 2001). In the lay public, dietary gluten is often linked to adverse health outcomes and, therefore, may be a promising target for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease (Price 2014).

    Description of the intervention

    Gluten (a collective term for a group of storage proteins called prolamins) is found in the endosperm of various cereals such as wheat, barley, rye and oat (Lebwohl 2015). Its peptides (i.e. short chains of amino acids) can enter the mucosa (i.e. a membrane that lines various cavities in the body and covers the surface of internal organs of the small intestine) where it can cause an immune reaction leading to chronic inflammation (Lebwohl 2017).

    Coeliac disease is the most important gluten‐related disease and is defined as a highly complex and long‐term autoimmune disease which often occurs in people who are genetically predisposed (Fasano 2012). Other gluten‐related diseases include non‐coeliac gluten sensitivity, dermatitis herpetiformis, gluten ataxia, and other neurological diseases (Lerner 2019). Gastrointestinal problems, such as malabsorption, diarrhoea, or abdominal distention, often occur in people affected by gluten‐related diseases and the only effective treatment is a life‐long gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet (Fasano 2012; Ludvigsson 2011).

    Beside the indisputable benefits of a strict gluten‐free diet in coeliac disease, gluten‐reduced and gluten‐free diets have also gained strong popularity in the general population. The global gluten‐free food market was estimated to be worth around USD 4.3 billion in 2017 (Financial Times 2017). A national survey in the USA demonstrated that the interest in gluten‐free food products has increased in recent years, and in 2013, every third adult reported that they were trying to minimise or avoid gluten consumption (NPD Group 2013). According to the Gallup Organisation, every fifth American includes gluten‐free foods in their diet (Brenan 2018), even though the prevalence of coeliac disease is only 0.5% in North America (Singh 2018) and approximately 1.0% in the overall Western populations (Dubé 2005). Data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) show that the number of people without coeliac disease who avoid gluten increased “from 0.5% in 2009/10 to 1.7% in 2013/14 in the United States” (Choung 2016). Additionally, 3.7% of the general population in UK are avoiding gluten (Aziz 2014).

    Dietary gluten has been linked to a wide range of adverse health outcomes including inflammation, an increased risk for obesity, neuropsychiatric symptoms and cardiovascular events−independent of coeliac disease (Eaton 2015; Jamnik 2015; Schiltz 2009; Soares 2013; Ehteshami 2018). Moreover, it is argued that a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet prevents or might mitigate several diseases such as fibromyalgia, obesity and endometriosis (Choung 2016; Niland 2018).

    How the intervention might work

    Dietary fibre is a key component of an optimal diet and its consumption is recommended by different dietary guidelines (Lichtenstein 2006). A gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet is typically lower in dietary fibre, B vitamins (such as thiamin, riboflavin, niacin) and micronutrients (such as iron, magnesium, selenium and calcium),and associated with increased risks of micronutrient deficiencies (Dhingra 2012; Kim 2017; Thompson 2000; Niland 2018). In people with coeliac disease, a gluten‐free diet may increase the risk for metabolic syndrome (Ciccone 2019) and adversely affects cardiovascular risk factors including increases in body mass index, total cholesterol, and fasting blood glucose (Potter 2018). Owing to proposed anti‐inflammatory effects by gut microbiome–induced production of short‐chain fatty acids, anti‐oxidant properties and/or hypocholesterolaemic effects that are associated with dietary fibre intake, a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet may be suboptimal for the primary prevention of cardiovascular diseases (Anderson 2009; Jonnalagadda 2011; Melini 2019; Potter 2018). Moreover, a recent investigation of data from nearly 40,000 participants of the UK Biobank suggests that gluten intake is associated with metabolic health markers, but all associations were weak and not clinically meaningful (Behrendt 2021a).

    Why it is important to do this review

    The evidence on the benefits of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet is contradictory: there is a rising trend to avoid gluten owing to its proposed adverse health outcomes (Miller 2016), but there are also concerns that a gluten‐free or gluten‐restricted diet may be suboptimal (due to a reduced consumption of whole‐grain, a dietary key component (Niland 2018; Kim 2017)).

    Currently, there is limited evidence regarding the consumption of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet (except for the management of gluten‐related diseases). For example, the long‐term gluten consumption was not associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease in the Nurses' Health Study (NHS; NHS I and NHS II) with a follow‐up of 26 years (Lebwohl 2017). International guidelines make also no recommendations for the intake of gluten to prevent cardiovascular disease or improve cardiovascular risk factors. A systematic review assessing the effects (i.e. health‐related benefits and risks) of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet in people without coeliac disease will, therefore, provide evidence whether this dietary approach is efficient to prevent adverse cardiovascular outcomes.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects (i.e. health‐related benefits and risks) of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease in the general population, including those at increased risk of cardiovascular disease.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered the following study designs.

    • Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) with randomisation at the individual level, including cross‐over trials

    • Non‐randomised studies of interventions (NRSIs), including:

      • quasi‐randomised studies (controlled studies using inappropriate strategies of randomly allocating interventions)

      • cohort studies (preferably longitudinal cohorts)

      • controlled before‐and‐after studies

      • interrupted time series

    We included NRSIs because we were interested in health‐related effects and associations of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet. Although NRSIs are generally at a higher risk of bias than RCTs, we included these study types owing to a perceived higher directness than RCTs. For example, NRSIs (particularly prospective cohort studies) of health behaviours may include longer durations of follow‐up and outcome data (e.g. cardiovascular diseases and risk factors) than those which become apparent in the short term within RCTs.

    NRSI were defined as any quantitative studies estimating the association of an intervention or exposure with a health outcome of interest that do not use randomisation to allocate individuals to different groups. This includes studies in which allocation occurs in the course of usual treatment decisions or according to peoples’ choices (often called observational studies). Case‐control studies were excluded due to their retrospective nature.

    We included studies reported as full‐text, those published as abstract only, and unpublished data. Cluster‐RCTs were not considered because they carry a higher risk of allocation bias and the intervention of interest (gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet) is neither complex nor allocated to entire groups of people (Giraudeau 2009; Hahn 2005).

    Types of participants

    We included adults (aged 18 years or older) from the general population, including those at increased risk for cardiovascular diseases (e.g. adults with modifiable risk factors such as hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, overweight/obesity or type 2 diabetes).

    The following participants were excluded.

    • Those who have experienced a previous myocardial infarction or stroke, or both

    • Those who have undergone a revascularisation procedure (coronary artery bypass grafting or percutaneous transluminal coronary angioplasty)

    • Those with angina or angiographically‐defined coronary heart disease

    • Those with a confirmed diagnosis of coeliac disease

    • Those with type 1 diabetes (owing to the fact that this patient population presents an overall higher risk for coeliac disease)

    Studies including a heterogeneous study population (e.g. adults with and without comorbidity as mentioned above) were considered when they provided subgroup analyses or the proportion of the eligible predefined population exceeded 80%. If studies did not provide information on the subset of eligible participants or the proportion of eligible participants did not exceed 80%, we intended to contact the trialists for more details.

    Types of interventions

    We included RCTs and NRSI comparing the provision of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet or providing recommendations to decrease gluten consumption (i.e. low or a lower gluten intake (using the definitions of the study authors)) with no intervention or a usual diet.

    We included follow‐up (i.e. observation period, duration of intervention) of 12 weeks or longer, because this time period would be sufficient to estimate behavioural changes, sustained adherence and potential effect changes for public health interventions. Follow‐up was defined as the time elapsed since the start of the intervention/exposure measurement.

    To avoid confounding, we excluded studies addressing other dietary co‐interventions (e.g. low‐carbohydrate diet, Mediterranean diet, very low‐calorie diet) or multifactorial interventions (such as lifestyle interventions or exercise), or both.

    Types of outcome measures

    Outcome data were assessed at the longest available follow‐up (with a minimum follow‐up/observation time of 12 weeks, see above). In case a published report did not report on one of these outcomes, we intended to access the protocol and/or contact the authors to ascertain whether the outcomes were measured but not reported. Furthermore, we planned to include relevant studies measuring these outcomes but not reporting the data at all, or not in a usable format as part of a narrative description.

    We included studies that assessed any of the primary and secondary outcomes listed below.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Fatal endpoints

      1. Cardiovascular mortality (e.g. fatal myocardial infarction)

      2. All‐cause mortality

    2. Non‐fatal endpoints (assessed as number of participants with at least one event during follow‐up)

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following electronic databases on 22 June 2021.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (Cochrane Library 2021, Issue 6)

    • Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, MEDLINE Daily and MEDLINE (Ovid, 1946 to 21 June 2021)

    • Embase (Ovid, 1980 to 2021 week 24)

    • CINAHL Plus (EBSCOHost, 1937 to 22 June 2021)

    • Web of Science Core Collection (Clarivate Analytics, 1900 to 22 June 2021)

    Search strategies for each database are available in Appendix 1.

    As part of the search strategy, RCT and observational filters have been used. The RCT filter used for MEDLINE was the Cochrane sensitivity‐maximising RCT filter, while for Embase, terms as recommended in the Cochrane Handbook have been applied (Lefebvre 2019). For Web of Science, an adaptation of the Cochrane RCT filter was used and for CINAHL the Cochrane CINAHL RCT filter was applied (Glanville 2019).

    We used search filters for observational studies provided by the Ovid Expert Search for MEDLINE and Embase (Ovid Resource Center). For Web of Science, an adaptation of the Ovid filter was applied and for CINAHL, the SIGN observational studies filter was applied (SIGN).

    We searched all databases from their inception to the present, and we imposed no restriction on publication language or status.

    Searching other resources

    We checked reference lists of all included studies and any relevant systematic reviews for additional studies. We also screened any relevant retraction statements and errata for relevant studies. Furthermore, we searched websites of manufacturers producing gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free food products (as listed by the Celiac Disease Foundation, celiac.org) for additional data. However, no other relevant references were identified.

    We searched ClinicalTrials.gov (www.ClinicalTrials.gov) for ongoing or unpublished trials using the following keywords: “gluten reduced” OR “gluten‐free” OR gluten restricted” OR “low gluten” on 12 March 2021. However, no ongoing or unpublished study met the inclusion criteria of this review. We also planned to search the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (apps.who.int/trialsearch), however, due to the COVID‐19 pandemic, this platform has not been available.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (DG, SL or AE) independently screened titles and abstracts of all the potential relevant studies/references identified by the searches. These studies/references were coded as 'retrieve' (eligible or potentially eligible/unclear) or 'do not retrieve' (excluded, not meeting inclusion criteria). In a second step, the full‐text reports/publications of the studies coded as 'retrieve' were screened (independently by the same review authors who performed the title and abstract screening) for eligibility, reasons for exclusion were documented. Any disagreements were resolved through discussion or, if required, a third author was consulted (LS, CS). Duplicates and collated multiple reports of the same study were labelled so that each study rather than each report was the unit of interest in this review. The selection process was recorded by completing a PRISMA flow diagram and 'Characteristics of excluded studies during full‐text screening' were documented (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    We used a data collection form for study characteristics and outcome data which was piloted using one eligible study. Two review authors (CS, LS) extracted the following study characteristics and outcome data, if available.

    • Study information data: study design, total duration, sample size of each group, number of participants lost to follow‐up/withdrawn, number of participants analysed, number of study centres and location, study setting, and date of study

    • Participants: mean age, age range, gender, other baseline data (e.g. health status, smoking history, cardiovascular risk, BMI), inclusion criteria and exclusion criteria

    • Type of intervention: duration, frequency and dosage of the intervention (e.g. recorded by food frequency questionnaires), details on the comparison (e.g. gluten intake category)

    • Type of outcomes: primary and secondary outcomes specified and collected, and time points reported

    • Adjusted and unadjusted outcome measures (for NRSIs)

    • Confounders: uncontrolled confounders and list of variables authors have included in analyses for adjusted estimates

    • Methods used to control for confounders

    • Notes: funding and notable conflicts of interest of study authors

    Disagreements were resolved by consensus. One review author (CS) transferred data into the Review Manager 5.4 file (Review Manager 2014). The transferred data were double‐checked, i.e. data that were entered into the Review Manager 5.4 (by CS) were compared with the data included in the data extraction form by a second review author (LS or AE).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Randomised controlled trials

    Risk of bias in randomised controlled trials (RCTs) was assessed using version two of the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool (RoB2), as outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2019a).  Of interest of this review was the effect of the assignment to the intervention (the intention‐to‐treat (ITT) effect), thus, we performed all risk of bias assessments on this effect. Two review authors (CS, LS) independently assessed the risk of bias of specific results of a trial according to the following domains.

    • Bias arising from the randomisation process

    • Bias due to deviations from intended interventions 

    • Bias due to missing outcome data

    • Bias in measurement of the outcome

    • Bias in selection of the reported result

    We used the signalling questions in the RoB2 tool and rated each domain as 'low risk of bias', 'some concerns' or 'high risk of bias'. The overall risk of bias for the result was the least favourable assessment across the domains of bias. 

    Non‐randomised studies of interventions

    Risk of bias for NRSIs was assessed using the 'Risk Of Bias In Non‐randomised Studies — of Interventions' (ROBINS‐I, version 2016), taking into account the following domains (Sterne 2016; Sterne 2019).

    • Bias due to confounding

    • Bias in selection of participants into the study

    • Bias in classification of the intervention (exposure)

    • Bias due to deviations from the intended intervention (exposure; the effect of interest is the effect of assignment to the intervention (exposure) at baseline)

    • Bias due to missing data

    • Bias in measurement of outcomes

    • Bias in selection of the reported result

    These domains were judged as being at ‘low’, ‘moderate’, ‘serious’, or ‘critical’ risk of bias and reasons for each bias classification were documented. We also used ROBINS‐I to predefine the review question, confounding domains, and co‐interventions/exposures.

    • Review question: is a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet effective for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease?

    • Confounding factors: the following confounders were defined a‐priori being relevant to interfere with all or most outcomes: age (i.e. advanced age, 65 years or above), gender, smoking status, education/socioeconomic status, energy intake (defined as kcal/day; i.e. a difference in 200 kcal/day across study arms), BMI, history of hypertension, history of hyperlipidaemia, medication intake (e.g. antihypertensives, aspirin, statins) and physical activity.

    • Co‐interventions: to avoid confounding, we excluded studies addressing other dietary co‐interventions (e.g. low‐carbohydrate diet, Mediterranean diet, very low‐calorie diet) or multifactorial interventions (such as those including lifestyle interventions or exercise), or both. Other co‐interventions were not (yet) identified.

    To illustrate risk of bias judgements for RCTs and NRSIs, we used the robvis tool (McGuinness 2021) to create traffic light plots. 

    Assessment of bias in conducting the systematic review

    We conducted the review according to a published protocol and any deviations from it are reported in the Differences between protocol and review section within this systematic review.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous outcomes (i.e. fatal endpoints and myocardial infarction), we planned to summarise the effect using risk ratios (RRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs). Since the NRSIs (NRSIs) (Behrendt 2021b; Lebwohl 2017; Zong 2018) used Cox proportional hazard models to calculate hazard ratios (HRs), and not providing the number of 'non‐cases' we could not calculate the corresponding risk ratio. Instead, we used the HR, see also Differences between protocol and review.

    For continuous outcomes collected on the same scale, we intended to pool the effect using a mean difference (MD) with corresponding standard deviation (SD) and 95% CI; for continuous outcomes collected with different scales (e.g. quality of life), we planned to use a standardised MD (SMD) with 95% CI. We intended to interpret the magnitude of the SMD using the approach suggested by Cohen 1988: (i) small/minor SMD: 0.2 or less; (ii) medium SMD: 0.2 to 0.8; (iii) large SMD: 0.8 or greater.

    We applied a clinically minimal important differences (clinically MID) to aid the interpretation of the effect measures. For example, for systolic blood pressure and diastolic blood pressure a clinically MID of 2 mmHg (Ettehad 2015; Lewington 2002), and for low‐density lipoprotein levels a clinically MID of 0.5 mmol/L (Silverman 2016), was regarded as relevant.

    Both adjusted and unadjusted outcomes were extracted, however, we only referred to adjusted effect measures in our analyses. If more than one adjusted effect measure was reported, we selected the effect estimate which had been adjusted for most confounders.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We would have collected data on studies irrespective of whether individuals or groups were allocated to an intervention or control group. The analysis would have considered the level at which the allocation occurred for the same outcome (Higgins 2019b). This approach was not necessary in this systematic review as no unit of analysis issue was present (the unit of analysis was the individual participant).

    Dealing with missing data

    We planned to contact study authors, investigators or sponsors to verify key study characteristics and obtain missing numerical outcome data if necessary (e.g. when a study was identified as an abstract only). If this approach was not successful, we intended to input missing data using appropriate statistical methods (McKenzie 2019). Where this was not possible (and the missing data were thought to introduce bias), we intended to explore the impact of including such studies in the overall assessment by conducting a sensitivity analysis.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We used the I² statistic to measure heterogeneity across studies in each analysis. Additionally, we considered the chi² test and visually inspected the forest plots for signs of heterogeneity (e.g. non‐overlapping CI). If substantial heterogeneity had been identified, it would have been reported and explored by conducting prespecified subgroup analysis.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If more than 10 studies had been identified, funnel plots would have been conducted to explore possible small‐study biases (Higgins 2019c).

    Data synthesis

    Statistical analysis was conducted using Review Manager 5.4 (Review Manager 2014), and Stata software version 15.1 (StataCorp 2017).

    For NRSIs, the following meta‐analyses were performed, and summary risks based on a random‐effects model were estimated per outcome.

    • Low‐ versus high‐gluten intake category (in NRSIs): In two (Lebwohl 2017; Zong 2018) of the three NRSIs, the lowest intake category of gluten was defined as the reference category (i.e., HR = 1). For our analyses, we converted the ratios of the highest intake category (used in the NRSIs) into ratios corresponding to the lowest intake category using the model described in Schlesinger 2019. This method allowed us to investigate the association of a gluten‐reduced (or gluten‐free) diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease. The highest intake category provided in the NRSIs was used as our reference.

    • Dose‐response meta‐analyses (in NRSIs).

      • We investigated the association between gluten intake as a continuous variable by performing a linear dose‐response meta‐analysis as described in Orsini 2006 and Greenland 1992. Study‐specific slopes (linear trends) and 95% CIs from the natural logs of the HRs (and CIs) across the gluten‐intake categories were computed for outcomes providing these data (Table 1).

      • We also explored whether there is a non‐linear dose‐response relationship between gluten intake (as a continuous variable) and the development of cardiovascular disease. Cubic spline regression models were performed and non‐linearity was evaluated using a likelihood ratio test (Durrleman 1989). Cubic splines for each study with more than three categories of gluten intake were calculated, using three fixed knots at 10%, 50%, and 90% through the total distribution of the reported intake, and combined them using multivariable meta‐analysis for outcomes providing these data (Table 1).

      • These analyses require at least three exposure categories: (i) the quantified exposure value (mean or medians of gluten intake for each category), (ii) effect estimates with respective 95% CIs, and (iii) the number of cases and person‐years. If studies did not report on distribution of person‐years in single categories but provided information on number of cases and total person‐years across all categories, or number of total participants plus follow‐up period, we estimated the missing information as previously described (Aune 2012; Chene 1996).

      • The summary risk ratios of the linear dose–response meta‐analyses were presented for each decrement of 5 g/day of gluten intake (Lebwohl 2017). The ratios of the highest intake category (used in the NRSIs) were converted into ratios corresponding to the lowest intake category using the model described in Schlesinger 2019 (see above).

    We planned to carry out the following subgroup analyses for all primary and secondary outcomes listed under Types of outcome measures.

    If sufficient numbers of studies were available, we would have performed sensitivity analyses to explore whether methodological heterogeneity is a result of different risks of bias. We would have done this by repeating meta‐analysis after exclusion of the following.

    • Studies in which missing data could not be reliably replaced

    Owing to the low number of studies available, these analyses could not be performed.

Follow‐up assessments took place every four years up to the end of follow‐up: in 2012 (to estimate the risk for cardiovascular outcomes , follow‐up 26 years), and in 2012/2013 (to evaluate the risk for type 2 diabetes, follow‐up between 22 and 28 years). For participants who were diagnosed with (i) coronary heart disease, (ii) cancer, or (iii) diabetes during the follow‐up, the cumulative average gluten value before the diagnosis was carried forward until the end of follow‐up. Among 123,600 participants (73,666 women(NHS‐I) and 49,934 men (HPFS)) who completed the FFQ, 110,017 (89.0%) participants (64,714 (87.8%) women and 45,303 (90.7%) men) were eligible for analysis to estimate the incidence of cardiovascular outcomes. To estimate the risk of type 2 diabetes, 230,892 participants (179,362 women (NHS‐I, NHS‐II) and 51,530 men (HPFS)) completed the FFQ and 202,114 (87.5%) participants (160,206 (89.3%) women and 41,908 (81.3%) men) were included in the analysis. 

    The multicentre (22 centres) NRSI by Behrendt 2021b is based on data from the UK Biobank (UK Biobank 2007; UK Biobank 2009) and was initiated in 2006. The study investigated the association between dietary gluten intake and all‐cause mortality, as well as cardiovascular mortality (including ischaemic heart disease mortality, stroke mortality, and others [not further defined]). Baseline assessment took place between the years 2006 and 2010 and included a self‐completed touch screen questionnaire, a personal interview, and physical measurements in an initial cohort of more than 500,000 participants.  After applying predefined exclusion criteria, 159,265 participants (median (interquartile range (IQR)) age: 57 (49 to 62) years, 52.1% being female) were analysed after a median follow‐up of 11.1 (10.6 to 11.9) years.  

    The RCT by Campagna 2020 was conducted from November 2016 to March 2017 in Italy. In total, 60 healthy normal‐weighted participants were included, there from, 30 participants (15 female) were randomised to an eucaloric gluten‐free diet, and 30 participants (15 female) to an eucaloric control diet for six months. Amongst other outcomes (which are not relevant for our review question), cardiovascular risk factors (blood pressure, low‐density lipoprotein level, BMI) before and after the diet were recorded.

    Ascertainment of gluten intake

    The NRSIs by Lebwohl 2017 and  Zong 2018 assessed diet in the “previous year” was by using a validated FFQ. Baseline assessment took place in 1986 (HPFS), in 1984 and 1986 (NHS‐I), and in 1991 (NHS‐II). After completing the baselineZong 2018 FFQ, participants were excluded if they reported (i) implausible daily energy intake (< 800 or > 4200 kcal); (ii) missing gluten data; (iii) a previous diagnosis of myocardial infarction, angina, stroke or cancer (iv) a previous coronary artery bypass graft surgery; (v) a previous diagnosis of coeliac disease; (vi) a previous diagnosis of diabetes (this exclusion criteria was only applied in the cohorts evaluating risk of type 2 diabetes (Zong 2018)); and (vii) missing age data. Moreover, participants were excluded if they only completed the baseline questionnaire or if a diagnosis of coeliac disease was reported during follow‐up.  To assess gluten intake, participants were asked how often they consumed gluten containing food items within the previous year (assessment took place at baseline and then every four years using the validated FFQ, see above). Gluten intake was estimated based on predefined gluten containing ingredients including wheat, wheat flour, rye, rye flour, barley, barley malt flour, cooked cereal, bulgur, couscous, farina, beer and pasta (Willett 1997).  These diet ingredients were identified by product labels and other information provided by manufacturers of commercially prepared foods, and by recipes from cookbooks in home‐made foods. Gluten consumption per food source (serving) were quantified by multiplying the serving size with the number of gluten containing ingredients per serving (using the conservative estimate of 75% defined by Andrén Aronsson 2016; Kasarda 2013). Furthermore, participants were divided into five gluten intake categories using the method defined by Willett 1986.  The level of gluten intake was provided in g/d in each study (thereby an increase in gluten intake by 1 g/d is equivalent to half a slice of white bread). In the study of Lebwohl 2017 (evaluating cardiovascular outcomes) with 26 years of follow‐up, the lowest median gluten intake category in g/day ranged between (median, range) 2.8 (0.0 to 3.4, NHS‐I) and 3.5 (0.0 to 4.3, HPFS). Whereas the highest gluten intake category in g/day ranged between 7.1 (6.2 to 26.7, NHS‐I) and 9.4 (8.1 to 38.4, HPFS).  In the study of Zong 2018 (evaluating type 2 diabetes as cardiovascular risk factor) with up to 28 years of follow‐up, the lowest gluten intake in g/day ranged between (median, range) 2.6 (0.0 to 3.2, NHS‐I), 3.6 (0.0 to 4.3, NHS‐II) and 3.5 (0.0 to 4.3, HPFS). Whereas the highest gluten intake category ranged between 6.8 (5.9 to 23.6, NHS‐I), 8.8 (7.7 to 27.0, NHS‐II) and 9.4 (8.1 to 33.5, HPFS). 

    Behrendt 2021b used a web‐based dietary questionnaire (Oxford WebQ, UK Biobank 2007) to gather information on the previous 24‐hour dietary intakes.  After completing this assessment, participants were excluded if they reported suspected or confirmed pregnancy, self‐reported history of malabsorption/coeliac disease, missing lifestyle risk factors (i.e. smoking status, alcohol intake, and/or physical activity), missing socioeconomic factors (i.e. income, the highest qualification, ethnic background), and missing data for percentage body fat.  Gluten intake was estimated based on predefined gluten containing ingredients (Behrendt 2021a), and gluten intake was estimated using the conservative approach also described in  Lebwohl 2017 and Zong 2018 (see above). In contrast to Lebwohl 2017 and Zong 2018, Behrendt 2021b did not provide gluten intake categories defining low‐ and high‐gluten intake in g/day, instead a median gluten intake of 8.5 g/day with an interquartile range from 5.1 to 12.4 g/day was reported.

    In the RCT by Campagna 2020 participants in the gluten‐free diet group received a complete supply of gluten‐free products for six months (pasta, bread, cookies, and flour). The adherence to the gluten‐free diet was assessed with a validated score developed by Biagi 2009. Moreover, participants were instructed to keep a food diary (before the start of the intervention, then daily, and after six months) to monitor dietary changes.  Participants who did not follow a strict gluten‐free diet were excluded (the study authors, however, did not report on any excluded participants during the study).  The participants in the control group received a complete supply of analogous products containing gluten for 6 months. Moreover, each participant was instructed on foods containing gluten or potentially containing gluten, and was asked to not consume gluten outside the diet/food assigned.

    Ascertainment of outcomes

    For all participants in the NRSIs of Lebwohl 2017 who reported cardiovascular outcomes, medical records were obtained. Myocardial infarction was defined after the World Health Organization criteria. The study authors considered definite and probable myocardial infarction together (Li 2015). 

    Participants in the NRSI of Zong 2018 who reported type 2 diabetes received a validated questionnaire by post (Hu 2001). Zong 2018 reported that the diagnosis of type 2 diabetes was confirmed if at least one of the following events was reported according to the National Diabetes Data Group 1979 criteria: (i) one or more classic symptoms (excessive thirst, polyuria or frequent urination, weight loss, hunger) plus fasting plasma glucose ≥7.8 mmol/L or random plasma glucose levels ≥11.1 mmol/L; (ii) ≥2 elevated plasma glucose concentrations on different occasions (fasting glucose ≥7.8 mmol/L, random plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L and/or plasma glucose ≥11.1 mmol/L after ≥2 hours shown by oral glucose tolerance testing) in the absence of symptoms; or (iii) treatment with hypoglycaemic medication. The diagnostic criteria changed in June 1998, and a fasting plasma glucose of 7.0 mmol/L was considered as threshold for the diagnosis of diabetes”(Zong 2018).

    In the NRSI of  Behrendt 2021b, mortality data including the date and underlying primary cause of death were provided by the National Health Service (NHS) Information Centre for participants from England and Wales and by NHS Central Register, Scotland for participants from Scotland. International Statistical Classification of Diseases and Related Health Problems, Tenth Revision (ICD‐10) codes were used. 

    In the RCT by Campagna 2020 cardiovascular risk factors (i) systolic blood pressure (mmHg), (ii) diastolic blood pressure (mmHg), (iii) low‐density lipoprotein level (mmol/L) and BMI (kg/m²; weight and height were recorded while participants were wearing light clothing and no shoes) were measured at baseline and after six months (i.e. after the intervention). 

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 97 references during full‐text screening. The main reason for exclusion was a wrong population (64 references). Other reasons for exclusion were absence of outcome of interest, wrong study design, wrong intervention or follow‐up less than 12 weeks. Further details are shown in Figure 1 and the Characteristics of excluded studies table.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We used the ROBINS‐I tool to assess risk of bias per outcome (cardiovascular mortality, all‐cause mortality, non‐fatal myocardial infarction, cardiovascular risk factor (type 2 diabetes)) for NRSIs. None of the domains across outcomes and studies showed a "critical" risk of bias (cardiovascular mortality (Appendix 2), all‐cause mortality (Appendix 3), non‐fatal myocardial infarction (Appendix 4), cardiovascular risk factor type 2 diabetes (Appendix 5)). Most bias domains were judged with a "moderate" risk of bias. Although a variety of dietary and non‐dietary confounding factors (covariates) were considered in these three NRSIs (Table 2), it cannot be fully excluded that our findings are attributable to unknown confounders rather than to the effects of the exposure. Whereas the overall risk of bias across outcomes was judged to be "moderate" in the studies of Lebwohl 2017 and Zong 2018, it was judged with "serious" in Behrendt 2021b. The overall "serious" risk of bias in Behrendt 2021b mainly results from concerns that are based on the classification of the exposure. Our bias judgements are also illustrated by traffic light plots (cardiovascular mortality (Figure 2), all‐cause mortality (Figure 3), non‐fatal myocardial infarction (Figure 4), type 2 diabetes (Figure 5 )).

    Cardiovascular mortality 

    Cardiovascular mortality was reported in two non‐randomised studies of interventions (NRSIs) (Behrendt 2021b; Lebwohl 2017) including 269,282 participants. Lebwohl 2017 included a total of 110,017 participants (59% female), of which 2286 (2.1%) died due to myocardial infarction.  Behrendt 2021b reported that from a total of 159,265 participants (52.1% female), 1,078 (0.7%) died due to different cardiovascular diseases including ischaemic heart disease and stroke. 

    Low‐ versus high‐gluten intake category

    Low‐certainty evidence may show no association between gluten intake and cardiovascular mortality (adjusted hazard ratio (HR) 1.00, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.95 to 1.06; 2 NRSIs (3 cohorts), unclear number of participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1).

    All‐cause mortality 

    All‐cause mortality was reported in one NRSI (Behrendt 2021b). In total, 6259 (3.9%) deaths occurred in the cohort including 159,265 participants.  

    Low‐ versus high‐gluten intake category

    It is unclear whether gluten intake is associated with all‐cause mortality (adjusted HR 1.00, 95% CI 0.99 to 1.01; 1 NRSI (1 cohort), unclear number of participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2).

    No data for dose‐response analyses were available.

    Myocardial infarction

    Non‐fatal myocardial infarction was reported in one NRSI (Lebwohl 2017). From a total of 110,017 participants (59% female), 4,243 (3.9%) participants developed this condition.

    Low versus high gluten intake category

    Low‐certainty evidence suggested that gluten intake may not be associated with the development of non‐fatal myocardial infarction adjusted HR 0.99, 95% CI 0.89 to 1.10; 1 NRSI (2 cohorts), 43,798 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3).

    Dose‐response analysis

    Lebwohl 2017 also reported data for a dose‐response analyses combining cases of non‐fatal and fatal myocardial infarction.  The effect estimate of the linear dose–response meta‐analysis for lowering gluten intake by 5 g/day showed no association with fatal and non‐fatal myocardial infarction (adjusted HR 1.02, 95% CI 0.98 to 1.06; 1 NRSI (2 cohorts); Analysis 2.1). A non‐linear dose‐response analysis also showed no association when a higher gluten intake was compared with a reference dose of 3.1 g/day, although there may be some indication for non‐linearity (P for non‐linearity = 0.01; Figure 7).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    In three non‐randomised studies of interventions (NRSIs) with a prospective observational design and a maximum follow‐up of more than 25 years, very low‐certainty evidence suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake is associated with all‐cause mortality. Furthermore, our findings indicate that low‐certainty evidence may show little or no association between gluten intake and cardiovascular mortality and non‐fatal myocardial infarction. Low‐certainty evidence (from one NRSI) suggested that a lower compared with a higher gluten intake may be associated with a slight increased risk to develop type 2 diabetes — a major cardiovascular risk factor. These findings were independent of age, ethnicity, body mass index (BMI), family history of diabetes, smoking status, alcohol intake, physical activity, menopausal status and postmenopausal hormone use, oral contraceptive use, multivitamin use, total energy intake, alternative healthy eating index (including food intake, vegetable intake, carbohydrate intake (Chiuve 2012)), and intakes of magnesium, folic acid and cereal fibre. One randomised controlled trial (RCT) with 60 participants suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake affects systolic blood pressure (very low‐certainty evidence). This RCT found also no difference between the interventions (gluten‐free diet versus usual diet) for other cardiovascular risk factors including diastolic blood pressure, low‐density lipoprotein levels and BMI. No study reported data on adverse events or on other outcomes. Funding sources do not appear to have distorted the results in any of the studies.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The current review question is focused on whether there is a causal relation between diet and cardiovascular diseases. We identified one RCT and three NRSIs (reporting data from four cohorts). Considering the fact that the RCT included only 60 participants (the NRSIs more than 450,000 participants), and the two study types reported on different outcomes, most of the identified evidence (which is based on the NRSIs) is interpreted as associations (not effects). Taking into account that the majority of the participants from the NRSIs were health professionals from the USA who may be better educated about disease risks than the general population, and that they are the ones who are more careful about their diet, the applicability of the evidence to the general population may be limited. Furthermore, the major gluten sources for citizens from the USA and UK are wheat products, generalising the evidence to populations who primarily consume other dietary gluten sources (for example rye) seems inappropriate. Moreover, the gluten content of food items was estimated by the authors of the primary NRSIs and was based on ingredients containing wheat, rye or barley. Other (minor) gluten sources such as gluten in some brand of soy sauce were not considered (Lebwohl 2015; Kobayashi 2004). Taking into account that the quantity of gluten in these food items is low, it most likely can be neglected ‐ unless when a strict gluten‐free diet is investigated. The general population in Western countries consumes between 10 g/day to 20 g/day of gluten — an increase in gluten intake by 1 g/day is equivalent to half a slice of white bread. The highest gluten intake reported in the NRSIs was 9.4 g/day (median). This rather “low” gluten consumption may be insufficient to observe long‐term physiological changes or gluten‐related associations on cardiovascular diseases.

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, for the outcomes cardiovascular mortality, all‐cause mortality and myocardial infarction, certainty of evidence was very low to low suggesting that a gluten‐reduced diet (low gluten intake) has little to no effect on the prevention of coronary heart disease. Reasons for downgrading the certainty were (i) risk of bias (primarily due to a moderate risk of bias for confounding, a moderate risk of bias in the selection of participants into the study, a moderate or serious risk of bias in the classification of the exposure, and a moderate risk of bias in selection of the reported results) and (ii) indirectness (due to middle‐aged USA nurses and health professionals reducing applicability to the decision context and the fact that the major gluten sources for citizens from the USA and UK are wheat products, generalising the evidence to populations who primarily consume other dietary gluten sources (e.g. rye)). For type 2 diabetes (a cardiovascular risk factor), the certainty of evidence was considered as low suggesting that a gluten‐reduced diet (low‐gluten intake) may be associated with a slightly increased risk of type 2 diabetes. Reasons for downgrading the certainty of this outcome were also risk of bias and indirectness. Other cardiovascular risk factors such as systolic blood pressure, low‐density protein level and BMI were evaluated in a single RCT including 60 participants. Very low‐certainty evidence suggested that it is unclear whether gluten intake affects these cardiovascular risk factors. The evidence was very uncertain due to concerns regarding risk of bias and imprecision (particularly due the very low sample size).

    Potential biases in the review process

    The strengths of this review are the large sample sizes and extensive accounting for confounding factors (in all three NRSIs) and long follow‐up duration (over 20 years) with repeated measurement of exposure (in two NRSIs). Furthermore, in these two NRSIs cumulative average intake of gluten was used to reflect long‐term gluten intake, minimising random variation and changes of diet over time. Overall, the study findings were consistent across the cohorts including both adult men and women. Although we excluded studies focusing on participants who reported a diagnosis of coeliac disease, we could not explore whether some study participants maintained a very low‐ or gluten‐free diet. But we believe that the long observation period (starting in 1984/1986 /1991) may have preceded the popular gluten reduction that has arisen more recently.

    One major limitation, however, is the possibility of residual or unmeasured confounding related to all observational studies. Hence, the observed association between a gluten‐reduced diet and a higher risk for type 2 diabetes could be attributable to residual confounding rather than the exposure. A reduced gluten intake is related with a lower cereal fibre intake (Zong 2018), and possibly with a lower intake of other beneficial nutrients affecting the risk for type 2 diabetes as well as other non‐communicable diseases (Reynolds 2019). Therefore, the correlated nature of gluten intake and cereal fibre adds complexity to the interpretation of our results. Considering that gluten is usually found in whole grain food that is also rich in fibre, the association between gluten intake, other (often beneficial) nutrient intake and the development of different non‐communicable diseases needs to be further established (Maki 2014).

    Only one RCT with a very small sample size addressing cardiovascular risk factors was identified emphasising the need of controlled trials in this research area. Results from RCTs would also allow us to report actual effects rather than associations of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet for the prevention of cardiovascular disease.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    This is the first systematic review investigating long‐term effects of a gluten‐reduced or gluten‐free diet on the prevention of cardiovascular disease in people without coeliac disease (i.e. in the general population). Although there are many studies published in people with diseases such as irritable bowel syndrome (e.g. Hajiani 2019), non‐coeliac gluten sensitivity (e.g. Tovoli 2019) or fibromyalgia (e.g. Slim 2017), studies evaluating long‐term effects of a gluten‐reduced diet for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease are rare (we only identified three NRSIs with a long‐term follow‐up, and one small RCT). Ehteshami 2018 published the results of a “short‐term” RCT evaluating the effects of a gluten‐free diet (<2 g/day) with a control diet on cardiovascular risk factors in 50 participants with metabolic syndrome. After eight weeks, the participants following a gluten‐free diet improved in waist circumference, glycaemic control and triglyceride levels. For blood lipids and blood pressure, no differences between the groups were identified. Although study participants were randomised, the authors reported that overall energy intake in the gluten‐free diet group was lower than in the control group. This contamination and the short‐term follow‐up could explain the observed benefits. Study results from other ongoing primary studies cannot be expected in the near future as such studies were not identified at all in our population of interest.

    Findings from a series of systematic reviews and meta‐analyses recently published by Reynolds 2019 indicated that an increased intake of cereal fibre and whole grains probably lowers the risk for type 2 diabetes, all‐cause and cardiovascular‐related mortality, incidence of cardiovascular disease, and stroke. This finding could explain the observed associations between a reduced gluten intake (which is associated with a reduced intake of cereal fibre) and the slightly increased risk for type 2 diabetes. However, no association of a gluten‐reduced diet was observed for the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease.
