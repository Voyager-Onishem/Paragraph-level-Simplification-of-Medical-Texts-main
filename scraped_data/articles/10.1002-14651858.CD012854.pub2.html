Interventions for increasing immunosuppressant medication adherence in solid organ transplant recipients

    • Background

    Non‐adherence to immunosuppressant therapy is a significant concern following a solid organ transplant, given its association with graft failure. Adherence to immunosuppressant therapy is a modifiable patient behaviour, and different approaches to increasing adherence have emerged, including multi‐component interventions. There has been limited exploration of the effectiveness of interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy.

    Objectives

    This review aimed to look at the benefits and harms of using interventions for increasing adherence to immunosuppressant therapies in solid organ transplant recipients, including adults and children with a heart, lung, kidney, liver and pancreas transplant.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Kidney and Transplant Register of Studies up to 14 October 2021 through contact with the Information Specialist using search terms relevant to this review. Studies in the Register were identified through searches of CENTRAL, MEDLINE, and EMBASE, conference proceedings, the International Clinical Trials Register (ICTRP) Search Portal and ClinicalTrials.gov.

    Selection criteria

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs), quasi‐RCTs, and cluster RCTs examining interventions to increase immunosuppressant adherence following a solid organ transplant (heart, lung, kidney, liver, pancreas) were included. There were no restrictions on language or publication type.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two authors independently screened titles and abstracts of identified records, evaluated study quality and assessed the quality of the evidence using the GRADE approach. The risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane tool. The ABC taxonomy for measuring medication adherence provided the analysis framework, and the primary outcomes were immunosuppressant medication initiation, implementation (taking adherence, dosing adherence, timing adherence, drug holidays) and persistence. Secondary outcomes were surrogate markers of adherence, including self‐reported adherence, trough concentration levels of immunosuppressant medication, acute graft rejection, graft loss, death, hospital readmission and health‐related quality of life (HRQoL). Meta‐analysis was conducted where possible, and narrative synthesis was carried out for the remainder of the results.

    Main results

    Forty studies involving 3896 randomised participants (3718 adults and 178 adolescents) were included. Studies were heterogeneous in terms of the type of intervention and outcomes assessed. The majority of studies (80%) were conducted in kidney transplant recipients. Two studies examined paediatric solid organ transplant recipients. The risk of bias was generally high or unclear, leading to lower certainty in the results. Initiation of immunosuppression was not measured by the included studies.

    There is uncertain evidence of an association between immunosuppressant medication adherence interventions and the proportion of participants classified as adherent to taking immunosuppressant medication (4 studies, 445 participants: RR 1.09, 95% CI 0.95 to 1.20; I² = 78%). There was very marked heterogeneity in treatment effects between the four studies evaluating taking adherence, which may have been due to the different types of interventions used. There was evidence of increasing dosing adherence in the intervention group (8 studies, 713 participants: RR 1.14, 95% CI 1.03 to 1.26, I² = 61%).  There was very marked heterogeneity in treatment effects between the eight studies evaluating dosing adherence, which may have been due to the different types of interventions used. It was uncertain if an intervention to increase immunosuppressant adherence had an effect on timing adherence or drug holidays. There was limited evidence that an intervention to increase immunosuppressant adherence had an effect on persistence.

    There was limited evidence that an intervention to increase immunosuppressant adherence had an effect on secondary outcomes. For self‐reported adherence, it is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the proportion of participants classified as medically adherent to immunosuppressant therapy (9 studies, 755 participants: RR 1.21, 95% CI 0.99 to 1.49; I² = 74%; very low certainty evidence). Similarly, it is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the mean adherence score on self‐reported adherence measures (5 studies, 471 participants: SMD 0.65, 95% CI ‐0.31 to 1.60; I² = 96%; very low certainty evidence). For immunosuppressant trough concentration levels, it is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the proportion of participants who reach target immunosuppressant trough concentration levels (4 studies, 348 participants: RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.68 to 1.40; I² = 40%; very low certainty evidence). It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication may reduce hospitalisations (5 studies, 460 participants: RR 0.67, 95% CI 0.44 to 1.02; I² = 64%; low certainty evidence). There were limited, low certainty effects on patient‐reported health outcomes such as HRQoL. There was no clear evidence to determine the effect of interventions on secondary outcomes, including acute graft rejection, graft loss and death. No harms from intervention participation were reported.

    Authors' conclusions

    Interventions to increase taking and dosing adherence to immunosuppressant therapy may be effective; however, our findings suggest that current evidence in support of interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy is overall of low methodological quality, attributable to small sample sizes, and heterogeneity identified for the types of interventions. Twenty‐four studies are currently ongoing or awaiting assessment (3248 proposed participants); therefore, it is possible that findings may change with the inclusion of these large ongoing studies in future updates.

    • Interventions to increase immunosuppressant medication adherence after a solid organ transplant

    What is the issue?

    Solid organ transplantation refers to the transplantation of the heart, lungs, kidney, pancreas or liver. After a solid organ transplant, immunosuppressant medication works to suppress the immune system and ensure the short and long‐term survival of the organ transplant. Taking the immunosuppressant medication at the correct time and at the correct dose is a critical part of post‐transplant care. However, non‐adherence to immunosuppressant medication is frequent and can be a common reason for transplant failure. Clear evidence is required to identify effective interventions that increase immunosuppressant medication adherence following a solid organ transplant.

    What did we do?

    We searched the literature to find studies that described an intervention to increase immunosuppressant medication adherence after a heart, lung, kidney, pancreas or liver transplant. We collected information from the studies and combined this to identify if an intervention was helpful. We examined the quality of these interventions to judge how certain we could be that the effects we observed were reliable.

    What did we find?

    We found 40 studies that involved 3718 adults and 178 adolescents. The majority of studies included participants with a kidney transplant. We found that the types of interventions varied across the studies, and 27 studies included more than one approach to increase immunosuppressant medication adherence. We found some evidence to support the effectiveness of interventions to increase taking the correct dosage of medication. It was unclear whether using an intervention increased adherence to immunosuppressant medication following a solid organ transplant. It was also unclear if interventions affected concentrations of immunosuppressant medication in the blood, rates of transplant rejection, death, the need for hospital admission and health‐related quality of life. There was low confidence in the information about the effects of interventions as some studies were small, the types of interventions varied, and studies defined and measured medication adherence in different ways. We didn't find enough information to assess whether certain types of interventions were more effective than other types.

    Conclusions

    Our findings suggest that the current research evidence examining interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy is of low quality, attributable to variability in the type of intervention delivered, the overall low number of participants receiving the intervention, and significant variation in how medication adherence is defined and measured. Existing data provided information that indicated the possible benefits of the interventions for increasing adherence in terms of taking medication and taking the correct dose. No harms related to intervention participation were identified. There are 24 studies either yet to be completed or final results published that weren't included in this review, so a future update of this review may show more certainty in whether an intervention can increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication following a solid organ transplant.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Overall, we conclude that the current low certainty evidence may suggest a potential benefit of interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication in increasing dosing adherence in the solid organ transplant population. However, the quality of the evidence is at present insufficient to provide direction to practitioners working with post‐transplant patients. Specific strategies to maintain clinically advised levels of adherence to immunosuppressants haven't been identified, and such strategies remain absent from treatment guidelines to date. It is important to note that this review identified 22 ongoing studies and two studies awaiting assessment (3248 proposed participants), which reinforces that immunosuppressant medication adherence interventions remain an important priority in post‐transplant settings. A future update of this review may have different results and lead to a better understanding of the effectiveness of interventions to increase immunosuppressant medication adherence.

    Implications for research

    Electronic Monitoring (EM) is considered the gold standard in the measurement of medication adherence as it allows for intensive recording of each phase of medication‐taking behaviour on a day‐to‐day basis, which reflects adherence over time (Fine 2009). However, there are limitations to EM as a form of measurement. For example, removing medication from an EM device is not proof of medication ingestion; device failure can occur, or there may be an inability to engage with technology, in addition to the personal or health system expense of EM devices (Vrijens 2016). When considering surrogate objective markers of adherence, 15 studies reported the immunosuppressant trough concentration levels; however, there was considerable heterogeneity in the clinically relevant cut‐offs applied to define non‐adherence. Furthermore, it has been highlighted previously that changes in blood levels may not be a useful surrogate marker of adherence, given that a range of factors other than patient non‐adherence can affect intra‐individual variability of dose‐corrected immunosuppressant trough concentration levels, including the specific analytical assay, diarrhoea, drug‐drug interactions, generic medication substitutions, diet and genetic polymorphisms (Bessa 2016). The consequences of non‐adherence may not be immediately clinically evident; therefore, the impact of non‐adherence on clinical outcomes is difficult to assess in studies of small sample sizes and short follow‐up duration. Multi‐measurement approaches are found to have the highest sensitivity (72%) and specificity (42%), particularly self‐report, assay and clinician reports, when compared to EM (Fine 2009). It is recommended that the most appropriate adherence assessment should include multiple methods, including indirect surrogate markers of adherence. In this multi‐method approach, clear operationalisation of adherence measurement is critical for transparency in interpreting research findings. In addition, classifying patients as ‘adherent’ versus ‘non‐adherent’ serves to increase heterogeneity in comparing evidence on different intervention types; therefore, it is recommended to avoid categorisation of adherence in the absence of a strong rationale for this form of adherence operationalisation.

    Sixty‐eight per cent of interventions in this review were multi‐component interventions, which included two or more behaviour change techniques. It is difficult to untangle the ‘black box’ of multi‐component interventions to identify the effective components, and it is recommended that future studies describe intervention components using the Behaviour Change Technique taxonomy (Michie 2013) to provide clarity in intervention description and identification of active components when studying intervention effectiveness. Lastly, there is a need to integrate economic evaluations into trial methodology in order to identify unnecessary healthcare expenditure through ineffective interventions to reduce non‐adherence to immunosuppressant medication.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Solid organ transplantation refers to the transplantation of the heart, lungs, kidney, liver or pancreas from a deceased or living donor (Linden 2009). Solid organ transplantation is regarded as a highly successful form of intervention following irreversible organ failure, with transplant success rates varying across organ types and the age of the recipient. Median graft survival for an adult kidney transplant recipient is estimated to be 11.7 years for a deceased donor and 19.2 years for a living donor. In paediatric (0 to 18 years) kidney transplant recipients, graft survival is estimated as 91.5% for deceased donors and 96.4% for living donors (ERA‐EDTA 2015). For heart transplantation, one‐year graft survival is estimated at 13 years (Lund 2016) and 15.7 to 21.5 years for paediatric recipients (Rossano 2016). For lung transplantation, median graft survival is estimated as 8 years for adults (Yusen 2016) and 5.4 years for paediatric recipients (Goldfarb 2016). For liver, one‐year graft survival is estimated as 79% for adults and 84% for children > 2 years of age (Adam 2003). Five‐year graft survival for a pancreas transplant is estimated at > 88% (Gruessner 2013), with long‐term graft survival estimated as 9.6 years for paediatric recipients (Spaggiari 2017).

    Adequate suppression of the immune system is required for short‐ and long‐term survival of the organ transplant; thus, adherence to immunosuppressive therapy forms the central part of the prevention of organ rejection, graft loss and death following an organ transplant. Immunosuppressive strategies and pharmacological agents are consistent across all solid organ transplants and most commonly involve lifelong oral administration of a calcineurin inhibitor (CNI) and an antimetabolite, with or without corticosteroids, in order to sustain a graft free from rejection and minimise the potential for acute or chronic graft loss, or death following transplantation (Butler 2004; Posadas Salas 2014).

    There remains a significant imbalance between organ availability and numbers listed for transplantation, with a shortage of organs for transplantation reported worldwide (Rudge 2012). The cost benefits of optimised post‐transplant graft survival are substantial when considering the healthcare cost savings through long‐term successful transplantation (Anyanwu 2002; Sagmeister 2002; Whiting 2004), as graft failure incurs significant hospitalisation costs, in addition to anti‐rejection treatment and life‐sustaining therapies. The importance of adhering to immunosuppressive therapy cannot be overemphasised, with the odds of organ failure increasing seven‐fold for non‐adherent recipients versus adherent recipients (Butler 2004). This suggests that near‐perfect adherence is essential for reduced patient morbidity and overall reduced healthcare costs (Low 2015). Medication adherence is defined as a dynamic process where patients take medications as prescribed by the healthcare provider. Medication adherence contains three distinct but interrelated stages – initiation, implementation, and persistence (De Geest 2018; Vrijens 2012). Initiation involves starting a medication regimen. Implementation requires taking medications as prescribed, including correct dosage and timing, and avoiding drug holidays. Persistence is a continuation of correct medication taking for the prescribed duration. Measurement of adherence differs across each phase, raising challenges in defining and analysing non‐adherence in each stage. Adequate adherence is achieved when the gap between the individual’s actual medication‐taking behaviour and the prescribed regimen has no negative effect on the therapeutic outcome (Vrijens 2012; Fine 2009).

    Accurate estimates of non‐adherence are difficult to obtain, given the variability in the type of adherence assessment and study quality (Nieuwlaat 2014). Objective, direct measures of adherence to medication regimens post‐transplant, such as direct observation, are difficult to capture in clinical trial settings. Indirect proxy measures of medication adherence, including self‐report assessments, yield superior rates of medication adherence; however, are open to considerable reporting biases (Moran 2017). In transplantation research, objective, indirect surrogate measures of immunosuppressant medication adherence using clinical parameters, such as pharmacy refill data, electronic measurement of pillbox use (Denhaerynck 2005), assay of medication or fluctuation in medication trough levels (O'Regan 2016), and/or clinical outcomes such as rejection or organ failure are reported to yield the highest immunosuppressant non‐adherence rates (Denhaerynck 2005; De Bleser 2009). There is also a paucity of evidence on non‐adherence to other prescribed medications in the post‐transplant medication regimen.

    Meta‐analyses of all solid organ transplant types reported a 22.6% non‐adherence rate to immunosuppressant therapy. Rates of non‐adherence varied by type of transplant, with the highest rates observed for kidney transplants (36%) and the lowest for liver transplants (6%) (Dew 2007). The rate of non‐adherence may also vary by age of the transplant recipient, with a rate of non‐adherence to immunosuppressants in paediatric recipients estimated as 6 cases per 100 person‐years (Dew 2009).

    Description of the intervention

    Post‐transplant immunosuppressant therapy involves a complex programme of medication adherence, including daily dosing schedules, monitoring and management (Posadas Salas 2014). Specific strategies to maintain clinically advised levels of adherence to immunosuppressants haven't been identified, and such strategies remain absent from treatment guidelines to date. Previous work in 2009, similar to this review, focused on adherence to immunosuppressant therapies for kidney, heart and liver transplants and identified that interventions focusing on increasing treatment adherence largely applied a combination of approaches to increase adherence, namely incorporating educational/cognitive, counselling/behavioural, or psychological/affective domains (De Bleser 2009). Method of intervention delivery varied widely across studies, including pharmacy, nursing and patient‐led interventions. Authors concluded that limited improvements in adherence to immunosuppressants were evident however, no single type of intervention emerged as the most effective in increasing adherence, and poor methodological quality was evident in 58% of studies identified. Such findings have been reported for other chronic and acute disease populations (Bangalore 2007; Nieuwlaat 2014).

    It is becoming increasingly recognised that reliance on technology is an emerging strategy in healthcare for supporting patient‐oriented self‐management, and different interventional platforms and innovative technologies are being developed to increase adherence in chronic health conditions (Nieuwlaat 2014). Individually tailored medical mobile apps, in particular, show potential as an inexpensive and accessible method of intervention delivery to increase adherence to complex medication regimens (Dayer 2013). This review provides an opportunity to identify and examine the efficacy of new innovative intervention platforms for increasing adherence, in addition to more commonly identified intervention delivery platforms, such as tailoring educational materials, reinforcement, feedback, and behavioural skills training (Mullen 1992).

    How the intervention might work

    It is often assumed that the transplant recipient should be motivated by the potential benefits of successful transplantation to follow the prescribed immunosuppressant regimen; however, estimated non‐adherence rates question this assumption. In order to increase medication adherence, it is vital first to accurately assess the behaviour and to understand what is driving non‐adherence. Reasons for general non‐adherence to prescribed medications are multi‐dimensional and can be attributed as ‘intentional’ or ‘non‐intentional’. Intentional non‐adherence involves conscious decision‐making to alter or abstain from the prescribed regimen, whilst non‐intentional non‐adherence arises from forgetting, misunderstanding medication instructions or administering incorrect doses (DiMatteo 2012). Limited evidence is available for examination of reasons for non‐adherence post‐transplant; however, factors such as psychosocial barriers were associated with poorer immunosuppressant medication adherence post‐transplant in adult recipients. For children and adolescents, the drivers of non‐adherence differ from the adult population and can be influenced by the wider family network, with lower family cohesion, parental distress and child distress for paediatric recipients associated with poorer immunosuppressant medication adherence post‐transplant (Dew 2007; Dew 2009). Given the complexity of barriers to adherence, strategies to increase adherence must be capable of addressing the multi‐dimensionality of medication adherence. Interventions which identify the reasons for the target behaviour (e.g. intentional versus non‐intentional behaviour) and systematically develop intervention components to address these behavioural drivers are likely to be more effective than interventions whereby a systematic, theory‐driven approach is not applied (Davies 2010; Davis 2015: Michie 2011). Additionally, most intervention studies focus on modifiable factors at the patient level for increasing adherence to immunosuppressant medication, e.g. lack of knowledge, treatment‐related factors and psychosocial factors; however, some factors are outside of the control of the patient. For example, service level factors, including the organisation and delivery of adequate post‐transplant care (Sanders‐Pinheiro 2021) and policy level factors, such as the cost of immunosuppressant medication for patients (Denhaerynck 2018), have been associated with poorer immunosuppressant medication adherence.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Adherence is the single most important modifiable factor that affects treatment outcomes in chronic disease management. Consequently, the identification of successful modes of intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy is outlined as a research priority in kidney disease (Tong 2015). Given the reported high non‐adherence rates, coupled with the critical importance of adherence to the immunosuppressant regimen to maximise and maintain successful transplantation, clear evidence is required to identify effective interventions which increase adherence after a solid organ transplant.

    Objectives

    We aimed to look at the benefits and harms of using interventions for increasing adherence to immunosuppressant therapies in solid organ transplant recipients, including paediatric and adult heart, lung, kidney, liver and pancreas transplant recipients.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs), quasi‐RCTs (RCTs where allocation to treatment was obtained by alternation, use of alternate medical records, date of birth or other predictable methods), and cluster RCTs examining interventions to increase adherence following a solid organ transplant (heart, lung, kidney, liver, pancreas).

    Types of participants

    All solid organ transplant recipients, including adults and children.

    Inclusion criteria

    All solid organ transplant recipients, including first or subsequent transplants and multi‐organ transplants.

    Exclusion criteria

    Studies including populations without a solid organ transplant were included only if the data for the solid organ transplant recipients could be analysed separately.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies addressing interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication in comparison to a control arm. Comparisons of interventions to usual routine care were also included. Given the wide variation in types of interventions to address adherence and methods of adherence measurement, interventions of any sort intended to affect adherence with prescribed, self‐administered immunosuppressant medications were included, but not restricted to:

    • Patient educational interventions (e.g. books, leaflets, posters, videos, interactive modules)

    • Pre‐planned telephone contacts with nurses/medical team

    • Electronic medication management interventions

    • Pharmacy interventions

    • Medical mobile apps

    • Cognitive behavioural therapy/counselling/behavioural interventions

    • Motivational interviewing

    • Coaching/self‐efficacy training

    • Behavioural contracting

    • Service‐level interventions

    • A combination intervention including one or more of the above‐outlined strategies.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    The ABC taxonomy for medication adherence has been identified as the gold‐standard approach for measurement of medication adherence (Vrijens 2012) and guided outcome selection. The three phases of medication adherence within the ABC taxonomic approach were identified as primary outcomes. The phases of the ABC taxonomy were categorized as follows:

    1. Initiation of immunosuppressant medication regimen

    2. Implementation of immunosuppressant medication regimen. This phase of adherence included:

      • Taking adherence: the proportion of prescribed immunosuppressant medications taken

      • Dosing adherence: the proportion of days with the correct dosage taken, as per the prescribed immunosuppressant medication regimen

      • Timing adherence: the proportion of doses taken on time, as per the prescribed immunosuppressant medication regimen

      • Drug holidays: the proportion of drug holidays taken within the prescribed immunosuppressant medication regimen

    3. Persistence with immunosuppressant medication regimen. Non‐persistence was defined as discontinuation of the prescribed immunosuppressant medication regimen.

    The above phases of immunosuppressant medication adherence were measured objectively via electronic monitoring (EM) devices and pharmacy reconciliation data, where possible.

    Secondary outcomes

    We included both subjective and objective surrogate measures of adherence as a measure of the indirect effect of immunosuppressant medication adherence on clinical outcomes. Potential measures of adherence included, but were not restricted to:

    1. Patient behaviours

    • Self‐reported adherence

    2. Clinical parameters

    • CNI levels

    • CNI level variability

    • Absolute lymphocyte count

    • Clinically assessed organ rejection

    • Biopsy‐proven organ rejection

    • Death (any cause)

    • Non‐fatal events (e.g. any adverse experience related to the study participation)

    • Health service usage (e.g. clinical attendance, hospitalisations)

    • Health‐related quality of life (HRQoL).

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Kidney and Transplant Specialised Register up to 14 October 2021 through contact with the Information Specialist using search terms relevant to this review. The Cochrane Kidney and Transplant Specialised Register contains studies identified from several sources.

    1. Monthly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL)

    2. Weekly searches of MEDLINE OVID SP

    3. Searches of kidney and transplant journals, and the proceedings and abstracts from major kidney and transplant conferences

    4. Searching the current year of EMBASE OVID SP

    5. Weekly current awareness alerts for selected kidney and transplant journals

    6. Searches of the International Clinical Trials Register (ICTRP) Search Portal and ClinicalTrials.gov.

    Studies contained in the Register were identified through searches of CENTRAL, MEDLINE, and EMBASE based on the scope of Cochrane Kidney and Transplant.     Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    The search strategy described was used to obtain titles and abstracts of studies that may be relevant to the review. The titles and abstracts were screened independently by two authors, who discarded studies that were not applicable; however, studies and reviews that might include relevant data or information on studies were retained initially. Two authors independently assessed the retrieved full text of included abstracts to determine which studies satisfied the inclusion criteria. Disagreements between authors in the screening process were resolved by the inclusion of a third author.

    Data extraction and management

    Data extraction was carried out independently by two authors using standard data extraction forms. Studies reported in non‐English language journals were translated before assessment. Translation was completed by a native speaker from the lead institution, RCSI University of Medicine and Health Sciences. Where more than one publication of one study existed, reports were grouped together, and the publications that included data relating to relevant outcomes were used in the analyses. Where relevant outcomes were only published in earlier versions, these data were used. Differences between authors were reconciled by discussion with a third author.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    The following items were independently assessed by two authors using the risk of bias assessment tool (Higgins 2020) (see Appendix 2).

    • Was there adequate sequence generation (selection bias)?

    • Was allocation adequately concealed (selection bias)?

    • Was knowledge of the allocated interventions adequately prevented during the study?

      • Participants and personnel (performance bias)

      • Outcome assessors (detection bias)

    • Were incomplete outcome data adequately addressed (attrition bias)?

    • Are reports of the study free of suggestion of selective outcome reporting (reporting bias)?

    • Was the study apparently free of other problems that could put it at risk of bias?

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous outcomes (e.g. proportion adherent, organ rejection, death), results were expressed as risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI). Where continuous scales of measurement were used to assess the effects of treatment (e.g. biochemical indices of adherence to immunosuppressants, HRQoL measures), the mean difference (MD) was used, or the standardised mean difference (SMD) if different scales were used (Higgins 2020).

    Unit of analysis issues

    No issues with unit of analysis were identified, as no cluster RCT designs or cross‐over designs were included

    Dealing with missing data

    Any further information required from the original author was requested by written correspondence (e.g. emailing the corresponding author), and any relevant information obtained in this manner was included in the review. Evaluation of important numerical data such as screened, randomised patients, as well as intention‐to‐treat, as‐treated and per‐protocol population, was carefully performed. Attrition rates (e.g. drop‐outs, losses to follow‐up and withdrawals) were investigated. Issues of missing data and imputation methods (e.g. last‐observation‐carried‐forward) were critically appraised (Higgins 2020).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    Heterogeneity was assessed by visual inspection of the forest plot. We quantified statistical heterogeneity using the I² statistic, which describes the percentage of total variation across studies that is due to heterogeneity rather than sampling error (Higgins 2003). A guide to the interpretation of I² values was as follows.

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    The importance of the observed value of I² depends on the magnitude and direction of treatment effects and the strength of evidence for heterogeneity (e.g. P‐value from the Chi² test or a CI for I²) (Higgins 2020).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Due to the small number of studies per comparison or outcome, we were unable to investigate the existence of small study bias using funnel plots.

    Data synthesis

    Data were pooled using the random‐effects model to ensure the robustness of the model chosen and susceptibility to outliers. We summarised information for outcomes in which meta‐analysis was not possible due to insufficient observations using descriptive narrative synthesis. Narrative outcome reporting was included for all outcomes not included in the meta‐analyses and tabulated in the review.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    There were insufficient observations to perform sub‐group analysis.

    Sensitivity analysis

    There were insufficient observations to perform sensitivity analysis.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    The main results of the review were presented in the 'Summary of findings' table. The table presents key information concerning the quality of the evidence, the magnitude of the effects of the interventions examined, and the sum of the available data for the main outcomes (Schunemann 2020a). The 'Summary of findings' table also includes an overall grading of the evidence related to each of the main outcomes using the GRADE (Grades of Recommendation, Assessment, Development and Evaluation) approach (GRADE 2008; GRADE 2011). The GRADE approach defines the quality of a body of evidence as to the extent to which one can be confident that an estimate of effect or association is close to the true quantity of specific interest. The quality of a body of evidence involves consideration of within‐trial risk of bias (methodological quality), directness of evidence, heterogeneity, the precision of effect estimates and risk of publication bias (Schunemann 2020b).     Implementation of immunosuppressant medication adherence

    Thirteen studies reported implementation of immunosuppressant medication adherence (ADMIRAD 2013; Chisholm‐Burns 2013; Cukor 2017; Hardstaff 2002; Henriksson 2016; IMAKT 2019; Joost 2014; Jung 2017; Klein 2007; MAESTRO‐Tx 2017; Paterson 2019; PRIMA 2019; Reese 2017). The findings for the sub‐categories of implementation are reported below.

    Primary outcomes
    Taking adherence

    Nine studies included taking immunosuppressant medication adherence. Four studies reported the proportion of participants classified as adherent and were included in the meta‐analysis (ADMIRAD 2013; Joost 2014; Klein 2007; PRIMA 2019). There is uncertain evidence of an association between immunosuppressant medication adherence interventions and the proportion of participants classified as adherent to taking immunosuppressant medication (Analysis 1.1 (4 studies, 445 participants): RR 1.09, 95% CI 0.95 to 1.20; I² = 78%). There was very marked heterogeneity in treatment effects between the four studies evaluating taking adherence, which may have been due to the different types of interventions used. Hardstaff 2002 reported findings from the intervention group only and reported that 83% missed at least one dose in the 12‐month study period. Henriksson 2016 reported findings from the intervention group only and reported that 97.8% took immunosuppressant medication during the study period.

    TAKE‐IT 2014 measured taking adherence for paediatric participants and reported 100% taking adherence on 78% of days for the intervention group versus 100% taking adherence on 68% of days for the control group (OR = 1.66; 95% CI 1.15 to 2.39; P = 0.006). IMAKT 2019 reported no difference in median taking adherence over 12 months, with high adherence in both groups (89.5% (47.7, 98.5) versus 92.6% (59.5, 96.1)). Jung 2017 reported that taking adherence did not differ between intervention and control groups.

    Dosing adherence

    Ten studies included dosing immunosuppressant medication adherence. Eight studies reported the proportion of participants classified as adherent to correct dosing and were included in the meta‐analysis (Chisholm‐Burns 2013; Cukor 2017; Joost 2014; Klein 2007; MAESTRO‐Tx 2017; Paterson 2019; PRIMA 2019; Reese 2017). Reese 2017 was a three‐arm study, and data for arm one versus control and arm two versus control were included separately. There was low certainty evidence of an association between immunosuppressant medication adherence interventions and the proportion of participants classified as adherent to correct dosing of immunosuppressant medication (Analysis 1.2 (8 studies, 713 participants): RR 1.14, 95% CI 1.03 to 1.26; I² = 61%), although there was very marked heterogeneity in treatment effects between the eight studies evaluating taking adherence, which may have been due to the different types of interventions included. Hardstaff 2002 reported findings from the intervention group only and reported that 3% took the correct dose of immunosuppressant medication 100% of the time. Jung 2017 reported that dosing adherence did not differ between intervention and control groups.

    Timing adherence

    Ten studies included the timing of immunosuppressant medication adherence, and six were included in the meta‐analysis (ADMIRAD 2013; Joost 2014; Klein 2007; MAESTRO‐Tx 2017; Paterson 2019; PRIMA 2019). It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication reduces the proportion of participants classified as adherent to the correct timing of immunosuppressant medication (Analysis 1.3 (6 studies, 632 participants): RR 1.10, 95% CI 0.98 to 1.23; I² = 63%; low certainty evidence). Henriksson 2016 reported findings from the intervention group only and reported that 15% of the intervention group took their medication at the right time during the full intervention.

    TAKE‐IT 2014 measured timing adherence for paediatric participants and reported 100% timing adherence on 73% of days for the intervention group versus 100% timing adherence on 61% of days for the control group (OR = 1.74; 95% CI 1.21 to 2.5; P = 0.003). IMAKT 2019 reported no difference in median timing adherence over 12 months, with high adherence in both groups (89.5% (46.7 to 97) versus 87.6% (53.7 to 94.1)). Jung 2017 reported that timing adherence did not differ between intervention and control groups.

    Drug holidays

    Five studies included drug holidays from immunosuppressant medication adherence, and four were included in the meta‐analysis (ADMIRAD 2013; Joost 2014; Klein 2007; PRIMA 2019). It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication reduces the number of drug holidays from immunosuppressant medication (Analysis 1.4 (4 studies, 445 participants): RR 1.12, 95% CI 0.77 to 1.62; I² = 87%; low certainty evidence). Jung 2017 reported that dosing adherence did not differ between intervention and control groups.

    Persistence

    Two studies included persistence with immunosuppressant medication adherence. ADMIRAD 2013 reported a marginally higher persistence rate in the intervention group (85.1% versus 71.9%; P = 0.08). Chisholm 2001 reported a difference in persistence with immunosuppressant medication treatment between the intervention and control group (75% versus 33.3%, P < 0.05).

    Secondary outcomes ‐ subjective measures of immunosuppressant medication adherence
    Self‐reported adherence

    Twenty studies included self‐reported immunosuppressant medication adherence (Bessa 2016; Cote 2018; Garcia 2015; Harrison 2016; Huang 2007b; IMAKT 2019; Joost 2014; Kaiser 2019; Klein 2007; MAESTRO‐Tx 2017; mHeart 2021; Paterson 2019; PRIMA 2019; Reese 2017; Santana 2011; Schmid 2017; Suhling 2014, Taber 2020; TACmono 2021; TAKE‐IT 2014). Nine studies reported the proportion of participants classified as adherent and were included in the meta‐analysis. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the proportion of participants classified as medically adherent measured by self‐reported adherence measures (Analysis 2.1 (9 studies, 755 participants): RR 1.21, 95% CI 0.99 to 1.49; I² = 74%; very low certainty evidence).

    Five studies reported mean self‐reported immunosuppressant medication adherence scores and were included in the meta‐analysis. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the mean adherence score on self‐reported adherence measures (Analysis 2.2 (5 studies, 471 participants): SMD 0.65, 95% CI ‐0.31 to 1.60; I² = 96%; very low certainty evidence). Cote 2018 and PRIMA 2019 also reported self‐reported adherence using a Visual Analogue Scale and reported no difference in self‐reported adherence between the intervention and control groups.

    Eight studies did not provide sufficient detail to be included in the meta‐analysis. Cote 2018, MAESTRO‐Tx 2017 and Santana 2011 reported no difference in self‐reported adherence between the intervention and control groups. IMAKT 2019 reported a reduction in the percentage of adherent participants in the control group from 3 to 12 months (P < 0.001), with no change in the percentage of adherent participants over time in the intervention group. Kaiser 2019 reported a decrease in self‐reported adherence over time; however, the rate of change or change per group wasn't specified. Harrison 2016 and Schmid 2017 included self‐reported adherence only as part of a combined composite adherence score.

    TAKE‐IT 2014 was not included in the meta‐analysis as it included self‐reported adherence measures for paediatric participants. TAKE‐IT 2014 reported that self‐reported adherence on the MAM‐MM was high overall and did not differ between intervention and control groups (self‐reported taking adherence: 98.3 ± 4.5 versus (97.1 ± 6, P = 0.2); self‐reported timing adherence: 95 ± 7.9 versus 92.9 ± 9.3; P = 0.2).

    Secondary outcomes ‐ objective surrogate measures of adherence to immunosuppressant medication adherence
    Immunosuppressant trough concentration levels

    Seventeen studies reported immunosuppressant trough concentration levels (ADMIRAD 2013; Bessa 2016; Chisholm 2001; Garcia 2015; Cukor 2017; Harrison 2016; IMAKT 2019; Jung 2017; Klein 2007; Kaiser 2019; Levine 2019; mHeart 2021; Paterson 2019; PRIMA 2019; Reese 2017; Suhling 2014; TAKE‐IT 2014).

    Four studies reported the proportion of participants with immunosuppressant trough concentration levels at target and were included in the meta‐analysis. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant medication increases the proportion of participants who reach target immunosuppressant trough concentration levels (Analysis 3.1 (4 studies, 348 participants): RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.68 to 1.40; I² = 40%, very low certainty evidence). Klein 2007 reported serum concentration levels at the group level only and reported a difference between the percentage of serum concentrations at target levels between the intervention and control groups (98/125 (78%) versus 62/121 (51%), P < 0.001).

    Thirteen studies reported mean immunosuppressant trough concentration levels.

    • ADMIRAD 2013 examined dose simplification from twice‐daily TAC to once‐daily TAC and reported evidence for a difference in mean immunosuppressant trough concentration levels. Average TAC concentrations in the once‐daily and twice‐daily groups were 7.2 ± 1.8 ng/mL 1.8 and 8.1 ± 1.9 ng/mL (P = 0.0004).

    • Paterson 2019 examined dose simplification from twice‐daily TAC to once‐daily TAC and reported evidence for a difference in mean immunosuppressant trough concentration levels. Mean TAC concentrations in the once‐daily and twice‐daily groups were 4.83 ± 1.44 ng/mL and 6.2 ± 1.08 ng/mL ng/mL (P = 0.002).

    • Bessa 2016 reported no evidence for a difference in mean TAC trough levels (9.4 ± 4.1 ng/mL versus 9.8 ± 3.2 ng/mL; P > 0.05).

    • Cukor 2017 reported no evidence for a difference in mean TAC trough levels (5.7 ± 1.8 ng/mL versus 6.4 ± 3.5 ng/mL; P = 0.5).

    • Garcia 2015 reported uncertainty of differences in mean serum levels of TAC over three months (8.74 ± 1.56 ng/mL versus 8.78 ± 1.8 ng/mL).

    • Reese 2017 reported no evidence for a difference in mean TAC trough levels (9.17 ± 1.41 ng/mL versus 9.08 ± 1.42 ng/mL; P = 0.14).

    • Jung 2017 reported no evidence for a difference in mean immunosuppressant trough concentration levels (5.3 ± 1.2 ng/mL versus 5.0 ± 1.2 ng/mL; P = 0.282).

    • PRIMA 2019 reported uncertainty of differences in intra‐individual variability of serum trough levels of the index medication between the intervention and control group (12.5 (8.2 to 18.4) versus 13.3 (8.4 to 18.7); P = 0.906, η² = 0.0001).

    • mHeart 2021 reported no difference in TAC concentration variability (CV > 30%) between the intervention and control groups (41% versus 47%; P = 0.526).

    • IMAKT 2019 reported no difference in TAC SD between the intervention and control group (2.1 (1.18 to 2.5) versus 2.3 (1.6 to 2.9)).

    • Kaiser 2019 reported no evidence for a difference in TAC concentration variability between the intervention and control groups.

    • Levine 2019, in a three‐arm study, reported no evidence for a difference in coefficient of variability of TAC levels between intervention 1 (mobile app + smartwatch) and control groups at three months (33.8 versus 32.8; P = 0.81) and no evidence for a difference between intervention 2 (mobile app) and control groups at three months (33 versus 32.8; P = 0.45).

    • Harrison 2016 reported immunosuppressant trough concentration levels as part of a combined composite adherence score.

    • TAKE‐IT 2014 included mean immunosuppressant trough concentration levels for paediatric participants and reported no difference between intervention and control groups for the SD of TAC trough concentration levels (1.6 (0.9 to 2.5) versus 1.4 (0.9 to 2.1); P = 0.5).

    Acute graft rejection

    Eleven studies (ADMIRAD 2013; Bessa 2016; Garcia 2015; Henriksson 2016; Joost 2014; Jung 2017; Klein 2007; Kantachuvessiri 2016; TAKE‐IT 2014; PRIMA 2019; Taber 2020) reported acute rejection episode events, and eight were included in the meta‐analysis. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant adherence reduces acute graft rejection (Analysis 4.1 (8 studies, 820 participants): RR 0.75, 95% CI 0.33 to 1.71; I² = 69%; low certainty evidence). Kantachuvessiri 2016 reported no evidence of a difference in organ rejection between the intervention and control group (data not presented in the abstract). Jung 2017 reported acute graft rejection events in intervention or control groups. TAKE‐IT 2014 was not included in the meta‐analysis as it included acute graft rejection episodes for paediatric participants; it was uncertain whether acute rejection rates differed between the intervention and control group (1.69 versus 1.06 events/100 patient‐months).

    Graft loss

    Seven studies (Bessa 2016; Breu‐Dejean 2016; Garcia 2015; Henriksson 2016; Jung 2017; Taber 2020; TAKE‐IT 2014) reported graft loss, and five were included in the meta‐analysis. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant adherence prevents graft loss (Analysis 4.2 (5 studies, 467 participants): RR 1.24, 95% CI 0.29 to 5.40; I² = 44%; low certainty evidence). Jung 2017 reported no graft loss events in intervention and control groups. TAKE‐IT 2014 was not included in the meta‐analysis as it included graft loss data for paediatric participants. It reported no graft losses during the study period.

    Death

    Six studies (Bessa 2016; Breu‐Dejean 2016; DeVito Dabbs 2016; Garcia 2015; Suhling 2014; Taber 2020) reported death during the study follow‐up period. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant adherence prevents death (Analysis 4.3 (6 studies, 652 participants): RR 1.04, 95% CI 0.77 to 1.40; I² = 0%; low certainty evidence).

    Hospital admission

    Seven studies (Bessa 2016; Chisholm‐Burns 2013; DeVito Dabbs 2016; Henriksson 2016; Suhling 2014; Taber 2020; TRANSAFE‐Rx 2018) reported hospital admissions. It is uncertain whether an intervention to increase adherence to immunosuppressant adherence prevents hospital readmission. (Analysis 4.4 (5 studies, 460 participants): RR 0.67, 95% CI 0.44 to 1.02; I² = 64%; low certainty evidence). DeVito Dabbs 2016 reported hospital readmissions as the median number of hospitalised days and found no evidence of a difference in the percentage of lung transplant recipients who had at least one hospital readmission or cumulative rehospitalisation days during the follow‐up period. TRANSAFE‐Rx 2018 reported evidence for lower rates of hospitalisation in the intervention group (0.65 versus 1.08 hospitalisations/person‐years; P = 0.007).

    Health‐related quality of life

    Six studies reported HRQoL, with heterogeneity across types of measures.

    • DeVito Dabbs 2009 reported a difference in SF‐36 physical function scores between the intervention and control groups (F(1,27) = 7.6, P < 0.0125, r = 0.46, t‐scores not reported). There was no evidence of a difference in SF‐36 mental function scores between the intervention and control groups.

    • Joost 2014 reported no difference in SF‐36 scores between the intervention and control groups (data not presented by group).

    • Liu 2019c reported no improvement at any measurement interval following the intervention; however, the method of measurement of QoL was not reported.

    • Schmid 2017 reported that psychological distress measured by the Brief Symptom Inventory 18 decreased in both groups, and HRQoL measured by the Fragebogen Alltagleben scale increased over the first year post‐transplant, with no scores or statistical analysis results provided. On the End‐Stage Renal Disease Symptom Checklist Transplantation Module, the intervention group reported less symptoms at 12 months in two subscales, cardiac and renal dysfunction, and side effects of corticosteroids (median = 0, IQR = 0.2 versus median = 0.4, IQR = 0.6; U = 133, P = 0.004, r = 0.42).

    • Urstad 2012 reported a difference in SF‐12 mental function scores between the intervention and control groups (effect size = 0.53, units not reported, P = 0.001).

    • Peers4path 2014 reported improved QoL related to medication taking in the intervention group in adolescent patients (data not provided).

    No harms were reported in relation to intervention participation

    Subgroup and sensitivity analysis 

    Overall, the planned subgroup and sensitivity analyses to explore sources of heterogeneity were not possible due to the lack of data observations and heterogeneity in the measurement of outcomes.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    In this review, we have summarised 40 studies (103 reports) involving 3896 randomised participants (3718 adults, 178 adolescents) reporting the effects of a range of types of interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy and related outcomes following a solid organ transplant. Nearly all studies focused on adult transplant recipients, with only two studies including adolescents. The majority of studies included kidney transplant recipients (80%). There was considerable heterogeneity in intervention duration, ranging from two to 12 months, and length of follow‐up, ranging from three to 120 months. Twenty‐seven out of 40 interventions were multi‐component interventions, with more than one behaviour change technique included. Risks of bias in the included studies were often unclear, particularly as five of the included studies were conference abstracts and information required to judge the risk of bias wasn't presented fully.

    There was low certainty evidence that interventions may increase dosing adherence to immunosuppressant medication, suggesting adherence interventions may support clinically important increases in the correct implementation of the immunosuppressant medication regimen. Based on a meta‐analysis of low‐certainty evidence, it is uncertain whether adherence interventions increased taking and timing adherence and reduction of drug holidays. A meta‐analysis of low certainty evidence showed limited evidence that an intervention to increase immunosuppressant adherence had an effect on secondary outcomes that represent surrogate clinical markers of immunosuppressant non‐adherence, including self‐reported adherence, drug trough concentration levels, acute graft rejection, graft loss and death, however it may reduce hospitalisations. Interpretation of effects of trough concentration levels of immunosuppressant medication was complicated by variability in target trough concentration levels across studies, in addition to consideration of dosing strategies and pharmacokinetics for once‐daily versus twice‐daily immunosuppressant medication formulations. It is important to note that clinical events such as death and graft loss are rare in short‐term follow‐up after an organ transplant (Foroutan 2019) therefore, the studies included in this review were underpowered to examine the potential effects for these outcomes, and the current absence of evidence for these outcomes is insufficient to guide clinical practice at present. There was limited effect on patient‐reported health outcomes such as HRQoL. Due to the heterogeneity of intervention types and small sample sizes, we didn't have enough information to assess intervention efficacy by type of intervention.

    Overall, the currently available data in this review may provide some provisional evidence that interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy may be of benefit clinically when attempting to increase dosing adherence post‐transplant. Interventions to increase adherence are difficult to study, given the variability in intervention content. Due to heterogeneity in the measurement and definition of adherence and outcomes and limitations in studies based on the reporting of methods, the confidence in the evidence was downgraded from high confidence to low confidence. None of the included studies included an economic evaluation of intervention cost‐effectiveness. Given the use of technology in several of the interventions, combined with health care professional time in delivering education, feedback and behavioural interventions (Oberjé 2013), it is unknown if the interventions summarised here were cost‐effective and could be pragmatically integrated into routine clinical care

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We aimed to provide a comprehensive analysis of the benefits and harms of interventions to increase adherence to immunosuppressant therapy following a solid organ transplant. Whilst some evidence in support of adherence interventions was reported, we could not robustly assess the effect of these adherence interventions, as there was considerable heterogeneity in intervention type, sample size and measurement of the primary and secondary outcomes. This limited the potential to reasonably combine study results for meta‐analysis, which potentially limits the internal validity of the results of the review. The limited number of studies identified for inclusion prevented examination of possible sources of heterogeneity in subgroup analysis, including the impact of age, type of organ transplant, first versus subsequent transplant, objective versus subjective adherence and intervention type. Notably, there was no evidence of harm from participation in the intervention, indicating a low risk of adverse events in the considered interventions

    Quality of the evidence

    We assessed the quality of evidence using standard risks of bias domains specified by the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool and GRADE methodology. Five studies had data extracted from conference abstracts, which limited the comprehensive risk of bias assessment. The majority of studies were judged to be at high risk of bias for performance bias, as participants were unblinded in all studies. It must be noted that blinding participants to the intervention is difficult in this setting, given the required active participation in multi‐component interventions. Most studies were also judged to be at high risk of detection bias. Again, this is perhaps unrealistic to achieve in this setting, given the required engagement with technology and/or healthcare staff for medication management. Selection bias and reporting bias were judged to be at low risk across the majority of the studies. Using GRADE, the certainty of the evidence was assessed as low for all reported outcomes due to uncertain or high risk of bias, substantial inconsistency in intervention type and outcome measurement, and small, heterogeneous study populations. Further consideration is needed when assessing the risk of bias in complex, multi‐component behavioural interventions, as these methodologies may be susceptible to poorly understood forms of bias arising from participation (McCambridge 2014).

    We identified inconsistencies in defining and measuring immunosuppressant medication adherence and surrogate markers of immunosuppressant medication adherence across the included interventions. The ESPACOMP Medication Adherence Reporting Guideline (EMERGE) group have called for transparency and consistency in measuring adherence and reporting medication adherence enhancing trials (De Geest 2018). The ABC Taxonomy for describing and measuring adherence is considered state‐of‐the‐art when studying adherence in clinical populations (Vrijens 2016) and was applied in this review to clearly delineate between the separate phases of adhering to an immunosuppressant medication regimen and reduce the heterogeneity of outcome measurement across intervention types.

    Potential biases in the review process

    A strength of this review includes its conduct according to a pre‐specified protocol. The main potential bias relates to the heterogeneity in the definition and measurement of the primary outcome. This resulted in pooled data for meta‐analyses which may have yielded unreliable effect estimates and precluded meta‐analysis for some outcomes. Another potential bias must be acknowledged in relation to our search strategy. Whilst the Cochrane Kidney and Transplant Specialised Register was systematically searched for this review, it is possible that some potentially eligible studies were missed due to publication after our last search date or missed conference proceedings.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Our findings are more comprehensive than previously published systematic reviews in this area and, overall, contain findings that are more cautious. A systematic review of 12 intervention studies in heart, kidney and liver transplant recipients (De Bleser 2009) reported that combination interventions as part of a team approach in post‐transplant care may be effective in increasing adherence. Similarly, in a systematic review of 12 studies in kidney transplant patients, Low 2015 reported that multi‐dimensional interventions targeting behavioural risk factors may be most effective for enhancing medication adherence. This review was unable to form any conclusions on the effectiveness of different intervention types because of the limited number of studies of each type identified here. Similar to this review, both De Bleser 2009 and Low 2015 highlight limitations and differences in operational definitions of adherence and poor reporting of intervention and usual care content. Our findings also concur with those of Nieuwlaat 2014, who examined interventions to enhance medication adherence in chronic health conditions in a Cochrane review and reported that significant heterogeneity in intervention types and outcome measurement, and a high risk of bias, resulted in inconsistent effects across studies.
