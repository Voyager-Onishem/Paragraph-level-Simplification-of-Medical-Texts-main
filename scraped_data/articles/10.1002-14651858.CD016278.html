Tranexamic acid for preventing postpartum haemorrhage after caesarean section

  • Christa Rohwer
  • Anke Rohwer

Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی
  • Français

    • Rationale

    Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) is common and potentially life‐threatening. The antifibrinolytic drug tranexamic acid (TXA) is recommended for treating PPH; it reduces the risk of death from haemorrhage by one‐third when given soon after bleeding onset, but not overall risk of death. Interest in whether TXA may be effective in preventing PPH is growing. Evidence indicates that TXA given more than three hours after injury to bleeding trauma patients increases mortality. Potential harm becomes critical in prophylactic use of TXA. Reliable evidence of the effect and safety profile of TXA is required before widespread prophylactic use can be considered.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of TXA for preventing PPH compared to placebo or no treatment (with or without uterotonic co‐treatment) in women during caesarean birth.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and WHO ICTRP to 20 February 2024 and searched reference lists of retrieved studies.

    Eligibility criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) evaluating the use of TXA alone or plus uterotonics during caesarean birth for preventing PPH. Trials needed to be prospectively registered (i.e. before starting recruitment). We applied a trustworthiness checklist.

    Outcomes

    The critical outcome was blood loss ≥ 1000 mL, measured using estimated or calculated methods.

    Important outcomes included maternal death, severe morbidity, blood transfusion, the use of additional surgical interventions to control PPH, thromboembolic events, use of additional uterotonics, hysterectomy, maternal satisfaction, and breastfeeding at discharge.

    Risk of bias

    We assessed risk of bias in the included studies using Cochrane's RoB 1 tool.

    Synthesis methods

    Two review authors independently selected trials, extracted data, and assessed risk of bias and trial trustworthiness. We pooled data using random‐effects meta‐analysis. We assessed the certainty of the evidence using GRADE.

    Included studies

    We included six RCTs with 15,981 participants. All 12 trials in the previous version of this review were not included after review of trial registrations and trustworthiness checklists. Most included studies involved women at low risk of PPH and were conducted in high‐resource settings.

    Synthesis of results

    Prophylactic TXA in addition to standard care compared to placebo in addition to standard care or standard care alone

    TXA results in little to no difference in estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL (risk ratio (RR) 0.94, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.79 to 1.11; 4 RCTs; n = 13,042; high certainty evidence), resulting in 8 fewer per 1000 women having estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL (from 30 fewer to 16 more). TXA likely results in a slight reduction in calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.76 to 0.92; 2 RCTs; n = 4327; moderate certainty evidence), resulting in 53 fewer per 1000 having calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL (from 75 fewer to 25 fewer).

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of TXA on maternal death (one event in placebo group, none in TXA group). No trials measured severe morbidity.

    TXA likely results in little to no difference in blood transfusion (RR 0.88, 95% CI 0.72 to 1.08; 5 RCTs; n = 15,740; moderate certainty evidence), resulting in 4 fewer per 1000 women requiring a blood transfusion (from 10 fewer to 3 more).

    TXA results in little to no difference in additional surgical interventions to control PPH (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.86 to 1.22; 4 RCTs; n = 15,631; high certainty evidence), resulting in 1 more per 1000 women requiring additional surgical intervention (from 4 fewer to 7 more).

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of TXA on thromboembolic events (RR 1.40, 95% CI 0.22 to 8.90; 4 RCTs; n = 14,480; very low certainty evidence), resulting in 1 more per 1000 women having a thromboembolic event (from 2 fewer to 17 more).

    TXA results in little to no difference in the need for additional uterotonics (RR 0.88, 95% CI 0.78 to 1.00; 4 RCTs; n = 15,728; high certainty evidence), resulting in 15 fewer per 1000 women requiring additional uterotonics (from 27 fewer to 0 fewer).

    The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of TXA on hysterectomy (RR 0.80, 95% CI 0.20 to 3.29; 2 RCTs; n = 4546; very low certainty evidence), resulting in 3 fewer per 10,000 women requiring a hysterectomy (from 11 fewer to 31 more).

    One trial measuring maternal satisfaction reported no difference between groups at day two postpartum.

    No data were available on breastfeeding.

    Overall, studies had low risk of bias. We downgraded the certainty of evidence mainly for imprecision.

    Authors' conclusions

    Prophylactic TXA in addition to standard care during caesarean birth results in little to no difference in estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL and likely results in a slight reduction in calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL compared to placebo.

    There were no data for severe morbidity due to PPH. Event rates for further interventions to control PPH were low and similar across groups. Prophylactic TXA thus results in little to no difference between groups for additional surgical interventions (32 versus 31 per 1000), and likely results in little to no difference between groups for blood transfusions (31 versus 36 per 1000) and use of additional uterotonics (107 versus 121 per 1000).

    There were very few events for the outcomes maternal death (1 in placebo group), thromboembolic events (2 versus 3 per 1000), and hysterectomy (1 per 1000 in each group). Evidence for these serious adverse events is therefore very uncertain.

    Decisions about implementing routine prophylactic TXA during caesarean birth should not only consider outcomes related to blood loss, but also the relatively low rates of PPH morbidity and uncertainty of serious adverse events.

    Most studies included women at low risk of PPH, thereby precluding any conclusions about women at high risk of PPH.

    Cost associated with routine use of an additional drug for all caesarean births needs to be considered.

    • What are the benefits and risks of tranexamic acid for preventing heavy bleeding after caesarean births?

    Key messages

    • The clotting medicine tranexamic acid (TXA) given during caesarean birth does not prevent heavy bleeding when blood loss is measured by collecting blood and weighing surgical sponges, or when health providers estimate the amount of blood loss. When blood loss is calculated using the proportion of red blood cells in blood before and after the caesarean birth, there is likely a slight reduction in heavy bleeding.

    • TXA probably does not make a difference in the number of women who need further treatment to control heavy bleeding.

    • The effect of TXA on harmful effects is uncertain.

    What is the issue?

    Heavy bleeding after giving birth (postpartum haemorrhage) is a common and sometimes life‐threatening complication of birth. Most women receive drugs that stimulate the womb to contract (uterotonics) after vaginal or caesarean birth to prevent postpartum haemorrhage. Tranexamic acid (TXA) is a medication used to decrease blood loss in surgery and health conditions associated with increased bleeding. It works by helping to prevent the breakdown of blood clots. If a woman is bleeding heavily after birth, it decreases blood loss. We do not know if TXA can help prevent heavy bleeding after caesarean birth.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to know if fewer women receiving TXA at the time of surgery had heavy bleeding after a caesarean section. We also wanted to find out if receiving TXA during surgery was associated with any harmful effects.

    What did we do?

    We searched for and selected all studies that addressed this question. We used a checklist to make sure we only included studies that we could trust. We made judgements about the quality of the studies before comparing and summarising the results of the studies. Lastly, we rated our confidence in the findings.

    Why is this important?

    We need to find out whether TXA is effective in preventing heavy bleeding after birth when given to women at the time of a caesarean birth. If TXA works, it could reduce excessive blood loss, the use of further treatment, and possibly complications from heavy bleeding and death.

    What did we find?

    We identified six studies with a total of 15,981 women that looked at the effects of preventive TXA. We found that TXA results in little to no difference in heavy bleeding after birth when blood loss is measured by collecting blood and weighing surgical sponges, or when health providers estimated the amount of blood loss, compared to placebo (dummy treatment). TXA likely results in a slight reduction of heavy bleeding when blood loss is calculated using the proportion of red blood cells in the blood before and after the caesarean birth. We are very uncertain about the effect of TXA on maternal death, and there is no evidence on the effect of TXA on women developing serious illness.

    It is likely that TXA has no effect on the need to receive a blood transfusion, and makes no difference to whether a women needs further surgical treatment. We are very uncertain about the effect of TXA on blood clots. TXA results in little to no difference to whether women require additional drugs to help the womb contract, and we are very uncertain about the effect of TXA on the need for surgical removal of the womb. There does not seem to be a difference between groups in maternal satisfaction, and there is no evidence on the effects of TXA on breastfeeding.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    Most studies included women at low risk of heavy bleeding after birth, preventing any conclusions about women at high risk of heavy bleeding after birth. Across trials, few women experienced harmful effects; however, we cannot be certain that this is the case in the real world.

    What does this mean?

    We found little to no difference in the number of women experiencing heavy bleeding after birth after they were given TXA preventatively during caesarean birth, and we are very uncertain about the effect of TXA on death, blood clots, and surgical removal of the womb. As these are harmful effects, health providers should take the benefit and the potential harms into account when considering routine treatment with TXA of women during caesarean birth.

    There does not seem to be a difference in the number of women needing additional treatment for heavy bleeding after receiving TXA.

    Giving every women who gives caesarean birth TXA preventatively during birth is a serious decision with many implications. We need to weigh up blood loss outcomes, as well as the relatively low rates of serious illness after postpartum haemorrhage and uncertainty regarding serious unwanted effects.

    How up‐to‐date is this evidence?

    The evidence is current to 20 February 2024.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Prophylactic tranexamic acid (TXA) in addition to standard care during caesarean birth results in little to no difference in estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL and likely results in a slight reduction in calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL when compared to placebo.

    There were no data for severe morbidity due to postpartum haemorrhage (PPH). Event rates for further interventions to control PPH were low and similar across groups. Prophylactic TXA thus results in little to no difference between groups for additional surgical interventions (32 versus 31 per 1000), and likely results in little to no difference between groups for blood transfusions (31 versus 36 per 1000) and use of additional uterotonics (107 versus 121 per 1000).

    There were very few events for the outcomes maternal death (1 event in the placebo group), thromboembolic events (2 versus 3 per 1000), and hysterectomy (1 per 1000 in each group). The evidence for these serious adverse events is therefore very uncertain.

    Decisions about implementing routine prophylactic TXA during caesarean birth should not only consider outcomes related to blood loss, but also the relatively low rates of PPH morbidity and uncertainty of serious adverse events.

    Most studies included women at low risk of PPH, therefore we could draw no conclusions about women at high risk of PPH.

    The cost associated with routine use of an additional drug for all caesarean births should also be taken into consideration.

    Equity‐related implications for practice

    Although TXA is not a very expensive drug, in resource‐constrained settings any additional intervention used routinely at all caesarean births will impact available expenditure on other services.

    Implications for research

    Further randomised controlled trials (RCTs) on the effectiveness of TXA to prevent PPH after caesarean birth should focus on women at high risk of PPH. There is also a need for RCTs on other interventions to prevent PPH after caesarean birth. Researchers should ensure that they use sound methods and the core outcome set for PPH when conducting RCTs.

    Further research on the methods of measuring blood loss after caesarean birth may be required to determine a gold standard method.

    Further research is also required to investigate strategies to reduce the risk of drug error deaths from inadvertent spinal administration of TXA.

    Equity‐related implications for research

    Research should be relevant to the needs of individuals in low‐ and middle‐income countries, where the burden of mortality from haemorrhage is greatest.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) is one of the most common obstetric emergencies, occurring in up to 12% of all births. It is a leading cause of maternal mortality, particularly in low‐resource settings, where 99% of deaths occur [1, 2, 3, 4]. Many survivors of PPH require costly, urgent care, and prolonged hospital stays. Some women undergo a hysterectomy to control the bleeding, thereby denying them the chance of bearing further children. The trauma of severe postpartum bleeding can also have a significant psychological impact and adversely affect a mother’s ability to breastfeed and bond with her baby [5].

    Primary PPH is often defined as blood loss greater than 500 mL after a vaginal birth, or greater than 1000 mL after a caesarean birth, within 24 hours of birth [6]. However, many patients, such as those with anaemia or with existing cardio‐respiratory or hepatic disease, are less able to compensate for blood loss, and even small amounts of blood loss can be harmful.

    A 2024 World Health Organization (WHO) systematic review and modified Delphi expert consensus on strategies for early detection and first‐response management of PPH at caesarean birth concluded that the same PPH definition should apply to both vaginal and caesarean birth; that for the intraoperative phase, early detection should be accomplished via quantitative blood loss measurement, complemented by monitoring the woman's haemodynamic status; and that first response should be triggered once the woman loses at least 500 mL of blood with continued bleeding or when she exhibits clinical signs of haemodynamic instability, and include immediate administration of uterotonics and tranexamic acid, examination to determine aetiology, and rapid initiation of cause‐specific responses. [7]

    Most cases of PPH are attributed to uterine atony, although bleeding due to trauma to the genital tract, surgical bleeding after caesarean birth, retention of placental tissue, and failure of the coagulation system are other common causes [2].

    Implementation of evidence‐based third stage of labour care [8], in particular the use of prophylactic oxytocin, has been the focus of efforts to reduce PPH. However, the number of patients suffering PPH remains stubbornly high, and in some populations is in fact increasing. With the global increase in caesarean births, the contribution of post‐caesarean birth haemorrhage to maternal mortality is increasing. In South Africa, post‐caesarean haemorrhage is the only cause of maternal mortality that continues to increase [9].

    There is a need for other effective interventions beyond the use of uterotonic drugs.

    Description of the intervention and how it might work

    Tranexamic acid (TXA) is an antifibrinolytic drug that inhibits fibrinolysis by blocking the interaction between plasmin and fibrin. Since it was first identified in the early 1960s, TXA has primarily been used for heavy menstrual bleeding and for reducing bleeding during surgical procedures in high‐risk patients. More recently, early treatment with TXA has been shown to be effective in patients with life‐threatening haemorrhage caused by trauma and childbirth [10, 11]. In the CRASH 2 Trial, TXA given within one hour reduced mortality by 32%; given between one and three hours, by 21%; and beyond three hours TXA increased mortality by 44% (P = 0.004). The mechanism for this increase in mortality is unclear. TXA has multiple pharmacological effects. A pharmacovigilance study found a 32.6‐fold increase in adverse renal vascular and ischaemic conditions in women of childbearing age [12]. The World Maternal Antifibrinolytic (WOMAN) trial assessed the effects of intravenous TXA in 20,060 participants with a clinical diagnosis of PPH. The results showed that TXA reduces the risk of bleeding to death after PPH by one‐third (risk ratio 0.69, 95% confidence interval 0.52 to 0.91; P = 0.008) when given within three hours of birth, with no apparent increase in adverse events [13]. As a result, the WHO recommends early intravenous TXA as a treatment for established PPH [14]. However, neither the overall risk of death, nor the risk of hysterectomy, nor both (the predefined primary outcome) was reduced.

    Many deaths from PPH occur on the day of birth, most within the first few hours after. Thus, for many women who suffer a PPH, treatment is too late. This, coupled with the knowledge that TXA is most effective when given shortly after the onset of bleeding, provides reason to believe that prophylactic TXA could reduce the risk of PPH. The growing interest in prophylactic TXA has led to a growth in the number of randomised trials exploring its effects in the obstetric population. However, the quality of many of these trials has been questioned, and uncertainties around the effects of TXA remain unresolved [15].

    Why it is important to do this review

    This is an update of a Cochrane review first published in 2010 [16] and previously updated in 2015 [17].

    The authors of the 2015 update concluded that "TXA (in addition to uterotonic medications) decreases postpartum blood loss and prevents PPH and blood transfusions following vaginal birth and caesarean birth in women at low risk of PPH based on studies of mixed quality" [17]. Several new trials have been conducted in recent years, and there was a need to update this review using current methodology.

    The WHO recommends intravenous TXA as a treatment for severe bleeding after childbirth [14]. In the only trial powered to measure mortality (the WOMAN trial), TXA reduced mortality ascribed to haemorrhage, but not overall mortality. Increasing TXA use by giving it to patients shortly before caesarean birth or immediately after cord clamping could confer substantial additional health benefits, but requires robust evidence of its effects, including safety. Regarding the antifibrinolytic action of TXA, concerns about an increased risk of thrombosis associated with its use persist, although no such increased risk has been observed in randomised trials [18]. Nevertheless, further reassurance based on evidence from the obstetric population may be prudent before widespread prophylactic use is considered. There is high‐quality evidence from the Clinical Randomisation of an Antifibrinolytic in Significant Haemorrhage 2 (CRASH 2) trial that TXA given to bleeding trauma patients more than three hours after injury causes a 44% increase in mortality [19]. The evidence of an adverse effect on mortality by an as‐yet‐unexplained mechanism becomes critical when considering prophylactic use, in which the great majority of those exposed to TXA do not stand to benefit from the antifibrinolytic effects, and trials are typically underpowered to measure mortality.

    The original review has been split into two reviews, one of TXA for caesarean birth (this review) and one of TXA for vaginal birth. In addition to updated searches, this review includes revisions made to comply with changes in methodological approaches introduced since the previous version was conducted. Furthermore, in light of quality and integrity concerns affecting trials on this topic, we have implemented additional measures to help ensure that only the most reliable evidence is included.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of tranexamic acid for preventing postpartum haemorrhage in comparison to placebo or no treatment (with or without uterotonic co‐treatment) in women during caesarean birth.

    Methods

    We followed MECIR (Methodological Expectations for Cochrane Intervention Reviews) [20] when conducting the review and PRISMA 2020 [21] for the reporting.

    Several amendments have been made to this review since the protocol and the previous updates were published. Details are available in Supplementary material 8. The title of the review has changed since the protocol was published. The protocol is publicly available at www.cochranelibrary.com/cdsr/doi/10.1002/14651858.CD007872/full. This update of the review was split into two separate reviews. One ongoing review includes participants with vaginal births, while the current review includes participants with caesarean births. We have removed the 500 mL outcome, as this threshold relates to the definition of PPH in vaginal births. After splitting the review, only the 1000 mL outcome was considered, as this is a clinically meaningful outcome to measure and report on in caesarean births.

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We searched for all published, unpublished, and ongoing randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing the use of TXA alone or in addition to uterotonics during caesarean birth to prevent PPH. We excluded quasi‐RCTs (e.g. those randomised by date of birth or hospital number or alternation). We identified no cluster‐RCTs in our search.

    Since the first version of the review was published, concerns regarding the integrity of several trials in obstetrics including those assessing the effects of TXA for preventing PPH have been raised [15, 22]. For this reason, we added the inclusion criterion that trials be prospectively registered (before the first participant was enrolled) in a registry. We also applied current Cochrane trustworthiness criteria.

    Types of participants

    Women undergoing any caesarean birth (emergency or planned) at any age or within any setting of care. We included women at low risk of PPH or with specific risk factors for PPH as reported by authors.

    We considered studies that only included a subset of relevant participants for inclusion. For example, in studies that included both caesarean and vaginal births, only relevant data that were stratified according to type of birth were extracted. If this information was not clear, we contacted the trial authors for additional information.

    Types of interventions

    TXA used during caesarean birth to prevent PPH, alone or in combination with standard treatment (e.g. other uterotonics) compared with placebo, no intervention, or standard treatment alone (e.g. other uterotonics).

    We included any dose of TXA given at any time point shortly before or after the birth. We considered intravenous, intramuscular, and oral routes of administration, but excluded topical application of TXA, which is the subject of an existing Cochrane review [23].

    Outcome measures

    Critical outcomes

    Blood loss of ≥ 1000 mL after caesarean birth. We considered blood loss that was measured up to 48 hours.

    We included the following measurements of blood loss.

    • Estimated blood loss: gravimetrically estimated measures of blood loss (using surgical drapes or sponges that collect blood to measure blood loss) and provider‐estimated measures of blood loss (subjective, visual estimation of how much blood was lost).

    • Calculated blood loss (calculations based on haematocrit or haemoglobin levels pre‐ and post‐caesarean birth, height and weight).

    Important outcomes

    • Maternal death before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Severe morbidity (defined as maternal deaths or severe morbidity events including major surgery (laparotomy, uterine artery ligation, internal iliac artery ligation, B‐Lynch suture, hysterectomy, extensive vaginal repair), admission to the intensive care unit (ICU), or vital organ failure (temporary or permanent)) before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Receipt of blood transfusion before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Receipt of additional surgical interventions to control PPH (including laparotomy, compression suture of uterus, devascularisation of the uterus and hysterectomy) before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Thromboembolic events including pulmonary embolism and deep vein thrombosis before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Receipt of additional uterotonics including oxytocin, misoprostol, ergometrine, carbetocin, or others reported by authors, before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Hysterectomy before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Maternal satisfaction before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Breastfeeding at discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Clinical diagnosis of PPH (independent of amount of blood loss) as reported by authors.

    • Mean blood loss (mL):

      • estimated blood loss: gravimetrically estimated measures of blood loss (using surgical drapes or sponges that collect blood to measure blood loss) and provider‐estimated measures of blood loss (subjective, visual estimation of how much blood was lost);

      • calculated blood loss (calculations based on haematocrit or haemoglobin levels pre‐ and post‐caesarean birth, and weight).

    • Myocardial infarction before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Stroke before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Seizures before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Organ failure/dysfunction before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • ICU admission before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Nausea before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Vomiting before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Headache before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Maternal well‐being before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Postpartum anaemia (haemoglobin < 9 g/dL) before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    • Skin reactions before hospital discharge or as reported by authors.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    For this update, the Information Specialists of the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group searched the Group's Trials Register for records available to 20 June 2021. One review author (KK) carried out further updated searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2024, Issue 2), MEDLINE (OvidSP, 1946 to 20 February 2024), Embase (OvidSP 1980 to 2024 week 8), and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (trialsearch.who.int/) on 20 February 2024; these searches were limited to evidence added since 20 June 2021. The search strategies for each database are provided in Supplementary material 1.

    Searching other resources

    We searched the reference lists of retrieved studies. We did not apply any language or date restrictions.

    Data collection and analysis

    For methods used in the previous version of this review, see [16] and [17].

    For the current review, we used the following methods for assessing the reports that were identified as a result of the updated search.

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (CC and KK, or CR and AR) independently assessed the titles and abstracts of studies identified by the search for potential relevance. We obtained the full‐text reports of studies deemed potentially relevant, and the same review authors independently assessed the full texts for inclusion in the review. Any disagreements were resolved through discussion or consultation with all review authors if necessary.

    We created a study flow diagram to map out the number of records identified, included, excluded, or awaiting classification.

    All studies meeting our inclusion criteria were evaluated by two review authors (KK and CC, or CR and AR) against predefined criteria to select studies that, based on available information, were deemed to be sufficiently trustworthy to be included in the analysis. We resolved discrepancies through discussion and consultation with all authors. The criteria, according to the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Trustworthiness Screening Tool [24], were as follows.

    Research governance

    • Are there any retraction notices or expressions of concern listed on the Retraction Watch Database relating to this study?

    • Was the study prospectively registered (for those studies published after 2010)? If not, was there a plausible reason?

    • When requested, did the trial authors provide/share the protocol and/or ethics approval letter?

    • Did the trial authors engage in communication with the Cochrane review authors within the agreed timelines?

    • Did the trial authors provide individual participant data upon request? If not, was there a plausible reason?

    Baseline characteristics

    • Is the study free from characteristics of the study participants that appear too similar (e.g. distribution of the mean (standard deviation) excessively narrow or excessively wide, as noted by [25])?

    Feasibility

    • Is the study free from characteristics that could be implausible (e.g. large numbers of participants with a rare condition (such as severe cholestasis in pregnancy) recruited within 12 months)?

    • In cases with (close to) zero losses to follow‐up, is there a plausible explanation?

    Results

    • Is the study free from results that could be implausible (e.g. massive risk reduction for main outcomes with small sample size)?

    • Do the numbers randomised to each group suggest that adequate randomisation methods were used (e.g. is the study free from issues such as unexpectedly even numbers of participants ‘randomised’ including a mismatch between the numbers and the methods, if the authors say ‘no blocking was used’ but still end up with equal numbers, or if the authors say they used ‘blocks of 4’ but the final numbers differ by 6)?

    Where a study was classified as ‘high risk’, we attempted to contact the study authors to address any possible lack of information/concerns. In cases where we were unable to obtain contact details for the study authors, or where adequate information remained unavailable, the study was categorised as ‘awaiting classification’ and the reasons and communications with the trial author (or lack of) described.

    Abstracts

    We included data from abstracts only if, in addition to the trustworthiness assessment, the study authors confirmed in writing that the data to be included in the review came from the final analysis and will not change. If such information was not available/provided, the study was categorised as ‘awaiting classification’ (as above).

    Data extraction and management

    We designed a form to extract data using Covidence [26]. Two review authors (CC and KK, or CR and AR) extracted the data using the agreed‐upon form. Any discrepancies were resolved through discussion.

    When information regarding any of the above was unclear, we attempted to contact the authors of the original reports to provide further details.

    In addition to the main outcomes and details on trial design, we systematically extracted the following data for each trial.

    • Characteristics of participants, including risk for PPH, type of caesarean birth

    • Inclusion and exclusion criteria

    • Characteristics of the intervention, including dose, timing, route of administration

    • Co‐interventions, e.g. use of other uterotonics

    • Duration and technique of assessment of blood loss

    • Loss to follow‐up

    Authors of studies potentially eligible for inclusion in the review were not involved in making eligibility decisions for these studies.

    Risk of bias assessment in included studies

    Two review authors (CC and KK, or CR and AR) independently assessed the validity of each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions [27]. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion. Authors of studies potentially eligible for inclusion in the review were not involved in making decisions about risk of bias for these studies.

    (1) Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups.

    We assessed the method as:

    • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

    • high risk of bias (any non‐random process, e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (2) Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment and assessed whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered, sealed, opaque envelopes);

    • high risk of bias (open random allocation; unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes, alternation; date of birth);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (3) Blinding of participants, personnel, and outcome assessors (checking for possible performance and detection bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind study participants, personnel, and outcome assessors from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We considered studies to be at low risk of bias if they were blinded, or if we judged that lack of blinding would be unlikely to affect the results.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low, high, or unclear risk of bias for participants;

    • low, high, or unclear risk of bias for personnel;

    • low, high, or unclear risk of bias for outcome assessors.

    (4) Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias due to the amount, nature, and handling of incomplete outcome data)

    We described for each included study the completeness of data, including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We state whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. Where study authors conducted sensitivity analysis of missing data for specific outcomes, we considered this when making judgements about attrition bias.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data; missing outcome data balanced across groups);

    • high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups; ‘as treated’ analysis done with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (5) Selective reporting (checking for reporting bias)

    We described for each included study how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (where it was clear that all the study’s prespecified outcomes and all expected outcomes of interest to the review have been reported);

    • high risk of bias (where not all the study’s prespecified outcomes have been reported; one or more reported primary outcomes were not prespecified; outcomes of interest are reported incompletely and so cannot be used; the study fails to include results of a key outcome that would be expected to have been reported);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (6) Other bias (checking for bias due to problems not covered by (1) to (5) above)

    We described for each included study any important concerns we had about other possible sources of bias.

    We assessed whether each study was free of other problems that could put it at risk of bias:

    • low risk of other bias;

    • high risk of other bias;

    • unclear if there is risk of other bias.

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we presented results as summary risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI).

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we used the mean difference (MD) with 95% CI, as the outcomes were measured in the same way across trials.

    In future updates of this review, we will use the standardised mean difference (SMD) to combine data from trials that measure the same outcome but use different methods [28].

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cluster‐randomised trials

    If in future updates of this review we identify eligible cluster‐randomised trials, we will include them in the analyses along with individually randomised trials. We will adjust their sample sizes using the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions [28] using an estimate of the intracluster correlation coefficient (ICC) derived from the trial (if possible), from a similar trial, or from a study of a similar population. If we use ICCs from other sources, we will report this and conduct sensitivity analyses to investigate the effect of variation in the ICC. If we identify both cluster‐randomised trials and individually randomised trials, we will synthesise the relevant information. We will consider it reasonable to combine the results from both if there is little heterogeneity between study designs, and interaction between the effect of intervention and the choice of randomisation unit is considered to be unlikely.

    We will also acknowledge heterogeneity in the randomisation unit and perform a subgroup analysis to investigate the effects of the randomisation unit.

    Cross‐over trials

    Cross‐over trials are irrelevant for this intervention and are therefore not included.

    Multi‐armed trials

    For outcomes reported in multi‐armed trials, we combined all relevant experimental intervention groups of the trial into a single group, and all relevant control intervention groups into a single control group. We combined both the sample sizes and the numbers of people with events from all groups for dichotomous outcomes. For continuous outcomes, we combined means and standard deviations as per the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions [28]. Where we considered one of the arms irrelevant, we excluded it from the analysis [28].

    Dealing with missing data

    We noted levels of attrition in the included studies. We planned to explore the impact of including studies with high levels of missing data in the overall assessment of treatment effect by using sensitivity analysis.

    For all outcomes, we carried out analyses, to the greatest degree possible, on an intention‐to‐treat basis, that is we attempted to include all participants randomised to each group in the analyses, and to analyse all participants in the group to which they had been allocated, regardless of whether or not they received the allocated intervention. Where data were missing because the outcome was not measured in all participants, the reason for missing data was unrelated to the outcome, and missing data were balanced between groups, the denominator for each outcome in each trial was the number randomised minus any participants whose outcomes were known to be missing (modified intention‐to‐treat analysis). Where no explanation was given for missing outcome data, missing data were not balanced between groups, and the missing data were suspected to be related to the outcome, we contacted the study authors and planned to conduct sensitivity analyses.

    Reporting bias assessment

    We planned to assess reporting biases if 10 or more studies were included in the meta‐analysis. In the current update, no meta‐analyses included more than 10 studies. If more studies are included in future updates, we will investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We will visually assess funnel plot asymmetry.

    Synthesis methods

    We carried out statistical analysis using RevMan [29]. We used random‐effects meta‐analysis for combining data, as we anticipated some clinical heterogeneity sufficient to expect that the underlying treatment effects differed between trials. The random‐effects summary was treated as the average of the range of possible treatment effects, and we discuss the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. We planned that if the average treatment effect was not considered clinically meaningful, we would not combine trials. If we used random‐effects analyses, we presented the results as the average treatment effect with 95% CIs, and the estimates of Tau2 and I2.

    We used the I2 and Chi2 statistics to measure heterogeneity among the trials in each analysis. We regarded heterogeneity as substantial if I2 was greater than 30%, and either Tau2 was greater than zero, or there was a low P value (less than 0.10) in the Chi2 test for heterogeneity.

    For the critical outcome (blood loss ≥ 1000 mL), we conducted separate meta‐analyses for estimated blood loss and calculated blood loss. For estimated blood loss, where authors reported on gravimetrically estimated and provider‐estimated blood loss, we regarded gravimetrically estimated blood loss to be superior to provider‐estimated blood loss. We conducted sensitivity analysis to explore whether there was a difference in the pooled effect if we chose provider‐estimated blood loss as the superior measure.

    Investigation of heterogeneity and subgroup analysis

    For analyses with substantial heterogeneity, we performed random‐effects analysis and subgroup analyses.

    For the critical outcome (blood loss ≥ 1000 mL), we conducted subgroup analyses on the various ways of measuring estimated blood loss (gravimetrical versus provider estimated).

    We planned to assess the following subgroups for all outcomes.

    • Participants at high, low, and mixed risk for PPH

    • Participants with and without anaemia (as defined by trial author)

    • Different doses of TXA

    • Different routes of administering TXA

    The important outcomes (e.g. thromboembolic events) are included in the subgroup analysis because of their potential association with the use of TXA, which is especially important in women undergoing caesarean birth because their risk of thromboembolic events is higher than in women with vaginal birth.

    We planned to assess subgroup differences by interaction tests available within [29] and report the results of subgroup analyses quoting the Chi² statistic and P value, and the interaction test I² value.

    Equity‐related assessment

    We did not investigate health inequity in this review.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We conducted sensitivity analysis for the outcome estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL. In the main analysis, we regarded gravimetrically estimated blood loss to be superior to provider‐estimated blood loss if the study authors reported both measures. We conducted sensitivity analysis to explore whether there was a difference in the pooled effect if we chose provider‐estimated blood loss to be the superior measure.

    We planned to perform sensitivity analyses for aspects of the review that might have affected the results, for example where there was a risk of bias associated with the quality of some of the included trials, or where there were high levels of missing data.

    We planned to perform sensitivity analysis for the critical outcome (blood loss ≥ 1000 mL). However, we did not perform sensitivity analysis as risk of bias was low across studies, and where trials had missing data for this outcome, study authors conducted sensitivity analyses showing that missing data had no impact on the effect.

    Certainty of the evidence assessment

    For this update, we assessed the certainty of the evidence for each outcome using the GRADE approach [30]. We used GRADEpro GDT [31] to create summary of findings tables. We used the GRADE approach to produce a summary of the intervention effect and a measure of certainty for each of the outcomes described below. Based on the five GRADE considerations (study limitations, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias), we downgraded the certainty of the evidence from high by one level for serious (or by two levels for very serious) limitations.

    We performed a GRADE assessment on 10 outcomes, namely blood loss > 1000 mL (estimated blood loss and calculated blood loss), maternal death, severe morbidity, receipt of blood transfusions, receipt of additional surgical interventions to control PPH, thromboembolic events, receipt of additional uterotonics, hysterectomy, maternal satisfaction, and breastfeeding.

    GRADE judgements were informed by the Standard Operating Procedures for grading evidence for guidelines put forward by the WHO. We judged imprecision by considering the range of the CI of the relative effect as well as the total cumulative study population and the total number of events per outcome. We downgraded for imprecision if there were very few events (fewer than 30) and if the CI was very wide. The threshold for suggested appreciable benefit for the relative effect was 0.75, and the threshold for suggested appreciable harm was 1.25. Where the CI crossed these thresholds, we downgraded by one, two, or three levels depending on the width of the CI.

    Authors of studies potentially eligible for inclusion in the review were not involved in making decisions about the certainty of evidence.

    Consumer involvement

    We did not involve consumers in the review due to pressured timelines and limited resources. Our outcomes were informed by core outcome sets [32] that involved consumers.

    Participants

    Six trials involving a combined total of 15,981 randomised participants investigated the efficacy of TXA for preventing PPH in participants giving birth by caesarean section (Alam 2023 [55]; Lee 2023 [56]; Ogunkua 2022 [57]; Pacheco 2023 [58, 59]; Sentilhes 2021 [60, 61, 62, 63, 64]; Shakur‐Still 2023 [65]). The two biggest trials combined comprised 15,551 participants (i.e. 97% of the total number of participants included in this review) (Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021).

    One trial included 27 participants giving birth vaginally (n = 15) and by caesarean section (n = 12) (Alam 2023).

    Three trials included participants having either elective or emergency caesarean births (Alam 2023; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021). Lee 2023 included only elective caesarean births, while Ogunkua 2022 included only elective repeat caesarean births. Shakur‐Still 2023 did not specify whether participants underwent elective or emergency caesarean births.

    One trial included participants who had one or more risk factors for PPH (Shakur‐Still 2023), while another trial included both high‐ and low‐risk women (Lee 2023). All other trials included women at low risk of PPH (Alam 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021).

    Interventions

    All included studies assessed the effect of TXA in addition to standard care.

    Four trials administered oxytocin to all participants on delivery of the baby (Lee 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Sentilhes 2021; Shakur‐Still 2023), and Sentilhes 2021 specified that additional prophylactic uterotonic agents were given if necessary. Sentilhes 2021 reported administering 5 or 10 international units (IU) or 100 µg carbetocin, while Lee 2023 specified that the dose of oxytocin was 5 IU intravenous injection. Ogunkua 2022 did not specify the dose. Pacheco 2023 administered oxytocin at the discretion of the physician, and it is unclear whether all participants received it.

    Alam 2023 stated that standardised protocols were followed for oxytocin after delivery, but the details are unclear. In Shakur‐Still 2023, all participants received prophylactic uterotonics, but no further detail was provided.

    Timing of TXA

    TXA was administered at different times in the trials.

    Caesarean section

    • At least 10 minutes prior to skin incision and again after placental delivery (Ogunkua 2022).

    • 10 minutes prior to skin incision (Lee 2023).

    • During the 3 minutes after birth, after the prophylactic uterotonic agent had been administered and the cord had been clamped (Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021).

    • During surgical site skin preparation (Alam 2023).

    • 1 hour (± 30 minutes) before caesarean section (Shakur‐Still 2023).

    Route and dose of TXA

    Intravenous TXA was administered as a fixed dose in all six trials: 1 g in five trials (Alam 2023; Lee 2023; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021; Shakur‐Still 2023) and 2 g in one trial (Ogunkua 2022).

    The Shakur‐Still 2023 trial also included a group receiving 1 g intramuscular TXA and a group receiving 4 g oral TXA.

    Comparisons

    Five trials compared TXA to placebo (normal saline) (Alam 2023; Lee 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021).

    One trial compared different routes of administering TXA (intravenous, intramuscular, and oral) with a control (standard care) group (Shakur‐Still 2023).

    Measurement of blood loss ≥ 1000 mL

    Two trials calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL using formulae that included haematocrit and haemoglobin levels as well as relevant patient details (Lee 2023; Sentilhes 2021). One trial measured blood loss ≥ 1000 mL using provider estimation of physicians (Ogunkua 2022), and one trial estimated intraoperative blood loss of more than 1 litre, assessed on the basis of data obtained from the anaesthesia record and the operative report (Pacheco 2023).

    Settings

    Five trials were conducted in high‐resource settings (Alam 2023; Lee 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021), and one trial was conducted in lower‐resource settings (Shakur‐Still 2023). The high‐resource settings included the USA (Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023), Canada (Alam 2023), France (Sentilhes 2021), and Singapore (Lee 2023). Shakur‐Still 2023 was conducted in Zambia and Pakistan.

    Random sequence generation and allocation concealment

    All included trials used computer‐generated randomisation and a method of central randomisation or sequentially numbered drug containers and were judged to be at low risk of bias for both the generation of the randomisation sequence and allocation concealment.

    Blinding of participants, personnel, and outcome assessors

    Three trials reported that participants, clinicians, and investigators were blind to allocation and were assessed as being at low risk of bias (Alam 2023; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021). Ogunkua 2022 reported that both participants and providers were blind to allocation (low risk of bias), although there was no information on blinding of outcome assessment (unclear risk of bias). Shakur‐Still 2023 was not blinded, as TXA was administered via different routes and no placebo was given; the study was therefore judged to have a high risk of detection and performance bias. Blinding was unclear in Lee 2023, as the drugs were prepared by an unblinded staff member and then handed to a blinded staff member, making it possible that staff members were unblinded at this point (unclear risk of bias).

    Incomplete outcome data

    Most of the included trials had minimal missing outcome data overall and presented outcome data on an intention‐to‐treat basis (Alam 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021; Shakur‐Still 2023). Two trials had missing outcome data for the outcome calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL (Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021). However, both of these studies performed sensitivity analysis showing that the missing data did not impact the effect. In Sentilhes 2021, outcome data were missing for gravimetrically estimated blood loss. Although the proportion of missing data was large (20% and 21% in the TXA and placebo groups, respectively), the study authors reported that they did not include data for this outcome from the first five months of the trial due to unreliable measurements. We therefore judged the missing outcome data to be balanced across groups and unrelated to the outcome. In addition, Pacheco 2023 had missing data for safety outcomes. We contacted the trial authors, who reported that they were unable to contact these participants for follow‐up. Missing data were balanced across groups (8% in each group). We thus judged these studies to be at low risk of attrition bias. We assessed Lee 2023 as at unclear risk of attrition bias as some participants withdrew, and it is possible that they were at a high risk of PPH. Furthermore, three participants were excluded from the analysis due to significant intraoperative events, although the study objective includes women at high risk of PPH.

    Selective reporting

    All trials were prospectively registered, and data on all prespecified outcomes were presented. We therefore judged all trials as being at low risk of bias for this domain.

    Other bias

    We had no concerns regarding any other aspects of trials, therefore we assessed all trials as being at low risk of bias for this domain.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This review is a substantive update to the previous version. We included six RCTs involving a combined total of 15,981 participants who underwent elective or emergency caesarean birth (Alam 2023; Lee 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021; Shakur‐Still 2023). One trial included women at high risk of PPH (Shakur‐Still 2023); one trial included both women at high and low risk of PPH (Lee 2023), while the four remaining trials included women at low risk of PPH (Alam 2023; Ogunkua 2022; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021). Risk of bias was low across most studies, with unclear or high risk of performance and detection bias in two small studies that did not contribute a large proportion to the meta‐analysis.

    High certainty evidence shows that TXA in addition to standard care results in little to no difference in estimated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL when compared to placebo with standard care or standard care alone. TXA in addition to standard care compared to placebo with standard care or standard care alone likely results in a slight reduction in calculated blood loss ≥ 1000 mL. There were no available data on the effect of TXA on severe morbidity. TXA likely makes little to no difference to the risk of receiving a blood transfusion, and results in little to no difference in additional surgical interventions to control bleeding compared to placebo. The evidence is very uncertain about the effect of TXA on thromboembolic events and hysterectomy. TXA likely results in little to no difference in the need for additional uterotonics compared to placebo. One trial measured maternal satisfaction using a Likert scale questionnaire and reported no evidence of a difference between groups in maternal satisfaction at day two postpartum (Sentilhes 2021). No data were available on breastfeeding at discharge.

    Limitations of the evidence included in the review

    We presented a stratified analysis for the critical outcome, blood loss ≥ 1000 mL, as blood loss was measured differently across studies. These measures were: calculated blood loss (based on pre‐ and postoperative haematocrit or haemoglobin), gravimetrically estimated blood loss (weighing of sponges, measuring of collected fluid), and provider‐estimated blood loss (visual estimation of blood loss). We analysed estimated blood loss (gravimetrically estimated and provider estimated) and calculated blood loss separately. A systematic review of evidence regarding surgical blood loss measurement found that none of the above methods is ideal, and that a gold standard measure is lacking[163].

    There is no published guidance on which measure is preferred, as neither the publication on the PPH Core outcome set [32], nor the WHO guideline on assessment of PPH, provide an answer [164]. The gravimetric and provider estimation methods are both associated with measurement inaccuracies. It is difficult to separate blood from amniotic and other fluids used during surgery when using gravimetric methods, and provider‐estimated methods are highly subjective. The calculation method is dependent on normovolaemia for accuracy. The fact that both the hypervolaemia of normal pregnancy and the hypovolaemia of pre‐eclampsia revert towards normovolaemia after pregnancy may cause substantial errors in calculations based on preoperative compared with postoperative haemoglobin or haematocrit levels. In addition, TXA has effects on both the endothelium [165] and renal function [12] which may affect intravascular volume and cause changes in haematocrit/haemoglobin concentrations unrelated to blood loss.

    We therefore downgraded the certainty of evidence for the outcome calculated blood loss by one level, given our concern that the measurements may be influenced by physiological changes after birth and TXA. In our review, effect estimates for calculated blood loss favour TXA when compared to placebo, while those for estimated blood loss show no difference between groups, indicating a possible overestimation of the effect of TXA for calculated blood loss. Morbidity due to excessive blood loss was low and similar across groups.

    Risk of bias in the included studies was mostly low, with unclear and high risk of performance and detection bias in two small studies that had very little weight in the meta‐analysis (Lee 2023; Shakur‐Still 2023). We therefore did not downgrade the certainty of evidence due to study limitations.

    As part of the GRADE assessment, we made judgements about imprecision based on the range of the CI of the relative effect as well as the total number of participants and events per outcome. We downgraded for imprecision if there were very few events (fewer than 30) and if the CI was very wide. The threshold for suggested appreciable benefit for the relative effect was 0.75, and the threshold for suggested appreciable harm was 1.25. Where the CI crossed these thresholds, we downgraded by one, two, or three levels depending on the width of the CI. Reasons for imprecision judgements are included in the footnotes of summary of findings Table 1.

    Most trials measured blood loss over a 48‐hour period (Lee 2023; Pacheco 2023; Sentilhes 2021), whereas one trial only measured it for 24 hours postoperatively (Ogunkua 2022), which could lead to a difference in measurements. However, the latter trial's contribution to the meta‐analysis was small relative to other studies.

    The studies included in the meta‐analysis were undertaken in mostly high‐resource settings. The study population included mostly women at low risk of PPH, except in one trial with a small sample size (Shakur‐Still 2023). Our findings are therefore not directly applicable to women at high risk of PPH.

    All trials assessed the effect of TXA in addition to standard care, which mostly comprised routine administration of oxytocin after delivery. Furthermore, rates of additional uterotonics differed across trials, ranging from 6% to 50% of participants.

    Additionally, most of the data (95% randomised women) included in this review came from two trials in which TXA was administered after birth, immediately after cord clamping. This differs from much of the recent, high‐quality evidence for the effects of TXA in other surgical procedures, in which TXA was given before skin incision [166, 167].

    Limitations of the review processes

    We have updated the outcomes in this review, that is we added blood transfusion to important outcomes and removed haemoglobin below 6 g/dL. The reason is the importance of blood transfusion as an outcome when evaluating an intervention for preventing haemorrhage. Blood transfusions are used in the treatment of severe haemorrhage, but they are costly, associated with significant adverse reactions, and may not be available in low‐resource settings. Blood transfusion is an outcome assessed in other Cochrane reviews on PPH [168] and TXA [23, 169].

    We included six studies in this review, after applying the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth trustworthiness criteria [24]. There are many studies in the awaiting classification category, as further required information from trial authors was not yet received. The author team decided to strictly apply this trustworthiness tool due to known concerns about data integrity in this field of study [15, 22].

    There were very few events for some outcomes, namely maternal mortality, myocardial infarction, stroke, and thromboembolic events. Rare events often lead to a wide 95% CIs around the relative effect. The sample size for these outcomes was large, and thus it is likely that randomisation has achieved prognostic balance [28]. Where events are rare and sample sizes are big, it is more reliable to use the absolute effect for clinical decision‐making [170, 28]. Myocardial infarction, stroke, and seizures are all potential adverse effects of TXA. Reported events were too few for meaningful statistical analysis, but the fact that there were seven of these events with TXA versus one with placebo, and 53/7296 versus 39/7211 ICU admissions (RR 1.35, 95% CI 0.89 to 2.03), is reason for caution before recommending widespread prophylactic use.

    We planned to conduct subgroup analysis on risk of PPH, presence of anaemia, dose of TXA, and route of TXA. However, our included trials were homogeneous with regard to all of these characteristics. The Shakur‐Still 2023 trial was the only study that investigated different routes of administration of TXA, was conducted in low‐resource settings, and included women at high risk for PPH. The trial included 116 participants, and the data made only a small contribution to the various meta‐analyses. Furthermore, the 95% CIs of the RRs were very wide, and there was good overlap with the 95% CIs of other trials included in the analyses. Statistical heterogeneity was mostly absent. These subgroup analyses were therefore precluded.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We are aware of six other systematic reviews that have been published on this question since 2020 [171, 172, 173, 174, 175, 176]. The large number of reviews in such a short period of time is indicative of the importance and relevance of this clinical question. Most of these reviews found a larger effect on the reduction of PPH compared to our findings. However, these reviews did not apply the trustworthiness tool. Two reviews [171, 172] that reported a larger effect did not include any of the trials included in our review, but included studies we excluded or assessed as awaiting classification. Similarly, the previous version of this review also found a larger effect in the reduction of PPH. None of the trials included in the previous version of this review were included in this version, as we are awaiting additional information from study authors. Two systematic reviews [173, 174] reported that the lack of high‐quality evidence made it difficult to establish conclusions with high certainty.

    Two previous Cochrane reviews of trials in surgical patients observed less bleeding and fewer blood transfusions with TXA [177, 169].

    The effect of TXA on mortality following emergency surgery was reported to be uncertain in a Cochrane review that included three trials (260 participants) [169]. A large, high‐quality RCT showed a decrease in all‐cause mortality and death due to bleeding in trauma patients who received a short course of TXA versus placebo [178]. The findings of our review are consistent with previous reports on the need for further well‐designed studies with a larger number of participants to evaluate the effect of TXA on maternal mortality, severe morbidity, and additional surgical interventions to prevent PPH.

    Although the association of TXA with thromboembolic events has been hypothesised, it has not been proven to date [178, 179, 180, 23, 181]. Our review is in agreement with other publications suggesting that larger studies are required to assess this outcome and establish the safety of TXA.

    Seizures are a known dose‐dependent adverse effect of TXA [182]. There were very few events (three with TXA versus one with placebo) in this review.

    Mild side effects are more common in TXA versus placebo or no intervention [183, 184]. Our review found little or no increase in mild side effects.

    Risk of drug error deaths

    There appear to be increasing reports in recent years of deaths from inadvertent use of TXA instead of bupivacaine for spinal analgesia at caesarean birth [185]. A plausible explanation is the increasing use of TXA at caesarean birth, resulting in TXA being kept on the anaesthetic drug trolley. Both medications are supplied in 10‐millilitre clear glass ampoules, and some manufacturers’ ampoules are similar in appearance. Informal discussions with senior obstetricians in several countries have confirmed that such cases have occurred, but were not reported due to medicolegal concerns [GJH personal communication]. The global extent of the problem is therefore difficult to determine.
