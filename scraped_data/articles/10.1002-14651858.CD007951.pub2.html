Cochrane Database of Systematic reviews Review - Intervention

Hemodialysis for lithium poisoning

  • Eric J Lavonas
  • Jennie Buchanan
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 16 September 2015 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD007951.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Lithium salts, particularly lithium carbonate, are frequently used to treat bipolar disorder and mania. Lithium poisoning, which can occur as a result of reduced renal elimination, prescribing error, drug‐drug interactions, or deliberate overdosage, produces neurologic injury that can be permanent. Hemodialysis is often recommended to treat lithium poisoning. Although hemodialysis clearly enhances the elimination of lithium, it is unclear whether this translates into improved patient outcomes. Evidence from observational studies, generally of low methodological quality, shows similar outcomes in patients managed with or without the use of hemodialysis.

    Objectives

    To determine whether hemodialysis, applied in addition to standard therapy, reduces the likelihood, severity, or duration of neurological sequelae following lithium poisoning.

    Search methods

    We ran the search on 15 May 2015. We searched the Cochrane Injuries Group's Specialised Register, Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL, The Cochrane Library), MEDLINE (OvidSP), Embase Classic+Embase (OvidSP), CINAHL Plus, clinical trials registers and four other databases. We screened the reference lists of relevant studies, textbook chapters, and review articles, and performed a Google search to identify grey literature.

    Selection criteria

    In the context of this review, hemodialysis was defined as any extracorporeal technique to filter and extract toxicants from the serum, including all forms of hemodialysis, hemofiltration, and continuous renal replacement techniques, but not peritoneal dialysis. We included any clinical trials in which patients were randomly allocated to receive, or not receive, hemodialysis in addition to standard care for lithium poisoning.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two authors reviewed the abstracts of all identified articles. If either author identified an article as potentially meeting the inclusion criteria, both authors reviewed the full text of the article.

    Main results

    No randomized controlled trials of hemodialysis therapy for lithium poisoning were identified.

    Authors' conclusions

    Although the use of hemodialysis to enhance the elimination of lithium in patients with lithium poisoning appears logical, there is no evidence from randomized controlled trials to support nor refute the use of hemodialysis in the management of patients with lithium poisoning.

    • Hemodialysis for lithium poisoning

    Background

    Lithium salts, particularly lithium carbonate, are frequently used to treat bipolar disorder and mania. Lithium poisoning, whether due to reduced excretion by the kidneys or overdose, can cause permanent injury to the nervous system. Hemodialysis is a process in which a person's blood is filtered outside of their body, by a machine, and then returned to the person's body. Hemodialysis effectively removes lithium from the body, and has been used to treat lithium poisoning for at least 40 years. However, it is not known whether hemodialysis, performed in addition to standard therapy with intravenous fluids, reduces or eliminates damage to the nervous system.

    Search date

    The information in this review is up to date to 15 May 2015.

    Study characteristics

    This review found no randomized controlled trials assessing the use of hemodialysis to treat lithium poisoning.

    Key results

    Although the use of hemodialysis to remove lithium from patients with lithium poisoning is logical, no randomized clinical trials support or disprove a benefit from this therapy.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Although the use of hemodialysis to enhance the elimination of lithium in patients with lithium poisoning is logical, there is no research from randomized controlled trials on its benefits and harms in patients with lithium poisoning. Most patients with lithium poisoning recover fully, but the available data do not provide a reliable way to predict which patients will have a good or poor outcome. Until higher‐quality evidence can be developed, the decision to use hemodialysis in addition to standard therapy with intravenous fluids will continue to be based on clinical judgment. Hemodialysis treatment should ideally be given as part of a randomized controlled trial.

    Implications for research

    There are no randomized controlled trials on which to assess the clinical effects of hemodialysis for lithium poisoning, nor is there a strong preponderance from observational studies suggesting that a benefit is present. A randomized controlled trial of hemodialysis for lithium poisoning is ethical and reasonable. If a randomized trial is not feasible, useful information may be gained from a prospective observational study with standardized outcome assessment, utilizing propensity adjustment methods to reduce the bias imparted by confounding by indication.

    Background

    Lithium salts, particularly lithium carbonate, are frequently administered for the treatment of bipolar disorder and mania. Lithium is a metal, and therefore cannot be metabolised. It is eliminated almost exclusively (95%) through the kidney, with a small amount (5%) eliminated in sweat and saliva (Baldessarini 2001). Lithium clearance is dependent on the glomerular filtration rate (GFR). Steady‐state lithium concentrations are highly dependent on hydration status; because lithium causes a nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, mild dehydration can lead to lithium accumulation and further dehydration. Lithium is also subject to a large number of pharmacokinetic interactions, most notably with hydrochlorothiazide, other diuretics, angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors, and non‐steroidal antiinflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) (Juurlink 2007). Possibly for these reasons, unintentional lithium poisoning is more common in older people (Juurlink 2007). However, as with other psychiatric medications, lithium is frequently taken in deliberate overdose.

    Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index. Serum lithium levels of 0.6 to 1.2 mmol/L are typically targeted for therapeutic effect (Amdisen 1977). Although there is no clear relationship between serum or cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) lithium concentrations and toxic effects (Hansen 1978), signs of lithium poisoning, including altered mental status, tremor, cerebellar dysfunction, and extrapyramidal signs, are common when serum lithium levels exceed 1.5 to 2.0 mmol/L (Gadallah 1988). As a result, lithium poisoning is a common medical condition. The 2013 report of the American Association of Poison Control Centers lists 2883 cases of lithium poisoning treated in healthcare facilities, including 1333 cases with moderate or major severity outcomes and five deaths (Mowry 2014). Unintentional ingestions and adverse reactions outnumbered intentional overdoses by nearly a 2:1 margin.

    Lithium poisoning is generally classified as acute (associated with a deliberate overdose in a patient not concurrently taking lithium therapeutically), acute‐on‐chronic (associated with a deliberate overdose in a patient on lithium therapy), or chronic (related to gradual accumulation due to chronic overdosage, pharmacokinetic interactions, and/or impaired elimination) (Greller 2015). The severity of lithium poisoning is typically graded based on a standard scale (Hansen 1978).

    Hemodialysis is a medical procedure in which blood is removed from a large vein, filtered to balance electrolytes, remove excess fluids, and remove endogenous and exogenous toxins, and returned to the body. Because of its low molecular weight (74 daltons), water solubility, relatively low volume of distribution (0.8 L/kg), and minimal protein binding, lithium is highly amenable to removal by hemodialysis. The renal clearance of lithium (15 to 20 ml/min) increases four‐ to ten‐fold during hemodialysis, to 87 to 160 ml/min (Jacobsen 1987; Jaeger 1993; Eyer 2006). Because lithium is slow to partition between the serum/plasma and intracellular compartments, a rebound in serum lithium levels commonly follows hemodialysis sessions (Amdisen 1969; Von Hartitzch 1972). This rebound does not affect the CSF; spinal fluid lithium concentrations continue to decline after hemodialysis (Amdisen 1969; Jaeger 1985).

    Although the kinetics of lithium elimination are well understood, the question of whether enhancing the elimination of lithium through hemodialysis improves outcomes is more complex. Concentrations of lithium measured in the serum (Hansen 1978; Gadallah 1988; Jaeger 1993; Eyer 2006), red blood cells (El Balkhi 2009), or CSF (Amdisen 1969; Jaeger 1985) do not correlate well with clinical severity. Rapid fluid shifts associated with hemodialysis have the potential to cause harm, and one retrospective study reported a temporal association between hemodialysis and neurologic deterioration (Swartz 1994).

    Description of the condition

    Whether due to acute, chronic, or acute‐on‐chronic poisoning, lithium poisoning can produce alteration in mental status, tremors, hyperreflexia/clonus, cerebellar dysfunction, extrapyramidal signs, cardiac conduction abnormalities, diabetes insipidus, seizures, and death. Death from lithium poisoning is uncommon; when it occurs, it is generally related to complications (e.g. aspiration pneumonitis) or co‐morbidities. Cardiac conduction abnormalities and diabetes insipidus are both concentration‐dependent phenomena that develop in a small minority of patients; these typically resolve after lithium levels decline. However, neurologic sequelae of lithium poisoning, including impaired cognition, cerebellar dysfunction, and parkinsonian findings, may be permanent. This condition is sometimes referred to as the syndrome of irreversible lithium‐effectuated neurotoxicity (SILENT) (Adityanjee 2005). The incidence of this condition, and the proportion of patients who present with acute, chronic, or acute‐on‐chronic lithium poisoning who develop SILENT, are not known.

    Description of the intervention

    Standard therapy for lithium poisoning consists of discontinuation of lithium therapy, discontinuation of any medications that would impair the elimination of lithium, and intravenous hydration. In addition, many subject‐matter experts recommend hemodialysis to enhance the elimination of lithium, though the specific clinical indications for hemodialysis vary (Hansen 1978; Amdisen 1988; Schneider 2000;Miller 2004;Decker 2015; Greller 2015). Subject‐matter experts do not agree on specific indications for hemodialysis. Common recommended indications include alterations in mental status, renal insufficiency, or serum lithium levels exceeding an arbitrarily‐defined threshold, typically ≥ 4.0 or 5.0 mEq/L for acute poisoning and ≥ 2.0 mEq/L for chronic poisoning (Amdisen 1988; McKnight 2012; Decker 2015; Greller 2015).

    How the intervention might work

    When a critical toxic concentration (perhaps 1.5 to 2.0 mEq/L) is exceeded, lithium toxicity appears to be both a dose‐ and time‐dependent phenomenon. Hemodialysis rapidly removes lithium from the serum, creating a steep concentration gradient that reduces the levels of lithium in the central nervous system and other target tissues more quickly than the reduction achieved by hydration alone (Amdisen 1969; Von Hartitzch 1972; Jaeger 1985). In theory, reducing the area under the lithium concentration‐time curve by rapidly reducing serum and/or CSF lithium concentrations might reduce the severity of neuronal injury.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Hemodialysis is widely advocated as a means of reducing the risk of permanent neurological sequelae following lithium poisoning. According to standard reference sources (textbooks and review articles), this recommendation appears to be based on a theoretical model of the problem, rather than on the results of human or animal experimentation or prospective clinical research (Amdisen 1988; Schneider 2000;Osborn 2001; Miller 2004; Thundiyil 2007; Hung 2014; Decker 2015; Greller 2015). A recent study showed substantial variation in recommended treatment when standardized lithium poisoning cases were presented to a large number of physicians in a single specialty (Roberts 2014).

    Evidence from observational studies, generally of low methodologic quality, shows similar outcomes in patients managed with or without the use of hemodialysis. Most of these were retrospective: studies with prospective data collection were designed to study other aspects of lithium poisoning, such as pharmacokinetics, and therefore should be regarded as having a retrospective study design with regard to this clinical question. All are subject to confounding by indication (because sicker patients were more likely to receive hemodialysis in addition to standard therapy) and few clearly describe the method by which outcomes were assessed.

    In the United States, the use of hemodialysis to treat lithium poisoning seems to be increasing over time (Holubek 2008). Hemodialysis is somewhat expensive, particularly when its application requires that a patient be transferred from one hospital to a second hospital with the capability to perform hemodialysis. The hemodialysis procedure is associated with a 1% risk of significant complications, including catheter‐related complications and neurological injury induced by fluid and electrolyte shifts. A systematic review of 93 published case reports suggest that it may actually worsen neurological outcomes in lithium poisoning (Swartz 1994). This review investigates whether hemodialysis and related modalities of extracorporal removal provide a safe and effective means of mitigating neurotoxic sequelae of lithium poisoning.

    Objectives

    To determine whether hemodialysis, applied in addition to standard therapy, reduces the rate, severity, or duration of neurological sequelae following lithium poisoning.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomized controlled trials investigating the effect of hemodialysis on outcomes in lithium poisoning in humans, regardless of language, date, or state of publication (abstract versus full report).

    Types of participants

    Patients (of any age) with the laboratory‐confirmed diagnosis of acute, chronic, or acute‐on‐chronic lithium poisoning. Lithium poisoning may be defined by each study author, with no attempt to define, a priori, which inclusion criteria are appropriate for each study. Laboratory confirmation may consist of a measured serum lithium level greater than a threshold defined by the individual study author. We excluded studies recruiting patients who had been simultaneously exposed to multiple neurotoxic agents.

    Types of interventions

    Hemodialysis (including high flow intermittent hemodialysis and hemofiltration) or related methods of extracorporeal drug elimination (e.g. continuous arterio‐venous hemofiltration, continuous veno‐venous hemofiltration, continuous renal replacement therapy, etc.).

    For the purposes of this review, hemodialysis is defined as standard intermittent dialysis, using either a standard or high‐flux membrane, including slow continuous hemodialytic techniques (e.g. continuous arterio‐venous hemofiltration, continuous veno‐venous hemofiltration, continuous arterio‐venous hemodialysis, continuous veno‐venous hemodialysis, or slow continuous ultrafiltration).

    We did not consider studies of peritoneal dialysis because this intervention is rarely studied or recommended for lithium removal.

    We planned to compare results obtained with hemodialysis with results obtained with standard care. Standard care for lithium poisoning includes cessation of lithium administration, cessation of any medications that reduce lithium elimination (e.g. thiazide diuretics, angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors), intravenous hydration with isotonic crystalloid solution, and treatment of comorbid conditions.

    Had studies of slow continuous hemodialytic techniques been found, these would have been the subject of a planned subgroup analysis.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Neurologic abnormalities, using any valid assessment criteria (for example, formal neuropsychological assessment, validated tests of various domains of neurological function (e.g. Trail Making A and B, etc., Bowie 2006), formal cognitive assessment (e.g. Folstein mini‐mental status examination (Folstein 1975)), or structured physician assessment)

    Secondary outcomes

    • Mortality

    • Incidence and severity of disability (ability to work or perform other activities of daily living)

    • Length of hospital stay

    • Complications of therapy

    Search methods for identification of studies

    In order to reduce publication and retrieval bias we did not restrict our search by language, date or publication status.

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Injuries Group Trials Search Co‐ordinator and the authors searched the following:

    1. Cochrane Injuries Group Specialised Register (15 May 2015);

    2. Cochane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL, The Cochrane Library, issue 4, 2015);

    3. Ovid MEDLINE(R), Ovid MEDLINE(R) In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE(R) Daily and Ovid OLDMEDLINE(R) (1946 to 15 May 2015);

    4. EMBASE Classic + EMBASE (1947 to 15 May 2015);

    5. CINAHL Plus (1937 to 15 May 2015);

    6. ISI Web of Science: Science Citation Index Expanded (SCI‐EXPANDED) (1970 to 15 May 2015);

    7. ISI Web of Science: Conference Proceedings Citation Index‐Science (CPCI‐S) (1990 to 15 May 2015);

    8. ZETOC: British Library's table of contents of journal articles and conference proceedings (http://zetoc.mimas.ac.uk/ ) (15 May 2015);

    9. TOXNET (15 May 2015);

    10. Current Awareness in Clinical Toxicology (http://www.npis.org/cact.html) (January 1999 to 4 June 2015);

    11. ISRCTN (http://www.isrctn.com/) (4 June 2015);

    12. Clinicaltrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov) (4 June 2015).

    Search strategies are reported in Appendix 1.

    We adapted the MEDLINE search strategy illustrated in Appendix 1 as necessary for each of the other databases. We used search filters, a modified version of the "Cochrane Highly Sensitive Search Strategies", for identifying randomized trials in MEDLINE and Embase (Lefebvre 2011).

    Searching other resources

    The authors searched the reference lists of all relevant studies, chapters on lithium poisoning and extracorporeal removal of toxins from several major English‐language textbooks (Schneider 2000; Miller 2004; Osborn 2001; Thundiyil 2007; Hung 2014; Greller 2015) and all review articles published since 2010 (Bayliss 2010; McKnight 2012; Decker 2015). In addition, the authors contacted experts in the field in order to identify ongoing and unpublished studies as well as running general Internet searches, using terms selected from the search strategy, to identify grey literature.

    Data collection and analysis

    We performed a systematic review following the instructions given in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors independently scanned the titles and abstracts of all articles identified by the search strategy. If either or both authors identified the article as possibly meeting the inclusion criteria, the authors obtained the full text of the article from the University of Colorado Health Sciences Library. Both authors then reviewed the full text of each article to make a final determination whether the article met the inclusion criteria. Disagreement between review authors was resolved by consensus.

    Had we identified any articles meeting the inclusion criteria, we would have analysed them according to the criteria described in Appendix 2.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    We found 504 articles through database searching and an additional 36 articles through the review of references (Figure 1). We reviewed the full text copy of 25 articles. None were clinical trials. We did not identify any ongoing or completed trials in the trials registries searched. Several textbook chapters and review articles affirmed that no clinical trials have been conducted, and the experts contacted could identify no clinical trials that might meet the inclusion criteria.

    Included studies

    We identified no studies meeting the inclusion criteria.

    Excluded studies

    The available literature contains single case reports and retrospective observational studies. Neither study design meets the inclusion criteria. The retrospective studies are described individually in the Characteristics of excluded studies section.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We identified no studies meeting the inclusion criteria. If studies are identified in the future, they will be assessed according to the criteria in Appendix 2.

    Effects of interventions

    We identified no studies meeting inclusion criteria.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    To date, no clinical trials have been published evaluating the use of hemodialysis for the treatment of lithium poisoning. We did not identify any unpublished or ongoing trials.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    No randomized controlled trials were identified, and there are no studies included in this review.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The search for studies was conducted by an independent information scientist, employed by the Cochrane Injuries Group, and independent review of studies by the co‐author (JB) would likely have identified any relevant studies.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    In 2015, the Extracorporeal Treatments in Poisoning (EXTRIP) workgroup (www.extrip‐workgroup.org) published a systematic review and treatment recommendations for the use of dialysis in lithium poisoning (Decker 2015). A total of 166 articles were found, mostly case reports, yielding a very low quality of evidence for all recommendations. The workgroup concluded that lithium is dialyzable, and recommended dialysis in cases of severe lithium poisoning, defined by various combinations of elevated serum lithium concentrations, renal function, and alteration of mental status. The reason for the different conclusions between the EXTRIP review and the current review is methodological; in addition to clinical trial evidence, the EXTRIP workgroup's methodology also permits consideration of observational studies, case series, case reports, animal studies, and in vitro studies, and explicitly considers pharmacokinetic data when making treatment decisions (Lavergne 2012). EXTRIP authors note that their expert opinion was based on pharmacokinetic considerations and very low quality clinical data. In contrast, this review includes only randomized controlled trials.

    The authors of textbook chapters, review articles, and other published reports generally recommend the use of hemodialysis for select patients with lithium poisoning. Clinical indications vary, but typical recommendations include the use of hemodialysis for patients who have altered mental status, chronic lithium poisoning, and/or renal impairment. Most but not all authors acknowledge the lack of clinical trials data to support these treatment recommendations.
