Calcium channel blockers for pulmonary arterial hypertension

Abstract

    • Background

    Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is one of several forms of pulmonary hypertension: a chronic disease of the pulmonary vasculature. The mean age at diagnosis is around 50 years old, with increasing prevalence in people over 70 years old (10% to 17%). The median survival to be approximately seven years with one‐, three‐, five‐, and seven‐year survival rates from time of diagnostic right‐sided heart catheterization were 85%, 68%, 57%, and 49%, respectively. Several studies showed that calcium channel blockers (CCBs) reduce right ventricular hypertrophy and improve long‐term haemodynamics in PAH.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the clinical efficacy and harms of CCBs for people with PAH.

    Search methods

    The search strategy was provided by the Cochrane Airways Group Trials Search Co‐ordinator. The following databases were searched from their inception until September 2014: the Cochrane Airways Group Register of Trials (CAGR); the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Clinical Trials (CENTRAL) (The Cochrane Library,Issue 8 2014); MEDLINE (1948 to September 2014); EMBASE (1974 to September 2014); ClinicalTrials.gov; WHO trial portal; the Chinese Biomedical Databases (1979 to September 2014); CNKI: the Chinese Journals Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014), the Chinese Journals Full Text Database Century Journals (1979 to September 2014), the Chinese Doctoral Degree Thesis Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014), the Chinese Outstanding Master Degree Thesis Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014); VIP Database (1989 to September 2014) and WANFANG Database (1993 to September 2014). No language restriction was applied.

    Selection criteria

    Fully published randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing CCBs with placebo or other treatment, or comparing CCBs as an adjunct to other treatments with other treatments alone, in patients with PAH.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methods expected by Cochrane.

    Main results

    We found one RCT to include in this review but it was published only in abstract form with no data for evaluation.

    Authors' conclusions

    Currently, as there is lack of valid evidence, the efficacy and safety of CCBs is unproven in the treatment of PAH. However, the search strategy used for this review did identify four controlled clinical trials without randomization, three of which suggested treatment with CCBs may be beneficial in PAH. No adverse side effects of CCBs were reported. Confirmation of these findings by RCTs is recommended.

    • Calcium channel blockers for pulmonary arterial hypertension

    Background

    The blood pressure in the arteries of the lungs is normally much lower than the blood pressure in the rest of the body. Pulmonary hypertension occurs when the pulmonary arterial pressure gradually rises which causes damage to the heart, and can finally lead to heart failure and death. Calcium channel blockers are drugs which can relax the pulmonary arteries and decrease resistance and pressure of these vessels; this may benefit patients with pulmonary hypertension.

    What we did

    We searched for randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing calcium channel blockers with placebo.

    What we found

    We found only one trial. This trial has not been published in full and instead was presented as an abstract at a conference. Therefore we had only limited data from this trial to consider. The trial assessed the use of calcium channel blockers for pulmonary hypertension and did not reveal any improving effect on patient's exercise capacity (a primary outcome of this review) by calcium channel blocker.

    We believe more research is needed, specifically RCTs should be done on this topic. This is because evidence from several non‐randomized controlled studies suggest possible benefits, but we cannot be certain without RCTs.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is no valid evidence of benefit or harm in the use of CCB for PAH.

    Implications for research

    Some non‐randomised controlled clinical trials suggested a potential benefit of CCB in people with PAH. Since CCBs are currently inexpensive and are used worldwide, RCTs comparing effects of CCB with placebo or other interventions (e.g. inhalation of epoprostenol or nitric oxide), or RCTs comparing CCB as an adjunct to other interventions with other interventions alone are recommended. Besides exercise capacity, WHO/NYHA functional class, pulmonary haemodynamics or patients' survival, outcome measures should include adverse effects such as the incidence of edema, hypotension and cardiac arrhythmias to ensure safety.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is one of several forms of pulmonary hypertension; a chronic disease of the pulmonary vasculature, leading to a progressive increase in pulmonary vascular resistance, right heart failure and death (Rubin 1993; Simonneau 2009).

    Considerable international effort has been expended to classify pulmonary arterial hypertension. Most recently, the Nice classification (Simonneau 2013), which was a revision to an earlier classification from the World Health Organization (WHO), classified PAH in four categories:

    1. idiopathic PAH (IPAH) IPAH is rare disease with an incidence of about two to three per million per year and a prevalence of about 15 per million. Adult women are more prone to this condition than men.(Rudarakanchana 2001);

    2. heritable PAH;

    3. drug and toxin induced;

    4. PAH associated with connective tissue disease, HIV infection, portal hypertension, congenital heart disease, and schistosomiasis.

    Recently, several national surveys in Spain described the epidemiology of PAH. The mean age at diagnosis was around 50 years, with increasing prevalence in people over 70 years old (10% to 17% according to the registries). PAH is believed to be much more prevalent in emerging countries (Badesch 2010; Humbert 2006; Peacock 2007).

    The pathophysiological basis of the increase in pulmonary vascular resistance is hypertensive vascular disease in small arteries and pulmonary arterioles. Multiple cellular and molecular factors play vital roles in vasoconstriction and are involved in remodeling of the vessel wall (Escribano 2010).

    The most common presenting symptoms are dyspnoea and fatigue, followed by anginal chest pain, syncope and right heart failure (McLaughlin 2006). By expert consensus, PAH is regarded as a mean pulmonary artery pressure (mPAP) greater than 25 mm Hg in the setting of normal or reduced cardiac output and a normal pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PWP) less than 15 mmHg (Barst 2004). However, the delay between the first onset of symptoms and the diagnosis of PAH may be up to three years. Based on data from the Registry to Evaluate Early and Long‐term Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension Disease Management (REVEAL Registry), patients with PAH can expect a median survival time of more than seven years. Kaplan‐Meier survival rate estimates for one, three, five, and seven years from time of diagnostic right‐sided heart catheterization, were 85%, 68%, 57%, and 49%, respectively (Benza 2012). Crucial prognostic factors in PAH include symptoms (as assessed by WHO/New York Heart Association (NYHA) functional class), exercise endurance (six‐minute walk distance) and haemodynamics (right atrial pressure, cardiac index and mPAP) (McLaughlin 2004).

    Although significant progress has been made in understanding the epidemiology, pathophysiology, symptoms, diagnosis and prognosis of patients with PAH, leading to notable improvements in the quality and efficacy of clinical care, a cure is not currently available.

    Description of the intervention

    Calcium channel blockers (CCB) are agents that disrupt the movement of calcium (Ca2+) through calcium channels, which are classified into two categories: dihydropyridine and non‐dihydropyridine. It is possible to administer these drugs orally or intravenously. However, CCBs are now only recommended for patients with PAH who have a positive response during acute vaso‐reactivity testing and who show sustained haemodynamic improvement (Galie 2004).

    Side effects of these drugs may include but are not limited to: dizziness, headache, flushing, peripheral oedema, tachycardia, bradycardia, auriculo‐ventricular block (AVB), myocardial depression, haemodynamic deterioration, orthostatic hypotension, constipation and gingival hyperplasia (Bertram 2007; Editorial 2009; Elliott 2011; Furberg 1995; Laurence 2006; Rang 2003).

    How the intervention might work

    Media hypertrophy and pulmonary arterial stenosis, due to proliferation of smooth muscle cells, are constant features of all forms of pulmonary hypertension (Pietra 2004). Working by blocking voltage‐gated calcium channels (VGCCs) in blood vessels, CCBs decrease intracellular calcium, inhibiting the contraction of pulmonary artery smooth muscle cells (McMurtry 1976). The lower contraction of the vascular smooth muscle causes an increase in arterial diameter. Several studies showed that CCBs reduce right ventricular hypertrophy and improve long‐term haemodynamics in PAH, but only in a small group of patients who show sustained haemodynamic improvement (Rich 1987; Rich 1992).

    Why it is important to do this review

    CCBs are sometimes used in clinical practice, however there are some concerns over the use of CCBs in people with PAH. The arguments mainly focus on the effectiveness of this therapy and whether the side effects negate the possible benefits of CCB for these people. Consequently, there is a need to systematically review the use of CCBs for PAH.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the clinical efficacy and harms of CCBs for people with PAH.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials. We planned to consider studies of any route of administration (intravenous or oral), any dose of CCB and any duration of administration.

    Types of participants

    Adults and children with a diagnosis of pulmonary arterial hypertension who require medical treatment for their condition. We planned to include all eligible people, whether their response to the acute pulmonary vasodilator test was positive or not.

    We planned to include studies if diagnosis was based on clinical findings, echocardiography or computed tomography (CT) and ideally right heart catheterisation, although the latter is not explicitly an inclusion criteria. A resting mPAP ≥ 25 mmHg and a mean PWP < 15 mmHg through right heart catheterisation is always used as a gold standard to make the diagnosis of PAH, but we did not regard this as a necessary inclusion criterion.

    We planned to exclude studies in people with other severe co‐morbidities. We planned to exclude studies in people with pulmonary hypertension (PH) associated with left heart diseases; PH associated with lung respiratory diseases, hypoxia or both; chronic thromboembolic disease, embolic disease or both; and miscellaneous conditions, unless separate data on people with PAH are reported.

    Types of interventions

    We planned to consider trials in which a CCB was taken alone or in combination against any comparator in people with PAH.

    We planned to include the following interventions.

    1. CCB versus placebo.

    2. High‐dose versus low‐dose CCB.

    3. CCB versus anticoagulation treatment.

    4. CCB in combination with anticoagulation treatment versus anticoagulation treatment.

    5. CCB versus another pulmonary vasodilator.

    6. CCB in combination with another pulmonary vasodilator versus another pulmonary vasodilator.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Exercise capacity (six‐minute walk test/6MWD).

    2. WHO/NYHA functional class.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Cardiopulmonary haemodynamics: mean pulmonary artery pressure (PAP), pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), cardiac index (CI), cardiac output, systemic arterial oxygen saturation and systemic oxygen transport.

    2. Borg dyspnoea scores and dyspnoea‐fatigue ratings.

    3. Signs and symptoms of pulmonary hypertension.

    4. Pulmonary function studies.

    5. Mortality.

    6. Quality of life.

    7. Adverse events (including dizziness, headache, flushing, peripheral oedema, tachycardia, bradycardia, auriculo‐ventricular block (AVB), myocardial depression, haemodynamic deterioration, orthostatic hypotension, constipation, gingival hyperplasia).

    Reporting in the trial of one or more of the outcomes listed here was not an inclusion criterion for the review.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We identified trials by searches of the following databases:

    • Cochrane Airways Group Register of Trials (CAGR) (all years to September 2014);

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Clinical Trials (CENTRAL) (The Cochrane Library, issue 8 of 12, 2014)

    • Ovid MEDLINE (1948 to September 2014)

    • Ovid EMBASE (1974 to September 2014)

    We used the search strategy provided by the Cochrane Airways Group Trials Search Co‐ordinator to search MEDLINE, EMBASE and CENTRAL. We combined the search strategy with the Cochrane Highly Sensitive Search Strategy for identifying randomised trials in MEDLINE ( Lefebvre 2011).

    The database search strategies are listed in Appendix 1. We searched all databases from their inception up to September 2014, and there was no restriction on language of publication.

    Searching other resources

    We searched the Chinese Biomedical Databases (1979 to September 2014); CNKI: the Chinese Journals Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014), Chinese Journals Full Text Database Century Journals (1979 to September 2014), Chinese Doctoral Degree Thesis Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014), Chinese Outstanding Master Degree Thesis Full Text Database (1979 to September 2014); VIP Database (1989 to September 2014) and WANFANG Database (1993 to September 2014).

    We searched for ongoing trials in:

    1. ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov);

    2. Current Controlled Trials (www.controlled‐trials.com).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (ZF, YC) independently screened the titles and abstracts of all the articles identified by the literature search to identify possible included trials. The same review authors obtained full‐text documents and independently assessed whether the trials met the inclusion criteria. We resolved disagreements by discussion. If the search results had contained insufficient information to make a decision about eligibility, we planned to contact trial authors of the original reports to obtain further information. For the author we contacted, we recorded the trial ID, name and contact details of trialists, date of query to trialist, method of query (for example, telephone) and response of trialist, and whether the study was to de included or excluded. We recorded all of the information in an additional database. One review author (HL) was responsible for arbitrating when any disagreement on the methodological issues of trial selection arose.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (ZF, YC) planned to independently extract the methodological details and data from publications. Data for extraction include study title; design; study population size; duration; number of drop‐outs, withdrawals, loss to follow‐up, and participants analysed in the different treatment groups; inclusion and exclusion criteria; intervention (route and dosage) and outcomes (Higgins 2011a). When disagreement occurred, the third review author, Dr. Hanmin Liu would arbitrate.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    We planned to review the relevant studies and assess methodological quality using criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011b). We also planned to discuss areas of disagreement with the third author, Dr. Hanmin Liu, and to require that consensus be reached before analysis of the results. Criteria for assessing methodological quality are described in Appendix 2.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We expected to encounter both dichotomous and continuous data. We planned to analyse different comparisons separately. We planned to use the risk ratio (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) and control events rates for reporting dichotomous data. We planned to treat short ordinal scales (for example, WHO functional class, NYHA class) as dichotomous measures (we planned to arrange the data into three categories: improvement, unchanged or deterioration in class). We planned to analyse linear scales (for example, cardiopulmonary haemodynamics) as continuous data. We planned to express continuous data as mean differences (MD) with 95% CI.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was planned to be the individual.

    Dealing with missing data

    For missing data on prespecified primary outcomes, we planned to contact the study investigators to request the data. If these were not available, we planned to perform sensitivity analyses to assess how sensitive results are to reasonable changes in the assumptions that are made. We also planned to address the potential impact of missing data on the findings of the review in the discussion section.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to assess clinical and methodological heterogeneity before pooling. If data were similar enough, we planned to carry out assessment for statistical heterogeneity using the Chi2 test with significance being set at P < 0.1. We planned to use the I2 statistic to estimate the total variation across studies (Higgins 2003). We planned to use the following thresholds to describe heterogeneity: I2 < 40% may not be important heterogeneity; 30% to 60% as moderate heterogeneity; 50% to 90% as substantial heterogeneity; and 75% to 100% as considerable heterogeneity (Deeks 2011). If there was evidence of statistical heterogeneity, we planned to perform subgroup analyses to determine the possible reason(s). We planned to perform a sensitivity analysis to explore whether the heterogeneity was a result of low quality trials or not. If so, we planned to exclude the lowest quality trials.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to create standard funnel plots for the primary outcome(for example, 6MWD) to investigate the potential for publication bias influencing the analysis. We planned to assess reporting bias according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Sterne 2011):

    • no ‐ low risk of reporting bias: all outcomes were reported in detail;

    • probably yes ‐ moderate risk of reporting bias: at least one of the outcomes are mentioned but not in detail; or

    • yes ‐ high risk of reporting bias: at least one of the outcomes are not reported.

    We planned to resolve disagreements between review authors arising at any stage by discussion or with the help of a third party if necessary.

    Data synthesis

    In the absence of significant heterogeneity we planned to combine results using mean difference for continuous data, and odds ratio (OR) for dichotomous data. If there was significant heterogeneity (for example, the ratio of the Chi2 test and its degrees of freedom is greater than 1.0, and I2 > 30%), we planned to use the random‐effects model to assess treatment effect. We planned for data entry to be carried out by two review authors independently and to analyse the data using Review Manager 5.3 (RevMan 2014).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We intended to explore the following potential sources of heterogeneity using subgroup analyses or meta‐regression:

    1. IPAH versus non‐IPAH;

    2. severity of the PAH;

    3. different dosages or usage of CCB;

    4. different comparators;

    5. different age groups of participants.

    Sensitivity analysis

    If we found a sufficient number of trials we planned to perform sensitivity analyses to test the robustness of the evidence as follows:

    1. exclusion of studies with inadequate concealment of allocation;

    2. exclusion of studies in which the outcome evaluation was not blinded;

    3. comparing the difference of pooling analysis results by using a fixed‐effect model and a random‐effects model. Robust evidence should not be transposed by changing the effect model.

    Results

    Description of studies

    See: Characteristics of included studies; Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Results of the search

    Electronic searches yielded a total of 1789 references (Figure 1). We retrieved the full text of 13 studies. One study met the inclusion criteria (Yatsyshyn 2010). However, Yatsyshyn 2010 was published only in abstract form and the authors are not contactable. We excluded 12 studies with reasons (see Characteristics of excluded studies. Searches in databases of Chinese literature did not yield any studies that could be included.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    There is extremely limited information on the use of CCB for PAH. Given that we could only include one RCT which published only in abstract form, and the authors are not contactable for study details, we were not able to perform a valid quality evaluation for this study. Yatsyshyn 2010 randomized 67 people with PAH to two groups which received telmisartan (n = 33) or the CCB, nifedipine (n = 34). Results did not show any exercise capacity improvement due to CCB. Participantsin the nifedipine‐group deteriorated by 22.3 meters, while participants in the telmisartan group improved by 14.4 meters in the six‐minute walk test (mean nifedipine‐corrected effect + 35.9 meters in favour of telmisartan, 95% CI 21.5 to 95.0; P = 0.24).This systematic review found no evidence that treatment with CCB improves exercise capacity, WHO/NYHA functional class or pulmonary haemodynamics in people with PAH.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The sole RCT of CCB for PAH that we found (Yatsyshyn 2010) was published in abstract form only, and the authors are not contactable, so we are not able to evaluate this study.

    Quality of the evidence

    The RCT we found was published only in abstract form which cannot be used for valid quality evaluation. Although some clinical trials (Rich 1992; Rozkovec 1988; Wimmer 1988) highlighted some possible benefits associated with the use of CCB, they cannot be used to establish practice recommendations, and future RCTs are needed.

    Potential biases in the review process

    As the sole RCT, Yatsyshyn 2010, which meets the inclusion criteria was published only in abstract form, and the authors are not contactable, we have not been able to obtain the relevant study data to assess its quality and validity. Including this study may introduce bias, but if this study was reported in full text in the future, or we successfully contact the authors for details of this study, we can consider re‐evaluating it in the updated version of this review.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    The search strategy used for this review did identify another four controlled clinical trials, but no randomized controlled clinical trial data. Three of these studies found CCB can be beneficial to the pulmonary vascular haemodynamics or survival of people with PAH. No adverse side effects of CCB were reported in these four clinical trials.

    Rozkovec 1988 compared the treatment effects of epoprostenol (prostacyclin) for PAH with nifedipine in a short term study. Incremental infusion of eporpostenol and sublingual nifedipine were given sequentially to 14 participants with moderate to severe pulmonary hypertension. Both eporpostenol and nifedipine caused a significant fall in pulmonary vascular resistance and pressure and a rise in cardiac output, but the observed responses with nifedipine were less prominent than those with epoprostenol.

    Rich 1992 treated 64 people with primary pulmonary hypertension with high doses of CCB. Survival rates of people who responded to CCB were compared with those of people who did not respond to CCB, and also with people enrolled in the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Registry on primary pulmonary hypertension. Ninety four percent of people who responded to CCB survived, but only 55% of people who did not respond to CCB survived. The survival rate of people who responded to CCB was significantly higher than both that of people who did not respond to CCB, and that of people on the NIH registry.

    Wimmer 1988 investigated 14 patients with PAH between two to 20 years old. Nifedipine effects were compared with oxygen effects which showed nifedipine had a more pronounced and beneficial effect with a selective action on the pulmonary vascular bed.

    In contrast to the above clinical trials, Krasuski 2005 did not show any benefit from CCB for PAH individuals. The study enrolled 60 PAH patients, all of whom received inhalation of Nitric Oxide (NO). At baseline, 31 patients were given medications with vasodilating properties (28 of them received CCB) and 29 patients were not. Similar reductions in mean pulmonary artery pressure and pulmonary vascular resistance were seen between patients receiving and not receiving vasodilators.
