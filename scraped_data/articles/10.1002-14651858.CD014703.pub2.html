Endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery

    • Background

    Spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA) occurs when a tear in the inner layer of the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) allows blood to flow between the layers of the SMA, forcing the layers apart, and creating two lumens. Abdominal pain is the most prevalent clinical manifestation. Other people may have no symptoms or experience nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, or blood in their stools. For people with SIDSMA who are not suspected of intestinal necrosis or intra‐abdominal bleeding, medical treatment and endovascular therapy are the main treatment options. There is no consensus on the optimum first‐line management strategy.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA).

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. The latest search date was 3 August 2021.

    Selection criteria

    We planned to include all randomized controlled trials (RCTs) which compared endovascular therapy and medical treatments for SIDSMA. We planned to exclude studies where participants were treated with open surgery.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methods. Our primary outcomes were endovascular intervention rate and recurrent abdominal pain. Our secondary outcomes were open surgery rate, remodeling rate of SMA, new aneurysm formation of SMA, SMA occlusion, new dissection of SMA, death, symptom relief rate and complications of endovascular therapy. We planned to use GRADE to assess certainty of evidence for each outcome.

    Main results

    We did not identify any RCTs to include in any analysis.

    Authors' conclusions

    We were not able to include any RCTs that compared endovascular therapy versus medical treatment in people with SIDSMA. High‐quality RCTs that evaluate the benefits and harms of these interventions are needed to help determine the optimal strategy for managing SIDSMA.

    • Endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery

    Key message

    There is not enough evidence to compare the benefits and harms of the two main options for treating people with spontaneous dissection of the mesenteric artery (SIDSMA).

    Why is this question important?

    Blood vessels are made up of different layers. If one of these layers rips or ruptures, blood can flow between the layers, pushing the layers apart and creating two channels for the blood to flow through, which stops the blood from delivering oxygen and nutrients. A spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA) occurs after a rupture in one of the main blood vessels in the abdomen (stomach area), called the superior mesenteric artery, happens for no clear reason and not in any other main vessels. People may not show any symptoms, or they may have abdominal pain, feel sick, vomit, have diarrhea or blood in their stools.

    There are two main treatment options for people with SIDSMA. The first is medical treatment, where people are given nutrients through a line straight into the blood. This means the stomach and intestines (where food is digested) do not need as much blood. This gives the ruptured vessels time to recover. The second option is endovascular therapy, where a small incision is made in the groin area or in the elbow pit and a small tube (stent) is placed into the blood vessel to seal the rupture and restore normal blood flow. Medical treatment is not always successful and the stent may not continue to maintain normal blood flow for a long time after endovascular therapy.

    It is important to know about the benefits and harms of both of these options so that doctors and people with SIDSMA can make the best decision about treatment.

    What did we do?

    We searched the literature for randomized controlled trials which compared endovascular therapy with medical treatment for managing SIDSMA. In randomized controlled trials the treatments people receive are decided at random, and these give the most reliable evidence about treatment effects.

    What did we find?

    We did not find any studies comparing endovascular therapy with medical treatment.

    How certain are we with the evidence?

    There is a lack of evidence to help answer this question. High‐quality studies are needed to help inform the best treatment options.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is current to 3 August 2021.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    We were not able to include any randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that compared endovascular therapy versus medical treatment in people with spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA). Currently, there is insufficient high‐quality evidence to help determine the benefits and harms of the two therapy options. We found one ongoing RCT. Until results are published, treatment option decisions will be informed by non‐randomized studies, such as cohort studies.

    Implications for research

    Our review highlights the need for a robust RCT to address the optimal first‐line therapy for SIDSMA. Designing and conducting a high‐quality study for this topic may be challenging. The most important outcomes to be measured are endovascular intervention rate and recurrent abdominal pain. Other key factors to be evaluated are morphological changes of the superior mesenteric artery (SMA), symptom relief rate, and complications of the two treatments. A multicenter RCT would provide high‐level evidence and is a useful way to validate the superiority of the two therapies. Designing a multicenter study would need data from prospective studies. Although there are ongoing prospective studies, their designs are based on data from retrospective studies. The estimation of an accurate sample size for a multicenter RCT may be difficult due to the lack of data from prospective studies. Furthermore, given the nature of endovascular therapy where stents are implanted, there is always concern about the durability of stents in the long term. Therefore, possible follow‐up periods could be one year, three years, five years, or longer. In addition, a cost analysis should be considered as an outcome in future studies.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA) occurs when a tear in the inner layer of the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) allows blood to flow between the layers of the SMA, forcing the layers apart, and creating two lumens. The true lumen is lined by endothelium; the false lumen is within the layers of the media (middle layer of the SMA). Spontaneous dissection refers to its unknown etiology that is not due to trauma, iatrogenic injury, or any other known cause. Isolated dissection means that it is not associated with other dissections, such as aortic or celiac artery dissection. The clinical presentations vary extensively, and can be asymptomatic, often identified as an incidental finding. People who are asymptomatic account for over 10% of those with SIDSMA in the literature (Kimura 2018); their SIDSMA can be managed with observation (Kimura 2017). Over 90% of people with symptoms develop abdominal pain (Qiu 2019). Other symptoms include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and hematochezia (blood in stools). In severe cases, there is rupture of the SMA, peritonitis, bowel infarction, or even death (Heo 2017; Sartelet 2003; Wang 2018). Historically, SIDSMA was not frequently recognized. Despite the lack of specific laboratory tests, the increased use of computed tomography has facilitated its diagnosis. As a result, an increasing number of cohort studies, mainly from Korea, China, and Japan, have recently been reported, which suggests an underestimation of the incidence of this disease, and a genetic predisposition (Garrett 2014; Kimura 2018; Luan 2016). Most of the people are relatively young (in their 50s) and male (over 80% (Bjorck 2017)). Tobacco use, hypertension, and the angle between the SMA and distal aorta seem to be associated with this disease (Wu 2017). The SMA originates from the aorta at an acute angle, and runs roughly parallel to the aorta. The dissection usually takes place 1 cm to 3 cm from the opening of the SMA, where the SMA transitions, with a curve from a stationary retroperitoneal position into a mobile root; and high shear stress is placed on the anterior vascular wall around the convex portion of the SMA (Park 2011). The false lumen of the dissection can compress the true lumen, leading to stenosis of the true lumen, and subsequent bowel ischemia. The false lumen itself may be patent or thrombosed, and can form an aneurysm. More than five classification systems, based on morphology, have been developed for SIDSMA (Jia 2017). The earliest was reported in Sakamoto 2007. More sophisticated classification systems are now being used, due to the increased numbers of cases reported in recent years, and additional imaging detection of SIDSMA (Qiu 2019).

    Description of the intervention

    Traditionally, there are three ways to treat the disease (Garrett 2014; Wang 2018). These include medical treatment, endovascular therapy, and open surgery. We will not investigate open surgery in this review, since it usually takes place when bowel necrosis or intra‐abdominal hemorrhage is suspected, or when cases are complicated. The principle of medical treatment is to reduce the demand of blood supply of the intestines by fasting and providing parenteral nutrition; endovascular treatment relies on the use of stents to restore the patency of the SMA and normal blood perfusion.

    Medical treatment

    The majority of people with SIDSMA are managed conservatively. The core of medical treatment is fasting. Antihypertensive drugs, analgesia, anticoagulation, and antiplatelet therapy may also be administered, if necessary (Ahn 2019; Heo 2017; Loeffler 2017; Mizuno 2018). The blood supply to the intestines is reduced due to the stenosis of the true lumen of the SMA; fasting reduces the blood flow needed by the intestines. If the need and supply of blood is met, symptoms should be alleviated, providing time for the body to develop collateral circulation (for example, by recruiting collateral flow pathways). Control of hypertension may reduce the risk of further tearing the vascular wall. If there is a high risk of thrombosis in the distal part of the SMA, anticoagulation and antiplatelet therapy could reduce the risk. It may take several days to evaluate the effects of medical treatments. The rate of pain resolution can reach up to 96% (Heo 2017). However, deterioration is possible, and is reported to take place in over 10% of the people under medical management, who then require endovascular therapy or open surgery (Jang 2020; Wang 2018). Therefore, close observation during medical treatments is necessary. Approximately 20% of people undergoing medical management suffer recurrence of abdominal pain, at a median follow‐up of 103 days (Heo 2017). Medical treatments are successful when the symptoms are relieved. Complete remodeling shows full recovery from the SIDSMA, and is defined as the absence of residual arterial dissection, and the absence of arterial narrowing on the follow‐up computed tomography image (Heo 2017; Qiu 2019). The complete remodeling rate following medical treatments has been reported to range from 25% to 64%, with 2% to 8% of people developing aneurysms in the SMA (Heo 2017; Loeffler 2017; Tomita 2016; Wang 2018). This unsatisfactory remodeling rate casts doubts on the long‐term effect of medical treatments.

    Endovascular therapy

    The first case of endovascular therapy was recorded in 2000 (Leung 2000). With major advances in imaging and therapeutic interventions in the last two decades, an increasing number of cases treated in this way have been reported. Endovascular therapy aims to achieve normal blood perfusion by restoring the patency of the SMA. In addition, the use of stents could stabilize vessel integrity, to facilitate remodeling and prevent late degeneration and aneurysm formation. Both transfemoral artery access and left brachial artery access are widely used. Bare, self‐expanding stents are preferred for the sake of preserving the remaining patent SMA branches (Luan 2016). Coil embolization may also be used for large, patent, false lumen or dissecting aneurysms (Jia 2019). Long‐term antiplatelet therapy is usually recommended as adjunctive therapy. Endovascular therapy provides satisfactory short‐term results. Both technical success rate and symptom relief rates were approximately 95%, with a complication rate less than 5% (Luan 2016; Qiu 2019). The complete remodeling rate has been reported to be over 80% (Qiu 2019). Nevertheless, stent patency varies during follow‐up, and there are limited data on mid‐ to long‐term adverse events, especially for stenosis, migration, and fracture of stents (Hang 2019; Kim 2017; Qiu 2019).

    How the intervention might work

    SIDSMA is a significant pathology affecting the SMA, and is likely to cause bowel ischemia. Medical treatments increase the tolerance of bowel ischemia, and provide a window of time to form collateral circulation. Endovascular therapy uses stents to reopen the area of the SMA compressed by the false lumen, and treat intestinal ischemia directly.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Currently, both endovascular therapy and medical treatments are used in people who present with SIDSMA, and there is no consensus on the optimum first‐line management strategy. There are concerns over longer‐term outcomes for both strategies. Medical treatment is not always successful for everyone, and the remodeling rate may not be satisfactory during follow‐up. Endovascular therapy may be more effective initially, but long‐term data on patency of stents are lacking. To date, there is no Cochrane Review that assesses the efficacy and safety of these two management strategies. This review aims to present the current best evidence comparing these approaches to aid decision‐making and improve management strategies for healthcare professionals and people with SIDSMA.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered randomized controlled trials (RCTs) assessing endovascular therapy and medical treatments for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA) for inclusion in this review. We planned to include studies involving a mixed population with SIDSMA and isolated dissection of the celiac artery if more than 80% of the participants had SIDSMA.

    Spontaneous dissection refers to unknown etiology that is not due to trauma, iatrogenic injury, or any other known cause. Isolated dissection means that it is not associated with other dissections, such as aortic dissection and celiac artery dissection.

    Types of participants

    We planned to include studies with participants who were diagnosed with SIDSMA and received either medical treatments or endovascular therapy as first‐line therapy. The diagnosis should have been based on computed tomography, digital subtraction angiography, or both. We set no limitations on participants' gender, age, ethnicity, or symptoms.

    We planned to exclude studies with participants treated with open surgery, as these cases generally involve bowel necrosis or intra‐abdominal hemorrhage.

    Types of interventions

    We planned to include studies that compared endovascular therapy (any technique) to medical treatment. We planned to include any endovascular therapy, with no restrictions on artery access, types of stents, or the application of coil embolizations. We planned no limitation on adjunctive drug administration, providing these were administered to both study arms.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Endovascular intervention rate (at 12 months)

    • Recurrent abdominal pain (measured by 10‐point visual analog scale (VAS), or 10‐point numerical rating scale (NRS), or other recognized scales at 12 months)

    Secondary outcomes

    • Open surgery rate (SIDSMA‐related open surgery)

    • Remodeling rate of the SMA (absence of residual arterial dissection and the absence of arterial narrowing); measured by computed tomography or digital subtraction angiography

    • New aneurysm formation of the SMA: measured by computed tomography or digital subtraction angiography

    • SMA occlusion (if patent at the start of follow‐up): measured by computed tomography or digital subtraction angiography

    • New dissection of the SMA: measured by computed tomography or digital subtraction angiography

    • Death (including death from any cause and SMA‐related death, including procedure‐related death)

    • Symptom relief rate (including abdominal pain measured by 10‐point visual analog scale (VAS), or 10‐point numerical rating scale (NRS), or other recognized scales; presence or absence of additional symptoms)

    • Complications of endovascular therapy (including access site problems, technical failure, stenosis, migration, and fracture of stents, measured by computed tomography or digital subtraction angiography)

    The time points we expected to report were to have been primarily guided by any included studies, but were likely to be one week and 30 days for symptom relief rate, and 12 months for the secondary outcomes.

    Secondary outcomes have been adapted from Heo 2017; Loeffler 2017; and Qiu 2019.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist conducted systematic searches of the following databases for randomized controlled trials, without language, publication year or publication status restrictions:

    • Cochrane Vascular Specialized Register via the Cochrane Register of Studies (CRS‐Web);

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via the Cochrane Register of Studies Online (CRSO);

    • MEDLINE (Ovid MEDLINE Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE Daily and Ovid MEDLINE);

    • Embase Ovid;

    • CINAHL EBSCO.

    The Information Specialist developed search strategies for other databases from the search strategy designed for MEDLINE. Where appropriate, they were combined with adaptations of the highly sensitive search strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying randomized controlled trials and controlled clinical trials as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Lefebvre 2022). Search strategies for major databases are provided in Appendix 1.

    The Information Specialist searched the following trials registries:

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (who.int/trialsearch);

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov).

    The most recent searches were run on 3 August 2021.

    Searching other resources

    We searched references of relevant articles retrieved from the electronic search for additional citations.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Four review authors (CQ, YH, QZ, and TS) independently screened all titles and abstracts identified from the searches to identify those that might meet the inclusion criteria. We retrieved the full text of studies identified as potentially relevant by at least one author. The same review authors independently screened the full‐text articles for inclusion or exclusion. We resolved any disagreement by discussion, or if necessary, consulted at least one extra review author (HZ, LT, DL, or ZW). We illustrated the study selection process in a PRISMA diagram (Liberati 2009). We reported all articles excluded after full‐text assessment in a 'Characteristics of excluded studies' table and provided the reasons for their exclusion. We planned to collate multiple reports of the same study, so that each study, not each report, was the unit of interest in the review.

    Data extraction and management

    If studies were identified, we planned that two review authors (CQ and YH) would independently extract data from the eligible studies, using an adapted data extraction form provided by Cochrane Vascular. Any disagreements would have been resolved by discussion, or if necessary, we would consult with at least one extra review author (HZ, LT, DL, or ZW). Two review authors (CQ and YH) planned to enter extracted data into Review Manager (Review Manager 2020). An additional two review authors (QZ and TS), had planned to check for accuracy and consistency against the data presented in the study papers.

    We planned to extract the following data from the selected studies.

    • Trial design

    • Diagnosis methods of SIDSMA

    • Type of intervention (medical treatment and endovascular therapy)

    • Demographic characteristics of participants

    • Primary outcomes

    • Secondary outcomes

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    For all included studies, we planned that two review authors (CQ and YH) would independently assess each study for risk of bias according to the following criteria, as recommended by the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021).

    • Random sequence generation (selection bias)

    • Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    • Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)*

    • Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)*

    • Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    • Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    • Other sources of bias

    We intended to judge all selected studies as having low, high, or unclear risk of bias based on these criteria. We planned to resolve disagreements by discussion, or if necessary, by consulting with at least one extra review author (HZ, LT, DL, or ZW).

    * Blinding of participants and personnel and blinding of outcome assessment are not possible due to the nature of the types of intervention (i.e. presence of stents on imaging indicates endovascular treatment). Therefore, we planned to consider these two domains as at high risk of bias.

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    If studies were included, we planned to use risk ratio (RR) to express results for dichotomous outcome measures, and associated 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for uncertainty of the point estimate of effects.

    Continuous data

    If studies were included, we planned to calculate mean difference (MD) and standard deviation (SD), with corresponding 95% CIs for continuous outcome measures. We planned to use standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95% CIs to combine outcomes from trials that measured the same outcome using different scales (Higgins 2021).

    Time‐to‐event data

    If studies were included, we planned to use survival analysis to report time‐to‐event data, and express the intervention effect as a hazard ratio (HR), with associated 95% CIs.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We planned to base the unit of analysis on the individual participant.

    Dealing with missing data

    We planned to contact the study authors for any missing or unreported data. If there was no response, we planned to record missing and unavailable data for each selected study, and to use intention‐to‐treat analysis when possible. As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to assess the inter‐study statistical heterogeneity by visual inspection of forest plots. We also planned to use the Chi² test and I² statistic to measure the amount of heterogeneity for both the primary and secondary outcomes. We planned to consider both the magnitude and direction of effects and strength of evidence for heterogeneity (e.g. the P value from the Chi² test, or a confidence interval for I²) according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021). If we detected heterogeneity, we planned to explore the reasons for this through subgroup analysis. As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to investigate publication bias via funnel plots if there were at least 10 studies included in the review, as recommended by the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021).

    Data synthesis

    We planned to carry out statistical analysis using Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020). If there was a low degree of inter‐study heterogeneity, we planned to use a fixed‐effect model of meta‐analysis to synthesize the data. If the included studies were similar, but substantial statistical heterogeneity was detected (where the Chi2 test P value was less than 0.1, or the I² test had a value of 75% or more), we planned to use a random‐effects meta‐analysis. We planned to only undertake meta‐analyses if the treatments, participants, and the underlying clinical question were similar enough for pooling to make sense. If meta‐analysis was not possible, we planned to report the results using a narrative synthesis. As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    If an adequate number of studies (at least two studies) were identified, we planned to perform subgroup analyses according to the morphology classification of SIDSMA and participants' symptoms (symptomatic, asymptomatic). As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We intended to repeat the analyses by excluding studies with mixed SIDSMA and isolated dissection of celiac artery populations; and by including only studies with low risk of selection bias, to test whether key methodological factors or decisions had affected the main result. For the purpose of this analysis, we planned to include trials judged as low risk of bias for both selection bias domains, that is both random sequence generation and allocation concealment, in the sensitivity analysis. As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We planned to prepare a summary of findings table to present the evidence for endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for people with SIDSMA. We intended to include the following outcomes in our table: endovascular intervention rate; recurrent abdominal pain; open surgery rate; remodeling rate of SMA; new aneurysm formation of SMA; SMA occlusion, and new dissection of the SMA. We have included an example of a summary of findings table in this review (Table 1). We planned to assess the certainty of the evidence using criteria devised by the GRADE approach and use GRADEpro GDT software to create the table (GRADEpro GDT). We would have assigned one of four levels of certainty (high, moderate, low, or very low) to the evidence; based on risk of bias, inconsistency, imprecision, indirectness and publication bias (Atkins 2004; Schünemann 2021). As no studies were included, we did not do this.

    Results of the search

    We did not find any randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to include. Database searches identified 94 records, and we assessed 86 records after removing duplicates. We excluded two studies (NCT03916965; NCT05171842), and assessed one study as ongoing (ChiCTR1900022854).

    Included studies

    We did not find any studies that met the inclusion criteria.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded two studies as they were not randomized (NCT03916965; NCT05171842). NCT03916965 was a single‐centre observational prospective study focusing on the patency of stents placed in the SMA and SMA remodeling. NCT05171842 was a single‐arm, multicenter prospective trial with the purpose of demonstrating the superiority of endovascular therapy. See Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Ongoing studies

    We assessed one study as ongoing as no results have been released yet (ChiCTR1900022854). This directly compares medical treatment and endovascular treatment for SIDSMA. See Ongoing studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    It was not possible to assess the risk of bias as we did not identify any studies that met the inclusion criteria.

    Effects of interventions

    We did not include any RCTs, so it was not possible to investigate the benefits or harms of endovascular therapy versus medical treatment for spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We did not include any randomized controlled trials (RCTs).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We did not find any RCTs that reported the results of medical treatment and endovascular therapy for treating spontaneous isolated dissection of the superior mesenteric artery (SIDSMA). Through our search strategies, we assessed 86 study reports and identified one ongoing RCT that compared medical treatment and endovascular therapy. As no results have been released yet, we were not able to include this study in any analysis.

    Given the low overall prevalence of SIDSMA, we acknowledge that designing and conducting an appropriate study for this topic is difficult. The growing use of computed tomography in recent years has made SIDSMA diagnosis easier, resulting in a rise in the number of people diagnosed with the condition. This fact emphasizes the significance of the investigation and encourages additional research. This reinforces the importance of this review and serves as an incentive for further investigation.

    Quality of the evidence

    We did not find any RCTs that we were able to include, so it was not possible to assess methodological quality, risk of bias or the certainty of the evidence.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We did not identify any RCTs that were eligible for inclusion. The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist performed a comprehensive search of the literature and we selected studies according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    An earlier systematic review was published on this topic (Wang 2018). Wang 2018 included 35 studies (seven retrospective cohort studies and 28 case series) and reported that conservative management was the most common initial treatment, accounting for 87% (95% CI 0.76 to 0.96) of symptomatic SIDSMA patient management. During follow‐up, approximately 12% (95% CI 0.06 to 0.19) of symptomatic people with SIDSMA treated conservatively required secondary intervention. No asymptomatic people treated conservatively required secondary intervention. In terms of morphologic changes, complete remodeling was reported in 25% (95% CI 0.19 to 0.32) of people, while 12% (95% CI 0.05 to 0.20) of people had morphologic progression. The pooled estimate of long‐term all‐cause mortality of both asymptomatic and symptomatic people with SIDSMA was 1% (95% CI 0.00 to 0.02).

    There are several retrospective studies with a moderate sample size (> 50 participants) that report treatment results. In one single‐center study with 88 symptomatic people and 28 asymptomatic people, all asymptomatic people received medical treatments and had no abdominal pain in the follow‐up. Of the symptomatic people, five (6%) received primary open surgery or SMA stenting and 83 people received primary medical treatment, with 96% achieving pain resolution (Heo 2017). After pain resolution, 16 people who had primary medical treatment experienced a late recurrence of abdominal pain. Two people (12%) required open surgery. Follow‐up computed tomography angiography showed partial or complete remodeling in 63% of both the symptomatic and asymptomatic people. Three single‐center studies compared endovascular therapy and conservative treatments (Gao 2021; Qiu 2019; Shi 2020). Gao 2021 included 88 symptomatic people and six asymptomatic people. People with severe mesenteric ischemia requiring emergent endovascular repair were excluded from this study. Qiu 2019 included 106 symptomatic people and six asymptomatic people. Shi 2020 only included symptomatic people. All three reported that the remodeling rate was significantly higher in endovascular therapy. Two studies found that more adverse events occurred in the conservative group (Gao 2021; Qiu 2019). Qiu 2019 reported that the stent patency was over 90% during a mean follow‐up of 30 months. In Shi 2020, unsuccessful conservative treatment was detected in 50% of low‐risk people who had primary conservative treatments. Dissection length > 50 mm was an independent risk factor for failed conservative treatment. There were two multicenter studies (Mizuno 2018; Loeffler 2017). Loeffler 2017 included 162 symptomatic people and 65 asymptomatic people and excluded SIDSMA with bowel ischemia; Mizuno 2018 included 221 people and excluded SIDSMA with aneurysm formation. Both studies reported that most of the symptomatic people had relief of symptoms with conservative treatment. The two studies had limited follow‐up, and there was no information on remodeling (Loeffler 2017; Mizuno 2018).

    In addition to these retrospective studies, three prospective studies are now underway (NCT03916965; NCT05171842; ChiCTR1900022854). NCT03916965 is a single‐center observational study that aims to collect data on the patency of stents deployed in the SMA and SMA remodeling. Secondary outcomes include relief of symptoms and recurrence of the dissection. This study is enrolling both symptomatic and asymptomatic people and intends to enroll over 100 people with a two‐year follow‐up; it is now in the follow‐up phase. NCT05171842 is a single‐arm multicenter study and will enroll only symptomatic people, excluding people with peritonitis. In earlier publications, the reintervention rate for conservative treatments was estimated to be 12%, and the reintervention rate for endovascular therapy was estimated to be 4%. Therefore, a total of 136 participants are needed to demonstrate the superiority of endovascular treatments (Type I error = 0.025; Type II error = 0.1, 10% loss during the follow‐up). Stent patency rate and dissection remodeling will also be collected at the same time. ChiCTR1900022854 is an RCT, identified as ongoing for this systematic review, that will enroll 45 symptomatic participants in each group. It excludes people with peritonitis or severe intestinal ischemia. The primary outcome is the one‐year reintervention rate. Secondary outcomes include disease‐related adverse events, the reintervention rate, imaging outcome, and symptom remission rate. These three prospective studies will provide useful information for treating SIDSMA once the results are available.
