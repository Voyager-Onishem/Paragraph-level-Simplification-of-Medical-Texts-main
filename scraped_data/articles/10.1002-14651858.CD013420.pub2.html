Psychological interventions for asthma in children and adolescents

    • Background

    Rates of asthma are high in children and adolescents, and young people with asthma generally report poorer health outcomes than those without asthma. Young people with asthma experience a range of challenges that may contribute to psychological distress. This is compounded by the social, psychological, and developmental challenges experienced by all people during this life stage. Psychological interventions (such as behavioural therapies or cognitive therapies) have the potential to reduce psychological distress and thus improve behavioural outcomes such as self‐efficacy and medication adherence. In turn, this may reduce medical contacts and asthma attacks.

    Objectives

    To determine the efficacy of psychological interventions for modifying health and behavioural outcomes in children with asthma, compared with usual treatment, treatment with no psychological component, or no treatment.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Airways Group Specialised Register (including CENTRAL, CRS, MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, CINAHL EBSCO, AMED EBSCO), proceedings of major respiratory conferences, reference lists of included studies, and online clinical databases. The most recent search was conducted on 22 August 2022.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing psychological interventions of any duration with usual care, active controls, or a waiting‐list control in male and female children and adolescents (aged five to 18 years) with asthma.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methods. Our primary outcomes were 1. symptoms of anxiety and depression, 2. medical contacts, and 3. asthma attacks. Our secondary outcomes were 1. self‐reported asthma symptoms, 2. medication use, 3. quality of life, and 4. adverse events/side effects.

    Main results

    We included 24 studies (1639 participants) published between 1978 and 2021. Eleven studies were set in the USA, five in China, two in Sweden, three in Iran, and one each in the Netherlands, UK, and Germany. Participants' asthma severity ranged from mild to severe. Three studies included primary school‐aged participants (five to 12 years), two included secondary school‐aged participants (13 to 18 years), and 18 included both age groups, while one study was unclear on the age ranges. Durations of interventions ranged from three days to eight months. One intervention was conducted online and the rest were face‐to‐face.

    Meta‐analysis was not possible due to clinical heterogeneity (interventions, populations, outcome tools and definitions, and length of follow‐up). We tabulated and summarised the results narratively with reference to direction, magnitude, and certainty of effects. The certainty of the evidence was very low for all outcomes. A lack of information about scale metrics and minimal clinically important differences for the scales used to measure anxiety, depression, asthma symptoms, medication use, and quality of life made it difficult to judge clinical significance.

    Primary outcomes

    Four studies (327 participants) reported beneficial or mixed effects of psychological interventions versus controls for symptoms of anxiety, and one found little to no difference between groups (104 participants). Two studies (166 participants) that evaluated symptoms of depression both reported benefits of psychological interventions compared to controls. Three small studies (92 participants) reported a reduction in medical contacts, but two larger studies (544 participants) found little or no difference between groups in this outcome. Two studies (107 participants) found that the intervention had an important beneficial effect on number of asthma attacks, and one small study (22 participants) found little or no effect of the intervention for this outcome.

    Secondary outcomes

    Eleven studies (720 participants) assessed asthma symptoms; four (322 participants) reported beneficial effects of the intervention compared to control, five (257 participants) reported mixed or unclear findings, and two (131 participants) found little or no difference between groups. Eight studies (822 participants) reported a variety of medication use measures; six of these studies (670 participants) found a positive effect of the intervention versus control, and the other two (152 participants) found little or no difference between the groups. Across six studies (653 participants) reporting measures of quality of life, the largest three (522 participants) found little or no difference between the groups. Where findings were positive or mixed, there was evidence of selective reporting (2 studies, 131 participants). No studies provided data related to adverse effects.

    Authors' conclusions

    Most studies that reported symptoms of anxiety, depression, asthma attacks, asthma symptoms, and medication use found a positive effect of psychological interventions versus control on at least one measure. However, some findings were mixed, it was difficult to judge clinical significance, and the evidence for all outcomes is very uncertain due to clinical heterogeneity, small sample sizes, incomplete reporting, and risk of bias. There is limited evidence to suggest that psychological interventions can reduce the need for medical contact or improve quality of life, and no studies reported adverse events.

    It was not possible to identify components of effective interventions and distinguish these from interventions showing no evidence of an effect due to substantial heterogeneity. Future investigations of evidence‐based psychological techniques should consider standardising outcomes to support cross‐comparison and better inform patient and policymaker decision‐making.

    • Psychological treatment to help children and adolescents with asthma

    Key messages

    1. The available evidence suggests that psychological interventions could be beneficial for children and adolescents with asthma.
    2. Due to the differences between studies, it was very hard to compare the data, so the results of this review are very uncertain and difficult to interpret.

    Background

    Children and adolescents with asthma are more likely to experience symptoms of psychological distress (e.g. anxiety, depression, or both) than those without asthma. Psychological treatment might help reduce this distress and therefore improve how children and adolescents are able to manage their asthma.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out whether psychological treatment was better than usual care, treatment without a psychological component, or no treatment for improving the following outcomes.

    1. Symptoms of anxiety
    2. Symptoms of depression
    3. Medical contacts (such as hospital admissions or emergency department visits)
    4. Asthma attacks
    5. Asthma symptoms
    6. Medication use
    7. Quality of life

    We also wanted to know whether psychological treatment had any unwanted effects in children and adolescents with asthma.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that had evaluated psychological treatment (such as behavioural therapies, cognitive therapies, or counselling) compared to standard asthma care, treatment without a psychological component, or no treatment in girls and boys aged five to 18 years with asthma.

    What did we find?

    We included 24 studies that had enroled 1639 children and adolescents. We found mixed results for nearly all outcomes in this review: many treatments showed benefits, but others showed no evidence of an effect. The results of some studies suggest that psychological interventions could be beneficial for children and adolescents with asthma, particularly to reduce symptoms of anxiety or depression, reduce asthma attacks, reduce asthma symptoms, and improve medication use. For most studies, it was difficult to judge how important the benefits were because the assessment scales were not well described.

    There is limited evidence that psychological interventions could reduce the need for medical contact or improve quality of life. No studies reported unwanted effects of treatment.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    Our results are very uncertain and difficult to interpret because the studies used different theories to develop their treatments, different tools to measure results, different follow‐up periods, and different definitions for the outcomes (e.g. one study measured daytime symptoms while another measured total asthma symptoms). Because of these important differences, it was difficult to make comparisons between the studies. The number of children and adolescents was small in most studies, and some studies reported only part of their results. More high‐quality research is needed to strengthen the evidence base for using psychological treatment in children and adolescents with asthma, and to determine which types of psychological treatment might be most helpful.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is current to February 2023.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Most studies reporting symptoms of anxiety, depression, asthma attacks, asthma symptoms, and medication use found a positive effect of psychological interventions versus controls on at least one measure. However, all outcomes had mixed findings; it was difficult to judge the magnitude of effects; and the evidence for all outcomes is very uncertain due to clinical heterogeneity, small sample sizes, incomplete reporting, and risk of bias. In addition, most scales and measures did not have established minimal clinically important differences, meaning the clinical significance of any benefits are unclear. There is little evidence that psychological interventions can reduce medical contacts or improve quality of life, and no studies reported adverse events.

    Due to substantial heterogeneity in intervention design, tools used to examine the outcomes, and population characteristics, we were unable to identify specific components of effective interventions and distinguish them from interventions showing no evidence of an effect. There is limited evidence to support the use of psychological interventions to reduce hospital admissions, emergency department visits, and asthma symptoms. We observed no overall evidence of an effect for quality of life, and there were mixed results related to medication use. Readers should interpret these results with great caution due to the substantial heterogeneity across different aspects of the psychological interventions themselves, the characteristics of participants evaluated (asthma severity, age, socio‐economic status), and the tools used to measure outcomes.

    Implications for research

    Psychological interventions are continuing to evolve and develop in response to research evidence and strategic support from organisations and governments. However, the safety and efficacy of psychological interventions for asthma in children and adolescents are unclear, largely due to the variability between studies of these interventions. Varied approaches to meet the objectives and needs of the target audience should not be stifled; however, future research programmes that examine the latest evidence‐based psychological techniques should consider standardising outcome assessment tools to support cross‐comparison and meta‐analysis. Researchers should also consider the extended range of outcomes to inform clinical care delivery needs, patient objectives, and policy requirements. In particular, researchers may consider identifying patient‐important outcomes by consulting directly with patients and their caregivers. Of the 24 studies included in this review, only five used validated questionnaires to assess symptoms of anxiety and depression. No studies reported adverse events, despite the seriousness of psychological distress and the potential for complications related to ineffective treatment delivery. Similarly, few studies reported asthma attacks, medication use, or quality of life. There is a need for methodologically rigorous studies (i.e. that take care to reduce potential risks of bias) that include larger samples and use standardised, validated outcome assessment tools to determine if psychological interventions can be effective in reducing psychological distress among children and adolescents with asthma.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Asthma is a complex and phenotypically diverse disease, characterised by chronic inflammation of the airways (GINA 2023). Though a universally accepted definition of asthma is lacking, the disease is generally defined by "a history of respiratory symptoms such as wheeze, shortness of breath, chest tightness and cough that vary over time and in intensity, together with variable expiratory airflow limitation" (GINA 2023).

    Asthma contributes substantially to the global burden of ill health. An estimated 262 million people worldwide experience symptoms of asthma (Lancet 2020). Countries with the highest prevalence rates include Brazil, the Netherlands, the UK, Sweden, and Australia (To 2012).

    Individuals with asthma may experience exacerbations of asthma symptoms, also known as asthma attacks. Because these attacks can be life‐threatening, they impose a significant psychological burden on patients and their communities (GINA 2023). Poor day‐to‐day control of asthma symptoms can also contribute to the psychological burden of the disease (Goodwin 2013). Psychological distress can itself trigger or exacerbate symptoms of asthma (Van Lieshout 2008).

    Rates of asthma are high in children and adolescents. Global prevalence rates indicate that 9% of children aged six to seven years and 11% of adolescents aged 13 to 14 years experience symptoms of asthma (Asher 2021). Young people with asthma generally report poorer health outcomes than those without asthma (Blanchard 2014). A 2014 survey of Australians aged 12 to 25 years found that half of young people with asthma experienced symptoms of heightened psychological distress (the experience of symptoms of anxiety and depression at subclinical levels; Drapeau 2012). This is double the rate of the wider community (Blanchard 2014). Poor asthma outcomes and poor mental health outcomes disproportionately affect people from less wealthy backgrounds (AIHW 2023; Evans 2012; GINA 2023).

    Children and adolescents with asthma face a range of challenges that may contribute to their increased experience of psychological distress. These include social isolation, restricted life choices, limitations on personal potential, embarrassment about their condition or treatment (or both), self‐consciousness, concerns about disease exacerbation, and fear of life‐threatening attacks (Blanchard 2014). These complex, interconnected issues compound the social, psychological, and developmental challenges experienced during childhood and adolescence (Cohen 2003; de Benedictis 2007), and likely contribute to higher levels of functional impairment (Akinbami 2002), lower quality of life (Goldney 2003), and higher rates of preventable hospitalisation and mortality in people with asthma compared to those without (Calmes 1998).

    Description of the intervention

    The relationship between asthma and symptoms of psychological distress is likely bidirectional (Baiardini 2015). On the one hand, psychological factors and negative life events can exacerbate inflammation and symptoms of asthma (French 1943; Sandberg 2000; Van Lieshout 2008). Psychological distress may exacerbate the immune response to external stimuli via increased activation of endocrine pathways (the sympathetic and adrenomedullary (SAM) system and the hypothalamic‐pituitary‐adrenocortical (HPA) axis; Chen 2007). Long periods of exposure to stress hormones can lead to down‐regulation of receptors to these molecules and dysregulation of inflammatory responses to asthma triggers. Production of Th2 cytokines and recruitment of eosinophils can be exaggerated under these conditions, promoting output of neurotransmitters that can increase inflammation (Wright 1998; Wright 2005). On the other hand, asthma in young people is associated with increased psychological distress and clinical diagnoses of psychological disorders (Dudeney 2017; Katon 2007; Pinquart 2010). Anxiety in people with asthma can develop into chronic and excessive psychological distress (Thomas 2011). Asthma may negatively affect psychological and mental health outcomes, as individuals with asthma may experience heightened psychological distress due to the persistent fear of asthma attacks (GINA 2018).

    Psychological interventions refer to theoretically informed psychological strategies underpinned by clinical formulation. Various types of interventions exist, underpinned by distinct theoretical frameworks. This review explores behavioural therapy, cognitive therapy, cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), relaxation, psychodynamic psychotherapy, counselling, group therapy, and family therapy. Behavioural therapies focus on identifying and changing learnt behaviours, cognitive therapies identify and manage thoughts and perceptions, and CBT incorporates aspects of both models. Relaxation techniques are designed to control psychological distress; in people with asthma, they may improve perception of respiratory symptoms. Psychodynamic therapy, counselling, group therapy, and family therapy all involve the discussion of issues with a licenced professional in group or individual formats. The current evidence assessing the efficacy of individual versus group therapy formats is generally mixed, and may differ based on which symptoms of psychological distress the patient is experiencing (Craigie 2009; Flannery‐Schroeder 2000; Manassis 2002; Wierzbicki 1987). All interventions can be delivered in person, via printed self‐help materials, or via the internet. Studies have shown that online or technology‐delivered psychological interventions (also known as e‐psychology, e‐health, or e‐mental health) are effective for providing psychiatric care and support; they may increase access to and improve quality of care (RANZCP 2019), and can be a cost‐effective treatment option for patients and providers alike (Solomon 2015).

    How the intervention might work

    Children and adolescents with asthma who experience psychological distress might not manage their asthma as well as those not experiencing psychological distress. Psychological distress may impact negatively on self‐efficacy and symptom perception, and thus on medication adherence and other health behaviours, including frequency of health service utilisation. In general, symptoms of anxiety or depression can lead to medication under‐ or overuse, both of which can result in poor asthma outcomes (Rietveld 2003). Psychological interventions may provide young people with techniques and strategies to reduce the negative impact of psychological distress on asthma self‐management and avoidance of asthma attacks (Duff 2001). These interventions could include behaviour therapy to increase adherence to treatment, cognitive therapy to address emotional reactions, relaxation techniques such as progressive muscle relaxation to reduce psychological distress, or family therapy to question and discuss familial concerns and allow for increased understanding and support by family members (Duff 2001). Positive cognitive experiences regarding asthma – specifically self‐efficacy, outcome expectations, and perceptions of barriers – are associated with better asthma control and adherence to medication in young people (Rhee 2018).

    We hypothesise that psychological interventions may be of value to children and adolescents with asthma. By reducing asthma‐related anxiety and psychological distress, psychological interventions may improve behavioural outcomes such as self‐efficacy and medication adherence, reduce inflammation, and ultimately reduce medical contacts and asthma attacks in children and adolescents.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Despite evidence that psychological interventions are beneficial in the treatment of psychological distress and psychological disorders, reports indicate that only one in five young people who could benefit from psychological treatment are receiving it (Bekker 2017). We aimed to evaluate the effectiveness of psychological interventions as a treatment method for children and adolescents with asthma. We hoped that that by synthesising the available evidence, our review could inform best practice guidelines and recommendations for health professionals who treat people with asthma, and aid in efforts to increase uptake of psychological interventions in this age group.

    This review built on a previous systematic review investigating psychological interventions for children with asthma (Yorke 2005). The main objectives of both reviews are similar; however, we used current Cochrane methodology and evaluated a full range of psychological interventions, including family therapy. We also amended the primary and secondary outcomes to align with current clinical practice, and we synthesised results following the Synthesis Without Meta‐analysis (SWiM) guideline.

    Objectives

    Our main objective was to determine the efficacy of psychological interventions for modifying health and behavioural outcomes in children with asthma, compared with usual treatment, treatment with no psychological component, or no treatment. Our secondary objectives were to compare the efficacy of different types of psychological interventions for children with asthma and to assess the comparative efficacy of individual and group formats of psychological therapy for children with asthma.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) with individual or cluster allocation. We included studies reported as full‐text articles or abstract only, as well as unpublished data. We did not restrict studies by the duration of follow‐up.

    Types of participants

    We included studies involving male and female children and adolescents (aged five to 18 years) with asthma diagnosed by a physician or according to international or national guidelines, irrespective of psychological symptoms at baseline. Participants could be recruited from inpatient or outpatient settings.

    Types of interventions

    We included trials that evaluated any of the following psychological interventions.

    1. Behavioural therapy: concerned with identifying the processes by which behaviour has been learnt (i.e. via association, reward, or observation) and modifying behaviour though methods such as systematic desensitisation, selective reinforcement, and positive modelling. The behaviour itself, rather than the underlying motivations, is the focus of behavioural interventions.

    2. Cognitive therapy: identification and constructive management of damaging thoughts, such as perceptions of helplessness or disproportionate fear of asthma attack, that can trigger asthma symptoms

    3. Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT): incorporating the key elements of both behavioural and cognitive models

    4. Relaxation techniques: designed to control stress and anxiety and thus improve breathing and respiratory function. Relaxation techniques include the following.

      1. Progressive relaxation: systematically tensing and releasing different parts of the body, sometimes via guided imagery

      2. Autogenic training: learning to attend to bodily feelings and mentally control them

      3. Hypnosis: deep relaxation that may be induced through mental imagery, often accompanied by autosuggestion to create positive thoughts and feelings

      4. Biofeedback: feedback of biological indicators that the subject must control via relaxation. May also be considered a behavioural intervention, as feedback can act as a reinforcer.

    5. Psychodynamic psychotherapy: including psychoanalysis, psychosomatic therapy, and hypnosis

    6. Counselling: talking over problems with a health professional. In supportive counselling, the counsellor aims to be a good listener and provide emotional support, rather than offering a more targeted psychotherapeutic intervention.

    7. Group therapy: psychotherapeutic interventions conducted in groups (e.g. group psychosomatic therapy)

    8. Family therapy: interventions that aim to change the relationships within families to help them deal with a wide range of problems more effectively

    Psychological interventions must have been created or delivered by a qualified professional. They could be delivered in person or online, and could be of any duration. Studies with co‐interventions (e.g. prescribed medications) were also eligible, provided the co‐interventions were not part of the randomised treatment. We excluded treatments involving educational approaches and breathing retraining techniques. Previous reviews have covered these types of interventions (Haby 2001; Wolf 2003).

    Eligible comparators were usual care, active controls (e.g. self‐help), and waiting‐list control.

    Types of outcome measures

    We explored our main objective through the following primary and secondary outcomes. Had meta‐analysis been possible, we would have explored our secondary objectives through subgroup analysis.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Primary efficacy of the intervention to reduce psychological distress: symptoms of anxiety or depression (or both), as measured by standard validated questionnaires such as the State‐Trait Anxiety Inventory (STAI), Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI), Beck Depression Inventory (BDI), or Kessler Psychological Distress Scale (K10)

    2. Medical contacts, as measured by hospital admissions, emergency department visits, or primary care visits (individually or combined)

    3. Asthma attacks, as measured by number of children with at least one asthma attack (defined as an acute increase in asthma symptom severity requiring use of corticosteroids or hospital admission) during follow‐up, or rate of asthma attacks

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Self‐reported asthma symptoms as measured on an asthma symptom scale such as Asthma Control Test (ACT) or Asthma Control Questionnaire (ACQ)

    2. Medication use (e.g. frequency of use, dosage); use of preventer inhalers; use of reliever inhalers

    3. Self‐reported quality of life as measured by validated questionnaires such as the Paediatric Asthma Quality of Life Questionnaire (PAQLQ)

    4. Adverse events/side effects

    We planned to group outcomes reported at different time points as follows.

    1. Less than three months after baseline

    2. Three months to less than six months after baseline

    3. Six months to less than 12 months after baseline

    4. 12 months and over after baseline

    Reporting of our prespecified outcomes was not an inclusion criterion for the review.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We identified trials from searches of the following databases and trial registries in August 2019, March 2020, February 2021, and August 2022.

    1. Cochrane Airways Trials Register (Cochrane Airways 2019), via the Cochrane Register of Studies

    2. Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) via the Cochrane Register of Studies (all years to 22 August 2022)

    3. MEDLINE Ovid SP (ALL; 1946 to 22 August 2022)

    4. Embase Ovid SP (1974 to 22 August 2022)

    5. PsycINFO Ovid SP (1967 to 22 August 2022)

    6. US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov)

    Searching other resources

    We checked the reference lists of included studies and relevant review articles for additional references. We searched for errata or retractions from included studies published as full‐text articles on PubMed to 22 August 2022 (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/)

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    We used Cochrane's 'Screen4Me' workflow to help assess the results of our search for RCTs. Screen4Me comprises the following three components.

    1. Known assessments: a service that matches records in the search results to records that have already been screened in Cochrane Crowd and have been labelled 'RCT' or 'Not an RCT'

    2. The RCT classifier: a machine‐learning model that distinguishes RCTs from non‐RCTs (Marshall 2018)

    3. Cochrane Crowd: Cochrane's citizen science platform where 'the crowd' helps to identify and describe health evidence (Noel‐Storr 2020)

    After using the Screen4Me workflow, two review authors independently screened the titles and abstracts of the records returned by the searches, coding them 'Yes' (eligible or potentially eligible) or 'No' using Covidence software (Covidence 2021). We retrieved the full‐text articles of all eligible and potentially eligible studies and two review authors independently screened them for inclusion, identifying and recording reasons for exclusion of ineligible studies. We resolved any disagreements though discussion and consensus. We used Covidence to identify duplicates, and we collated multiple reports of the same study so that each study, rather than report, was the unit of interest in the review. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram (Moher 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    To record study characteristics and outcome data, we used a data collection form that we had piloted on at least one study. One review author (KJS) extracted the following data, and another review author (OS, SC‐A, KVCC, KK, or KP) checked them for accuracy.

    1. Methods: study design, study location, clustering adjustments and effect, methods of analysis

    2. Participants: number, withdrawals, mean age, age range, sex, inclusion and exclusion criteria, socioeconomic data, severity of condition, diagnostic criteria, psychological symptoms at baseline, smoking history

    3. Interventions: study setting, intervention description, comparison, concomitant pharmacotherapy, duration of intervention

    4. Outcomes: primary and secondary outcomes specified and collected, time points reported

    5. Notes: funding for studies and notable conflicts of interest of trial authors

    Two review authors independently extracted the outcome data. We noted in the Characteristics of included studies table if outcome data were not reported in a usable way. We resolved disagreements by discussion and consensus. One review author (KJS) transferred the data into the Review Manager 5 file (Review Manager 2014). We double‐checked that data were entered correctly by comparing the data presented in the systematic review with the study reports.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors independently assessed the risk of bias of each study using the original Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB 1), which covers the following domains (Higgins 2011).

    1. Random sequence generation

    2. Allocation concealment

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel

    4. Blinding of outcome assessment

    5. Incomplete outcome data

    6. Selective outcome reporting

    7. Other bias

    We graded each potential source of bias as high, low, or unclear, providing a quote from the study report or a justification for the judgement in the Characteristics of included studies table. We summarised the risk of bias judgements across different studies for each of the domains listed. Where information on risk of bias related to unpublished data or correspondence with a trialist, we made a note of this in the Characteristics of included studies table. We resolved disagreements by discussion.

    When considering treatment effects, we took into account the risk of bias for the studies that contributed to the relevant outcome.

    Assessment of bias in conducting the systematic review

    We conducted this review according to our published protocol (Sharrad 2019), and we reported any deviations from it in the Differences between protocol and review section of the systematic review.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We planned to analyse dichotomous data as odds ratios (OR) and continuous data as mean differences (MDs), each with its corresponding 95% confidence interval (CI). Where appropriate, we planned to use the standardised mean difference (SMD) to pool results from various assessment scales. We planned to enter data presented as a scale with a consistent direction of effect.

    However, we were unable to undertake meta‐analysis, as treatments, participants, and underlying clinical questions were not sufficiently similar for pooling to make sense.

    Where multiple trial arms were reported in a single study, we included only the relevant arms. If we had combined two comparisons (e.g. intervention A versus usual care and intervention B versus usual care) in the same meta‐analysis, we would have either combined the active arms or halved the control group to avoid double‐counting.

    Had we performed any meta‐analyses, we would have used adjusted analyses (ANOVA or ANCOVA), where available. Where both change‐from‐baseline and endpoint scores were available for continuous data, we planned to use change‐from‐baseline data unless there was low correlation between measurements in individuals. Where studies reported outcomes at multiple time points, we planned to use the latest recorded time point.

    Where possible, we extracted intention‐to‐treat (ITT) or full analysis set data (where study authors imputed data for randomised participants who did not complete the study) instead of completer or per‐protocol analyses.

    Unit of analysis issues

    For dichotomous outcomes, we planned to use participants, rather than events, as the unit of analysis (i.e. number of children admitted to hospital, rather than number of admissions per child). However, if a study reported RRs, we planned to analyse the data on this basis.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted investigators or study sponsors to verify key study characteristics and obtain missing numerical outcome data where possible (e.g. when we could only find the abstract of a study). If we had undertaken meta‐analysis, and if the missing data were thought to introduce serious bias, we would have explored the impact of including such studies on the overall assessment of results through a sensitivity analysis.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to use the I2 statistic to measure statistical heterogeneity among the studies for each analysis. Had we identified substantial heterogeneity, we would have explored possible causes through prespecified subgroup analyses. To aid interpretation of the pooled estimates, we created a table outlining the key features of the included studies to enable easy comparison between trials contributing data to the review. We explored clinical heterogeneity narratively in the Discussion section.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Because we were unable to perform meta‐analysis, we could not investigate publication bias by creating and examining funnel plots.

    Data synthesis

    We planned to use a random‐effects model to synthesise data.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to conduct the following subgroup analyses of our primary outcomes.

    1. Type of psychological intervention (e.g. behavioural therapy, CBT, psychodynamic therapy)

    2. Individual versus group interventions

    3. Face‐to‐face versus online interventions

    4. Primary school‐aged participants (five to 12 years) versus secondary school‐aged participants (13 to 18 years)

    Had we performed any subgroup analyses, we would have used the formal test for subgroup interactions in Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2014).

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to conduct the following sensitivity analyses.

    1. Exclusion of trials with high risk of bias for random sequence generation or allocation concealment

    2. Exclusion of unpublished data/non‐peer reviewed studies

    3. Removal of small trials (those with fewer than 20 participants in each arm) to investigate the potential influence of study size on effect size (Fisher 2018)

    4. Exclusion of trials in which the intervention is supportive rather than providing targeted psychotherapeutic input

    We also planned to repeat the analysis using a fixed‐effect model.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    Using GRADEpro software (GRADEpro GDT), we created a summary of findings table of the following outcomes at longest follow‐up (maximum one year post‐treatment).

    1. Symptoms of anxiety and depression

    2. Medical contacts

    3. Asthma attacks

    4. Asthma symptoms

    5. Medication use

    6. Quality of life

    We used the five GRADE considerations (risk of bias, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the evidence for each outcome, following methods and recommendations described in Chapter 14 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Schünemann 2023). We justified all decisions to downgrade or upgrade the certainty of evidence using footnotes, and we made comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review where necessary.

    Study design and settings

    The studies were published between 1978 and 2021. Eleven studies were set in the USA (Alexander 1972; Beebe 2010; Bruzzese 2008; Bruzzese 2020; Halterman 2021; Hock 1978; Kohen 1996; Marsland 2019; Naar 2018; Pulgaron 2010; Warner 2006), five in China (Chiang 2009; Tong 2002; Tseng 2020; Wang 2012; Yang 2004), two in Sweden (Dahl 1990; Gustafsson 1986), three in Iran (Barikani 2021; Khoshnavay Fomani 2013; Taheri 2019), and one each in the Netherlands (Colland 1993), the UK (Lask 1979), and Germany (Loew 1996). Participants were recruited from hospitals (10 studies), schools (six studies), asthma clinics (five studies), a children's medical centre (one study), and a children's camp (one study). The setting was unclear in the remaining study.

    Participant characteristics

    The analyses included a total of 1639 children and adolescents. We included only studies with participants aged five to 18 years. Three studies included only primary school‐aged participants (five to 12 years; Colland 1993; Khoshnavay Fomani 2013; Kohen 1996); two included only secondary school‐aged participants (13 to 18 years; Bruzzese 2020; Halterman 2021), and 18 included participants across both age groups (Alexander 1972; Barikani 2021; Beebe 2010; Bruzzese 2008; Chiang 2009; Gustafsson 1986; Hock 1978; Lask 1979; Loew 1996; Marsland 2019; Naar 2018; Pulgaron 2010; Taheri 2019; Tong 2002; Tseng 2020; Wang 2012; Warner 2006; Yang 2004). The age range of participants was unclear in Dahl 1990. Hock 1978 included only boys, and all other studies with data on participant sex included boys and girls. Most studies included more boys than girls, though Bruzzese 2020 and Warner 2006 included more girls. Participants' asthma diagnoses ranged from mild to severe.

    Intervention characteristics

    The duration of interventions ranged from three days to eight months. The intervention in Bruzzese 2020 was conducted online, and Khoshnavay Fomani 2013 did not clearly describe the intervention format; all other interventions were conducted in person. Seven studies used a relaxation intervention (Alexander 1972; Chiang 2009; Hock 1978; Kohen 1996; Loew 1996; Tong 2002; Yang 2004), three involved family therapy (Bruzzese 2008; Gustafsson 1986; Lask 1979), three involved a type of behavioural therapy (Colland 1993; Dahl 1990; Khoshnavay Fomani 2013), two used multicomponent interventions (Bruzzese 2020; Naar 2018), three used motivational interviewing (Barikani 2021Halterman 2021; Taheri 2019), and one study each evaluated art therapy (Beebe 2010), counselling (Wang 2012), a problem‐solving intervention (Pulgaron 2010), a self‐management intervention based on psychological theory (Tseng 2020), a stress‐management intervention (Marsland 2019), and written disclosure (Warner 2006).

    Primary outcomes

    Symptoms of anxiety

    Five studies involving 431 participants used validated tools to measure symptoms of anxiety (Beebe 2010; Chiang 2009; Colland 1993; Marsland 2019; Yang 2004). The tools were the Beck Youth Inventory (BYI), Chinese Children's Manifest Anxiety Scale (CCMAS), Screen for Child Anxiety‐Related Emotion Disorder (SCARED), and STAI for Children. Marsland 2019 also used a tool focussed on symptoms of stress (Perceived Stress Scale). For all scales, lower scores represent better outcomes.

    Three studies (279 participants) reported a benefit of the psychological intervention versus control (Beebe 2010; Colland 1993; Yang 2004), though Colland 1993 provided no supporting data. One study (48 participants) reported mixed findings (Chiang 2009), and one study (104 participants) found little or no difference between the groups (Marsland 2019). No studies indicated the minimal clinically important difference (MCID) for the scales used, so the magnitude and clinical significance of any benefits are unclear. These results should be interpreted with caution, as the wide CIs, large standard deviations (SDs), and small standard errors (SEs; where reported) suggest uncertainty in the reported findings. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency, risk of bias, and imprecision. Analysis 1.1 presents the numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, scale metrics (where known), and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Beebe 2010 found a statistically significant difference in favour of the art therapy intervention, with lower anxiety scores on the BYI for this group (mean −14, SD 14.304) compared with the waiting‐list control (mean 0.545, SD 19.387) at six months' follow‐up (P = 0.030). Chiang 2009 measured anxiety with the CCMAS, finding that the relaxation group compared to the control group (asthma self‐management) experienced a greater reduction in general tendency to anxiety (−3.97 versus 1.03; β = 5.96, SE 2.51, P = 0.012, CI 1.34 to 10.59) and total anxiety (−5.62 versus 2.25; β = 9.21, SE 4.05, P = 0.027, CI 1.11 to 17.32) at three months of follow‐up. However, there was selective reporting of nonsignificant results. Colland 1993 collected data at three time points. Immediately after the intervention, the investigators identified a significant reduction in trait anxiety and asthma‐specific anxiety among participants who received behavioural self‐management compared to those who had an information session or no intervention. Six months later, trait anxiety levels no longer differed significantly between the intervention and control groups, but asthma‐specific anxiety was significantly lower in the intervention group among extremely anxious children. At one year of follow‐up, participants in the intervention group reported clinically significant reductions in anxiety during an asthma attack, compared with controls. Marsland 2019 found no significant difference in the Perceived Stress Scale scores (1.24 points lower for intervention participants; P = 0.43, SE = 1.55), and no difference in state or trait anxiety scores among participants who received stress management ('I Can Cope') compared to those who received asthma management or no intervention. Finally, Yang 2004 identified significantly lower SCARED anxiety scores among participants who received relaxation therapy compared to participants who received no intervention (mean 18.27, SD 6.95 versus mean 23.21, SD 7.26).

    The three studies with numerical data on anxiety used different tools (Beebe 2010; Chiang 2009; Yang 2004). Although all tools were in the same direction, one used change scores, while two used absolute values. Chiang 2009 reported skewed data (i.e. SD > half the mean), and we could not pool the remaining two studies because follow‐ups were too varied (six months versus four weeks).

    Symptoms of depression

    Two studies involving 166 participants measured symptoms of depression: Marsland 2019 used the Child Depression Index, and Yang 2004 used the Depression Self‐Rating Scale for Children (lower is better for both scales). Both studies reported that the psychological intervention had a beneficial effect compared to no intervention, but neither indicated the MCID for the scales used, so the magnitude and clinical significance of any benefits are unclear. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency, risk of bias, and imprecision. Analysis 1.2 presents the numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, scale metrics (where known), and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Marsland 2019 reported a significant difference in symptoms of depression in participants receiving the stress management intervention ('I Can Cope') compared with those receiving no intervention (2.68 points lower, P = 0.02, SE 1.29). Yang 2004 also identified a significant difference between the groups, with significantly lower depression scores among participants who received relaxation therapy compared to those who received no intervention (mean 8.62, SD 3.95 versus mean 12.97, SD 4.69).

    Medical contacts

    Five studies involving 636 participants reported medical contact outcomes, including hospital admissions, emergency department visits, and urgent care visits (Bruzzese 2008; Gustafsson 1986; Halterman 2021; Kohen 1996; Naar 2018). The two largest studies reported mixed findings (Naar 2018; 167 participants) or no difference between the treatment groups (Halterman 2021; 377 participants). The remaining three studies were much smaller (13 participants to 61 participants). Whilst four of the five studies found that the interventions reduced hospitalisations or emergency department visits compared to control, the evidence was very uncertain. No studies reported primary care visits. It was not possible to conduct meta‐analysis because the studies were heterogeneous in terms of interventions and outcomes. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency and risk of bias. Analysis 1.3 provides numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, outcome definitions used within the studies, and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Bruzzese 2020 compared the risk of asthma‐related urgent care visits among participants randomised to the Controlling Asthma Program for Adolescents (CAMP Air) e‐health intervention versus those randomised to active control (educational materials), reporting a 69% lower risk in the intervention group relative to the control group immediately after treatment, and a significant difference between the groups at one month of follow‐up (12% of the CAMP Air population required an asthma‐related urgent care visit compared with 41% of the control population; adjusted MD 0.31, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.99; P = 0.04). In Naar 2018, an evaluation of hospitalisation data 12 months after the intervention revealed that Multisystemic Therapy‐Health Care (MST‐HC) reduced frequency of hospitalisations compared with non‐directive family support (β = −0.882; P = 0.04; incidence rate ratio 0.414, 95% CI 0.175 to 0.978). After adjustment for baseline differences and covariates, the mean number of hospitalisations dropped from 0.87 at baseline to 0.24 by 12 months in the intervention group, with smaller declines observed in the control group (from 0.66 at baseline to 0.34 at 12 months). There was no significant difference between the groups in emergency department visits (β = −0.153; P = 0.63). Gustafsson 1986 followed 18 children over three and a half years and found a reduction in emergency hospitalisation days per year (from 2.67 to 0.17 days per year) after family therapy (P = 0.06), and no improvement in the control arm (usual care). In Halterman 2021 (377 participants, 3 arms) there were no differences reported between any of the groups in emergency visits or hospitalisations. By seven months of follow‐up, the number of participants with at least one emergency department presentation, urgent care visit, or hospitalisation was 17 (12%) in the School‐Based Asthma Care for Teens (SB‐ACT) group, two (16%) in the directly observed treatment group, and 15 (11%) in the asthma education group (P = 0.672). Kohen 1996 (28 participants, 4 arms) identified fewer emergency department visits in the experimental relaxation/mental imagery self‐hypnosis group compared to the traditional control (no intervention) group (Chi2 = 3.90; P < 0.05). The waking suggestion group and attention‐placebo control group showed no significant difference in emergency room visits compared to traditional control. However, due to the small number of participants in this study, the positive results observed must be interpreted with caution.

    Asthma attacks

    Three studies involving 129 participants reported asthma attacks/exacerbations (Beebe 2010; Hock 1978; Yang 2004). Investigators collected data on asthma attacks via medical charts/nursing records, parental report, or follow‐up phone calls. Hock 1978 and Yang 2004 found that the interventions had a beneficial effect compared to control, and Beebe 2010 (6 participants) found little or no difference between the groups. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency, risk of bias, and imprecision. Heterogeneity of interventions and outcome periods precluded meta‐analysis. Analysis 1.4 provides numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, outcome definitions used within the studies, and a brief narrative interpretation.

    In Beebe 2010, 3/22 children in the art therapy group and 4/22 children in the waiting list group experienced asthma attacks that required medical contact during the six months prior to enrolment in the study. In the six months following the study, two children in each group experienced an asthma attack. Hock 1978 found that relaxation training significantly reduced the rate of asthma attacks compared with usual care at follow‐up during the eighth week of treatment. Additionally, relaxation training combined with assertiveness training significantly reduced asthma attacks compared to all other groups (relaxation training alone or control). However, these effects did not last, with rates returning to pretreatment values 4 weeks after treatment. Yang 2004 also reported a reduced rate of asthma attacks (proportion of participants who reported more than one asthma attack at one month of follow‐up) in participants who received the relaxation intervention (61.3%) compared with those who received no intervention (90.9%).

    Secondary outcomes

    Asthma symptoms

    Eleven studies involving 720 participants reported asthma symptoms. Meta‐analysis was not possible because studies used different definitions of asthma symptoms (day symptoms, night symptoms, wheeze, total symptoms), different tools to measure them, and different recall periods (last two weeks, last week, daily symptom diary). Five studies used a self‐report or asthma symptom diary, four used validated tests (Asthma Sum Scale, ACT, Children's Health Survey for Asthma, ACQ), one used a "standard measure of asthma symptom severity" that was not further described, and one used an asthma symptoms checklist. Lower scores represent better outcomes for all scales except the ACT (used in Tseng 2020).

    Four studies (332 participants) reported consistent benefits of the psychological intervention versus control (Lask 1979; Naar 2018; Taheri 2019; Yang 2004), five studies (257 participants) reported mixed or unclear findings (Bruzzese 2008; Bruzzese 2020; Dahl 1990; Marsland 2019; Warner 2006), and two studies (131 participants) reported little or no difference between the groups (Chiang 2009; Tseng 2020). Most studies reported significant differences between intervention and control groups for at least one measure (e.g. for night symptoms but not day symptoms), but they used a variety of scales with unknown MCIDs, meaning the clinical significance of any benefits is unclear. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency, risk of bias, and imprecision. Analysis 1.5 provides numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, scale metrics (where known), and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Bruzzese 2008 reported a 67% reduction in the number of nights awakened by asthma symptoms in the past two weeks (from mean 2.0 (SD 1.3) nights to mean 0.7 (SD 1.8) nights) in participants who received family therapy, and a 19% increase (from mean 1.75 (SD 2.8) nights to mean 2.1 (SD 4.1) nights) in the control group (no intervention) at two months' follow‐up. There was no significant effect reported for daytime symptoms. Similarly, Bruzzese 2020 found that participants who received the CAMP Air intervention had a significantly reduced incidence of night waking in the prior two weeks compared with participants who received educational materials (adjusted MD 0.36 awakenings, 95% CI 0.22 to 0.61; P < 0.001), while there was no significant effect for daytime symptoms (adjusted MD 0.86 symptomatic days, 95% CI 0.64 to 1.14, P = 0.29). In Chiang 2009, asthma symptoms reduced from 9.46 to 6.93 in the experimental group (relaxation) and from 10.44 to 7.24 in the control group (asthma self‐management programme), with no significant between‐group difference over time (β = −0.70, SE 0.77, P = 0.369, CI −2.24 to 0.84). In Dahl 1990, daily charts kept by both children and parents showed a 50% reduction in subjective asthma symptoms in the intervention group (behavioural therapy). The diary cards used in Lask 1979 demonstrated a significant improvement in asthma symptom score in favour of family therapy compared with usual care (mean 7, SD 8 versus mean 21, SD 11; P < 0.01). Marsland 2019 had two intervention groups which both reported significantly fewer symptoms over the past two weeks on the Children's Health Survey for Asthma (mean 30.84, SD 3.91), compared with controls (mean 27.52, SD 4.99). However, twice‐daily asthma symptom diaries (completed by children and their parents) revealed no significant differences between the groups. Naar 2018 reported a significantly greater reduction in asthma symptoms measured on the Family Asthma Management System Scale (FAMSS) among participants who received MST‐HC compared with those who received non‐directive family support (from mean 2.75 to 1.43 versus from mean 2.67 to 2.58; β = −0.601, P = 0.03, CI −1.127 to −0.074). Similarly, in Taheri 2019, the mean score on the ACQ in the intervention group (motivational interviewing) was significantly less than in the control group (no intervention) at three months of follow‐up (MD −5.22, SD 4.02 versus MD −0.08, SD 1.27; P < 0.001). However, Tseng 2020 reported no significant difference in ACT scores between groups. In Warner 2006, there was a strong positive association between baseline symptoms and follow‐up symptoms. Among participants with high baseline asthma symptoms (above the median), the intervention (written disclosure) led to a larger drop in symptoms measured on the Asthma Sum Scale (mean −6.93, SD 6.58; 67% improved) compared to writing about behaviours (mean −2.40, SD 4.27; 40% improved), but this intervention effect was absent among participants with low baseline asthma symptoms (mean +1.92, SD 5.38; 15% improved versus mean +0.33, SD 3.45; 17% improved). Yang 2004 compared relaxation with no intervention and found significant differences in self‐reported daytime symptom scores (mean 0.98 (SD 0.29) versus mean 1.8 (SD 0.41), P < 0.01) and nighttime symptom scores (mean 1.15 (SD 0.42) versus mean 2.11 (SD 0.58), P < 0.01).

    Medication use

    Eight studies involving 822 participants provided data on medication use. Meta‐analysis was not possible because studies reported the outcome in different ways, including medication use, prescriptions, change in medication type, and as part of validated assessment tools (e.g. Asthma Medication Score, FAMSS, twice‐daily phone diary). Most scales or measures do not have established MCIDs, meaning the magnitude and clinical significance of benefits are difficult to judge. Three studies considered adherence a positive outcome (Barikani 2021; Halterman 2021; Naar 2018), while the rest investigated increased prescriptions or reliever use as proxies for poor control (Chiang 2009; Dahl 1990; Gustafsson 1986; Hock 1978; Marsland 2019).

    Overall, six studies (670 participants) reported benefits of the psychological intervention versus control (Barikani 2021; Dahl 1990; Gustafsson 1986; Halterman 2021; Hock 1978; Naar 2018) although Gustafsson 1986 provided no supporting data. The remaining two studies (152 participants) found little or no difference between the groups (Chiang 2009; Marsland 2019). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency, risk of bias, and imprecision. Analysis 1.6 presents numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, scale metrics (where known), and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Barikani 2021 used the Medication Adherence Scale (MARS) and found greater improvement among children in the motivational interviewing group (from mean 3.77, SD 0.80 to mean 4.62, SD 0.50) than those receiving usual care (from mean 4.08, SD 0.56 to mean 4.15, SD 0.52); the P value adjusted for baseline was below 0.001. Chiang 2009 used the Asthma Medication Score and found a reduction in medication use from baseline to after treatment among participants receiving the relaxation intervention (from mean 5.10, SD 3.34 to mean 2.75, SD 2.84) and among children on the asthma self‐management programme (from mean 4.17, SD 2.68 to mean 1.94, SD 2.18). The mixed‐model analysis showed a nonsignificant group‐by‐time interaction (β = 0.13, 95% CI −1.90 to 2.15; P = 0.90). Dahl 1990 identified a significant reduction in the use of beta‐2‐agonist doses in the behaviour therapy arm compared to the usual care arm, with no correlation between number of beta‐2‐agonist doses and objectively measured air flow obstruction. Of note, the study included only 19 children. In the intervention group in Gustafsson 1986 (family therapy), there was a significant reduction in total number of beta‐2‐agonist doses (P < 0.01) and use of inhaled beta‐2‐agonists at night (P < 0.05), whereas the control group (usual care) showed no reduction in either outcome. Four participants in the intervention group reported improvements in required doses of beta‐2‐agonist, compared to no participants in the control group. Steroid medication was also reduced in the family therapy group relative to control, though the difference was nonsignificant. Hock 1978 reported changes in prescribed medications, finding fewer changes in the relaxation training group (3/9 participants) compared with the combined relaxation and assertive training group (6/10 participants); however, the difference was nonsignificant. Similarly, Hock 1978 found no difference in the effect of assertive training (6/9 participants) versus medical control (5/6 participants). It is unclear if the investigators considered this outcome to be positive or negative. Halterman 2021 identified significantly fewer preventive medication prescriptions among teens in the asthma education (control) group compared to those in the SB‐ACT programme at three months, five months, and seven months of follow‐up (P < 0.001). Medication adherence improved in all groups during the intervention but had returned to baseline levels by seven months. Naar 2018 found significant time‐by‐treatment interactions in medication adherence scores based on a subscale of the FAMSS (β = 0.485; P = 0.03) and twice‐daily phone diary assessments (β = 0.193; P = 0.004). From baseline to 12 months of follow‐up, FAMSS adherence scores improved from 4.19 to 5.24 in the MST‐HC group and from 4.61 to 4.72 in the control group (non‐directive family support). Adherence scores based on phone diary assessments improved from 0.33 to 0.69 in the MST‐HC group and from 0.43 to 0.46 in the control group. Finally, Marsland 2019 reported lower use of beta‐agonist inhalers in the 'I Can Cope' arm, but the difference was nonsignificant (P = 0.15). The study authors reported that these analyses were underpowered and should be considered preliminary.

    Quality of life

    Six studies involving 653 participants reported quality of life. Meta‐analysis was not possible due to heterogeneity of intervention type, follow‐up duration, and assessment tool. Two studies used the Paediatric Asthma Quality‐of‐Life Questionnaire (PAQLQ), while the remaining four studies used different validated questionnaires or subscales. No scales had established MCIDs, so the magnitude and clinical significance of any benefits are unclear. Higher scores represent better outcomes for all questionnaires except the Pediatric Quality of Life Inventory (PedsQL) asthma module (used in Beebe 2010).

    One study (48 participants) reported a beneficial effect of the intervention versus control but provided limited supporting data (Khoshnavay Fomani 2013). Two studies (83 participants) reported mixed findings across subscales (Beebe 2010; Bruzzese 2008), and the three largest studies (522 participants) found little or no difference between groups (Halterman 2021; Marsland 2019; Pulgaron 2010). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence to very low due to inconsistency and risk of bias. Analysis 1.7 presents numerical data for individual studies along with follow‐up duration, scale metrics (where known), and a brief narrative interpretation.

    Beebe 2010 reported quality of life using the PedsQL asthma module, identifying an immediate improvement in worry scores in the art therapy group versus the waiting list group as reported by parents (P = 0.0144) and by children (P = 0.0142); this effect was sustained until six months of follow‐up (P = 0.024 for parent‐reported worry scores, P = 0.0279 for child‐reported worry scores). However, overall (total) quality of life scores were not significantly improved in the intervention group at six months (P = 0.177). Bruzzese 2020 reported the symptom domain of the PAQLQ, finding a mean score of 5.21 (SD 1.00) in the CAMP Air arm compared to 4.74 (SD 1.30) in the control arm (adjusted MD 0.42, 95% CI 0.01 to 0.82; P = 0.04). The study authors did not report the other two domains (emotions and physical activity), which may suggest selective reporting. Halterman 2021 found no differences between groups in quality of life as measured by the PAQLQ (child and caregiver versions). Mean change from baseline to seven months of follow‐up in the child version was 0.82 (SD 1.3) for SB‐ACT, 0.80 (SD 1.1) for directly observed treatment, and 0.83 (SD 1.3) for asthma education (P = 0.662). Khoshnavay Fomani 2013 (48 participants) identified a significant improvement in quality of life scores measured by the OXFORD Mini Asthma Quality of Life questionnaire in the rational emotive behaviour therapy arm compared to the usual care arm (P = 0.03). The intervention arm also showed significant improvements in the emotional and social dimensions of the tool compared to control (P = 0.01 for both dimensions). The study report did not provide total scores. Marsland 2019 used the Children's Health Survey for Asthma and found that both interventions ('I Can Cope' and 'Open Airways') were associated with improved physical health‐related quality of life compared to the no treatment group (P = 0.07 for 'I Can Cope', P = 0.05 for 'Open Airways'). However, there was no significant difference between the two intervention groups and there were no improvements identified in the emotional or activity‐related quality of life domains. Finally, Pulgaron 2010 measured quality of life using the Miami Paediatric Quality of Life Questionnaire, with separate child and parent scores reported for self‐competence and social competence subscales. MDs were nonsignificant for all subscales (see Analysis 1.7).

    Adverse events/side effects

    No studies reported adverse events.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This review contains data from 24 studies. Meta‐analysis was not possible for any outcomes owing to lack of quantitative data, clinical heterogeneity (populations, scales, tools, and periods of follow‐up), skew, and the combination of raw and change scores. Many studies observed effects in favour of the intervention for the outcomes of interest, but findings overall were mixed, and the evidence was of very low certainty.

    For the primary outcomes of symptoms of anxiety and depression, most studies found that the psychological intervention had a beneficial effect on at least one measure at the initial post‐treatment follow‐up, and one study showed sustained reductions in anxiety at final follow‐up. However, the sample sizes were small, studies used a mix of scales without established MCIDs, and the CIs of effect estimates were wide. For the primary outcome of medical contacts, three small studies found that psychological interventions reduced hospital admissions or emergency department visits, but the two larger studies found little or no difference between treatment groups. For the primary outcome of asthma attacks, studies used different definitions and measurement tools. Two studies found that the intervention had a significant beneficial effect, and one small study found no evidence of an effect. Studies did not provide MCIDs or assess the certainty of their results, so readers should interpret these findings with caution.

    Nine of the 11 studies reporting asthma symptoms found that the psychological intervention had a beneficial effect compared to the control group in at least one measure (e.g. daytime symptoms), but five of these nine studies also reported less positive findings. Moreover, two studies reported little or no difference between treatment groups. The studies are heterogeneous in terms of how they measured asthma symptoms, with no two studies using the same standardised tool. There was also heterogeneity across studies in the definition of asthma symptoms (day symptoms, night symptoms, wheeze, and total symptoms). The effect measures suggest that the intervention may reduce asthma symptoms, but the certainty of the evidence is very low. Different studies reported medication use as adherence (a positive outcome) or medication need (a negative outcome); however, most studies reported a positive effect of psychological interventions versus the control groups, although the clinical significance across scales is unclear. Only one of six studies that measured quality of life (total quality of life or subscales of a quality of life tool) reported a significant improvement with the psychological intervention. The largest studies found little or no difference between treatment groups. The data are limited by the heterogeneity of tools used to measure this outcome. While two studies did use the same validated tool (PAQLQ), the remaining four used different questionnaires.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The studies recruited participants with different severities of asthma from a variety of clinical and community settings. The samples represented both sexes and a range of ages (primary and secondary school). The most common interventions were based on relaxation, family therapy, or behavioural therapy; several other intervention types were represented by two or fewer studies. We were unable to perform subgroup or sensitivity analyses due to variation in inclusion criteria and outcome measures. As a result, we did not explore the relative efficacy of different types of psychological interventions for children with asthma or the comparative efficacy of individual and group formats of psychological therapy for children with asthma.

    The included studies supported a narrative description of our primary outcomes (symptoms of anxiety or depression, medical contacts, and asthma attacks), although evidence on medical contacts was based on hospitalisations and emergency department visits only, since no studies reported primary care visits. A narrative analysis was possible for three of our secondary outcomes (asthma symptoms, medication use, and quality of life). No study provided data on adverse events.

    Quality of the evidence

    Using the GRADE approach, we rated the certainty of the evidence as very low for all outcomes, meaning we have very little confidence in the effect estimates. We downgraded because of the significant heterogeneity observed across all psychological interventions (from theoretical foundations to intensity and duration of treatment, mode of delivery, setting, and intended objective), outcomes (different validated tools used between studies), outcome measures, and populations (age, asthma severity). Other factors impacting the certainty of the evidence were lack of blinding, subjective outcome reporting, inadequate reporting, and small sample sizes.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We identified several potential biases that could have affected this review. Interventions had to be either created or delivered by a qualified professional, meaning we excluded any intervention that was developed and delivered without input from clinical psychologists or an individual with similar expertise. Although this slightly limits the pool of available studies, it does ensure rigour of intervention design and delivery. We only included RCTs, meaning studies that used other research methodology (e.g. controlled clinical trials without randomisation, interrupted time series studies, pseudo‐RCTs, and other comparative studies) were all excluded. Again, although this criterion limits the available evidence base and the generalisability of findings, it strengthens the quality of our results.

    It is possible that we missed relevant research findings presented outside peer‐reviewed publications or online clinical trial registries. However, we believe our search strategy was sufficiently broad to capture the vast majority of relevant studies. To minimise potential biases in the review process, we followed Cochrane methodology when screening studies, extracting data, assessing risk of bias, analysing findings, and assessing the certainty of the evidence (Higgins 2011). No review authors were authors of any Included studies, Excluded studies, or Studies awaiting classification. Authors KS and KVCC are primary researchers involved in ongoing study ACTRN12621001282875 (Ongoing studies). This study has not yet started recruitment and was included as a trial registration.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    In line with the previous review on this topic, we found that while individual studies suggest psychological interventions may be beneficial in the management of children and young people with asthma, the overall findings are inconclusive (Yorke 2005). Although several studies evaluated a specific intervention (e.g. relaxation training) or outcome measure (e.g. depression and anxiety), meta‐analysis was not possible. In Yorke 2005, this was due to the use of varying interventions and outcome measures, small sample sizes, insufficient data, and poor study quality, and the previous review highlighted the need for robust randomised trials. While this update includes more RCTs, we were still unable to meta‐analyse data owing to considerable clinical heterogeneity, skew, and a lack of quantitative data. Our findings reveal the vast range of interventions and outcomes used in this area of research. As stated in Yorke 2005, one solution to this issue could be introducing a research programme with prespecified outcome measures and manualised interventions to evaluate the effect of psychological interventions in children and adolescents with asthma.
