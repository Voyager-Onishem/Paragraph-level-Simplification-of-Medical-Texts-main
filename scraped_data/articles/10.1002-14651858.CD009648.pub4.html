    • Background

    Many surgical approaches are available to treat varicose veins secondary to chronic venous insufficiency. One of the least invasive techniques is the ambulatory conservative hemodynamic correction of venous insufficiency method (in French 'cure conservatrice et hémodynamique de l'insuffisance veineuse en ambulatoire' (CHIVA)), an approach based on venous hemodynamics with deliberate preservation of the superficial venous system. This is the second update of the review first published in 2013.

    Objectives

    To compare the efficacy and safety of the CHIVA method with alternative therapeutic techniques to treat varicose veins.

    Search methods

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist searched the Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, AMED, and the World Health Organisation International Clinical Trials Registry Platform and ClinicalTrials.gov trials registries to 19 October 2020. We also searched PUBMED to 19 October 2020 and checked the references of relevant articles to identify additional studies.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that compared CHIVA to other therapeutic techniques to treat varicose veins.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed and selected studies, extracted data, and performed quantitative analysis from the selected papers. A third author solved any disagreements. We assessed the risk of bias in included trials with the Cochrane risk of bias tool. We calculated the risk ratio (RR), mean difference (MD), number of people needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB), and the number of people needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH), with 95% confidence intervals (CI). We evaluated the certainty of the evidence using GRADE. The main outcomes of interest were the recurrence of varicose veins and side effects.

    Main results

    For this update, we identified two new additional studies. In total, we included six RCTs with 1160 participants (62% women) and collected from them eight comparisons. Three RCTs compared CHIVA with vein stripping. One RCT compared CHIVA with compression dressings in people with venous ulcers. The new studies included three comparisons, one compared CHIVA with vein stripping and radiofrequency ablation (RFA), and one compared CHIVA with vein stripping and endovenous laser therapy. We judged the certainty of the evidence for our outcomes as low to very low due to inconsistency, imprecision caused by the low number of events and risk of bias. The overall risk of bias across studies was high because neither participants nor personnel were blinded to the interventions. Two studies attempted to blind outcome assessors, but the characteristics of the surgery limited concealment.

    Five studies reported the outcome clinical recurrence of varicose veins with a follow‐up of 18 months to 10 years. CHIVA may make little or no difference to the recurrence of varicose veins in the lower limb compared to stripping (RR 0.74, 95% CI 0.46 to 1.20; 5 studies, 966 participants; low‐certainty evidence). We are uncertain whether CHIVA reduced recurrence compared to compression dressing (RR 0.23, 95% CI 0.06 to 0.96; 1 study, 47 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may make little or no difference to clinical recurrence compared to RFA (RR 2.02, 95% CI 0.74 to 5.53; 1 study, 146 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and endovenous laser (RR 0.20, 95% CI 0.01 to 4.06; 1 study, 100 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    We found no clear difference between CHIVA and stripping for the side effects of limb infection (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.33 to 2.10; 3 studies, 746 participants; low‐certainty evidence), and superficial vein thrombosis (RR 1.05, 95% CI 0.51 to 2.17; 4 studies, 846 participants; low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may reduce slightly nerve injury (RR 0.14, 95% CI 0.02 to 0.98; NNTH 9, 95% CI 5 to 100; 4 studies, 846 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and hematoma compared to stripping (RR 0.59, 95% CI 0.37 to 0.97; NNTH 11, 95% CI 5 to 100; 2 studies, 245 participants; low‐certainty evidence). For bruising, one study found no differences between groups while another study found reduced rates of bruising in the CHIVA group compared to the stripping group. Compared to RFA, CHIVA may make little or no difference to rates of limb infection, superficial vein thrombosis, nerve injury or hematoma, but may cause more bruising (RR 1.15, 95% CI 1.04 to 1.28; NNTH 8, CI 95% 5 to 25; 1 study, 144 participants; low‐certainty evidence). Compared to endovenous laser, CHIVA may make little or no difference to rates of limb infection, superficial vein thrombosis, nerve injury or hematoma. The study comparing CHIVA versus compression did not report side effects.

    Authors' conclusions

    There may be little or no difference in the recurrence of varicose veins when comparing CHIVA to stripping (low‐certainty evidence), but CHIVA may slightly reduce nerve injury and hematoma in the lower limb (low‐certainty evidence). Very limited evidence means we are uncertain of any differences in recurrence when comparing CHIVA with compression (very low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may make little or no difference to recurrence compared to RFA (low‐certainty evidence), but may result in more bruising (low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may make little or no difference to recurrence and side effects compared to endovenous laser therapy (low‐certainty evidence). However, we based these conclusions on a small number of trials with a high risk of bias as the effects of surgery could not be concealed, and the results were imprecise due to the low number of events. New RCTs are needed to confirm these results and to compare CHIVA with approaches other than open surgery.

    • CHIVA method for the treatment of varicose veins

    Background

    Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) is a disorder in which veins fail to pump blood back to the heart adequately. As a result, it can cause varicose veins, skin ulcers, and superficial or deep vein thrombosis in the legs, and symptoms such as pain, fatigue, heaviness, warmth and swelling of the leg. The ambulatory conservative hemodynamic correction of venous insufficiency (CHIVA) method is a minimally invasive surgical technique to treat varicose veins. The CHIVA treatment aims to eliminate venous‐venous shunts (abnormal connections between veins) by disconnecting the escape points, preserving the saphenous vein (a vein from the top of the foot to the upper thigh area) and normal venous drainage of the limb's superficial tissues.

    How did we identify and evaluate the evidence?

    First, we searched the medical literature for randomized controlled trials (RCTs), clinical studies where people are randomly put into one or more treatment groups. This type of study provides the most robust evidence about the effects of treatment. We compared the results and summarized the evidence from all the studies. Finally, we assessed how certain the evidence was. To do this, we considered factors such as the way studies were conducted, study sizes and consistency of findings across studies. Based on our assessments, we categorized the evidence as very low, low, moderate or high certainty.

    What did we find?

    We found six RCTs that included 1160 participants who had CVI. Three RCTs compared the CHIVA method with vein stripping, one RCT compared the CHIVA method with compression dressings in people with venous ulcers. The newly included studies included three comparisons, one compared CHIVA with vein stripping (where a vein is removed or tied off) and radiofrequency ablation (RFA; which destroys the nerve fibres carrying pain signals to the brain), and one compared CHIVA with vein stripping and endovenous laser therapy (where veins are heated by a laser). The results showed that the CHIVA method may make little or no difference to the recurrence of varicose veins and may reduce slightly nerve injury and bruising in the lower limb compared to stripping. The CHIVA method showed similar numbers of limb infection and superficial vein thrombosis (inflammation and clotting in a vein) as vein stripping. Compared to RFA, CHIVA may make little or no difference to rates of limb infection, superficial vein thrombosis, nerve injury or hematoma (a severe bruise within the soft tissues), but may cause more bruising. Compared to endovenous laser, CHIVA may make little or no difference to recurrence and numbers of side effects.

    How reliable is the evidence?

    Further studies are needed to confirm these conclusions since they are based on a small number of clinical trials with methodological limitations such as a high risk of bias. In addition, participants and outcome assessors were not blinded to what treatments groups were given, and the results were imprecise due to the low number of events.

    How up to date is this review?

    The evidence in this Cochrane Review is current to 19 October 2020.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is low to very low‐certainty evidence that the ambulatory conservative hemodynamic correction of venous insufficiency (CHIVA) method may make little or no difference to the recurrence of varicose veins and side effects such as limb infection and superficial vein thrombosis in the lower limb compared to stripping. Based on low‐certainty evidence, CHIVA may slightly reduce hematoma and nerve injury compared to vein stripping.

    Very limited evidence means we are uncertain of any differences in recurrence when comparing CHIVA with compression (very low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may result in little or no difference in recurrence and side effects compared to radiofrequency ablation (RFA) and endovenous laser (low‐certainty evidence). However, CHIVA, compared with RFA, may result in more bruising (low‐certainty evidence). The CHIVA method may make little or no difference compared to stripping or RFA on quality of life at 24 months. However, we base these conclusions on a small number of trials with a high risk of bias as the effects of surgery could not be concealed, and the results were imprecise due to the low number of events. New RCTs are needed to confirm these results.

    Implications for research

    Randomized controlled trials are needed to clarify the efficacy on varicose veins recurrence and safety of the CHIVA method versus other techniques. These trials should also clarify if there are any differences in the drained or non‐drained CHIVA approach. To avoid detection bias, the outcome assessor should be an independent physician with a great knowledge of the techniques. Quality of life should be included as an outcome of interest, with a standardized instrument for measurement to assess participant satisfaction with the intervention. It is also of interest to compare the CHIVA method with surgical approaches other than open surgery.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) is a complex condition in which the veins do not efficiently return blood from the legs to the heart. CVI develops slowly, and its complications often appear years or even decades after the onset of symptoms (Kurz 1999). Its origin is multifactorial and clinical manifestations are widely varied, ranging from dilated cutaneous veins and varicose veins to oedema, skin discoloration and ulcers in advanced cases. However, the most common symptoms are pain, fatigue, heaviness, warmth and swelling of the leg, all of which are more intense when standing and under environmental conditions of heat and humidity (Vanhoutte 1997). Also, impairment of quality of life (QoL) in people with symptomatic CVI is reported (Kurz 2001). CVI can affect the superficial venous system or the deep venous system, or both, but the most common situation is the superficial venous system's involvement; the main manifestation is varicose veins (Table 1; Porter 1995).

    The estimated prevalence of CVI varies depending on the criteria used. Based on varicose veins in physical examination, prevalence has been estimated to range from 30% to 40% in the general adult population (Lee 2003), and affects up to 50% of women (Carpentier 2004). If we consider reflux detection in the superficial venous system by hemodynamic study, prevalence can range from 9% in males to 15% in females (Allan 2000).

    Treatment options for CVI have significantly advanced in recent years and involve both medical and surgical methods. The choice of therapy depends on the development stage (Agus 2001), and options are often used in combination. Treatment of CVI in general and specific treatments for varicose veins do not cure the disease, but they can prevent complications and improve signs and symptoms, even the healing of ulcers in some cases. General treatment for CVI includes hygienic and postural measures (such as elevation of the legs or walking around to keep the muscle pump working) (Hamdan 2012), compression therapy (Nelson 2000; Nelson 2011; O'Meara 2009), topical treatment (Aziz 2011; Cullum 2010; Jull 2008; Kranke 2004; O'Meara 2010; Palfreyman 2006), and drug therapy (Martinez‐Zapata 2005; O'Meara 2010). Specific treatment for varicose veins includes sclerotherapy (Tisi 2006; Whing 2021), laser therapy (Flemming 1999), radiofrequency ablation (RFA; Nesbitt 2011), and surgery (Whing 2021).

    Surgical treatment for varicose veins dates back to the early 20th century when Keller described removing the saphenous vein through a metal loop in 1905 (Keller 1905), and Lofgren described endoluminal stripping in 1906 (Lofgren 1977). Two years later, Babcock used a vein‐extractor similar to that used today (Lofgren 1977). In 1966, Muller described ambulatory phlebotomy (Muller 1966). Such techniques, however, cannot entirely prevent varicose vein recurrence (Blomgren 2004; Labropoulos 2005; Perrin 2000; Winterborn 2004), or remodelling of the venous network of subcutaneous tissue (Juan 2002).

    The introduction of duplex scanning in the study of CVI has provided in vivo knowledge of venous hemodynamics (Franceschi 1988). It enables a complete morphological study and allows mapping of the venous system's hemodynamics, providing precise information on any changes or abnormalities (Cavezzi 2007; Coleridge‐Smith 2006; Labropoulos 2001; Labropoulos 2005; Nicolaides 2000).

    Description of the intervention

    In 1988, Franceschi described a procedure for treating varicose veins based on venous hemodynamics with preservation of the superficial venous system. He named this procedure ambulatory conservative hemodynamic correction of venous insufficiency (cure conservatrice et hémodynamique de l'insuffisance veineuse en ambulatoire (CHIVA)) (Franceschi 1997).

    CHIVA can be performed via open surgery or endovascular procedures (laser, radiofrequency or sclerotherapy). It is based on modifying the venous system's hemodynamics to eliminate varicose dilations and preserve the saphenous vein. CHIVA decreases of the diameter of the saphenous vein (from 2.6 mm to 1.6 mm) and the femoral vein (from 0.7 mm to 0.4 mm) have supported this theory (Escribano 2003; Mendoza 2011).

    How the intervention might work

    The venous blood's physiological route is an anterograde circuit, draining from the superficial venous system to the deep venous system, and finally, to the heart. Primary varicose veins are characterized by a retrograde circuit or a venous‐venous shunt (Goren 1996). This abnormal circuit consists of a retrograde proximal reflux point (escape point). From these abnormal circuits, deep venous system blood is discharged into the superficial venous system, usually the saphenous veins. This pathological situation is caused by the dysfunction of the valves of the veins. The accumulation of blood in the veins causes a hydrostatic pressure column between the escape point and the point of re‐entry into the deep venous system. It generally comprises the saphenous vein and the perforating re‐entry vein. This blood in the deep venous system can re‐enter the superficial venous system. This closed circuit is the venous‐venous shunt.

    CHIVA treatment aims to eliminate the venous‐venous shunts by disconnecting the escape points, and preserving the saphenous vein and normal venous drainage of the limb's superficial tissues. The CHIVA method is a minimally invasive surgical procedure, usually performed under local anesthesia, based on the findings of a careful analysis of the hemodynamic superficial venous network by duplex ultrasound. The CHIVA method's principles are the fragmentation of the venous pressure column, disconnection of venous‐venous shunts, preservation of the re‐entry perforators and the abolition of undrained superficial varicose veins. Fragmentation of the venous pressure column and disconnection of venous‐venous shunts are generally implemented by open surgery, but sclerotherapy, laser or radiofrequency can also be used.

    Different strategies are described to apply CHIVA, named 'drained' and 'non‐drained' strategies. Drained strategies, such as CHIVA 1 and CHIVA 2, only disconnect the escape point or the insufficient collaterals to interrupt the venovenous shunt in the first surgical act, ensuring saphenous vein drainage and its patency. This strategy sometimes implies serial ultrasound surveillance and, in some cases, further planned surgical procedures (Juan 2005). In contrast, the non‐drained strategy, named 'CHIVA 1+2', consists of interrupting both the escape point and the insufficient saphenous collaterals in a single surgical procedure, generating a system that does not ensure saphenous patency due to the blockage of blood inside its lumen. In this case, this saphenous thrombosis is usually temporary, and new insufficient varicose veins are usual (Cappelli 2000), requiring further non‐planned interventions. Hence, some studies have advised against non‐drained CHIVA whenever possible (Escribano 2021; Mendoza 2018).

    Why it is important to do this review

    CHIVA is one of the most widely used methods in several countries and is one of the few strategies that treat varicose veins without seeking their destruction. This review aims to identify the currently available evidence to compare the CHIVA method with other techniques to treat CVI and aid decision‐making for healthcare professionals and patients.

    Objectives

    To compare the efficacy and safety of the CHIVA method with alternative therapeutic techniques to treat varicose veins.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included all randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing the CHIVA method with other therapeutic techniques to treat varicose veins.

    Types of participants

    We included men and women over 18 years of age with varicose veins at clinical stage C2 to C6 of the CEAP (Clinical, Etiology, Anatomy, Pathophysiology) classification (Porter 1995) (Table 1).

    Types of interventions

    We included RCTs that assessed the CHIVA method compared with other therapeutic techniques to treat varicose veins, such as drugs, sclerotherapy, compressive dressings and other surgical methods.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Recurrence of varicose veins: defined as the detection of new varicose veins by clinical or ultrasound evaluation after a minimum follow‐up of one year.

    • Side effects: defined as any of the following clinical events or complications with a minimum follow‐up of one month after the intervention:

      • bruises;

      • infection;

      • superficial or deep venous thrombosis;

      • lung embolism;

      • nerve injury;

      • hematoma.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Retreatment or re‐intervention: defined as the need for a second intervention due to recurrence of varicose veins or new varicose veins in the same leg and the same area.

    • Any secondary clinical or aesthetic change after a minimum follow‐up of one month after the intervention assessed by the following:

      • objective signs:

        • free from reflux: defined by reverse flow from the deep venous system to the superficial venous system and checked by duplex ultrasound;

        • oedema: measured by the dichotomous variable oedema and the continuous variables 'ankle perimeter circumference' and 'volume of the leg';

        • skin manifestations, such as venous ulcers and trophic alterations, which may include telangiectasia (small red points on the skin caused by permanently opened tiny blood vessels), reticular veins (dilated veins that show as a net‐like pattern on the skin), varicose veins (permanently dilated veins) or lipodermatosclerosis (hardening of the skin that may gain a red or brown pigmentation and is accompanied by wasting of the subcutaneous fat);

      • subjective symptoms:

        • pain;

        • cramps;

        • restless legs;

        • itching;

        • the feeling of heaviness in the legs;

        • swelling;

        • paresthesias (abnormal sensations, such as prickling, burning, tingling).

    • Global assessment measures of disease‐specific QoL, measured by validated scales (e.g. Chronic Venous Insufficiency Quality of Life Questionnaire (CIVIQ) or VEnous INsufficiency Epidemiological and Economic Studies Quality of Life (VEINES‐QoL)) or participant satisfaction, or both.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    There were no restrictions on language or publication status.

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Vascular Information Specialist conducted systematic searches of the following databases for RCTs and controlled clinical trials without language, publication year or publication status restrictions:

    • the Cochrane Vascular Specialised Register via the Cochrane Register of Studies (CRS‐Web searched on 19 October 2020);

    • the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) Cochrane Register of Studies Online (CRSO 2020, Issue 9);

    • MEDLINE (Ovid MEDLINE Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Ovid MEDLINE Daily and Ovid MEDLINE) (searched from 1 January 2017 to 19 October 2020);

    • Embase Ovid (searched from 1 January 2017 to 19 October 2020);

    • CINAHL EBSCO (searched from 1 January 2017 to 19 October 2020);

    • AMED Ovid (searched from 1 January 2017 to 19 October 2020).

    The Information Specialist modelled search strategies for other databases on the search strategy designed for CENTRAL. Where appropriate, we combined strategies with adaptations of the highly sensitive search strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying RCTs and controlled clinical trials (as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions, Chapter 6 (Lefebvre 2011). Search strategies for major databases are provided in Appendix 1.

    The Information Specialist searched the following trials registries on 19 October 2020:

    • the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (who.int/trialsearch);

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov).

    In addition, the review authors searched PubMed (last searched September 2020) using the search strategy given in Appendix 2.

    Searching other resources

    We scrutinized reference lists of identified RCTs, systematic reviews meta‐analyses and network meta‐analyses to find further trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (CT and PEP) independently assessed the eligibility of the studies identified in the search. We classified potentially eligible studies as included when the criteria were met, or excluded if they did not. In cases of disagreements, a third review author (MJM) would have independently evaluated the study and discussed it with the rest of the team. When necessary, we contacted the study authors for further information.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (SB and MJM) independently collected data using a previously tested standardized form. Data included methodological quality, characteristics of study participants, characteristics of the intervention and control groups, and outcome characteristics of each group of participants. We also specified whether the studies calculated the sample size needed and whether they included an intention‐to‐treat (ITT) analysis or a modified ITT (MITT). One review author (MJM) entered the data into Review Manager 5 and performed the appropriate analyses (Review Manager 2020). For this update, a second author checked the data (PEP).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (CT and PEP) assessed the risk of bias (quality) of the studies, examining the randomization method (sequence generation and allocation concealment). They also assessed the blinding of participants and investigators (caregivers and outcome assessors), the completeness of outcome data and the percentage of participants lost to follow‐up.

    Once this information was gathered, the review authors classified each study into low, unclear or high risk of bias, based on the criteria specified in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021).

    Measures of treatment effect

    For each study, we calculated risk ratios (RR) for dichotomous variables and mean differences (MD) for continuous variables with 95% confidence intervals (CI). If the continuous variables in the studies were measured using different scales, we calculated the standardized mean difference (SMD) and 95% CIs. For the primary outcomes, we also calculated the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs, when appropriate (see Data synthesis).

    Unit of analysis issues

    González‐Cañas 2021 stated the unit of analysis as the limb, and Zamboni 2003, the ulcer and participants. However, few participants contributed with more than one limb (11 participants for González‐Cañas 2021) and an ulcer (three participants for Zamboni 2003). Therefore, we analyzed the data as if the unit of analysis was the individual.

    Dealing with missing data

    If considered necessary, we contacted study authors to obtain additional information to clarify issues regarding missing data (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003). The main analysis was an 'available‐case analysis', analysing data as provided in the individual studies. For the ITT analysis, we inputted data for participants withdrawn or lost to follow‐up as negative events, using the worst‐case scenario for the CHIVA intervention group (negative events for the experimental group and positive events in the control or comparison group).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We examined the characteristics of each study to determine clinical heterogeneity. We examined statistical heterogeneity using Chi2, and I2 tests, which describe the variability in effect estimates due to heterogeneity between studies rather than chance. The I2 statistic is given as a percentage, with a measure of 0% meaning little to no variability in effect estimates between the studies and progressing amounts of variability with increased I2 statistic percentage values (Higgins 2021). We deemed an I2 statistic greater than 50% as substantial heterogeneity. We also studied the sources of heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We did not perform a funnel plot to assess reporting bias because we included fewer than 10 studies (Sterne 2011).

    Data synthesis

    We estimated the global effect for each variable through a meta‐analysis. We applied the statistical method of Mantel‐Haenszel for dichotomous measures and the inverse variance for continuous measurements, using a fixed‐effect model. When the I2 statistic was greater than 50%, we used a random‐effects model, indicating substantial heterogeneity. If heterogeneity was greater than 75%, we did not pool the results but reported them narratively. We performed all statistical analyses using Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020). We calculated NNTB or NNTH for primary outcomes when pooling was appropriate and if there was evidence of an effect.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned subgroup analysis for sources of clinical heterogeneity when data were available.

    • Type of procedure used to implement the CHIVA method: open surgery, sclerotherapy, laser, radiofrequency or any other.

    • Characteristic of the CHIVA method: drained versus non‐drained.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We conducted a sensitivity analysis to assess the strength of our results and explain possible heterogeneity between the studies. For this purpose and as mentioned in the Dealing with missing data section, we re‐analyzed the data by ITT and imputed data using the worst‐case scenario (imputed missing values for participants who withdrew or were lost to follow‐up as negative events in the experimental group and as positive events in the control group).

    We did not conduct sensitivity analyses by comparing unpublished studies with published studies or comparing studies with a high risk of bias with those having a low risk of bias due to lack of suitable data. If data become available in future updates, we will do this.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a table compiling and summarizing the evidence for the most clinically relevant comparison of CHIVA versus stripping. The study populations consisted of people with varicose veins. We selected the most important and clinically relevant outcomes (both desirable and undesirable) that were thought to be essential for decision‐making for the summary of findings Table 1. These outcomes were recurrence of varicose veins, bruises, limb infection, superficial vein thrombosis and nerve injury. Assumed control intervention risks were calculated by mean baseline risk from the study population for each outcome and the median control group risk from studies on meta‐analysis when data were obtained from two or more studies. We used the system developed by the GRADE working group for grading the certainty of the evidence as high, moderate, low and very low, based on the within‐study risk of bias, the directness of evidence, heterogeneity, the precision of effects estimates and risk of population bias (GRADE 2004).

    Results of the search

    This update identified 1473 citations. After removing 248 duplicates, we screened 1225 citations by title and abstract, and assessed 1188 articles as not relevant. We assessed the full‐text reports of 37 potentially relevant articles finding that 30 were not relevant, two were excluded with reasons and three articles corresponding to two additional new studies were included (González‐Cañas 2021; Wang 2016). This brings the total number of included studies to six (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003). See Characteristics of included studies table. There are no ongoing studies or studies awaiting classification.

    Included studies

    We included six RCTs, which recruited 1160 participants (62% women) aged 26 to 69 years (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003). See Characteristics of included studies tables.

    All participants had CVI and a clinical stage of the CEAP classification between 2 and 6. Iborra‐Ortega 2006 included only people with CEAP 2, whereas Zamboni 2003 included only people with venous ulcers and CEAP 6 (Table 1). Follow‐up of participants varied between studies, with a minimum of 18 months (Wang 2016) and a maximum of 10 years (Carandina 2008). Five studies compared CHIVA with stripping (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016), one study compared CHIVA with compression dressings (Zamboni 2003), one compared CHIVA with radiofrequency (RFA) (González‐Cañas 2021), and one compared CHIVA with endovenous laser (Wang 2016). All studies used open surgery to implement the CHIVA method. Three studies implemented the drained CHIVA method (Carandina 2008; Pares 2010; Wang 2016); and three the non‐drained CHIVA method (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Zamboni 2003).

    Only Pares 2010 specified a sample size calculation.

    Five studies reported the funding sources (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003), and one study did not specify this (Carandina 2008). Two studies reported relevant declarations of interest (González‐Cañas 2021; Wang 2016), and four studies did not report this (Carandina 2008; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Zamboni 2003).

    Excluded studies

    We excluded two additional studies that assessed CHIVA in this update (Maldonado‐Fernández 2016; Zmudzinsk 2017). Bringing the total number of excluded studies to seven (see Characteristics of excluded studies table). Five studies were not randomized (Maeso 2001; Maldonado‐Fernández 2016; Solis 2009; Zamboni 1995; Zamboni 2003), and two studies were not controlled (Zamboni 1996; Zmudzinsk 2017).

    Overall, the risk of bias was generally high, mainly because participants and outcome assessors were not blinded to the interventions (see Figure 2; Figure 3).

    Allocation

    Five studies adequately explained the randomization sequence generation (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Zamboni 2003). Only three studies specified allocation concealment (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006). We contacted the other three study authors, and two provided this information (Pares 2010; Zamboni 2003). Three studies specified that allocation was done by telephone (Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Zamboni 2003). Carandina 2008 blinded the allocation to the treating physicians, but it did not state how the blinding was done.

    Blinding

    Studies could not be blinded because the specific anatomical changes produced by the intervention were easily recognisable by the specialists, so all studies were at high risk for personnel performance bias. Where participants were aware of what treatment they received we assessed the participant performance bias risk also as high (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003). We assessed Carandina 2008 as unclear as it was not specified if participants were blinded. Four studies were at high risk of detection bias as assessors were aware of the treatments given (Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016; Zamboni 2003). In two studies, independent assessors evaluated the clinical or ultrasound results in an attempt to compensate for this bias. However, it is unclear if this assessment was done by a researcher who was not specialized in vascular surgery and therefore unable to identify the type of surgery, so we assessed these at unclear risk of detection bias (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021).

    Incomplete outcome data

    The percentage of participants lost to follow‐up was less than 12% in both groups in four studies (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Zamboni 2003), and 5.7% in the intervention group (CHIVA) in Carandina 2008. However, in the conventional surgery group of Carandina 2008 (stripping), the percentage of losses was 28%, so this was assessed as being at a high risk of bias. Wang 2016 did not report a flowchart of recruitment, however, it was assessed as being at low risk of bias because all participants were included in the analysis. González‐Cañas 2021 was at an unclear risk for attrition bias because the study did not report the total number of randomized participants in different groups, as their unit of eligibility was the limb.

    Selective reporting

    In Zamboni 2003, it was unclear if recurrence data were based on ultrasound or clinical parameters; data about ulcer healing time were incomplete and so were not included in this review. Therefore, we assessed this study at unclear risk of reporting bias. González‐Cañas 2021 changed the primary outcome specified in the protocol, and randomized seven participants twice (information obtained from the study authors), so we assessed this study at high risk of reporting bias. Carandina 2008, Iborra‐Ortega 2006, and Pares 2010 were at low risk of reporting bias as the results of all outcomes prespecified in the methods of the trial were reported. Wang 2016 was at an unclear risk of bias, as the paper did not explain the outcomes suitably in the methods section.

    Other potential sources of bias

    There were no other potential sources of bias so all included studies were at low risk of other bias.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 CHIVA compared to stripping for the treatment of chronic venous insufficiency

    Three RCTs compared the CHIVA method with vein stripping (Carandina 2008; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010), and one RCT compared the CHIVA method with compression dressings in people with venous ulcers (Zamboni 2003). The newly included studies had three comparisons; one RCT compared CHIVA with vein stripping and RFA (González‐Cañas 2021), and the other RCT compared CHIVA with vein stripping and endovenous laser therapy (Wang 2016).

    Comparison of CHIVA versus vein stripping

    We identified five RCTs that compared CHIVA with vein stripping (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016).

    Primary outcome
    Recurrence of varicose veins

    The five studies included 966 participants. The range of time to follow‐up was 18 months to 10 years. CHIVA may have little or no effect on the recurrence of varicose veins in the lower limb compared to stripping (RR 0.74, 95% CI 0.46 to 1.20; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1). The certainty of the evidence was downgraded two levels because of the overall high risk of bias (all studies presented limitations in the concealment of the interventions, and one presented selective reporting) and inconsistency. We used a random‐effects method as the I2 statistic was 61%.

    The subgroup analysis considered if there were any differences when the CHIVA method was drained or non‐drained and found no differences between subgroups (test for subgroup differences P = 0.19).

    The sensitivity analysis comparing published studies to non‐published was not performed since all studies were published. Further, we did not analyze sensitivity based on the risk of bias because all studies had a high risk of bias.

    The sensitivity analysis by ITT included the five trials, and an additional 30 participants using imputed data, giving 996 in total. There were no differences in the recurrence of varicose veins in the lower limb between CHIVA compared to stripping (RR 0.90, 95% CI 0.56 to 1.44; 5 studies, 996 participants; Analysis 1.12). In addition, there were no differences between the subgroup analysis considering the drained or non‐drained CHIVA method (test for subgroup differences P = 0.23).

    Side effects

    Four studies reported information for side effects in 846 participants (Analysis 1.2) (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016).

    Two studies provided data on bruises (González‐Cañas 2021; Pares 2010), but we were unable to pool the results because of the high heterogeneity (I2 = 99%). González‐Cañas 2021 showed similar reports of bruising between groups (99% with CHIVA compared to 97% with vein stripping), but in Pares 2010, the rates of bruising were reduced in the CHIVA group (45.5% with CHIVA compared to 71.8% with vein stripping).

    Three studies reported on limb infection (González‐Cañas 2021; Pares 2010; Wang 2016). CHIVA may have little or no difference in limb infection compared to stripping (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.33 to 2.10; 3 studies, 746 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2). The certainty of the evidence was low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in the concealment of the interventions and selective reporting) and serious imprecision (low number of events).

    Four studies included information on superficial vein thrombosis (González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016). CHIVA may make little or no difference to superficial vein thrombosis compared to stripping (RR 1.05, 95% CI 0.51 to 2.17; 846 participants; low‐certainty evidence). The same four studies reported data on nerve injury. Analysis indicated that CHIVA may slightly reduce nerve injury compared to stripping (RR 0.14, 95% CI 0.02 to 0.98; NNTH 9, 95% CI 5 to 100; 846 participants; I2 = 68%; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2). For both outcomes, the certainty of the evidence was low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in the concealment of the interventions and selective reporting), inconsistency and imprecision (low number of events).

    Two studies reported hematoma (González‐Cañas 2021; Wang 2016). CHIVA may reduce hematoma slightly compared to stripping (RR 0.59, 95% CI 0.37 to 0.97; NNTH 11, 95% CI 5 to 100; 245 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2). The certainty of the evidence was very low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in the concealment of the interventions and selective reporting) and very serious imprecision (very low number of events and participants).

    Secondary outcomes
    Retreatment

    One study reported data about re‐interventions after five‐year follow‐up (Iborra‐Ortega 2006). We are uncertain whether CHIVA reduces retreatment compared to stripping (RR 0.96, 95% CI 0.30 to 3.11; 100 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3). The certainty of the evidence was very low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in the concealment of the interventions) and very serious imprecision (very low number of events and participants).

    Clinical or aesthetic changes

    Objective signs

    Three studies reported free from reflux data, checked by echo‐Doppler (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Pares 2010). The studies had a combined total of 751 participants, but we did not pool the data due to the high heterogeneity (I2 = 96%).

    None of the studies reported oedema.

    One study reported aesthetic improvement monitored by the participant as a dichotomous outcome (Iborra‐Ortega 2006). We are uncertain whether CHIVA achieves more aesthetic improvement reported by the participant compared to stripping at five‐year follow‐up (RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.92 to 1.15; 95 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.5). Carandina 2008 (124 participants) described aesthetic improvement reported by the patient as a continuous outcome. We are uncertain whether CHIVA achieves more aesthetic improvement reported by the participant compared to stripping at 10‐year follow‐up (MD –0.16, 95% CI –2.66 to 2.34; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). Iborra‐Ortega 2006 reported aesthetic improvement monitored by the investigator as a dichotomous outcome. We are uncertain whether CHIVA achieves more aesthetic improvement reported by the investigator compared to stripping at five‐year follow‐up (RR 1.12, 95% CI 0.85 to 1.48; 96 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.7). The certainty of the evidence was very low in the above outcomes due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in concealing the intervention) and very serious imprecision (very low number of events and participants).

    Subjective symptoms

    Three studies reported cure or no clinical symptoms after treatment (Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016). CHIVA may lead to an increased cure or no clinical symptoms compared to stripping (RR 1.70, 95% CI 1.41 to 2.06; 710 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.8). The certainty of the evidence was low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in concealing the intervention) and a low number of events. We used subgroup analysis to investigate differences between drained or non‐drained CHIVA, but there none were none (test for subgroup differences P = 0.75).

    Two studies assessed clinical improvement (Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010). There was no evidence of a difference in clinical improvement after CHIVA compared to stripping (RR 0.93, 95% CI 0.71 to 1.21; 601 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.9). The certainty of the evidence was low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in concealing the intervention) and imprecision (low number of events). There were no differences between drained and undrained CHIVA subgroups (test for subgroup differences P = 0.34).

    One study reported pain assessed using a visual analogue scale of 100 mm, where no pain = 0 mm and worst pain = 100 mm (González‐Cañas 2021). CHIVA may make little or no difference to pain compared to stripping 24 hours postsurgery (MD –4.50 mm, 95% CI –12.39 to 3.39; 145 participants) and one month postsurgery (MD 1.70, 95% CI –5.54 to 8.94; 126 participants) (low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.10). The certainty of the evidence was downgraded due to imprecision for the low number of events and a high risk of bias because of a limitation in blinding and selective reporting. The outcome assessor was blinded, but the scar and the anatomic changes of the interventions were difficult to conceal.

    Global assessment measures

    One study provided information on QoL at 24 months measured using the CIVIQ‐20 and the 36‐item Short Form (SF‐36) questionnaires (González‐Cañas 2021). CHIVA may make little or no difference compared to stripping to QoL measured using the CIVIQ‐20 questionnaire (MD 1.80, 95% CI –3.03 to 6.63; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence) and SF‐36 questionnaire related with physical conditions (MD –5.30, 95% CI –12.49 to 1.89; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence); and mental conditions (MD –7.00, 95% CI –13.78 to –0.22; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.11). The certainty of the evidence was downgraded due to imprecision for a low number of events and a high risk of bias because of limitations in blinding and selective reporting. The outcome assessor was blinded, but the scar and the anatomic changes of the interventions were difficult to conceal.

    Comparison of the CHIVA method versus compression dressing

    One RCT included only participants with venous ulcers and compared CHIVA with compression in 47 people (Zamboni 2003). The unit of analysis was the ulcer and not the participants, but the data were analyzed because, except for one person, all participants had only one ulcer. We are uncertain whether CHIVA improves venous ulcer recurrence (RR 0.23, 95% CI 0.06 to 0.96; NNTH 3, 95% CI 2 to 17; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.1). There was no clear difference between the groups for the 'cure' of a venous ulcer when comparing CHIVA with compression dressing (RR 1.04, 95% CI 0.93 to 1.17; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 2.2). The certainty of the evidence was very low due to the high risk of bias (no concealment of the interventions and selective reporting) and very serious imprecision (very low number of events and participants).

    One study assessed QoL using the SF‐36 questionnaire, but presented no numerical data in the paper (Zamboni 2003). The median score differences (end of observation minus baseline) for the eight domains of the SF‐36 questionnaire were shown in a Kaplan‐Meier graph only. The study authors reported that the CHIVA method significantly improved the QoL after three‐year follow‐up.

    Zamboni 2003 did not report side effects.

    Comparison of the CHIVA method versus vein radiofrequency ablation

    One RCT compared CHIVA with vein RFA (González‐Cañas 2021). The certainty of the evidence was low due to the high risk of bias (difficulties in the concealment of the interventions and selective reporting) and serious imprecision (low number of events).

    Primary outcomes
    Recurrence of varicose veins

    González‐Cañas 2021 included 144 participants. There was no clear difference in recurrence of varicose veins in the CHIVA group compared to the RFA group (RR 2.02, 95% CI 0.74 to 5.53; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.1).

    Sensitivity analysis was not performed as only one study was included in this comparison.

    Side effects

    González‐Cañas 2021 provided information on side effects in 144 participants. Bruises may be slightly less frequent in the RFA group compared to CHIVA (RR 1.15, 95% CI 1.04 to 1.28; NNTH 8, 95% CI 5 to 25; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.2). There was no clear difference between CHIVA and RFA on limb infection (RR 3.68, 95% CI 0.42 to 32.13), superficial vein thrombosis (RR 1.01, 95% CI 0.70 to 1.47), nerve injury (RR 0.77, 95% CI 0.35 to 1.66) or hematoma (RR 0.69, 95% CI 0.41 to 1.16) (Analysis 3.2).

    Retreatment

    The study did not report retreatment.

    Clinical or aesthetic changes

    Objective signs

    González‐Cañas 2021 reported free from reflux, checked by echo‐Doppler. The study included 126 participants with at least one evaluation at six months or more postsurgery. CHIVA may not improve the outcome free from reflux compared to RFA (RR 0.49, 95% CI 0.38 to 0.65; NNTB 2, 95% CI 1 to 3; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 3.3).

    Subjective symptoms

    González‐Cañas 2021 reported pain assessed using a visual analogue scale of 100 mm (no pain = 0 mm and worst pain = 100 mm). There was no clear difference between CHIVA and RFA in pain 24 hours postsurgery (MD –0.30 mm, 95% CI –7.48 to 6.88; 145 participants; very low‐certainty evidence); or one month postsurgery (MD –0.20, 95% CI –7.94 to 7.54; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence) (Analysis 3.4).

    Global assessment measures

    González‐Cañas 2021 provided information on QoL at 24 months measured using the CIVIQ‐20 and the SF‐36 questionnaires. There was no clear difference between CHIVA and RFA in QoL measured using the CIVIQ‐20 questionnaire (RR 1.60, 95% CI –3.46 to 6.66; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence); and SF‐36 questionnaire related to physical conditions (RR –5.30, 95% CI –12.78 to 2.18; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence); and mental conditions (RR –4.80, 95% CI –11.89 to 2.29; 126 participants; low‐certainty evidence) (Analysis 3.5).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This systematic review included six RCTs with 1160 participants with chronic venous disease, CEAP 2 to 6. All included studies assessed the CHIVA method implemented by open surgery. Five trials compared CHIVA with vein stripping for varicose veins (Carandina 2008; González‐Cañas 2021; Iborra‐Ortega 2006; Pares 2010; Wang 2016), one compared CHIVA with compression for venous ulcers (Zamboni 2003), one compared CHIVA with RFA (González‐Cañas 2021), and one compared CHIVA with endovenous laser therapy (Wang 2016).

    Meta‐analysis showed that CHIVA might make little or no difference to the recurrence of varicose veins in the lower limb compared to stripping (low‐certainty evidence). Results are uncertain when comparing CHIVA with compressive dressings (very low‐certainty evidence). Furthermore, CHIVA may make little or no difference to recurrence compared to RFA and endovenous laser therapy (low‐certainty evidence).

    The subgroup analysis comparing drained and non‐drained strategies indicated no potential benefit for the drained CHIVA approach in recurrence of varicose veins compared to stripping, but further studies are needed to confirm this (test for subgroup differences not statistically significant).

    Regarding side effects, CHIVA may make little or no difference to bruising, limb infection and superficial vein thrombosis rates compared to stripping. CHIVA may slightly reduce hematoma and nerve injury compared to vein stripping. The study comparing CHIVA with compression dressing did not report side effects. Bruises may be slightly less frequent in the RFA group compared to CHIVA (low‐certainty evidence). There was no clear difference between CHIVA method and RFA and endovenous laser on limb infection, superficial vein thrombosis, nerve injury or hematoma. There was no evidence of a difference in retreatment following either CHIVA or stripping (very low‐certainty evidence). The studies comparing CHIVA with either compression, RFA, or endovenous laser did not report retreatment.

    There was either high heterogeneity or limited evidence for the objective clinical or aesthetic changes in studies comparing CHIVA with stripping. Hence, we are uncertain if there are any differences between the treatment groups. CHIVA may lead to an increased cure or no clinical symptoms compared to stripping (low‐certainty evidence). There was no clear difference between the CHIVA, compression and endovenous laser treatment for venous ulcer cure (very low‐certainty evidence). The CHIVA group had fewer free from reflux events than the RFA group (low‐certainty evidence).

    We are uncertain whether CHIVA achieves more clinical improvement or cosmetic results as perceived by participants and investigators compared to stripping (very low‐certainty evidence). CHIVA may make little or no difference to pain (at 24 hours postsurgery and one‐month postsurgery) and QoL compared to stripping or RFA (both low‐certainty evidence). Quality of life was assessed in the Zamboni 2003 study. The authors reported improved QoL after CHIVA compared to compressive dressings for venous ulcers, but did not provide data.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We have included all relevant studies that assessed the CHIVA method to treat varicose veins in adults. The studies were conducted in China, Italy and Spain, where the CHIVA method is frequently used (Faccini 2019; Milone 2011). However, this method is also implemented or considered in other countries such as Taiwan, Switzerland and the US (Chan 2011; Mendoza 2011; Mowatt‐Larssen 2012; Zmudzinski 2017).

    All participants had venous reflux, and the severity of the chronic venous disease was variable, ranging from aesthetic alterations to leg ulcers. Five studies compared CHIVA implemented by open surgery with vein stripping. Only one RCT with 47 participants compared CHIVA implemented by open surgery with compression for venous ulcers. One study compared CHIVA with radiofrequency and another study with endovenous laser.

    We identified no studies that compared CHIVA with other surgical approaches such as sclerotherapy. Evidence, therefore, is limited to the comparisons of CHIVA implemented by open surgery versus stripping, compression, RFA or endovenous laser therapy.

    All studies included the primary outcome 'recurrence'. From 18 months to 10 years, the length of follow‐up was sufficient to assess recurrence and side effects adequately.

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, the certainty of the evidence was low to very low due to inconsistency, imprecision caused by the low number of events and risk of bias. The global risk of bias was high for selective reporting in two studies and in all studies due to difficulties in concealing the intervention received. Participants and investigators were not blinded to interventions in all studies. Despite blinded outcome assessment being carried out in two clinical trials, the effectiveness of this was limited as the intervention has characteristic anatomical consequences that one vascular specialist can identify by ultrasound and the surgical scar. González‐Cañas 2021 introduced inconsistency in some outcomes. These were possibly related to modifications in the CHIVA method (a single surgical procedure was performed when sometimes two steps are required) and differences in the diagnostic criteria to assess reflux. A small number of studies reporting on the outcomes of interest, with a small number of overall events, resulted in concerns over imprecision. See summary of findings Table 1.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The methodological process of our review was rigorous. The search strategy was thorough without language restrictions. We contacted the main authors of included studies for further information. All trials included in the review were funded either by public organizations or non‐profit institutions. There were no conflicts of interest declared.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Although two narrative reviews on the CHIVA method have been published (Faccini 2019; Mendoza 2008), we found no record of any review with pooled results. Mendoza 2008 included randomized and non‐randomized clinical trials. Faccini 2019 described the technique graphically and discussed the economic perspective, but reported no screening methods for this review. Both reviews concluded that the CHIVA method improved subjective and objective outcomes more than or equal to stripping and had a lower recurrence rate and cost.
