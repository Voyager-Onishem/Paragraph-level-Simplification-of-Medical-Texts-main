Interventions for the symptoms and signs resulting from jellyfish stings

    • Background

    Jellyfish envenomation is common in many coastal regions and varies in severity depending upon the species. Stings cause a variety of symptoms and signs including pain, dermatological reactions, and, in some species, Irukandji syndrome (which may include abdominal/back/chest pain, tachycardia, hypertension, cardiac phenomena, and, rarely, death). Many treatments have been suggested for these symptoms, but their effectiveness is unclear. This is an update of a Cochrane Review last published in 2013.

    Objectives

    To determine the benefits and harms associated with the use of any intervention, in both adults and children, for the treatment of jellyfish stings, as assessed by randomised and quasi‐randomised trials.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and Web of Science up to 27 October 2022. We searched clinical trials registers and the grey literature, and conducted forward‐citation searching of relevant articles. 

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs of any intervention given to treat stings from any species of jellyfish stings. Interventions were compared to another active intervention, placebo, or no treatment. If co‐interventions were used, we included the study only if the co‐intervention was used in each group. 

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane. 

    Main results

    We included nine studies (six RCTs and three quasi‐RCTs) involving a total of 574 participants. We found one ongoing study. Participants were either stung accidentally, or were healthy volunteers exposed to stings in a laboratory setting. Type of jellyfish could not be confirmed in beach settings and was determined by investigators using participant and local information.

    We categorised interventions into comparison groups: hot versus cold applications; topical applications. A third comparison of parenteral administration included no relevant outcome data: a single study (39 participants) evaluated intravenous magnesium sulfate after stings from jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome (Carukia). No studies assessed a fourth comparison group of pressure immobilisation bandages. 

    We downgraded the certainty of the evidence due to very serious risk of bias, serious and very serious imprecision, and serious inconsistency in some results. 

    Application of heat versus application of cold

    Four studies involved accidental stings treated on the beach or in hospital. Jellyfish were described as bluebottles (Physalia ; location: Australia), and box jellyfish that do not cause Irukandji syndrome (Hawaiian box jellyfish (Carybdea alata ) and major box jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri, location: Australia)). Treatments were applied with hot packs or hot water (showers, baths, buckets, or hoses), or ice packs or cold packs. 

    The evidence for all outcomes was of very low certainty, thus we are unsure whether heat compared to cold leads to at least a clinically significant reduction in pain within six hours of stings from Physalia (risk ratio (RR) 2.25, 95% confidence interval (CI) 1.42 to 3.56; 2 studies, 142 participants) or Carybdea alata and Chironex fleckeri (RR 1.66, 95% CI 0.56 to 4.94; 2 studies, 71 participants). We are unsure whether there is a difference in adverse events due to treatment (RR 0.50, 95% CI 0.05 to 5.19; 2 studies, 142 participants); these were minor adverse events reported for Physalia stings. We are also unsure whether either treatment leads to a clinically significant reduction in pain in the first hour (Physalia : RR 2.66, 95% CI 1.71 to 4.15; 1 study, 88 participants; Carybdea alata and Chironex fleckeri: RR 1.16, 95% CI 0.71 to 1.89; 1 study, 42 participants) or cessation of pain at the end of treatment (Physalia : RR 1.63, 95% CI 0.81 to 3.27; 1 study, 54 participants; Carybdea alata and Chironex fleckeri: RR 3.54, 95% CI 0.82 to 15.31; 1 study, 29 participants). Evidence for retreatment with the same intervention was only available for Physalia , with similar uncertain findings (RR 0.19, 95% CI 0.01 to 3.90; 1 study, 96 participants), as was the case for retreatment with the alternative hot or cold application after Physalia (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.55 to 1.82; 1 study, 54 participants) and Chironex fleckeri stings (RR 0.48, 95% CI 0.02 to 11.17; 1 study, 42 participants). Evidence for dermatological signs (itchiness or rash) was available only at 24 hours for Physalia stings (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.63 to 1.65; 2 studies, 98 participants). 

    Topical applications

    One study (62 participants) included accidental stings from Hawaiian box jellyfish (Carybdea alata) treated on the beach with fresh water, seawater, Sting Aid (a commercial product), or Adolph's (papain) meat tenderiser. In another study, healthy volunteers (97 participants) were stung with an Indonesian sea nettle (Chrysaora chinensis from Malaysia) in a laboratory setting and treated with isopropyl alcohol, ammonia, heated water, acetic acid, or sodium bicarbonate. Two other eligible studies (Carybdea alata and Physalia stings) did not measure the outcomes of this review. 

    The evidence for all outcomes was of very low certainty, thus we could not be certain whether or not topical applications provided at least a clinically significant reduction in pain (1 study, 62 participants with Carybdea alata stings, reported only as cessation of pain). For adverse events due to treatment, one study (Chrysaora chinensis stings) withdrew ammonia as a treatment following a first‐degree burn in one participant. No studies evaluated clinically significant reduction in pain, retreatment with the same or the alternative treatment, or dermatological signs.

    Authors' conclusions

    Few studies contributed data to this review, and those that did contribute varied in types of treatment, settings, and range of jellyfish species. We are unsure of the effectiveness of any of the treatments evaluated in this review given the very low certainty of all the evidence. This updated review includes two new studies (with 139 additional participants). The findings are consistent with the previous review.

    • What are the benefits and harms of different treatments for jellyfish stings?

    Why is this question important?

    Jellyfish stings are common in coastal regions around the world. Specialised stinging cells on the jellyfish called nematocysts produce the sting. The stings of different jellyfish species produce different symptoms of varying severity. Milder symptoms include pain, redness, and itching at the sting site. However, reactions to some jellyfish species can be more serious, and very occasionally lead to death. Understanding the benefits and harms of different treatments will help to know how best to treat the effects of a jellyfish sting.

    How did we identify and evaluate the evidence?

    We searched the medical literature for studies of different treatments for jellyfish stings. We compared and summarised the results of the studies for different species of jellyfish. We also rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and size, and the consistency of findings across studies.

    What did we find?

    We found nine studies with 574 participants, assessing three groups of treatments. We found no studies that assessed a fourth type of treatment (tight bandages applied to the site of the sting). 

    The included studies all had small numbers of participants and problems related to their methods (e.g. because participants were aware of the type of treatment, or because many participants left the study before the end). We also found some differences in the findings between studies, which we were unable to explain. We used these issues to rate our confidence in the evidence.

    Hot or cold treatments

    Four studies compared hot or cold treatments. In two studies, people were stung accidentally by bluebottle jellyfish in Australia. In the other two studies, people were stung accidentally by Hawaiian box jellyfish or major box jellyfish in Australia and Hawaii, USA; these box jellyfish do not cause Irukandji syndrome (a condition that may lead to serious complications, and very occasionally to death). The studies looked at the effect of treatments on pain relief. Heat was applied to the sting site using a hot pack or hot water (with showers, baths, buckets, or hoses). Cold was applied using ice packs or cold packs. People were treated on the beach or at the hospital.

    Due to our limited confidence in the available evidence, we cannot tell whether applying heat or cold to a jellyfish sting reduces or stops pain within one hour of treatment; reduces the need for retreatment or switching to the alternative treatment; reduces skin reactions in the first 24 hours (itchiness, red marks, or rashes); or causes any harms (burns or temporary redness around the area of application). This finding relates directly to the types of jellyfish described in this section. 

    Topical treatments

    Four studies compared topical treatments that were applied to the skin on and around the sting site. In one study, people were treated on the beach after accidental stings by Hawaiian box jellyfish in Hawaii. In the remaining three studies, people volunteered to be stung in a laboratory setting.

    Treatments included: fresh water, seawater, Sting Aid (a commercial product), Adolph's meat tenderiser (papain, an enzyme present in papaya), isopropyl alcohol, ammonia, heated water, acetic acid, or sodium bicarbonate. In some of these treatments, vinegar was also applied to the sting site. 

    Due to our low confidence in the available evidence, we cannot tell whether applying any of these treatments to a jellyfish sting reduces or stops pain within six hours of treatment, or causes harm. One study withdrew a treatment (ammonia) because one participant had a chemical burn after this treatment. This finding relates directly to the types of jellyfish described in this section. These studies did not measure retreatment, switching to alternative study treatment, or skin reactions.

    Parenteral treatment

    These treatments are injected directly into the body (under the skin, into muscles, veins, or spine). In one study, people were treated in hospital after accidental stings by box jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome. Treatment included magnesium sulfate or a placebo (which looked like the treatment but had no active ingredients), which was given intravenously (directly into the bloodstream through a vein). 

    This study did not measure pain relief in a way that could be included in this review, and did not measure any of the outcomes that we were interested in.

    What does this mean?

    We have very little confidence in the available evidence. It is unclear whether any of the evaluated treatments reduce or stop pain, or provide other benefits after people have been stung by the jellyfish species in these studies. The findings in this review are only relevant to stings from a small number of jellyfish species that were in Australia, Malyasia, and Hawaii (USA). These findings therefore must not be used to decide treatment options for any other type of jellyfish. 

    How up‐to‐date is this review?

    The evidence in this Cochrane Review is current to October 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    We found very few studies contributing data to this review, and the studies we did find varied by treatment setting and range of jellyfish species. This reflects the difficulty of conducting research in this area. Although the evidence for all outcomes was of very low certainty, the results are relatively consistent with outcomes from non‐randomised evidence (Cegolon 2013; Montgomery 2016). 

    For people with symptoms and signs of jellyfish stings, and their first responders or treating clinicians

    Treatment will depend on the species of jellyfish, as the stings from different species produce symptoms of varying severity. However, we cannot be certain which is the best treatment for any species of jellyfish. Although our evidence was very uncertain, the data from the included studies evaluating heat application after non‐box jellyfish stings in Australia and Hawaii are consistent with the ARC 2010 guideline, which suggests starting treatment with heat after stings in non‐tropical Australia. We have found no evidence to refute heat application for non‐box jellyfish stings in non‐tropical Australia. 

    For box jellyfish envenomation, the evidence is more limited, but both the ARC 2010 and ILSF 2000 guidelines recommend the application of vinegar to inactivate the nematocyst. Our review did not find sufficient evidence to support these recommendations, but the recommendations are consistent with evidence from in vitro studies (Yanagihara 2017). 

    For policymakers

    The findings of this review were very uncertain. However, these findings do not refute the current international guidance. 

    For funders

    We did not undertake a cost‐benefit analysis of any of the treatments in this review. However, many treatments in this review are low cost and could be readily available in managed beach settings, such as the application of heat or cold/ice packs, vinegar, or commercial sting aid products. Given the paucity of evidence in this review, this creates funding opportunities for future research in order to inform healthcare decisions. 

    Implications for research

    General implications

    Practically, researchers in this field face a range of difficulties. Conducting research in a beachside setting, while participants are in acute pain, with limited options for blinding and species identification, are only a few of the hurdles to be dealt with. In addition, treatments need to be easily accessible and applied by first responders who may be volunteers with limited first‐aid training. 

    Design

    Although there is currently limited high‐quality evidence evaluating different treatments for the symptoms and signs resulting from jellyfish stings, we recognise the utility of non‐randomised evidence. Future review updates could incorporate other study designs alongside randomised controlled trials. Using Cochrane methodology to assess bias in other study designs, and using these data to supplement data from randomised controlled trials would provide a more pragmatic overview of treatment options.

    We propose that future studies include people stung accidentally in a beach environment, but could also include healthy volunteers stung in a laboratory setting. There is scope to consider other interventions in future trials. This review includes no evidence for pressure immobilisation bandages, and the data for parenteral administration of medications is also limited to only intravenous drug treatment. In addition, the studies included in this review did not specifically assess the effectiveness of vinegar or salt water. If interventions are trialed in a beach setting, they should be appropriate to a real‐life setting in which they could realistically be applied by a volunteer first responder without first‐aid training. 

    Measurement (endpoints)

    Outcomes in future studies should measure clinically important pain reduction as well as the cessation of pain. We encourage the use of validated measurement tools for pain as well as recognised interpretations of reduction in pain such as that in Bird 2001. We recognise the difficulty of measuring adverse effects due to treatment, and study investigators should clearly report whether events are due to the sting or the treatment or whether this information is unknown.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Jellyfish are free‐swimming marine invertebrates. All jellyfish possess specialised stinging cells called nematocysts (Tintinalli 2020). Physical or chemical stimuli trigger nematocysts, after which a barb is fired and venom is injected into the victim (see Figure 1). Because of the number of nematocysts that may discharge during a jellyfish 'sting' and the potential toxicity of the venom, a jellyfish sting may produce a range of signs and symptoms of varying severity.

    Humans typically come into contact only with surface‐dwelling jellyfish species found in temperate coastal regions. As most jellyfish stings go unreported, it is difficult to obtain accurate incidence statistics. However, numbers have been increasing globally in recent years, with more geographical areas affected and an increased number of jellyfish blooms (Cunha 2022; Schmitt 2013). Box jellyfish (class Cubozoa), in particular the family Chirodropidae, which includes the Indo‐Pacific box jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri ), are considered the most dangerous of jellyfish. Chironex fleckeri (the major box jellyfish) caused more than 70 deaths in the past century, mostly amongst young children in remote areas of Australia (Tintinalli 2020). Deaths are also rarely caused by Physalia physalis, known as the Portuguese man‐of‐war, or bluebottle (Stein 1989). Box jellyfish are estimated to cost the Australian government AUD 3 billion (3000 million) annually in medical costs and losses for tourism (Rowley 2022).

    Envenomation usually results in immediate stinging pain and a range of skin reactions, including erythema (redness of the skin), urticaria (red, raised, itchy rash), wheals (red swollen mark of the skin), vesicular formation, hypo/hyperpigmentation and/or superficial necrosis at the site of contact (Tintinalli 2020; Winter 2007). The pain of a jellyfish sting may be severe and may last for several weeks. Delayed hypersensitivity skin reactions may occur (O'Reilly 2001), as well as more permanent skin markings, including scarring and colour changes (Tintinalli 2020). Cubozoan, or box jellyfish, in particular Carukia barnesi, may cause Irukandji syndrome, which is characterised by pain and redness at the site of the sting, followed by generalised severe abdominal, back, or chest pain, as well as autonomic features such as tachycardia, hypertension, sweating, piloerection, agitation, and, uncommonly, heart complications (myocardial depression/failure with or without pulmonary oedema) (Currie 2005; Huynh 2003). Severe anaphylactic reactions to jellyfish stings are extremely rare (Amato 2020; Williamson 1996).

    Description of the intervention

    Therapy ideally comprises deactivation of attached nematocysts, neutralisation of venom effects, and provision of symptomatic relief (including pain relief) and supportive care. Therapy is often chosen based on the presumed causative species according to coastal region or other available information; in some cases, the presumed causative species may not be readily available. 

    A range of different treatment options are currently used. In this review, we grouped treatments into four categories (hot/cold; topical; parenteral; pressure immobilisation). 

    How the intervention might work

    Application of heat or cold is thought to work because of inactivation of the venom (protein), which can therefore relieve the pain. Topical treatments are diverse, but some attempt to deactivate undischarged stinging organelles (Hartwick 1980), although this may paradoxically cause increased venom release (Welfare 2014). Parenteral treatments such as magnesium sulfate have been used for conditions associated with abnormalities of catecholamine secretion and/or receptor activity, which can be seen with Irukandji syndrome (Corkeron 2003). Finally, pressure immobilisation is used in first‐aid treatment for a wide range of envenomations (Tintinalli 2020), although its use for management of jellyfish stings lacks a theoretical basis, and in vitro evidence suggests it may be harmful (Pereira 2000). 

    Currently, the Australian Resuscitation Council and Red Cross recommends 1) the use of vinegar in tropical Australia or a cold/ice pack if vinegar is unavailable for the treatment of jellyfish stings; and 2) hot water for stings in non‐tropical regions or for obvious bluebottle stings (ARC 2010; Australian Red Cross).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Jellyfish stings are a significant cause of morbidity, and less frequently mortality. Current treatment options are not clearly defined or evidence‐based. It is therefore important that effective, evidence‐based treatments are identified. This is an update of a previous Cochrane Review in which the evidence for treatments according to different species of jellyfish envenomation was uncertain (Li 2013). In this update, we planned to incorporate new studies, and to report the evidence in summary of findings tables, separated by types of interventions, and stratified where possible according to the species of jellyfish.

    Objectives

    To determine the benefits and harms associated with the use of any intervention, in both adults and children, for the treatment of jellyfish stings, as assessed by randomised and quasi‐randomised trials.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs, as these give the best quality evidence for assessing the effectiveness of an intervention. We included parallel study designs and split‐body studies (in which body parts on the same individual were treated with the intervention or placebo control, with the type of treatment allocated by randomisation). We also included studies that included the option for participants to cross‐over to the alternative intervention at a prespecified time point. We planned to include cluster‐RCTs, although we found no studies with this design. We included studies that were conducted 'in the field' as well as studies conducted within a laboratory setting. 

    Types of participants

    For the purposes of this review, anyone who reported being stung by any jellyfish was eligible for inclusion. We included participants that were stung accidentally in a beach environment ('in the field'), as well as healthy volunteer participants stung in a laboratory‐type setting. We considered all age groups. For 'in the field' studies, we took a pragmatic approach to trial conduct, and accepted the study authors' classification and specification of the causative organism. We considered all study participants presenting with any symptoms, not just those who presented with pain. No baseline thresholds of pain or any other symptoms were required for inclusion in the review.

    Types of interventions

    We included any intervention given at any dose, duration, or intensity. We included any comparison (an active comparator, placebo, or no treatment). For studies in which participants were treated concomitantly with another treatment (e.g. vinegar), we required all participants in the comparison group(s) to have received this concomitant treatment.

    We planned to report study characteristics and analyse the data as separate comparison groups according to the type of intervention:

    • applications of heat versus applications of cold;

    • topical applications (such as acetic acid, baking soda, lidocaine, or another commercial product);

    • parenteral route of administration (to include medication given by intravenous administration, as well as intramuscular, subcutaneous, or intrathecal administration);

    • pressure immobilisation bandages.

    Types of outcome measures

    In this review, we have separately reported the number of people who had cessation of pain after treatment and the number of people who had a clinically significant reduction in pain. For our primary outcome, we reported a composite outcome of these two measures in order to report the number of people who had at least a clinically significant reduction in pain. We chose 'within six hours' as the measurement point for this composite outcome because we judged that this was most clinically relevant.  

    Primary outcomes

    • At least a clinically significant reduction in pain within six hours. In this composite outcome, we included participants who reported cessation of pain and data that were also described by study authors as a clinically important or clinically significant reduction in pain according to their own definitions; we accepted measurements on a validated pain scale, such as the visual analogue scale (VAS). If study authors did not interpret a reduction of pain as clinically important, we used a cut‐off of 50% of the maximum possible pain relief or a 50% reduction in pain intensity. For studies in which more than one time point was reported, we included the final time point up to six hours from injury.

    • Adverse events due to treatment. We included:

      • the number of participants with any serious adverse event (defined as death or any life‐threatening event, requiring inpatient hospitalisation or prolongation of existing hospitalisation, resulting in persistent or significant disability/incapacity, or requiring intervention to prevent permanent impairment or damage);

      • adverse event withdrawals; and

      • the number of participants with a minor adverse event (defined as an adverse event that does not qualify as serious) at one day and one week.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Clinically significant reduction in pain (as described by study authors or 50% maximum possible pain relief, as above) at zero to one hour, one to six hours, six hours to one day, and one day to one week, as measured on a validated pain scale such as the VAS. 

    • Cessation of pain at the end of treatment as measured on a validated pain scale such as the VAS or as otherwise described by study authors.

    • Time to retreatment with the same intervention.

    • Retreatment with the same intervention at zero to one hour, one to six hours, six hours to one day, and one day to one week.

    • Retreatment with the alternative intervention at zero to one hour, one to six hours, six hours to one day, and one day to one week. We defined 'alternative treatment' as another treatment within the same study (e.g. if participants were allocated to heat applications, the number of participants who were later given the alternative treatment of cold applications was collected).  

    • Requiring supportive care at one day after the sting (e.g. dressings, oxygen supplementation, or positive‐pressure ventilation).

    • Requiring hospital treatment at one day after the sting (as inpatient or outpatient, including emergency department visits).

    • Dermatological signs up to one week after the sting, e.g. scarring, hyperpigmentation, or delayed hypersensitivity.

    • All‐cause mortality at one month after the sting.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    For this update we identified RCTs through literature searching with systematic and sensitive search strategies. We applied no restrictions on language or publication status. We searched the following databases for relevant trials:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2022; Issue 10 of 12) via the Cochrane Library (searched on 27 October 2022);

    • MEDLINE (Ovid SP; 1946 to 27 October 2022);

    • Embase (Ovid SP; 1974 to 27 October 2022);

    • Web of Science (SCI‐EXPANDED; 1900 to 27 October 2022).

    In consultation with the Information Specialist for Cochrane Pain, Palliative and Support Care, we developed a subject‐specific and sensitive search strategy in MEDLINE and other listed databases. We subtracted the previous review's search results (up to and including 2012) from the new search. Search strategies can be found in Appendix 1; Appendix 2; Appendix 3; Appendix 4.

    Searching other resources

    We searched the following clinical trials registers for ongoing and unpublished trials:

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (www.who.int/ictrp/en/) on 3 July 2020;

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov) on 24 July 2020.

    We carried out forward‐citation searching of identified included studies published since 2012 in Web of Science on 3 July 2020 (apps.webofknowledge.com). We conducted a search of the grey literature using OpenGrey on 3 July 2020 (www.opengrey.eu/). In addition, we scanned the reference lists of relevant systematic reviews.

    In the previous version of this review (Li 2013), we also searched the reference lists of eligible studies and relevant guidelines such as the 'Australian Resuscitation Council Guidelines to Envenomation ‐ Jellyfish Stings' and 'International Life Saving Federation Policy Statement ‐ Statements on Marine Envenomation' (October 2013) (ARC 2010; ILSF 2000); contacted content experts and authors (October 2012); and performed text word searches of one relevant conference proceeding, the 'International Jellyfish Bloom Symposium' (October 2013).

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors (SL and AW, RM, or MW) independently selected studies and extracted data from the newly included studies. We compared decisions at each stage. When areas of uncertainty were identified during full‐text review, we attempted to contact the original authors of the study through email or letter to ask for clarification. We resolved all disagreements by discussion, and we included or excluded studies once consensus was reached. We organised the translation of studies that were not published in English so that we could determine their eligibility.

    Selection of studies

    We used reference management software to collate the results of searches and to remove duplicates (EndNote). We sourced the full texts of all potentially relevant studies and considered whether they met the inclusion criteria (see Criteria for considering studies for this review). We reviewed abstracts at this stage and included them in the review only if they provided sufficient information and relevant results that included denominator figures for the intervention and control groups. We recorded the number of papers retrieved at each stage and reported this information using a PRISMA flow chart. We reported in the review brief details of closely related but excluded papers.

    Data extraction and management

    We used a piloted data extraction form to collect information and outcome data from studies (Appendix 5). We collected the following information.

    • Methods: type of study design, setting (e.g. hospital emergency department, beach), type of jellyfish and geographical region, dates of study, funding sources and study author declarations of interest.

    • Participants: inclusion and exclusion criteria; number randomised to each group; number of losses; number analysed in each group; baseline characteristics (age, gender, comorbidities, area of sting on the body).

    • Intervention and comparison: details of each treatment (type; dose or concentration; time; duration or application; concomitant treatment).

    • Outcomes: data for all reported review outcomes, including study author definitions, measurement tools, and time points.

    We considered the applicability of information from individual studies and generalisability of the data to our intended study population (i.e. the potential for indirectness in our review).

    In multi‐arm studies, we did not collect data on intervention agents that were not eligible for inclusion in the review.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (SL and RM or MW) independently assessed the risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2017), with any disagreements resolved by discussion. We completed a risk of bias table for each included study using the risk of bias tool in Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020).

    We assessed the following risk of bias domains for each included study.

    • Sequence generation (selection bias).

    • Allocation concealment (selection bias).

    • Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias).

    • Blinding of outcome assessors (detection bias).

    • Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias).

    • Selective reporting (reporting bias).

    • Other potential sources of bias.

    We assessed each domain as at low, high, or unclear risk of bias (see Appendix 6). For domains initially judged as 'unclear risk', we attempted to clarify the risk of bias by contacting the study authors. 

    Measures of treatment effect

    We collected dichotomous data for all outcomes except 'time to retreatment with the same intervention', which we expected to have been reported as continuous data.

    We reported dichotomous data as risk ratios (RRs), with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We calculated these effect estimates using the calculator in Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020). For data from single studies, we calculated effect estimates only for data with two study arms. We planned to present continuous data as a mean difference (MD) with 95% CIs, or a standardised mean difference (SMD) if studies used different measures; however, we found no studies measuring this effect in our review.

    Given that the evidence for all outcomes in the review was of very low certainty, we did not report number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) and number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) for estimates in which the effect favoured one treatment.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We planned to analyse any cluster‐randomised trials after taking into account the clustering effect to prevent unit of analysis errors, as detailed in Chapter 10 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2021). For split‐body studies, we planned to take account of the pairing (or matching) of parts within individuals so as to avoid unit of analysis errors; hence, we planned to consider the body part as the unit of analysis in this study design. Whilst this review did not include cross‐over study designs, some studies allowed participants the option of switching to the alternative study intervention after a certain time point. In order to avoid any risk of contamination of the data by those participants who used both treatments, we only used data from these studies that related directly to a participant's original allocated treatment; if participants switched to the alternative treatment, we used pain data collected before they used the alternative treatment.   

    The primary outcome ('at least a clinically significant reduction in pain within six hours') included a composite measure of a clinically significant reduction in pain (as defined by study authors) as well as cessation of pain. If studies reported both of these measures, we would only use one of these measures in analysis (clinically significant reduction in pain). For the outcome 'dermatological signs up to one week after the sting', we noted that one study included data for more than one type of dermatological sign; we did not combine these data in analysis because we could not be certain whether these data included the same participant.

    Dealing with missing data

    We analysed data for all participants in the groups to which they had been allocated, regardless of whether they had received the allocated intervention. When the original reports did not analyse participants in the groups to which they had been randomised, and sufficient information was provided in the study, we attempted to restore participants to the correct group for the purposes of meta‐analysis (i.e. we conducted modified intention‐to‐treat analysis, also known as available‐case analysis, when this was possible). When missing data were suspected, we contacted study authors to seek clarification or obtain the missing data, or both. We did not impute any data. 

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed for apparent evidence of inconsistency in our results by considering heterogeneity. We evaluated methodological heterogeneity by considering similarities amongst the included studies in terms of study design, participants, interventions, and outcomes, and used the data collected from the full‐text reports (as stated in Data collection and analysis). We planned to explore clinical and methodological heterogeneity through subgroup analysis; however, this was limited due to the inclusion of insufficient studies. We assessed statistical heterogeneity by calculating the ChI2 test or I2 statistic (Higgins 2003), judging an I2 value of 50% and a Chi2 P value of 0.05 or less as indicating substantial statistical heterogeneity (Deeks 2017).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to use a funnel plot to check for publication bias when more than 10 studies were included in the analysis. When we suspected reporting bias because of missing data, we attempted to contact study authors to obtain the missing information.

    Data synthesis

    We completed meta‐analyses of outcomes for which we had comparable effect measures from more than one study for each comparison group, and when measures of heterogeneity indicated that pooling of results was appropriate. We used the statistical calculator in Review Manager 5 to perform meta‐analysis (Review Manager 2020).

    We used the Mantel‐Haenszel random‐effects model to account for potential variability in participant conditions between studies. For continuous outcome data, we planned to use inverse variance employing a random‐effects model. The inverse variance method accounts for the weight of studies with different sample sizes.

    We calculated CIs at 95%. We considered imprecision in the results of analyses by assessing the CI around an effect measure, a wide CI suggesting a higher level of imprecision in our results. A small number of studies may also reduce precision (Guyatt 2011).

    Where possible, we stratified the analyses according to jellyfish species: bluebottles (Physalia species); box jellyfish that do not cause Irukandji syndrome (such as the major box jellyfish, Chironex fleckeri); jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome; and other jellyfish. We did not pool the data for effects that included more than one type of jellyfish species.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to conduct formal subgroup analysis by testing for interaction between subgroups, if evidence was available from more than 10 studies for a given outcome (Deeks 2017). However, because we found evidence from few studies, we have reported descriptive data in tables for the following subgroups:

    • severity of a sting: mild/moderate (not requiring hospitalisation) versus severe (requiring hospitalisation);

    • co‐interventions used (e.g. one study may assess the efficacy of warm water with analgesics as a co‐intervention, and another study may assess the efficacy of warm water with psychosocial support as a co‐intervention); and

    • participants' age group: children (≤ 18 years of age) compared with adults (19 to 64 years of age) and older adults (≥ 65 years of age).

    We did not report subgroup information for the outcome 'adverse events due to treatment' because the nature of this outcome measure was specific to the intervention rather than to our specified subgroups.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We explored the potential effect of decisions made as part of the review process. It was only feasible to conduct sensitivity analysis in which meta‐analysis was conducted in at least two studies, and we acknowledge the increased imprecision in the effect when studies were excluded during this process. In each of the following sensitivity analyses, we planned to compare the effect estimate with the main analysis. We excluded studies:

    • if data were unpublished;

    • with missing data;

    • published as abstracts (i.e. excluded if only published as abstracts without a full report);

    • judged to be at high risk of bias in at least one domain.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    Two of three review authors (SL, MW, RM) independently used the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of the body of evidence in the review (Guyatt 2008). The GRADE approach appraises the certainty of a body of evidence based on the extent to which we can be confident that an estimate of effect or association reflects the item being assessed. Factors that may decrease the level of certainty of a body of evidence are as follows.

    • Serious or very serious risk of bias

    • Serious or very serious inconsistency of results

    • Serious or very serious indirectness of evidence

    • Serious or very serious imprecision

    • Strongly suspected probability of publication bias

    We constructed summary of findings tables for the following two comparisons using GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT).

    • Applications of heat versus applications of cold. We stratified the presentation of results for this comparison, as well as the GRADE judgements, according to the species of jellyfish: bluebottles (Physalia); and Hawaiian box jellyfish and major box jellyfish.

    • Topical applications. We did not stratify the presentation of results for this comparison according to jellyfish species because we did not pool any data. We used the 'comments' section of the summary of findings table to indicate the type of species for which data were available.

    We planned to construct a summary of findings table for two other comparisons (parenteral administration and pressure immobilisation bandages), but found no relevant data.

    We selected seven outcomes for the summary of findings tables, which we judged to be most important to a person with symptoms and signs resulting from jellyfish stings, as follows.

    • At least a clinically significant reduction in pain within six hours. This composite outcome included the number of people with cessation of pain or a clinically significant reduction in pain as measured using validated pain scales and interpreted according to study authors' definitions. For studies in which more than one time point was reported, we included the final time point up to six hours from injury.

    • Adverse events due to treatment.

    • Clinically significant reduction in pain at zero to one hour (measured using validated pain scales and interpreted as an important reduction according to study authors' definitions).

    • Cessation of pain at the end of treatment (measured using a validated pain scale or according to study authors' definitions).

    • Retreatment with the same intervention at zero to one hour.

    • Retreatment with the alternative study intervention (e.g. heat or cold application in a trial that compared heat with cold) at zero to one hour.

    • Dermatological signs up to one week after the sting.

    Included studies

    We included nine studies with a total of 574 participants (Bowra 2002; DeClerck 2016; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; McCullagh 2012; Nomura 2002; Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b; Turner 1980). Three studies were quasi‐RCTs (Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b; Turner 1980), whilst the remaining studies were RCTs. Two of these nine studies (with 139 participants; DeClerck 2016; Isbister 2017a) were not included in the previous version of this review (Li 2013). Two included studies were split‐body studies, in which treatments were tested on stings applied to more than one part of the participant's body (DeClerck 2016; Nomura 2002). 

    We have reported study characteristics according to comparison group, as follows.

    Application of heat versus application of cold

    Three studies compared hot versus cold applications (Bowra 2002; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006), and a further three‐arm study compared hot versus cold and also included an air‐temperature study arm (Thomas 2001a). Specifically, these interventions were:

    • hot showers versus ice packs (Bowra 2002);

    • hot water immersion (bath or bucket, as appropriate to sting site and size) versus ice packs (Isbister 2017a);

    • hot water (via hose or bucket immersion) versus ice packs (Loten 2006);

    • heat pack versus cold pack versus air‐temperature pack (Thomas 2001a).

    In Isbister 2017a and Thomas 2001a, vinegar was applied as a co‐intervention to all stings. In three studies, participants were offered the opportunity to cross‐over to the alternative treatment at a prespecified time point (Bowra 2002; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006).

    All studies recruited participants who were accidentally stung in beach settings; in three studies, participants were treated on the beach by lifeguards (Bowra 2002; Loten 2006; Thomas 2001a), whilst in the remaining study, participants were treated in hospital emergency department (Isbister 2017a).

    Whilst the type of jellyfish could not always be confirmed, investigators used descriptions provided by participants, local information regarding jellyfish blooms, or symptoms consistent with a type of jellyfish sting to categorise the species of jellyfish. Bowra 2002 and Loten 2006 evaluated treatments in stings from bluebottles (Physalia). Two studies evaluated treatments in stings from box jellyfish that do not cause Irukandji syndrome; Thomas 2001a included stings from Hawaiian box jellyfish (Carybdea alata); and Isbister 2017a included stings from major box jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri). Jellyfish were native to local waters in Australia (Bowra 2002; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006), and in Hawaii in the USA (Thomas 2001a).

    All studies in this comparison group contributed outcome data to the review.

    In two studies, funding was provided in part by independent sources, which we did not consider to have influenced the planning, conduct, and analysis of these studies (Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006). One study received no external funding (Thomas 2001a), whilst in the remaining studies no details of funding sources were provided. In Loten 2006, we noted that the study was stopped early after an interim analysis.

    The main study characteristics are summarised in Appendix 7. For details on study characteristics, see Characteristics of included studies.

    Topical applications

    Four studies compared topical applications (DeClerck 2016; Nomura 2002; Thomas 2001b; Turner 1980). Each study evaluated more than two interventions, which were all active comparators against the other interventions within the study. Although some studies included applications of hot water, we have reported these studies within the topical applications comparison because these studies did not include a cold‐water comparison group. Specifically, these interventions were:

    • isopropyl alcohol, ammonia, heated water, acetic acid, and sodium bicarbonate (DeClerck 2016);

    • acetic acid, Adolph's (papain) meat tenderiser (enzyme powder used to breakdown meat fibres), and hot water (Nomura 2002);

    • fresh water, seawater, Sting Aid (a commercial product for bites and stings), and Adolph's meat tenderiser (Thomas 2001b);

    • vinegar, methylated spirits, Stingose (aluminium sulfate hydrate), and saltwater (Turner 1980).

    In Thomas 2001b, vinegar was applied as a co‐intervention to all stings. In one study, participants were offered the opportunity to cross‐over to the alternative treatment at a prespecified time point (Nomura 2002).

    Three trials recruited healthy volunteer participants and applied jellyfish stings in a laboratory setting (DeClerck 2016; Nomura 2002; Turner 1980). The remaining study recruited participants who were accidentally stung in beach settings, and these participants were treated on the beach by lifeguards (Thomas 2001b).

    Whilst the type of jellyfish could not always be confirmed in studies of accidental stings, investigators used descriptions provided by participants, local information regarding jellyfish blooms, or symptoms consistent with the type of jellyfish sting to categorise the species of jellyfish. Turner 1980 evaluated treatments in stings of Australian bluebottles (Physalia). Two studies evaluated treatments in box jellyfish that do not cause Irukandji syndrome (Nomura 2002; Thomas 2001b); these were both Hawaiian box jellyfish (Carybdea alata). In DeClerck 2016, participants were stung by an Indonesian sea nettle jellyfish (Chrysaora chinensis) imported from Malaysia.

    Two studies reported no data for the review outcomes (Nomura 2002; Turner 1980); the remaining studies contributed to at least one review outcome.

    In one study, funding was provided in part by independent sources, which we did not consider to have influenced the planning, conduct, and analysis of the study (DeClerck 2016). One study received no external funding (Thomas 2001b), whilst in the remaining studies no details of funding sources were provided.

    The main study characteristics are summarised in Appendix 8. For details on study characteristics, see Characteristics of included studies.

    Parenteral route of administration

    One study evaluated a drug given parenterally (McCullagh 2012). This study compared the intravenous administration of magnesium sulfate versus a placebo (normal saline). All participants were also given fentanyl. The study recruited participants who were accidentally stung in beach settings in Australia and treated in a hospital emergency department. The presumptive jellyfish species was Carukia (a box jellyfish species that can cause Irukandji syndrome). The main study characteristics are summarised in Appendix 9. For details on study characteristics, see Characteristics of included studies.

    Pressure immobilisation bandages

    No studies evaluated this intervention.

    We excluded 11 records after sourcing and reading full texts.     Allocation

    Four studies described a suitable method to randomise participants into groups and were assessed as at low risk of bias for sequence generation (Bowra 2002; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; McCullagh 2012). However, three studies used methods that resulted in a judgement of high risk of bias (DeClerck 2016; Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b).

    Three studies described a suitable method to conceal allocation and were assessed as at low risk of bias for allocation concealment (Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; McCullagh 2012). Two studies used methods that resulted in a judgement of high risk of bias (Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b). The remaining studies reported no methods to conceal allocation and were judged to be at unclear risk of bias.

    Blinding

    Blinding was not possible in five studies, which were assessed as at high risk of performance bias (Bowra 2002; DeClerck 2016; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; Thomas 2001a). We considered the methods used in two studies sufficient to effectively blind participants, and we judged these studies to be at low risk of performance bias (McCullagh 2012; Thomas 2001b).

    We judged the risk of detection bias to be high in five studies (Bowra 2002; DeClerck 2016; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; Thomas 2001a). We judged detection bias to be low risk in two studies because outcome assessors were blinded to the type of treatment given to participants (McCullagh 2012; Thomas 2001b).

    Incomplete outcome data

    We judged most studies with outcome data to be at low risk of attrition bias because study authors reported no losses, few losses that we did not expect to influence outcome data, or losses that were not relevant to our review outcomes (Bowra 2002; DeClerck 2016; Isbister 2017a; Loten 2006; McCullagh 2012). We judged the risk of attrition bias to be unclear in two studies because we could not determine the reliability of the imputation methods used to manage participant losses (Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b).

    Selective reporting

    One study was prospectively registered with a clinical trials register, and study authors reported outcomes consistent with the clinical trials register (Isbister 2017a); we judged this study to be at low risk of reporting bias. One study was retrospectively registered with a clinical trials register, and we could not effectively assess the risk of selective reporting bias using the clinical trials register (DeClerck 2016), so we judged the risk of reporting bias as unclear. In Loten 2006, we noted that outcomes described in the methods section were not reported in the study results, therefore we judged the risk of reporting bias to be high in this study. The remaining studies did not report clinical trial registration (Bowra 2002; McCullagh 2012; Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b), and were therefore judged to be at unclear risk of bias.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We identified no other sources of bias in four studies (Isbister 2017a; McCullagh 2012; Thomas 2001a; Thomas 2001b), which we judged to be at low risk of other bias. We judged Bowra 2002 to be at unclear risk of other bias because the study was available only as an abstract, and we assumed that the report was not peer‐reviewed.

Data for Isbister 2017a were adjusted by the study authors for baseline characteristics. We stratified the analysis according to the species of jellyfish.

    Minor adverse events

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included nine studies in which different treatments were used to treat stings from jellyfish. Species of jellyfish included bluebottle (Physalia ), Hawaiian box jellyfish (Carybdea alata ), major box jellyfish (Chironex fleckeri ), jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome (Carukia ), and Indonesian sea nettle (Chrysaora chinensis).

    We reported interventions according to three comparison groups: application of heat versus application of cold; topical applications; and parenteral treatment. We found no evidence for a fourth comparison group, pressure immobilisation bandages. We stratified the evidence according to jellyfish species: bluebottles; jellyfish that do not cause Irukandji syndrome (Hawaiian box jellyfish and box jellyfish), jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome (Carukia), and other types of jellyfish (Indonesian sea nettle).

    Application of heat versus application of cold

    We found very low‐certainty evidence for all outcomes in this comparison. Consequently, although the data from two small studies indicated that the application of heat following bluebottle stings may lead to at least a clinically significant pain reduction when compared with application of cold, we could not be certain of this finding. Regarding other jellyfish species (Hawaiian box jellyfish and major box jellyfish), data indicated little or no difference in the number of people who had a clinically significant reduction in pain, but we could not be certain of this finding. This primary outcome was a composite measure and included both cessation of pain and a clinically significant reduction in pain.

    For clinically significant reduction in pain within six hours, as a single outcome measure, we found data from one study that indicated that the application of heat leads to clinically improved pain relief following bluebottle stings, but data from another study of major box jellyfish did not show evidence of a difference. Again, we are uncertain of this finding given the very low certainty of the evidence. For the cessation of pain, as a single outcome measure, we are uncertain if there is a difference after heat or cold application for stings from either bluebottles or Hawaiian box jellyfish. There was also uncertain evidence of little or no difference in retreatment with the same intervention after applications of heat or cold for bluebottle stings, or for retreatment with the alternative intervention for either bluebottle stings or major box jellyfish. We combined data for two studies of bluebottle stings and found uncertain evidence of little or no difference in dermatological signs of itchiness or rash at 24 hours after applications of heat or cold. We also found uncertain evidence from two studies of little or no difference in minor adverse events due to either treatment.

    Topical applications

    We are similarly uncertain of the effects of a range of topical applications to treat the signs and symptoms of jellyfish stings given the very low certainty of evidence for all outcomes. One small study (treatments for Hawaiian box jellyfish stings with fresh water, salt water, meat tenderiser, or a commercial sting‐relieving product) with a high rate of attrition found little or no difference between treatments in cessation of pain. No other studies reported data for a clinically significant reduction in pain or cessation of pain. One study reported adverse events due to treatment, and withdrew ammonia as a study intervention following a first‐degree burn in one participant.

    Parenteral administration

    Only one small study reported medication given parenterally, with intravenous magnesium sulfate administered in a hospital setting after stings from jellyfish that cause Irukandji syndrome (Carukia). This study included no useable data for the outcomes in our summary of findings tables. 

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We found nine studies with a total of 574 participants. Each study evaluated treatments for the symptoms and signs resulting from jellyfish stings from different species. Most participants were stung accidentally at beaches in Australia and in Hawaii (USA), and results of these studies apply to the species of jellyfish native to these waters. In addition, the review is limited to the types of interventions in these studies. Other interventions are not included (for example, there is no evidence in this review for pressure immobilisation bandages). Consequently, this review does not provide a complete picture of treatment options for stings from all species of jellyfish.

    Some studies were conducted in laboratory settings. Whilst this research setting allowed for the control of some biases (such as blinding), as well as using a known jellyfish species, we note that these studies are problematic for other reasons. Participants in these laboratory studies were self‐selecting, having volunteered (for no cost or for a payment) to take part in a study involving pain management, and some were excluded from taking part in the study if they had any known allergic reactions or anaphylaxis to jellyfish stings. The highly controlled environment of these studies is not directly applicable to a real‐world scenario. 

    In addition, we note that some studies did not measure some of our outcomes, and for these outcomes we had no data. Again, this limits the completeness of the evidence for these treatments.

    Quality of the evidence

    Because the symptoms resulting from jellyfish stings vary by type and severity according to jellyfish species, we would need an overall large sample size for types of treatments, as well as the inclusion of different types of jellyfish species. In this review, we found few studies, each of which had a small sample size. Whilst most of the evidence assessed applications of heat and applications of cold, we combined data for no more than two studies for any given outcome, thus the combined sample sizes were also small. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence for each outcome for imprecision.

    An insufficient number of studies also limited the feasibility of considering the influence of other factors in our data. In particular, we could not conduct subgroup analyses; for example, we could not explore whether co‐interventions (such as vinegar) influenced responses to interventions in studies. We found moderate levels of statistical heterogeneity in some evidence of stings from Hawaiian box jellyfish and major box jellyfish, which we could not explain; these outcomes were therefore also downgraded because of inconsistency.

    We judged most studies to be at high risk of bias in at least one domain. Blinding of participants, personnel, and outcome assessment is clearly a difficult task for researchers in this field because of the differences between interventions and the subjective nature of outcomes, such as pain. This predisposes most studies in this field to a high risk of performance bias, detection bias, or both. We also noted a high rate of attrition in some studies. We therefore also downgraded the certainty of the evidence for each outcome due to study limitations caused by risk of bias. 

    We did not downgrade the certainty of this evidence for indirectness, as all studies provided relevant evidence. An insufficient number of studies precluded effective evaluation of the risk of publication bias in this review.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We conducted a thorough search, with two review authors assessing study eligibility, extracting data, assessing risk of bias in the included studies, and creating summary of findings tables, thereby reducing potential bias in the review process. We adopted broad inclusion criteria, with a comprehensive search strategy, and attempted to contact study authors for additional information when necessary.

    This review is an update of a previous version (Li 2013). During the updating process, we made changes to the review to meet current Cochrane standards, which included using the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of the evidence. For additional clarity, we edited the criteria for study inclusion and some sections of the methods. We reconsidered the types of outcome measures. We edited the primary outcome to be a composite measure of a clinically significant reduction in pain as well as data for cessation of pain; we also reported data for these outcomes as separate measures, and we added a measure to account for those studies in which participants were given the option to use the alternative treatment after a prespecified time point. We aimed to increase the available data from study reports and did not expect these changes to introduce bias to the review. However, we acknowledge that the composite measure of pain sometimes includes an overlap of data for the primary and secondary outcomes. In addition, our approach highlights the many different ways in which pain can be measured, and the complexity of measuring the effectiveness of interventions for pain reduction. 

    We reported the interventions as separate comparison groups in the review. Given the varied types of interventions, we believed that this provided the clearest presentation of data in the review results and summary of findings tables. However, we found that our comparison group of topical applications included some data for hot water; as these studies did not include a comparison with cold applications, we were confident that this did not affect the presentation of the data by separate comparison groups. In future updates of the review, we may need to evaluate methods to manage any overlap between comparison groups.

    In one study of magnesium sulfate (McCullagh 2012), we could not determine the causality of adverse events reported by study authors. These events included tachycardia, flushing, and sweating on administration of magnesium sulfate in one participant, and pulmonary oedema in four participants who had been given magnesium sulfate. Given that these are also symptoms of Irukandji syndrome, it is possible that the events were due to the sting rather than to treatment. Because of the small sample size in this study, we judged the risk of incorrectly assigning causality to be greater, and therefore did not report these events as being due to treatment. 

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We compared the results of our review with other reviews and systematic reviews (Cegolon 2013; Montgomery 2016; Ward 2012; Wilcox 2016). In addition to the RCTs included in our review, all of these reviews included non‐randomised trials and observational studies. The other reviews included more types of jellyfish (e.g. Aurelia aurita , Carybdeida marsupialis , Chiropsalmus quadrigatus , Chiropsalmus quadrumanus , Chrysaora quinquecirrha , Carukia barnesi , Carybdea rastoni , Morbakka , and Pelagia noctiluca).

    Results reported for RCTs in these reviews were consistent with our review. Data from non‐RCTs and observational studies indicate the efficacy of treatments such as analgesics, baking soda, hot water, ice packs, and (only for cubozoans and non‐Australian Physalia) topical vinegar. However, we believe the certainty of any findings from these reviews to be very low given that the evidence is based on study designs that are insufficiently robust and have high risk of bias.
