Cochrane Database of Systematic reviews Review - Intervention

Indwelling bladder catheterisation as part of intraoperative and postoperative care for caesarean section

  • Hany Abdel‐Aleem
  • Mohamad Fathallah Aboelnasr
  • Tameem M Jayousi
  • Fawzia A Habib
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 11 April 2014 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010322.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی
  • Français

    • Background

    Caesarean section (CS) is the most common obstetric surgical procedure, with more than one‐third of pregnant women having lower‐segment CS. Bladder evacuation is carried out as a preoperative procedure prior to CS. Emerging evidence suggests that omitting the use of urinary catheters during and after CS could reduce the associated increased risk of urinary tract infections (UTIs), catheter‐associated pain/discomfort to the woman, and could lead to earlier ambulation and a shorter stay in hospital.

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness and safety of indwelling bladder catheterisation for intraoperative and postoperative care in women undergoing CS.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group's Trials Register (31 December 2013) and reference lists of retrieved studies.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing indwelling bladder catheter versus no catheter or bladder drainage in women undergoing CS (planned or emergency), regardless of the type of anaesthesia used. Quasi‐randomised trials, cluster‐randomised trials were not eligible for inclusion. Studies presented as abstracts were eligible for inclusion providing there was sufficient information to assess the study design and outcomes.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed studies for eligibility and trial quality, and extracted data. Data were checked for accuracy.

    Main results

    The search retrieved 16 studies (from 17 reports). Ten studies were excluded and one study is awaiting assessment. We included five studies involving 1065 women (1090 recruited). The five included studies were at moderate risk of bias.

    Data relating to one of our primary outcomes (UTI) was reported in four studies but did not meet our definition of UTI (as prespecified in our protocol). The included studies did not report on our other primary outcome – intraoperative bladder injury (this outcome was not prespecified in our protocol). Two secondary outcomes were not reported in the included studies: need for postoperative analgesia and women’s satisfaction. The included studies did provide limited data relating to this review’s secondary outcomes.

    Indwelling bladder catheter versus no catheter ‐ three studies (840 women)

    Indwelling bladder catheterisation was associated with a reduced incidence of bladder distension (non‐prespecified outcome) at the end of the operation (risk ratio (RR) 0.02, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.00 to 0.35; one study, 420 women) and fewer cases of retention of urine (RR 0.06, 95% CI 0.01 to 0.47; two studies, 420 women) or need for catheterisation (RR 0.03, 95% CI 0.01 to 0.16; three studies 840 participants). In contrast, indwelling bladder catheterisation was associated with a longer time to first voiding (mean difference (MD) 16.81 hours, 95% CI 16.32 to 17.30; one study, 420 women) and more pain or discomfort due to catheterisation (and/or at first voiding) (average RR 10.47, 95% CI 4.71 to 23.25, two studies, 420 women) although high levels of heterogeneity were observed. Similarly, compared to women in the ‘no catheter’ group, indwelling bladder catheterisation was associated with a longer time to ambulation (MD 4.34 hours, 95% CI 1.37 to 7.31, three studies, 840 women) and a longer stay in hospital (MD 0.62 days, 95% CI 0.15 to 1.10, three studies, 840 women). However, high levels of heterogeneity were observed for these two outcomes and the results should be interpreted with caution.

    There was no difference in postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) due to uterine atony. There was also no difference in the incidence of UTI (as defined by trialists) between the indwelling bladder catheterisation and no catheterisation groups (two studies, 570 women). However, high levels of heterogeneity were observed for this non‐prespecified outcome and results should be considered in this context.

    Indwelling bladder catheter versus bladder drainage – two studies (225 women)

    Two studies (225 women) compared the use of an indwelling bladder catheter versus bladder drainage. There was no difference between groups in terms of retention of urine following CS, length of hospital stay or the non‐prespecified outcome of UTI (as defined by the trialist).

    There is some evidence (from one small study involving 50 women), that the need for catheterisation was reduced in the group of women with an indwelling bladder catheter (RR 0.04, 95% CI 0.00 to 0.70) compared to women in the bladder drainage group. Evidence from another small study (involving 175 women) suggests that women who had an indwelling bladder catheter had a longer time to ambulation (MD 0.90, 95% CI 0.25 to 1.55) compared to women who received bladder drainage.

    Authors' conclusions

    This review includes limited evidence from five RCTs of moderate quality. The review's primary outcomes (bladder injury during operation and UTI), were either not reported or reported in a way not suitable for our analysis. The evidence in this review is based on some secondary outcomes, with heterogeneity present in some of the analyses. There is insufficient evidence to assess the routine use of indwelling bladder catheters in women undergoing CS. There is a need for more rigorous RCTs, with adequate sample sizes, standardised criteria for the diagnosis of UTI and other common outcomes.

    • The effects of using of urinary catheter in women during and after a caesarean section

    Caesaren section (CS) has become the most common obstetric surgery, with one in three of pregnant women having a caesarean delivery. The use of urinary catheters (flexible elastic tube used to drain urine from the bladder) during and after CS is routinely used with caesarean delivery. Alleged benefits of using catheters include; maintains bladder drainage that may improve visualisation during surgery and minimise bladder injury, and less retention of urine after operation (inability to pass urine), but it could be associated with an increased incidence of urinary tract infection, urethral pain, voiding difficulties after removal of the catheter, delayed ambulation, and increased hospital stay.

    This review is based on five studies involving 1065 women undergoing CS. The studies were of moderate quality. The included studies did not use this review's criteria for diagnosis for UTI, so there are no data for this primary outcome. When considering UTI, as defined by the trial authors, there were no clear differences between groups. There were no data relating to bladder injury during the CS (the review's other primary outcome).

    Our analysis showed that the use of urinary catheter was associated with less retention of urine after CS. On the other hand, pain/discomfort due to catheterisation or at first voiding after CS, time to ambulate and hospital stay favoured non‐use of urinary catheter. There was no difference in the incidence of uterine bleeding due to uterine atony (relaxation of the uterus) after the delivery.

    The limited evidence in this review is based five trials of moderate quality and results should be considered in this context. There is not enough evidence to assess the routine use of indwelling bladder catheters in women undergoing CS. There is a need for more rigorous research on this topic and future trials should use a standardised criteria for the diagnosis of UTI and other common outcomes.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Based on the results of the review, there is insufficient evidence to change clinical practice in favour/disfavour of the routine use of indwelling urinary catheters for intra‐ and post‐operative care in patients undergoing caesarean section.

    Implications for research

    There is a need for more rigorous RCTs, with standard criteria to diagnose UTI. Because of the difficulty in double blinding, precautions should be taken to reduce the risk of bias in assessing the outcomes.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Caesarean section (CS) has become the most common obstetric surgery, with more than one‐third of pregnant women having lower‐segment CS. The rate of CS has increased in many parts of the world; 32.8% in US (Hamilton 2011) and 29.5% in Australia respectively (Ford 2008; Stavrou 2011). The rate reported in Latin America is 29% while in east Asia it has ranged from 19% to 35% with an overall rate of 27% (Betran 2007; Festin 2009). In the Arab countries, CS rates range from 5% in Mauritania to 26% in Egypt (Khawaja 2009).

    One of the preoperative procedures in preparation for CS is bladder evacuation.This can be achieved by encouraging the woman to urinate before the operation or by draining the bladder using urethral catheter or by fixing an indwelling urethral catheter. Routine indwelling bladder catheters are widely used in obstetric practice. A survey of UK obstetricians reported that for caesarean section under regional anaesthesia, the majority (82%) use an indwelling urinary catheter for both the procedure and postoperatively, with a minority using the catheter for the procedure only or, an in‐out catheter (Wee 2005). Urinary catheters are associated with an increased risk of urinary tract infections (UTIs), and the prevalence varies in the literature from 6% to 80% (Li 2011; Senanayake 2005). Indwelling catheters increase the risk of UTIs through different mechanisms including direct trauma to the urethral mucosa with their insertion, irritation, inflammatory reaction of the urethra, ascending nosocomial infection and chemical toxic effect of catheter materials (Liedberg H 1989). The risk of UTIs increases with the duration of catheterisation. Catheter‐associated urinary tract infection (CAUTI) can lead to such complications as cystitis, pyelonephritis, gram‐negative bacteraemia, and less commonly, endocarditis, vertebral osteomyelitis, septic arthritis, endophthalmitis, and meningitis. Complications associated with CAUTI cause discomfort to the patient, prolonged hospital stay, and increased cost and mortality (Klevens 2007).

    Description of the intervention

    An indwelling catheter is a drainage tube that is inserted into the urinary bladder through the urethra and is then left in place. This tube is connected to a closed collection system. Indwelling bladder catheters are used to evacuate urine and decompress the urinary bladder. Different types of catheters can be used, for example, standard Foley catheter, Nelaton, antibiotic‐impregnated, and silver alloy‐impregnated catheters. The duration of catheterisation varies from immediate removal after CS or until ambulation of the woman and up to 24 hours. Exceptionally, the catheter is maintained for more than 24 hours in certain indications.

    How the intervention might work

    Using an indwelling bladder catheter during CS to maintain bladder drainage may improve visualisation of the lower uterine segment, minimise bladder injury, facilitate development of bladder flap, and urine output can be monitored to help evaluate fluid status. In the postoperative period, an indwelling bladder catheter could be associated with decreased incidence of postpartum haemorrhage due to uterine atony, and it can avoid the need for the woman to get out of bed to urinate or use a bedpan while she is still recovering from the effect of anaesthesia/analgesia. Conversely, this intervention may be associated with increased incidence of UTIs (Li 2011). Other disadvantages may include: postoperative urethral pain, voiding difficulties after removal of the catheter, delayed ambulation, and increased hospital stay (Ghoreishi 2003).

    Why it is important to do this review

    The use of routine indwelling bladder catheters as part of postoperative care for CS has become a matter of debate. There is emerging evidence supporting the benefits of omitting catheterisation in women undergoing CS (Li 2011). Li et al ( Li 2011) conducted a systematic review and concluded that the non‐use of indwelling urinary catheters in CS is associated with fewer UTIs and no increase in either urinary retention or intraoperative difficulties. On the other hand, Yong 2011 reported that in a hospital audit (with a 25% CS rate and the majority (greater than 75%) of caesarean sections (elective or emergency) performed under regional anaesthesia), 51% of the women had urinary retention before implementation of a policy of routine catheterisation; while only 0.4% of women had this problem immediately after implementation of the policy. In a non‐randomised clinical trial (Senanayake 2005), there were 23 (6.7%) cases of postpartum uterine atony leading to haemorrhage among uncatheterised women undergoing elective CS. Urinary retention could lead to secondary uterine atony and postpartum haemorrhage (Dupuis 2011). One of the proposed advantages of catheterisation, i.e. to facilitate the development of bladder flap, has been challenged in a recent trial (Tuuli 2012). The authors concluded that omission of bladder flap does not increase intraoperative or postoperative complications. However, this trial was not powered to detect outcomes such as bladder injury and it did not recruit cases of emergency CS, planned vertical lower segment or previous laparotomies. Even if a catheter has to be used, there is a need to shorten the duration of bladder catheterisation to minimise the risk of UTI (Onile 2008). Consequently, the benefits and harms of Indwelling bladder catheterisation as part of postoperative care for CS need to be evaluated in a systematic way.

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness and safety of indwelling  bladder catheterisation for intra‐ and postoperative care in women undergoing caesarean section (CS).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Only randomised controlled trials (RCTs) with two arms were included. In future updates of this review, RCTs with more than two arms (multi‐armed RCTs) will be included if appropriate. Quasi‐randomised trials and cluster‐randomised trials were not eligible for inclusion. We included studies presented as abstracts if there was sufficient information to assess the study design and outcomes.

    Types of participants

    Pregnant women undergoing CS (planned or emergency), regardless of the type of anaesthesia. Planned/elective CS refers to one that is performed on a pregnant woman on the basis of an obstetrical or medical indication. The planned/elective CS is executed prior to labour and timed to suit the woman and staff. In contrast, CS carried out at short notice, usually during labour is termed an emergency caesarean.

    Exclusion criteria

    • Women undergoing CS (planned or emergency) in whom prior catheterisation is considered necessary (as judged by the surgeon/anaesthetist): for example, anticipated pelvic adhesions, haemodynamically unstable women, cases with obstructed labour, women with neurological disease or repair of fistula, women with severe pre‐eclampsia.

    • Women proven to have a urinary tract infection.

    • Women receiving antibiotics for any infection prior to CS.

    Types of interventions

    Intervention

    Indwelling bladder catheter inserted before CS and maintained after the operation.

    Comparisons

    • Indwelling bladder catheter during and after CS versus no catheter

    • Indwelling bladder catheter during and after CS versus bladder drainage

    Indwelling bladder catheter refers to a catheter inserted before operation and maintained after operation for any duration.

    No catheter refers to voluntary voiding before operation.

    Bladder drainage means evacuation of the bladder using an in‐out urethral catheter before operation. The catheter is either removed immediately or removed at the end of surgery.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    •  Urinary tract infection (UTI) (catheter‐related/non‐catheter‐related/not specified)

    • Intraoprative bladder injury

    Criteria for diagnosis of UTI is shown in Appendix 1.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Duration of surgery

    • Distention of urinary bladder at end of operation

    • Retention of urine

    • Need for catheterisation (re‐catheterisation)

    • UTI as defined by trialists

    • Pain/discomfort due to catheterisation

    • Time of first voiding after CS

    • Postpartum haemorrhage due to uterine atony

    • Time to ambulation

    • Need for postoperative analgesia

    • Length of hospital stay

    • Women's satisfaction

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We contacted the Trials Search Co‐ordinator to search the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group’s Trials Register (31 December 2013). 

    The Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group’s Trials Register is maintained by the Trials Search Co‐ordinator and contains trials identified from:

    1. monthly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL);

    2. weekly searches of MEDLINE;

    3. weekly searches of Embase;

    4. handsearches of 30 journals and the proceedings of major conferences;

    5. weekly current awareness alerts for a further 44 journals plus monthly BioMed Central email alerts.

    Details of the search strategies for CENTRAL, MEDLINE and Embase, the list of handsearched journals and conference proceedings, and the list of journals reviewed via the current awareness service can be found in the ‘Specialized Register’ section within the editorial information about the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group.

    Trials identified through the searching activities described above are each assigned to a review topic (or topics). The Trials Search Co‐ordinator searches the register for each review using the topic list rather than keywords.

    Searching other resources

    We searched reference lists of retrieved studies.

    We did not apply any language restrictions. 

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (MFA and TMJ) independently assessed for inclusion all the titles and abstracts retrieved from the search. Disagreements were resolved through discussion and consultation with HAA.

    Data extraction and management

    We designed a form to extract data. For eligible studies, two review authors (MFA and TMJ) extracted the data using the agreed form. We resolved discrepancies through discussion with HAA. We entered data into Review Manager software (RevMan 2012) and checked for accuracy.

    When information regarding any of the above was unclear or incomplete, we attempted to contact authors of the original reports to provide further details (two authors were approached through email, but did not respond).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    MFA and TMJ independently assessed risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). We resolved any disagreement by discussion and by involving HAA.

    (1) Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups.

    We assessed the method as:

    • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

    • high risk of bias (any non‐random process, e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number);

    • unclear risk of bias.   

    (2) Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment and assessed whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered sealed opaque envelopes);

    • high risk of bias (open random allocation; unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes, alternation; date of birth);

    • unclear risk of bias.   

    (3.1) Blinding of participants and personnel (checking for possible performance bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind study participants and personnel from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We considered that studies were at low risk of bias if they were blinded, or if we judged that the lack of blinding would be unlikely to affect results. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for participants;

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for personnel.

    (3.2) Blinding of outcome assessment (checking for possible detection bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind outcome assessors from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed methods used to blind outcome assessment as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias.

    (4) Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias due to the amount, nature and handling of incomplete outcome data)

    We described for each included study, and for each outcome or class of outcomes, the completeness of data including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We stated whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. 

    We assessed methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data; missing outcome data balanced across groups);

    • high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups; ‘as treated’ analysis done with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    We considered a cut‐off point of 20% attrition or more as indicative of high risk of bias. 

    (5) Selective reporting (checking for reporting bias)

    We described for each included study how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (where it was clear that all of the study’s prespecified outcomes and all expected outcomes of interest to the review had been reported);

    • high risk of bias (where not all the study’s prespecified outcomes had been reported; one or more reported primary outcomes were not prespecified; outcomes of interest were reported incompletely and so could not be used; study failed to include results of a key outcome that would have been expected to have been reported);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    6) Other bias (checking for bias due to problems not covered by (1) to (5) above)

    We described for each included study any important concerns we had about other possible sources of bias.

    We assessed whether each study was free of other problems that could put it at risk of bias:

    • low risk of other bias;

    • high risk of other bias;

    • unclear whether there is risk of other bias.

    (7) Overall risk of bias

    We made explicit judgements about whether studies were at high risk of bias, according to the criteria given in the Cochrane Handbook (Higgins 2011). With reference (1) to (6) above, we assessed the likely magnitude and direction of the bias and whether we considered it was likely to impact on the findings. We explored the impact of the level of bias through undertaking sensitivity analyses (we did not carry out sensitivity analyses as the primary outcome was not suitable for analysis) ‐ seeSensitivity analysis. 

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we presented results as summary risk ratio with 95% confidence intervals. 

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we used the mean difference as outcomes were measured in the same way between trials. We planned to use the standardised mean difference to combine trials that measure the same outcome, but used different methods. If necessary, we will use standardised mean difference in future updates.  

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cluster‐randomised trials and cross‐over trials were not eligible for inclusion.

    Dealing with missing data

    For included studies, we noted levels of attrition. There was no need to explore the impact of included studies with high levels of missed data in the overall assessment of treatment effect by using sensitivity analysis.

    For all outcomes, we carried out analyses, as far as possible, on an intention‐to‐treat basis, i.e. we attempted to include all participants randomised to each group in the analyses, and all participants were analysed in the group to which they were allocated, regardless of whether or not they received the allocated intervention. The denominator for each outcome in each trial was the number randomised minus any participants whose outcomes were known to be missing.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed statistical heterogeneity in each meta‐analysis using the T², I² and Chi² statistics. We regarded heterogeneity as substantial if an I² was greater than 30% and either the T² was greater than zero, or there was a low P value (less than 0.10) in the Chi² test for heterogeneity. 

    Assessment of reporting biases

    In future updates of this review, if there are 10 or more studies in the meta‐analysis we will investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We will assess funnel plot asymmetry visually. If asymmetry is suggested by a visual assessment, we will perform exploratory analyses to investigate it.

    Data synthesis

    We carried out statistical analysis using the Review Manager software (RevMan 2012). We used fixed‐effect meta‐analysis for combining data where it was reasonable to assume that studies were estimating the same underlying treatment effect: i.e. where trials were examining the same intervention, and the trials’ populations and methods were judged sufficiently similar. Where there was clinical heterogeneity sufficient to expect that the underlying treatment effects differed between trials, or where substantial statistical heterogeneity was detected, we used random‐effects meta‐analysis to produce an overall summary, if an average treatment effect across trials was considered clinically meaningful. The random‐effects summary was treated as the average range of possible treatment effects and we discuss the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. If the average treatment effect was not clinically meaningful, we did not combine trials.

    Where we use random‐effects analyses, the results are presented as the average treatment effect with 95% confidence intervals, and the estimates of  T² and I².

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Because the primary outcome (UTI) was not suitable for our analysis, we did not carry out subgroup analysis. In future updates, if we identify substantial heterogeneity, we will investigate it using subgroup analyses and sensitivity analyses. We will consider whether an overall summary is meaningful, and if it is, use random‐effects analysis to produce it.

    We plan to carry out the following subgroup analyses.

    1. Planned versus emergency CS

    2. Indwelling catheters removed immediately post‐CS versus delayed removal

    3. Different types of catheters (rubber, silicon, and medicated)

    4. Prophylactic antibiotics (for CS) versus no antibiotics

    5. Regional versus no‐regional anaesthesia

    The following outcome will be used in subgroup analysis: incidence of UTI diagnosed clinically or by investigations.

    We will assess subgroup differences by interaction tests available within RevMan (RevMan 2012). We will report the results of subgroup analyses quoting the χ2 statistic and P value, and the interaction test I² value.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We did not perform sensitivity analysis in the current review, but sensitivity analysis will be performed in future updates if there are trials with 20% or more attrition rate. Analysis will be done after exclusion of these trials. We will also carry out sensitivity analysis to explore the effects of fixed‐effect or random‐effects analyses for outcomes with statistical heterogeneity.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    The search of the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group's Trials Register retrieved 17 reports (see: Figure 1).We have included five studies in this review (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008). Ten studies (11 reports) were excluded (Ahmad 2012; Dal 2013; Dunn 2000; Millet 2012; Naguimbing‐Cuaresma 2007; Paterson‐Brown 1997; Saijan 2006; Symonds 1967; Tangtrakul 1994; Zhou 2012) and one is awaiting classification (Kolusari 2007). No further studies were identified from checking the reference lists of the included studies.

    Figure 1

    Study flow diagram.

    Included studies

    The five included studies were conducted in university/teaching hospitals in Nepal (Acharya 2012), Iran (Ghoreishi 2003), UK (Kerr‐Wilson 1986), Egypt (Nasr 2009) and Nigeria (Onile 2008).The five trials recruited 1090 women undergoing caesarean section (CS). Two of the studies recruited women planned for elective as well as emergency CS (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003) and three trials recruited women planned for elective CS (Kerr‐Wilson 1986,; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008). Only one study included women who has undergone a previous CS (Nasr 2009). The type of anaesthesia was regional in two studies (Acharya 2012; Kerr‐Wilson 1986), general in one study (Onile 2008) and both general/regional in two studies (Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). An indwelling bladder catheter was inserted before operation and removed ˜12 hours after the operation in two studies (Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009), after 24 hours in two studies (Acharya 2012; Onile 2008) or when the patient was ambulant in one study (Kerr‐Wilson 1986). In the control group (non‐catheter), women were encouraged to void before operation in three studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009) or the bladder was drained by an in‐out urethral catheter removed before the operation (Kerr‐Wilson 1986) or at the end of the operation (Onile 2008). The type of catheter used was Foley in three studies (Acharya 2012; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Onile 2008), Nelaton in one study (Kerr‐Wilson 1986) and not specified in two studies (Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). Prophylactic antibiotics were given to all women before CS in one study (Acharya 2012), not used in another study (Kerr‐Wilson 1986) and not reported in three studies (Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008). Use of postoperative analgesics was reported in Acharya 2012 and not reported in Ghoreishi 2003,, Kerr‐Wilson 1986, Nasr 2009 or Onile 2008. Diagnosis of urinary tract infections was quite variable among the studies. It was based on clinical symptoms and signs (Acharya 2012), urine microscopy and culture (Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Onile 2008), urinary symptoms and urine dipstick (Nasr 2009) and was not reported by one study (Ghoreishi 2003).

    Excluded studies

    Ten studies were excluded (Ahmad 2012; Dal 2013; Dunn 2000; Millet 2012; Naguimbing‐Cuaresma 2007; Paterson‐Brown 1997; Saijan 2006; Symonds 1967; Tangtrakul 1994; Zhou 2012). Causes of exclusion are detailed in Characteristics of excluded studies table.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Summaries of the risk of bias are shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3. Overall, four studies are considered of unclear risk of bias (Acharya 2012; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008) as many of the domains are not reported. The fifth trial (Ghoreishi 2003) is considered at high risk of bias, as three domains are considered at high risk of bias.

    Allocation

    In only one study (Nasr 2009), was allocation concealment adequate; closed opaque envelopes (low risk of bias). In the other four studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Onile 2008) allocation concealment was not reported (unclear risk).

    Blinding

    In four studies, performance and detection bias were not reported (unclear risk of bias) (Acharya 2012; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008). In one study (Ghoreishi 2003) uncatheterised patients were examined by the author at least twice per day (high risk of bias).

    Incomplete outcome data

    There was a low risk of bias in four studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Nasr 2009). In one study (Onile 2008) 25 women (12.5%) were excluded after randomisation.

    Selective reporting

    In four trials, there was a low risk of reporting bias (Acharya 2012; Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Nasr 2009; Onile 2008), all outcomes mentioned in the methods section were reported in the results section. In one study (Ghoreishi 2003) there was high risk of bias; some results were not specified in the outcomes, one outcome was reported for one group and not for the other group.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Four studies have a low risk of bias (did not stop early, and no baseline imbalances). In one study (Nasr 2009), there were baseline imbalances; more women in the catheter group received CS under general anaesthesia (27.1 % versus 2.8% in the no catheter group).

    Effects of interventions

    Comparison 1 ‐ Indwelling bladder catheter during and after CS versus no catheter

    This comparison included three studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009).

    Primary outcomes
    Urinary tract infection

    Data for urinary tract infection (UTI) was reported in two studies (Acharya 2012; Nasr 2009) but the trials did not use this review's criteria for diagnosing UTI (as outlined in Appendix 1), resulting in no useable data for this outcome. Ghoreishi 2003 did not report on this outcome.

    Bladder injury during operation (non‐prespecified outcome)

    This outcome was not reported in the included studies.

    Secondary outcomes
    Duration of surgery (non‐prespecified outcome)

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Distention of the urinary bladder at the end of the operation (non‐prespecified outcome)

    Distension of the bladder at the end of the operation was reported by one study (Nasr 2009) (Analysis 1.1).There were no cases of bladder distension in the catheter group versus 23 cases in the no catheter group (risk ratio (RR) 0.02; 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.00 to 0.35; 420 women).

    Retention of urine

    Retention of urine was reported by two studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003), the pooled analysis showed that catheterisation is associated with a reduced risk of retention of urine (RR 0.06; 95% CI 0.01 to 0.47; 420 women (Analysis 1.2)).

    Need for catheterisation

    The need for catheterisation was reported by three studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). The pooled analysis showed that the use of a catheter was associated with a significant reduction in the need for catheterisation (RR 0.03; 95% CI 0.01 to 0.16; 840 women (Analysis 1.3)).

    Urinary tract infection (as defined by the trialist) (non‐prespecified outcome)

    Urinary tract infection as defined by trialist was reported by two studies (Acharya 2012; Nasr 2009). Acharya 2012 used clinical symptoms and signs for diagnosis of UTI, whereas Nasr 2009 used urine dipstick for nitrites to diagnose UTI. There was no clear difference between catheter and no catheter group in the incidence of UTI, with very wide confidence intervals and substantial heterogeneity (average RR 20.21; 95% CI 0.61, 670.22; 570 women; T2 = 5.85, I2 = 91% (Analysis 1.4)).

    Pain/discomfort due to catheterisation (and/or at first voiding)

    Pain/discomfort due catheterisation (and/or at first voiding) was reported by two studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003). Among 420 women, the use of catheter was associated with significantly increased pain/discomfort (average RR 10.47; 95% CI 4.71 to 23.25, T2 = 0.22, I2 = 67% (Analysis 1.6)).

    Time to first voiding after CS (hours)

    Time to first voiding after CS (hours) was reported by one study (Nasr 2009). The use of catheter was associated with delayed time at first voiding after CS in comparison to the no catheter group (mean difference (MD) 16.81 hours; 95% CI 16.32 to 17.30, 420 women (Analysis 1.9)). However, it is unclear whether the time measurement included the duration of catheterisation (12 hours) or not.

    Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) due to uterine atony

    Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) due to uterine atony was reported by two studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003). Pooled analysis of 420 women showed that there were few events; four cases in the no catheter group versus zero in the catheter group. The use of catheter was associated with less but statistically insignificant incidence of PPH due to uterine atony (RR 0.20; 95% CI 0.02 to 1.69 (Analysis 1.5)) .

    Time to patient ambulation

    Time to ambulation (hours) was reported in three studies (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). The use of catheter was associated with longer time to ambulate (mean difference (MD) 4.34 hours; 95% CI 1.37 to 7.31, 840 women, T2 = 6.51, I2 = 95% (Analysis 1.7)), but substantial heterogeneity was observed.

    Need for postoperative analgesia

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Length of hospital stay (days)

    The length of hospital stay (days) was reported by three studies, involving 840 women (Acharya 2012; Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). Use of catheterisation was associated with a longer stay in hospital but this result should be interpreted with caution as we observed substantial heterogeneity among the included studies (MD 0.62; 95% CI 0.15 to 1.10, T2 = 0.17, I2 = 98% (Analysis 1.8)).

    Patient satisfaction

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Comparison 2 ‐ Indwelling bladder catheter during and after CS versus bladder drainage prior to CS

    This comparison included two studies with 225 women with planned CS (Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Onile 2008).

    Primary outcomes
    Urinary tract infection

    Data for urinary tract infection was reported in both studies but the trials did not use this review's criteria for diagnosing UTI (as outlined in Appendix 1), resulting in no useable data for this outcome.

    Bladder injury during operation (non‐prespecified outcome)

    This outcome was not reported in the included studies.

    Secondary outcomes
    Duration of surgery (non‐prespecified outcome)

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Distention of the urinary bladder at the end of the operation (non‐prespecified outcome)

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Retention of urine

    Retention of urine was reported by one study (Onile 2008). One case out of 86 cases developed retention in the bladder drainage group in comparison to none in the catheter group (89 women) (RR 0.32, 95% CI 0.01, 7.80; 175 women (Analysis 2.1)).

    Need for catheterisation

    The need for catheterisation was reported by Kerr‐Wilson 1986. The use of a catheter was significantly associated with less need for catheterisation (RR 0.04, 95% CI 0.00 to 0.70; 50 women (Analysis 2.2)). The trial authors reported that 11 of the 25 women in the bladder drainage group needed catheterisation, whereas none of the 25 women in the catheter group needed catheterisation.

    Urinary tract infection (as defined by the trialist) (non‐prespecified outcome)

    UTI as defined by trialists was reported two studies (Kerr‐Wilson 1986; Onile 2008), both of which used significant bacteriuria as a criterion for infection. There was no difference between groups (RR 1.27, 95% CI 0.58 to 2.77; 225 women (Analysis 2.3)).

    Pain/discomfort due to catheterisation (and/or at first voiding)

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Time to first voiding after CS (hours)

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) due to uterine atony

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Time to patient ambulation

    Time to ambulation (hours) was reported in one study (Onile 2008) and the mean time for patient ambulation was longer for the indwelling bladder catheter group (8.72 hours) compared to the group of women who underwent bladder drainage (7.82 hours) (MD 0.90 hours; 95% CI 0.25 to 1.55; 175 women (Analysis 2.4)).

    Need for postoperative analgesia

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Length of hospital stay

    The length of hospital stay (days) was reported by Onile 2008. There was no difference in length of hospital stay between the two groups (MD 0.09; 95% CI ‐0.44 to 0.62; 175 women (Analysis 2.5)). The mean time was 6.91 + 1.82 days in catheter group compared to 6.82 + 1.76 days in women who underwent bladder drainage.

    Patient satisfaction

    The included studies did not report on this outcome.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    The review included five studies (a total of 1090 women were recruited) of moderate quality; information relating to many risk of bias domains was not clear or not reported. For urinary tract infection (UTI) (one of this review's primary outcomes) four studies reported this outcome, but used different criteria for diagnosis (symptoms and signs, urine dipstick, urine culture), did not specify whether it was symptomatic or asymptomatic and if symptomatic, whether it was upper urinary tract (pyelonephritis) or lower urinary tract (cystitis/urethritis) infection. Since the included studies did not use this review's criteria for diagnosis of UTI (as prespecified in the protocol), data were not included in our analyses for this primary outcome. We did, however, include the data in a new secondary outcome (UTI as defined by trialists). We added a new primary outcome (bladder injury during operation) but this was not reported in any of the included studies.

    For the secondary outcomes, the pooled analysis showed that the use of an indwelling catheter was associated with less retention of urine in the postoperative period, less need for catheterisation and less distention of the bladder at the end of the operation (new outcome, not prespecified in our protocol). There was no difference in the incidence of postpartum haemorrhage (PPH) due to uterine atony. Urinary tract infection as defined by trialists was no different between the catheterised and non catheterised groups, nor between catheterised and bladder drainage groups. On the other hand, pain/discomfort due to catheterisation or at first voiding, time to ambulate and hospital stay favoured the no catheter group, but there was marked heterogeneity among the included studies.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The review included five studies with 1090 women who underwent CS (planned/emergency) under general/regional anaesthesia. Four studies were conducted in developing countries (Egypt, Iran, Nepal and Nigeria) and one study was conducted in UK. This limits the generalis ability (external validity) of the results. The studies, which included both planned and emergency section, presented the results together. This did not allow the evaluation of the impact of catheterisation in emergency versus planned CS. Women undergoing emergency CS are more prone to infectious morbidity than planned CS (Raees 2012).The use of prophylactic antibiotics, which could reduce the risk of infection, was not reported in three studies. A systematic review concluded that prophylactic antibiotics reduce the incidence of febrile morbidity, wound infection and urinary tract infection following both planned and emergency CS (Smaill 2010).

    Quality of the evidence

    The review included five studies, with sample sizes ranging from 50 to 420 women. The domain concerning selection bias was not reported by four of the five studies. Performance bias and outcome assessment bias, whilst difficult in such studies (catheter versus no catheter), did not report any precautions to minimise this risk. For the primary outcomes (bladder injury during operation and UTI), the outcomes were either not reported, or reported in a way not suitable for our analysis. The evidence in this review is based on secondary outcomes, with heterogeneity among studies in some of them and non‐reporting of others.

    Potential biases in the review process

    For the review methodology, the search was comprehensive and identified 17 studies. One was in Chinese (Zhou 2012) and one in Turkish (Kolusari 2007), the former was translated, the latter is awaiting translation. For the study design, we included only randomised controlled trials (RCTs). For the diagnosis of UTI, we used the criteria of CDC (Centers for Disease Control) (Appendix 1), in an attempt to standardise the methods for diagnosis and the interpretation of the results (catheter/non‐catheter‐related infection, symptomatic/asymptomatic). Unfortunately, none of the four studies that reported UTI, complied to these criteria and were not included in the analysis as a primary outcome but were included in the secondary (non‐prespecified) outcome: "UTI as defined by trialists". Data collection and analysis were carried out independently by review authors and discrepancies were resolved through discussion with a third review author.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Another systematic review (Li 2011) included three trials (two RCTs and one non‐RCT), involving a total of 1084 participants. The two RCTs are included in the present review (Ghoreishi 2003; Nasr 2009). The latter RCT did not report UTIs, this means that for UTI the Li 2011 review was based on one RCT and one non‐RCT. They used less strict criteria for diagnosis of UTI (UTIs were diagnosed clinically by either the presence of 100 coliform organisms/mL urine with pyuria (≥10 leucocytes/mm), or at least 105 of any pathogenic organism/mL urine on culture). They reported that in comparison with the use of indwelling urinary catheters, non‐use had a significantly lower incidence of UTIs (risk ratio (RR) 0.08; 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.01 to 0.64 (study design: RCT); RR 0.10; 95% CI 0.02 to 0.57 (study design: non‐RCT); a lower rate of discomfort at first voiding (RR 0.06; 95% CI 0.03 to 0.12); less time until first voiding (mean difference (MD) –16.81; 95% CI –17.31 to –16.31); and less time until ambulation (MD –6.01; 95% CI –6.68 to –5.35)). There were no statistically significant differences in the rate of urinary retention (RR 5.00; 95% CI 0.24 to 103.18 (study design: RCT); RR 0.74; 95% CI 0.04 to 15.18 (study design: non‐RCT)).

    In the present review, the incidence of retention of urine and need for catheterisation was significantly less in the catheter group. However, there is significant heterogeneity among the studies. Despite the limitations of the Li 2011 review (two studies, one is non‐randomised), the review concluded that the routine use of indwelling urinary catheters for caesarean delivery in haemodynamically stable patients is not necessary, and can be harmful. However, better and larger randomised trials are needed to confirm these findings.
