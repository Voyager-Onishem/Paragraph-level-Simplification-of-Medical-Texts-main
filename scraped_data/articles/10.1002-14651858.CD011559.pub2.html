Interventions for preventing osteoradionecrosis of the jaws in adults receiving head and neck radiotherapy

    • Background

    Osteoradionecrosis (ORN) of the jaws is among the most serious oral complications of head and neck cancer radiotherapy, arising from radiation‐induced fibro‐atrophic tissue injury, manifested by necrosis of osseous tissues and failure to heal, often secondary to operative interventions in the oral cavity. It is associated with considerable morbidity and has important quality of life ramifications. Since ORN is very difficult to treat effectively, preventive measures to limit the onset of this disease are needed; however, the effects of various preventive interventions has not been adequately quantified.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of interventions for preventing ORN of the jaws in adult patients with head and neck cancer undergoing curative or adjuvant (i.e. non‐palliative) radiotherapy.

    Search methods

    Cochrane Oral Health's Information Specialist searched the following databases: Cochrane Oral Health's Trials Register (to 5 November 2019), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2019, Issue 10) in the Cochrane Library (searched 5 November 2019), MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 5 November 2019), Embase Ovid (1980 to 5 November 2019), Allied and Complementary Medicine (AMED) Ovid (1985 to 5 November 2019), Scopus (1966 to 5 November 2019), Proquest Dissertations and Theses International (1861 to 5 November 2019) and Web of Science Conference Proceedings (1990 to 5 November 2019). The US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register (ClinicalTrials.gov) and the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform were searched for ongoing trials. No restrictions were placed on the language or date of publication when searching the electronic databases.

    Selection criteria

    We selected randomised controlled trials (RCTs) or quasi‐RCTs of adult patients 18 years or older with head and neck cancer who had undergone curative or adjuvant radiotherapy to the head and neck, who had received an intervention to prevent the onset of ORN. Eligible patients were those subjected to pre‐ or post‐irradiation dental evaluation. Management of these patients was to be with interventions independent of their cancer therapy, including but not limited to local, systemic, or behavioural interventions.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently selected trials from search results, assessed risk of bias, and extracted relevant data for inclusion in the review. Authors of included studies were contacted to request missing data. We used standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane.

    Main results

    Four studies were identified that met pre‐determined eligibility criteria, evaluating a total of 342 adults. From the four studies, all assessed as at high risk of bias, three broad interventions were identified that may potentially reduce the risk of ORN development: one study showed no reduction in ORN when using platelet‐rich plasma placed in the extraction sockets of prophylactically removed healthy mandibular molar teeth prior to radiotherapy (odds ratio (OR) 3.32, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.58 to 19.09; one trial, 44 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). Another study involved comparing fluoride gel and high‐content fluoride toothpaste (1350 parts per million (ppm)) in prevention of post‐radiation caries, and found no difference between their use as no cases of ORN were reported (one trial, 220 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). The other two studies involved the use of perioperative hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy and antibiotics. One study showed that treatment with HBO caused a reduction in the development of ORN in comparison to patients treated with antibiotics following dental extractions (risk ratio (RR) 0.18, 95% CI 0.43 to 0.76; one trial, 74 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). Another study found no difference between combined HBO and antibiotics compared to antibiotics alone prior to dental implant placement (RR 3.00, 95% CI 0.14 to 65.16; one trial, 26 participants; very low‐certainty evidence).

    Adverse effects of the different interventions were not reported clearly or were not important.

    Authors' conclusions

    Given the suboptimal reporting and inadequate sample sizes of the included studies, evidence regarding the interventions evaluated by the trials included in this review is uncertain. More well‐designed RCTs with larger samples are required to make conclusive statements regarding the efficacy of these interventions.

    • Dental treatments for preventing damage to the jawbones in people with cancer receiving radiotherapy to the head and neck

    Review question

    With this Cochrane Review we tried to find out the best way to prevent the death and exposure of bone in the jaws of people who have had radiotherapy for head and neck cancer. This is a condition called osteoradionecrosis (ORN).

    Background

    ORN of the jaws is a serious problem which can happen after people have radiotherapy as part of their treatment for head and neck cancer. ORN refers to the death and exposure of bone that result from damage to the tissues due to being exposed to radiation. ORN is very difficult to treat and it is important that steps are taken to prevent it. In this review, we looked at any treatments that have been used to prevent ORN.

    Study characteristics

    This review is up‐to‐date as of 5 November 2019. The review includes four studies involving 342 adults who had received radiotherapy for the treatment of head and neck cancer. The review looks at three different ways to prevent ORN:

    ‐ the use of platelet‐rich plasma (PRP) in bone after removal of healthy teeth prior to radiotherapy. Plasma is a part of blood that contains special proteins that help the blood to clot, and PRP is concentrated plasma which supports cell growth. Injecting PRP into damaged tissue may stimulate the body to grow new, healthy cells to make it heal more quickly;

    ‐ taking out teeth because of tooth decay makes the risk of developing ORN greater. Preventing tooth decay in people having radiotherapy is very important. We looked at a study comparing using fluoride gel with a toothpaste with a higher level of fluoride than normal to prevent tooth decay after radiotherapy;

    ‐ hyperbaric oxygen therapy is breathing oxygen in a pressurized chamber to improve blood supply, which may help heal damaged tissue. Two studies compared the use of hyperbaric oxygen therapy for taking out teeth or placing dental implants with antibiotics. Antibiotics are drugs which stop or slow the growth of bacteria.

    Key results

    One study showed no reduction in ORN when using platelet‐rich plasma in bone after the removal of healthy teeth. Another study found no difference between fluoride gel and toothpaste with a higher level of fluoride than normal as no cases of ORN were reported. A third study showed that treatment with hyperbaric oxygen therapy caused a reduction in the development of ORN in comparison to patients treated with antibiotics following the removal of teeth. The fourth study found no difference between combined hyperbaric oxygen therapy and antibiotics compared to antibiotics alone.

    Harmful effects of the different interventions were not reported clearly or were not important.

    Certainty of the evidence

    The level of certainty we have in these findings is very low. This was due to high risk of bias, not all studies mentioning important details, and the small number of people studied in the four included trials.

    Conclusion

    We do not have enough evidence to say which intervention works better to stop ORN of the jaws from happening or making it less severe in adults receiving head and neck radiotherapy. We suggest that more, well‐conducted, and bigger studies including more people, should be done in this area.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    This Cochrane Review found very low‐certainty evidence from four included studies that is insufficient to conclude whether or not platelet‐rich plasma, perioperative hyperbaric oxygen therapy, systemic antimicrobial prophylaxis, and fluoride gel and toothpaste are effective in preventing the development of osteoradionecrosis (ORN) of the jaws in adults receiving head and neck radiotherapy.

    Implications for research

    Well‐designed, large scale, multi‐institutional, double‐blinded randomised controlled trials, with more uniform study populations and adequately reported detailed dental protocols with long‐term follow‐up (preferably reported follow‐up of more than five years, or median follow‐up of surviving patients of more than three years) and standardized measures of late bone toxicities are required, in order to more accurately assess the effects of any interventions in the prevention of ORN. Long‐term follow‐up is necessary to prevent under‐reporting, as the risk for trauma‐related development of ORN remains indefinitely following radiotherapy (Epstein 1997; Marx 1987). Thorn 2000 found that 74% of ORN cases developed within the first three years after radiotherapy, but ORN could emerge on a traumatic basis for an extended period of time. Based on the ORN incidence rate of 3.0% since 1997 reported by Wahl 2006, an initial study with 80% power (a = 0.05, b = 0.20) would require 4025 irradiated patients for each control arm and experimental group, in order to detect a difference of 1%; this would be reduced further to 2518 irradiated patients for the 2% incidence rate estimated by Nabil 2012. Achieving such sample size will remain a challenge and hence, multicentre collaboration is the key strategy for future research.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Occurring in bone that has been irradiated previously, osteoradionecrosis (ORN) of the jaws is most commonly defined as an area of devitalized and exposed bone that fails to heal over a period of three to six months in the absence of local neoplastic disease (Marx 1983; Store 2000; Widmark 1989). Though the aetiology of this condition is not fully understood, the current leading hypothesis is that ORN is caused by radiation‐induced fibrosis and subsequent avascular atrophy of the affected tissues (Delanian 2004). ORN is associated with considerable morbidity and has important quality of life ramifications for cancer survivors (Chang 2012; Wang 2009). Clinical manifestations of ORN can range from asymptomatic bony exposures in early phases of the disease to secondary infections, pain, paraesthesia, chronic fistulas, and pathologic fractures, as well as dysgeusia, dysphagia, dyspnoea, and mechanical impairment of speech (Epstein 1987).

    The reported incidence in patients with a history of head and neck cancer treated by irradiation (of greater than 60 Gy) to the mandible or maxilla is highly variable (Clayman 1997; Wahl 2006). Clayman 1997 found the overall incidence (not stratified by risk) before 1968 reached 11.8%, while the number dropped to 5.4% between 1968 and 1992, presumably due to megavoltage and supervoltage photons and electrons replacing orthovoltage beams. More recently, an incidence of 3.0% has been reported in a sample of patients treated between 1997 and 2006 (Wahl 2006). The true incidence may in fact be higher due to under‐reporting of this condition because of inadequate or variable follow‐up periods in published studies (Nabil 2012; Peterson 2010).

    The pathophysiology of ORN to date remains poorly understood and highly debated. With an understanding that dentoalveolar surgical treatments (e.g. dental extractions or periodontal surgery) were risk factors for the development of this disease, early hypotheses of the pathogenesis were that ORN occurs as a result of radiation‐induced endarteritis leading to hypoxia, hypocellularity, and hypovascularity (Marx 1983). More recently, evidence to the contrary has instead suggested that ORN may not actually be the result of hypoxia and hypovascularity, but rather due to a radiation‐induced fibro‐atrophic process secondary to bone turnover in the jaws (Delanian 2004). Though bony turnover appears to occur in the jaws in the absence of operative therapy, this process is significantly increased with operative therapy, thereby explaining the correlation between operative therapy and ORN.

    Although ORN is a relatively uncommon complication, its repercussions may be devastating both in terms of patient health‐related quality of life and the burden to the healthcare system (Dempsey 1997; Kelishadi 2009). Given that survival rates of patients with head and neck cancer are increasing (Lavaf 2008; Rogers 2009; Studer 2007; Studer 2012), a corresponding increase in the incidence of ORN has subsequently been observed (Boje 2013). Furthermore, the risk for development of ORN remains long after completion of radiation therapy and this risk does not appear to dissipate over time (Epstein 1997; Marx 1987).

    Considering the difficulty in effectively treating ORN, the National Cancer Institute (NCI 2016) has underscored the importance of prevention by means of comprehensive pre‐irradiation oral/dental screening and management, as well as by the implementation of subsequent post‐irradiation preventive measures to reduce or obviate the need for invasive dental treatment.

    Description of the intervention

    Suggested interventions for the prevention of ORN are manifold, but may be classified broadly into pre‐ and post‐irradiation dental evaluation and management with specific interventions including local and systemic measures, as well as behavioural modification. Some of the suggested interventions include hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy, caries prevention interventions such as oral hygiene instruction and frequent dental recalls, local and systemic antimicrobials, local interventions such as the use of platelet‐rich plasma, and systemic medications such as pentoxifylline (a competitive phosphodiesterase inhibitor) and tocopherol (an antioxidant) (Delanian 2004; Peterson 2010; Wahl 2006).

    This review examined the evidence for the efficacy of both direct and indirect interventions to prevent ORN. Direct interventions are those intended to mitigate the immediate/proximal trauma‐related initiating events that may lead to ORN; other interventions actively modify the extent or progression of the subsequent pathologic fibro‐atrophic process involved in the altered wound healing response believed to be responsible for the development of ORN. Indirect interventions are those intended to mitigate attendant risk factors that may lead to trauma‐related initiating events or contribute passively to altered wound healing. For instance, trauma related to surgical procedures such as dental extraction may be considered a proximal cause, and any intervention that can modify the process of extraction or subsequent wound healing would constitute a direct intervention. Any measure to address an attendant risk factor predisposing to extraction/trauma, such as prevention of caries or periodontal disease, or a risk factor contributing to progression of the altered wound healing response, such as concomitant comorbidity or smoking habit, would be considered an indirect intervention.

    How the intervention might work

    There are two broad categories for interventions that may be effective for reducing the incidence of ORN in populations at risk. The first are interventions that would significantly reduce the amount of operative dental therapy required post‐radiotherapy, thereby reducing the frequency or extent of trauma following radiotherapy. The second are interventions that may be used as adjuncts to modify or arrest progression of the disease through the various stages of the fibro‐atrophic process involved in the pathogenesis of ORN (Delanian 2004).

    Why it is important to do this review

    The effectiveness of various interventions for the prevention of ORN of the jaws remains unquantified. Although it has been presumed that newer, more precise delivery techniques deployed in contemporary radiotherapy would reduce the incidence of ORN of the jaws (Ahmed 2009; Peterson 2010), the evidence remains equivocal, with low patient numbers and limited follow‐up time. Previous systematic reviews have not been designed specifically to deal with preventive interventions. Therefore, the rationale for conducting this Cochrane Review was to identify the preventive strategies that could be most effective in reducing the occurrence of ORN. Such evidence would enable subsequent improvements and validate current practice guidelines.

    Objectives

    The objective of this review was to assess the effects of interventions for preventing osteoradionecrosis of the jaws in adult patients with head and neck cancer undergoing curative or adjuvant (i.e. non‐palliative) radiotherapy.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) or quasi‐RCTs (i.e. RCTs whose patients are not allocated in a truly random manner) of any intervention for the prevention of osteoradionecrosis (ORN), as long as the incidence of ORN was reported as a primary or secondary outcome. We did not include cluster trials due to potential differences in the ancillary treatments between centres. We also excluded cross‐over trials due to the irreversible changes associated with radiotherapy.

    Types of participants

    We included all adult head and neck cancer patients who underwent radiotherapy, either curative or in an adjuvant setting, having received radiation to the mandible or maxilla or both. We excluded patients treated with palliative intent due to differences in treatment goals, increased incidence of extant comorbidities, variations in palliative radiotherapy regimens ‐ most of which involve a total radiation dose of less than 60 Gy with minimal associated risk for development of ORN, as well as the critically reduced overall survival rates leading to severely constrained follow‐up times. Re‐irradiated patients were also excluded due to the permanent/irreversible effects associated with prior radiation treatment which would result in inextricable confounding. Child and adolescent populations undergoing radiotherapy were excluded as they represent a unique population of patients with developing bone that might respond differently to irradiation than does mature bone.

    Types of interventions

    We reviewed any form of prophylactic intervention, such as local, systemic, or behavioural interventions or combinations thereof. We excluded interventions directly related to cancer therapy, such as modified radiotherapy or chemotherapy regimens; otherwise no limitations on interventions were defined for this review. Furthermore, no limitations on control interventions were defined. Examples of interventions included, but were not limited to, prophylactic or symptomatic extraction together with intra‐operative surgical technique (timing and number of extractions, atraumatic technique, alveoloplasty, mucosal coverage/primary closure, type of anaesthetic +/‐ epinephrine); endodontic or periodontal therapy; caries prevention measures (oral hygiene maintenance/ prophylaxis/topical fluoride application); fabrication of stents/radiation guards to prevent electron backscatter off metallic restorations; topical antibiotics; and ultrasound (promotes angiogenesis). Examples of systemic interventions included hyperbaric oxygen therapy; systemic medications such as prophylactic antibiotics, non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs, corticosteroids, pentoxifylline, tocopherol, and platelet‐rich plasma. Finally, examples of behavioural interventions included patient education such as counselling of patients on the nature of ORN, its risk factors, and commonly employed prevention strategies.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    The primary outcome for this study was incidence of ORN. This was defined as an area of devitalized and exposed bone that fails to heal over a period of three to six months in the absence of local neoplastic disease (Marx 1983; Store 2000; Widmark 1989). Specific definitions set by each of the included studies were noted.

    Secondary outcomes

    The secondary outcomes for this study were the reporting of any adverse effects of interventions, such as hospitalization, morbidity (e.g. pain, loss of sensation, effects on taste), and mortality.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    Cochrane Oral Health's Information Specialist conducted systematic searches in the following databases for randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials. There were no language, publication year or publication status restrictions:

    • Cochrane Oral Health's Trials Register (searched 5 November 2019) (Appendix 1);

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2019, Issue 10) in the Cochrane Library (5 November 2019) (Appendix 2);

    • MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix 3);

    • Embase Ovid (1980 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix 4);

    • AMED (Allied and Complementary Medicine) Ovid (1985 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix 5);

    • Scopus (1966 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix 6);

    • Proquest Dissertations and Theses International (1861 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix 7);

    • Web of Science Conference Proceedings (1990 to 5 November 2019) (Appendix Appendix 8).

    Subject strategies were modelled on the search strategy designed for MEDLINE Ovid. Where appropriate, they were combined with subject strategy adaptations of the highly sensitive search strategy designed by Cochrane for identifying randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions Chapter 6 (Lefebvre 2011).

    Searching other resources

    The following trial registries were searched for ongoing studies:

    • US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (clinicaltrials.gov; searched 5 November 2019) (Appendix 9);

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (apps.who.int/trialsearch; searched 5 November 2019) (Appendix 10).

    We also searched for unpublished studies (or 'grey' literature) such as technical reports, dissertations, or studies published in non‐English languages which may not have been indexed to major databases by contacting the authors of the identified trials.

    The indices from 2013 to 2018 of the following sources were handsearched under 'Clinical Trials': International Association for Dental Research conference abstracts, American Association for Dental Research conference abstracts, Canadian Association for Dental Research conference abstracts, British Society for Dental Research conference abstracts, Proceedings of the British Society for Oral Medicine, and Proceedings of the European Association of Oral Medicine. We checked references of identified publications for relevant studies, and contacted authors to identify missing and unreported trials. We also attempted to contact trial authors if we needed to clarify or to obtain relevant data on outcomes not reported in the study.

    We checked that none of the included studies in this review were retracted due to error or fraud.

    We did not perform a separate search for adverse effects of interventions used, we considered adverse effects described in included studies only.

    Data collection and analysis

     

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (Michael Duchnay (MD) and Hamid Reza Raziee (HRR) independently reviewed and selected trials from searches. The search was designed to be sensitive and include controlled clinical trials, these were filtered out early in the selection process if they were not randomised. We resolved disagreements through discussion and consensus, or by consulting the other review authors (Amir Azarpazhooh (AA) and Mohamed El‐Rabbany (ME).

    Data extraction and management

    The two review authors (MD, and HRR) independently performed data extraction for all studies that met the inclusion criteria, and recorded results in a specifically designed data extraction form (previously pilot tested) in an Excel spreadsheet. We resolved disagreements through discussion and consensus, or by consulting the other review authors (AA and ME). We extracted information for the following parameters when available:

    • participant characteristics (age, gender);

    • study location, including the setting where it took place (e.g. hospital, private clinic);

    • type of intervention(s) and number of participants in each group;

    • incidence and stage of ORN;

    • adverse events, such as pain, dysphagia, and paraesthesia;

    • treatment‐related parameters (e.g. intent, delivery technique, total dose, fractionation schedule of radiotherapy; timing and type of surgical oncologic intervention and chemotherapy regimen, if employed) and tumour‐related parameters (e.g. type, site, and stage).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Three review authors (MD, HRR and ME) evaluated the risk of bias in included trials by using the Cochrane risk of bias assessment tool independently (Higgins 2011). We resolved disagreements through discussion and consensus, or by consulting the other review authors (AA and ME). We evaluated seven domains: sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias), blinding of outcome assessors (detection bias), incomplete outcome data, selective outcome reporting, and other bias. We assigned a judgement of low, high or unclear risk of bias for each domain. We then graded overall results for each study into the following categories:

    • low risk of bias: low risk of bias for all key domains,

    • unclear risk of bias: unclear risk of bias for one or more key domains, and

    • high risk of bias: high risk of bias for one or more key domains.

    We recorded our judgements and justifications in 'Risk of bias' tables for each included study and generated a 'Risk of bias' summary figure and a 'Risk of bias' graph.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed the primary outcome of interest (the incidence of ORN), using RevMan 5.3 for statistical analysis (Review Manager 2014). We would have used risk ratio (RR), risk difference (RD) and mean difference (MD) when appropriate. We reported 95% confidence intervals (CI) for estimates of treatment effects. We would have analysed the secondary outcomes of interest for dichotomous outcomes such as adverse events, hospitalization, morbidity, mortality, when the data were available, using RR or RD. We would have summarised continuous outcomes such as the length of hospitalizations using MD with 95% CI when data were available. We would have used the standardized mean difference (SMD) with its 95% CI for studies that measure the same primary or secondary outcome using different scales. We presented qualitative reporting of the extent of ORN in a narrative fashion. For split‐mouth studies, we used the generic inverse variance option in RevMan, using the Becker‐Balagtas method (Curtin 2002) with a conservative intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) of 0.5 to obtain the required log odds ratio and its standard error.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We used the patient as the unit of analysis in parallel‐group studies. In split‐mouth studies, we chose a site pair within an individual participant to be the unit of analysis.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted trial authors to request missing data or to clarify methods whenever possible, but we received few responses. Missing standard deviations were obtained from standard errors and confidence intervals for group means, or from standard errors, confidence intervals, t values and P values for differences in means, respectively, following guidance included in Sections 7.7.3.2 and 7.7.3.3 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We intended to use RevMan 5.3 software (Review Manager 2014) to assess heterogeneity of treatment effects between trials. The following formal statistical analyses were intended to be used:

    • The Chi2 test, to assess whether observed variability in effect sizes between studies is greater than would be expected by chance. Since this test has low power when the number of studies included in the meta‐analysis is small, we planned to set the alpha probability at the 10% level of significance.

    • The I2 statistic to ensure that pooling of data was valid. We planned to grade the degree of heterogeneity as none (< 25%), low (≥ 25% to < 50%), moderate (≥ 50% to < 75%), and high (≥ 75%) (Higgins 2003). Where there was evidence of apparent or statistical heterogeneity, we planned to assess the source of the heterogeneity using sensitivity and subgroup analyses looking for evidence of bias or methodological differences between trials.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We assessed publication bias by investigating trial registries and by contacting authors of identified studies to provide protocols for the purpose of comparing protocols and full reports. We attempted to account for any disparities by using explanations from the original authors. We also planned to draw funnel plots of primary outcomes to assess the potential publication bias (small study effects). However, the small number of included studies precluded this analysis.

    Data synthesis

    We planned to perform meta‐analysis of trials reporting similar comparisons of the same specified ORN outcome measures. We intended to combine risk ratios for dichotomous data, and mean differences for continuous data (standardized mean differences where studies use different scales) employing a fixed‐effect model if there were less than three studies, or a random‐effects model for meta‐analyses incorporating three or more studies.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Should statistical heterogeneity be identified in our analyses, we planned to assess this heterogeneity by examining the types of participants and interventions for all outcomes in each study. We planned to evaluate studies more specifically to identify reporting of and heterogeneity for the following parameters: treatment‐dependent factors such as radiotherapy protocol and dose, surgical oncology interventions, and chemotherapy regimens, taking into account timing of each intervention. In addition, malignancy‐related factors, as well as patient‐dependent factors including measures for control of comorbidities, were to be assessed. Finally, we planned to include and analyse intervention‐specific subgroups for the different methods used as preventive interventions.  

    Sensitivity analysis

    Where data are available in future updates, we plan to develop sensitivity analyses based on the potential methodologies of target studies including: calculation of results excluding poorer quality studies deemed to be at higher risk of bias; and calculation of results excluding outlying results that were not consistent with a biological rationale or other studies on the subject. We also plan to examine the effect of including unpublished literature on the review's findings.

    Presentation of main results

    For this review, we created 'Summary of findings' tables for the main comparisons and primary and secondary outcomes. We used the five GRADE considerations (any limitations in the design of the studies, the directness (or applicability) of the evidence, the consistency of results, precision of the results, and the possibility of publication bias) to assess the certainty of the body of evidence from the included trials. We followed the methods and recommendations as described in Section 8.5 and Chapters 10 and 11 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions 5.1.0 (Higgins 2011) and used GRADEpro GDT software (GRADE 2015). The level of certainty for each of the comparisons was categorised as high, moderate, low, or very low. We used footnotes and comments in order to justify any decisions to upgrade or downgrade the level of certainty in the findings and aid the readers' understanding, as necessary, throughout the review.

    Results of the search

    A total of 514 original, non‐duplicate records were identified from the electronic searches, and 18 were found from additional sources. After title screening, 43 original, non‐duplicate abstracts were screened. 36 studies were discarded after examination of the titles and abstracts. Full‐text copies were obtained for the remaining seven records, which were then subjected to further assessment. Finally, four studies met the eligibility criteria for this review and were included (Batstone 2012; Horiot 1983; Marx 1985; Schoen 2007). We excluded two studies (Papas 2008; Toljanic 1996) and one is awaiting classification (Shaw 2019). The reference lists of these records were also screened in order to identify additional relevant studies, but none were found. See Figure 1 for the selection process.

    Included studies

    Characteristics of the trial designs and settings

    Except for the study by Batstone 2012, which was a spit‐mouth randomised trial, the remaining studies were parallel‐arm randomised controlled trials. All apart from Marx 1985 were single‐centre trials. The studies were conducted in Australia (Batstone 2012), France (Horiot 1983), USA (Marx 1985), and in the Netherlands (Schoen 2007). Batstone 2012 was reportedly funded by 3i Australia. The sources of funding for the remaining three studies were not specified.

    Characteristics of the participants

    The studies by Horiot 1983, Marx 1985, and Schoen 2007 randomised 220, 74, and 26 patients who had received radiation for the treatment of head and neck carcinoma, respectively. The details of the patients enrolled in the study by Horiot 1983 were not described in sufficient detail to permit detailed reporting. All patients enrolled by Marx 1985 had an indication for removal of one or more teeth in a segment of the mandible that had received a documented absorbed radiation dose of 6000 cGy or greater. Finally, all patients in the study by Schoen 2007 were patients seen following tumour surgery and scheduled to receive prosthodontic rehabilitation with implant‐retained lower dentures requiring implants in the anterior mandible.

    Conversely, Batstone 2012 randomised 22 adult head and neck cancer patients undergoing prophylactic dental extractions of healthy teeth prior to radiotherapy of the bilateral posterior mandibular teeth within the planned radiation, with the justification that posterior teeth have high incidences of caries post‐radiotherapy and therefore may be extracted prophylactically.

    Characteristics of the interventions and comparisons

    All four studies included in this review conducted unique sets of interventions and comparisons. Batstone 2012 was a split‐mouth trial, where one side was randomised to receive a control product (cellulose foam) in the extraction socket, and the other to receive platelet‐rich plasma (PRP), harvested using the 3i PCCS system in addition to cellulose foam. Patients were blinded as to the side receiving PRP. The surgical technique consisted of "atraumatic extraction, limited alveoloplasty, and primary closure" performed by a sole experienced operator. In Horiot 1983, one group received 1% sodium fluoride gel (450 parts per million (ppm)) in a custom‐made mouth guard following usual oral hygiene for a minimum of five minutes daily; the other group received high‐content fluoride toothpaste (1350 ppm) with instructions to brush for three minutes, twice per day following morning and evening meals. In Marx 1985, one group received 1 million units of aqueous penicillin G intravenously immediately prior to extraction surgery and 500 mg of phenoxymethyl penicillin four times daily for 10 days following surgery, while the other received no antibiotic, but instead underwent hyperbaric oxygen exposures encompassing 100% humidified oxygen breathing at 2.4 atmospheres absolute pressure for 90 minutes each session for 30 sessions (20 sessions prior to extraction and 10 sessions following tooth removal), conducted as once‐daily sessions, five or six days each week. Finally, in Schoen 2007, one group received perioperative prophylactic antibiotics (cefradine 1 g, three times daily for two weeks starting one day before implant surgery), while the other received perioperative prophylactic antibiotics combined with pre‐ and post‐surgery hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBO) (20 sessions of 100% oxygen at 2.5 atmospheres for four periods of 20 minutes, totaling 80 minutes per day, preceding implant surgery, and 10 sessions following implant surgery, in addition to the same antibiotic prophylaxis protocol).

    Characteristics of the outcomes

    Batstone 2012 and Marx 1985 evaluated the incidence of osteoradionecrosis (ORN) as a primary outcome while Horiot 1983 and Schoen 2007 evaluated ORN incidence as a secondary outcome. It should be noted that ORN was only defined in two of the four included studies (Batstone 2012; Marx 1985).

    In Batstone 2012, incidence of osteoradionecrosis (ORN) was the primary outcome measure, with wound pain reported as a secondary outcome (self‐reported Likert scale ranging from 1 to 10). For the purposes of this study, ORN was defined as exposed bone in a field of radiotherapy persisting for more than three months. Assessment therein was conducted by either of two experienced surgical trainees, who were blinded to the intervention. In Marx 1985, the primary outcome assessment was the binary "yes or no" clinical diagnosis of ORN, defined as the presence of exposed bone in the area of extraction persisting for greater than six months. Horiot 1983 assessed an intervention for the prevention of ORN with caries prevention as the primary outcome measure and ORN incidence as a secondary outcome, and the study by Schoen 2007, was designed to assess the treatment outcome of prosthodontic rehabilitation with implant‐retained lower dentures in an irradiated population with ORN reported as a secondary outcome measure related to the condition of peri‐implant tissues and implant survival. No definition of ORN was stated by Horiot 1983 and Schoen 2007. Further included studies details are described in the Characteristics of included studies tables.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded two clinical trials: Papas 2008 and Toljanic 1996, both of which did not evaluate the incidence of osteoradionecrosis in their patients.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Figure 2 presents a graph of the risk of bias depicting the review authors' judgements with regard to each risk of bias domain as percentages across all included studies. Figure 3 presents a summary of the review authors' judgements with regard to each risk of bias domain for each of the included studies. None of the four included trials met all the risk of bias criteria, thereby preventing any overall assessments that could be classified as low risk of bias. All the studies had one or more domains assessed to be at significant risk of bias, resulting in an overall high risk of bias designation for all four studies (Batstone 2012; Horiot 1983; Marx 1985; Schoen 2007). The high‐risk designations were mainly the result of lack of patient and operator blinding to the intervention.

    Allocation

    Random sequence generation

    Only the study by Schoen 2007 described the use of random sequence generation, stating that a computer program was used for this purpose. The remaining three studies made no mention of the use of any specific type of random sequence generation.

    Allocation concealment

    All four studies provided insufficient information with regard to allocation concealment and were assessed as at unclear risk of bias for this domain.

    Blinding

    Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    All four studies provided sufficient information to conclude that the blinding of personnel performing the interventions was not conducted. Except for the study by Batstone 2012, which blinded participants to intervention used, none of the remaining studies made efforts to blind participants of the study to interventions.

    Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    Batstone 2012 and Schoen 2007 made efforts to blind outcome assessors. Comparatively, Marx 1985 did not blind outcome assessors. The remaining study (Horiot 1983) did not provide sufficient information to determine if outcome assessors were blinded.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Three studies were deemed to be at low risk of attrition bias: Batstone 2012 reported a reduction of one subject for three of five time points, but neglected to specify which patients were affected or the reason they were not assessed at those time points; Marx 1985 did not report any attrition or loss to follow‐up in their study, with all 74 subjects completing a minimum six‐month follow‐up; and Schoen 2007 explicitly reported the number of patients assessed at one year and the reason for dropout. Finally, we assessed Horiot 1983 as at unclear risk of attrition bias, since it specified the number of patients entered and those evaluated, however, they failed to specify the reasons for dropout.

    Selective reporting

    Though no overt instances of selective reporting were identified, none of the four trials were registered prior to study implementation. As such, reporting bias cannot be fully ruled out and we assessed them as at unclear risk of reporting bias.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Given the split‐mouth design of the study by Batstone 2012, contamination between the two groups cannot be ruled out. No other potential sources of bias were identified in the remaining three studies.

    Significant clinical and methodological diversity and heterogeneity of the interventions and patient populations of the included studies precluded pooling of data and meaningful analysis of the subgroup parameters specified in the Methods section under Data collection and analysis. Of note, the results of the two studies evaluating the effects of hyperbaric oxygen therapy (HBO) were not combined because one study evaluated the incidence of ORN post‐dental extractions, while the other evaluated the incidence post‐placement of dental implants.

    Platelet‐rich plasma (PRP) versus no intervention/placebo

    Incidence of ORN

    Batstone 2012 found that there was no reduction in the incidence of ORN in the intervention arm. Of 22 patients (5 women and 17 men, mean age 54.5 years (range 30 to 68 years), mean radiation dose 65.7 Gray (range 60 Gray to 72.5 Gray), with mean time from extraction to radiotherapy of 34 days (range 11 to 72 days)), three developed ORN by the five‐year follow‐up (including one with bilateral disease) for an overall rate of 14%. ORN developed on three sides with PRP and one control side; however, the difference was not statistically significant (odds ratio (OR) 3.32, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.58 to 19.09, P = 0.18).

    Adverse effects

    Batstone 2012 reported that mean pain scores at two weeks, two, six, nine, and 12 months showed no significant differences between control and PRP sides as determined by use of a Likert scale (1 to 10). The difference in pain scores for the two sides were not statistically significantly different at the two‐week mark (P = 0.09, using a paired samples t test). No other data were presented on other measures of morbidity and mortality.

    Fluoride gel versus high‐content fluoride toothpaste

    Incidence of ORN

    Horiot 1983 reported no cases of ORN in either intervention arm, with follow‐up ranging from 14 to 36 months. The radiation doses for the included patients were not mentioned in the study. Of 190 patients who were evaluated in the study, 0 of 99 patients in the fluoride gel group developed ORN, and 0 of 91 patients in the high‐content fluoride toothpaste group developed ORN. Both groups had incidences of ORN that were lower than the 3.0% to 5.4% incidence expected for this patient population (Clayman 1997; Wahl 2006).

    Adverse effects

    No quantitative data were presented on measures of morbidity and mortality in the study by Horiot 1983.

    Hyperbaric oxygen therapy versus perioperative antibiotic prophylaxis

    Incidence of ORN

    Marx 1985 found that there was a reduction in the incidence of ORN in the intervention arm employing hyperbaric oxygen therapy compared to the intervention with perioperative antibiotic prophylaxis, with follow‐up at six months. The radiation doses for the included patients were not mentioned in the study. Of 74 patients, 13 developed ORN over the six‐month follow‐up period for an overall rate of 18%. ORN developed in 2 of 37 patients in the hyperbaric oxygen therapy group (5.4%), with 4 of a possible 156 non‐healing socket wounds (2.6%), and 11 of 37 patients in the penicillin group (29.7%), with 31 of a possible 135 non‐healing socket wounds (23.0%). The difference in ORN developed in patients from each intervention group was statistically significant (RR 0.18, 95% CI 0.43 to 0.76, P = 0.0123 using Fisher's exact test), as well as for the number of non‐healing extraction sites (P = 0.0001 using Fisher's exact test).

    Adverse effects

    No quantitative data were presented on measures of morbidity and mortality in the study by Marx 1985.

    Combined hyperbaric oxygen therapy and perioperative antibiotics versus antibiotics alone

    Incidence of ORN

    Schoen 2007 found that there was no reduction in the incidence of ORN in the intervention arm employing combined hyperbaric oxygen therapy and perioperative antibiotics as compared to antibiotics alone, with a follow‐up period of one year from the time of prosthesis delivery. Of 26 patients who underwent either intervention (9 women and 17 men, mean age 60.1 ± 7.5 years (range 47 to 77 years), mean radiation dose of 61.4 ± 12.9 Gray (range 46 Gray to 116 Gray)), one developed ORN over the one year follow‐up period. Of the total 26 patients who received either intervention, 4 of 13 patients in the combined hyperbaric oxygen therapy and perioperative antibiotic group were lost to one‐year follow‐up: one died due to tumour‐related disease, whereas three others died due to non‐tumour‐related disease; 4 of 13 patients in the perioperative antibiotic only group were lost to one‐year follow‐up: three died of tumour‐related disease, whereas one other died due to non‐tumour‐related disease. The lone case of ORN occurred in the combined hyperbaric oxygen therapy and perioperative antibiotic group who did not have an implant‐supported overdenture; in addition, two patients passed away during the osseointegration period due to medical complications not related to the implant surgery (intervention groups not specified). The difference in the incidence of ORN in the two intervention groups was not statistically significant (RR 3.00, 95% CI 0.14 to 65.16, P > 0.05).

    Adverse effects

    Schoen 2007 reported no serious adverse effects associated with hyperbaric oxygen therapy. Additionally, the authors reported the results of multiple quality of life questionnaires and clinical parameters with respect to oral status, functioning and other satisfaction measures at pre‐operative (T0), six weeks (T1), and 12 months (T2) time points following new denture placement. Though the authors found emotional functioning and pain to be significantly higher at T1 in the hyperbaric oxygen therapy group, there were no significant differences between the two groups at T2.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This Cochrane Review has attempted to identify non‐treatment‐dependent dental interventions that can reduce the occurrence of osteoradionecrosis (ORN). Due to the clinical and methodological diversity and heterogeneity of interventions and patient populations, and lack of reporting in the studies identified by our search, meta‐analysis was deemed not to be applicable.

    In the four included studies, three broad interventions were identified, one involving the use of platelet‐rich plasma placed in the extraction sockets of prophylactically removed healthy posterior mandibular teeth prior to radiotherapy (Batstone 2012), another involving the comparison of fluoride gel and high‐content fluoride dentifrice in preventing post‐radiation caries (Horiot 1983), and two comparing the use of perioperative hyperbaric oxygen (HBO) therapy to antibiotics, in the setting of post‐radiotherapy extractions (Marx 1985) and mandibular anterior implant placement (Schoen 2007), respectively.

    Based on a small number of patients, Batstone 2012 found that there was no reduction in ORN incidence in the intervention arm employing platelet‐rich plasma as compared to no intervention. No difference in either arm was found by Horiot 1983, as no cases of ORN were reported. Schoen 2007 found no difference between arms with only one case of ORN in the combined HBO and antibiotics arm. In one trial (Marx 1985), it was shown that treatment with HBO caused a significant reduction in the development of ORN in comparison to patients treated with antibiotics following dental extractions. However, it should once again be pointed out that this study was evaluated as having a high risk of bias. Additionally, adverse events and safety data were not reported clearly, but appeared not to be a factor in the studies in which dropout data were included or for which all participants completed the study. Therefore, while these results may appear promising, further high‐quality research is required to corroborate the results of this trial.

    It should be noted that the definition of ORN slightly differed between the included studies (with no definition outlined by Horiot 1983 and Schoen 2007). This in turn may have resulted in an increase of the incidence of ORN in those studies with a more liberal definition of ORN. However, all four studies were noted to follow patients for a minimum of six months.

    As a result of the small individual sample sizes and high overall risk of bias in all included studies, it was not possible to reach any definitive conclusions, and the certainty of the evidence was assessed as very low for all comparisons (summary of findings Table for the main comparison; summary of findings Table 2; summary of findings Table 3; summary of findings Table 4).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The occurrence of ORN is a rare though significant event, whose exact incidence has not been determined definitively; its risk factors also remain to be elucidated precisely. This Cochrane Review was based on randomised controlled trials (RCTs) of non‐treatment‐dependent interventions in the prevention of ORN. Studies of prevention are often more complex than studies of therapy, and so require significantly larger sample sizes, because preventive effects of intervention tend to be more subtle than well‐defined therapeutic effects with greater risk of confusing outcomes. In this respect, systematic reviews of disease prevention are of particular importance in the hope of overcoming type two error seen in individual studies, or the lack of effect secondary to insufficient sample size. The results of this systematic review are severely constrained due to lack of statistical power related to the small samples sizes of the studies that were included for assessment. More studies with greater statistical power or increased sample sizes and less pronounced clinical and methodological heterogeneity are required to enable meta‐analyses to confirm whether any of the identified interventions have a beneficial effect in preventing the occurrence of ORN.

    Quality of the evidence

    When considering the GRADE framework, the recommendations for use of all the interventions discussed in this review are very low. The certainty of the evidence is very low since reported data were often incomplete and clinical and methodological heterogeneity between studies precluded meta‐analysis. The sample size for each study was extremely limited (ranging from 9 to 99 individuals), reducing precision, and all four studies were determined to possess a high overall risk of bias, mainly due to a lack of blinding of participants and personnel, as well as incomplete reporting. Therefore, while the study by Marx 1985 appears to suggest some positive effects of HBO for the prevention of ORN, further high‐quality research is warranted.

    Potential biases in the review process

    An exhaustive literature search was conducted without language restrictions or date, and it is unlikely that any potentially relevant published studies were not included. The potential risk of bias in this review process is therefore minimal.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    To date, two systematic reviews evaluating the effectiveness of interventions for the prevention of ORN have been published. The first review by Nabil 2011 investigated the incidence and prevention of ORN after dental extraction in irradiated patients and found a total ORN incidence of 7%, which was reduced to 6% in conjunction with antibiotics, and 4% with prophylactic HBO therapy. They concluded, based on weak evidence, that prophylactic HBO therapy was effective in reducing the risk of developing ORN after post‐radiation extractions. These conclusions were drawn from a total of seven articles reporting on a combined 160 patients and 595 extractions. Indeed there was only one RCT (Marx 1985) that reported a clear and beneficial effect (i.e. a clinically and statistically significant reduction in the incidence of ORN) of HBO use as compared to the use of antibiotics without HBO following tooth extraction. An update and evaluation of any new studies and the current state of the literature in response to their call for more well‐designed RCTs was indicated, with a broader focus on the evidence for all non‐treatment‐dependent interventions in the prevention of ORN based on only the highest quality evidence.

    The second review, published by Peterson 2010, identified three studies that evaluated the use of HBO therapy to a comparison group in those requiring dental extractions, two retrospective cohort studies demonstrated a similar prevalence of ORN in HBO versus non‐HBO groups, while the lone prospective trial (Marx 1985), discussed as an historical comparison, showed a markedly elevated complication rate in the non‐HBO plus antibiotic versus HBO groups. These findings led the authors to conclude that only level III evidence supported the use of prophylactic HBO therapy prior to post‐radiotherapy dental extraction, and gave a C grade classification, which precluded any guideline recommendations. Limited prevention studies regarding hard tissue replacement in extraction sockets, postoperative antibiotics, and pre‐radiation extraction of impacted third molars were also reported; however, it was concluded that additional studies were required to evaluate these preventive options. A lone retrospective study examining the use of spacers in brachytherapy showed a marked decrease in the prevalence of ORN with the use of spacers (2.1% versus 40% with no spacer, P = 0.0004 upon univariate analysis). Peterson 2010 recognized the lack of appropriately designed RCTs, as well as a number of other factors as collective limitations in the field of ORN management.

    Both aforementioned systematic reviews, while varying in quality, appear to support this Cochrane Review’s findings of a lack of high‐quality experimental trials supporting the use of prophylactic HBO therapy, in addition to a continued dearth of well‐designed RCTs and few recent advances with respect to other interventions for the prevention of ORN of the jaws.
