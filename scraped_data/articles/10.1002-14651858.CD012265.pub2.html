Probiotics for the postoperative management of term neonates after gastrointestinal surgery

    • Background

    The intestinal microflora has an essential role in providing a barrier against colonisation of pathogens, facilitating important metabolic functions, stimulating the development of the immune system, and maintaining intestinal motility. Probiotics are live microorganisms that can be administered to supplement the gut flora. Neonates who have undergone gastrointestinal surgery are particularly susceptible to infectious complications in the postoperative period. This may be partly due to a disruption of the integrity of the gut and its intestinal microflora. There may be a role for probiotics in reducing the incidence of sepsis and improving intestinal motility, thus reducing morbidity and mortality and improving enteral feeding in neonates in the postoperative period.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the efficacy and safety of administering probiotics after gastrointestinal surgery for the postoperative management of neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation.

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, and trial registries in August 2023. We checked reference lists of included studies and relevant systematic reviews for additional studies.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that investigated the postoperative administration of oral probiotics versus placebo or no treatment in neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation who had one or more gastrointestinal surgical procedures. We applied no restrictions regarding the type or dosage of probiotics or the duration of treatment.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methods, and we used GRADE to assess the certainty of evidence.

    Main results

    We identified one RCT that recruited 61 neonates with a gestational age of 35 weeks or more. All infants were admitted to a neonatal intensive care unit and had surgery for gastrointestinal pathologies.

    There may be little or no difference in proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic) between infants who receive probiotics compared with those who receive placebo (odds ratio (OR) 0.64, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.16 to 2.55; 61 infants; low‐certainty evidence). Probiotics compared to placebo may have little or no effect on time to full enteral feeds (mean difference (MD) 0.63 days, 95% CI −4.02 to 5.28; 61 infants; low‐certainty evidence). There were no reported deaths prior to discharge from hospital in either study arm.

    Two weeks after supplementation, the infants who received probiotics had a substantially higher relative abundance of non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora (Bifidobacteriaceae) than those who received placebo (MD 38.22, 95% CI 28.40 to 48.04; 39 infants; low‐certainty evidence).

    Authors' conclusions

    This review provides low‐certainty evidence from one small RCT that probiotics compared to placebo have little or no effect on the risk of proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic) or time to full‐enteral feeds in neonates who have undergone gastrointestinal surgery.

    Probiotics may substantially increase the abundance of beneficial bacterial in the intestine of these neonates, but the clinical implications of this finding are unknown.

    There is a need for adequately powered RCTs to assess the role of probiotics in this population.

    We identified two ongoing studies. As neither reported the gestational age of prospective study participants, we are unsure if they will be eligible for inclusion in this review.

    • Benefits and harms of giving probiotics to newborns after digestive tract surgery

    Key message

    • Probiotics may have little or no effect on the risk of blood infection in infants born from 35 weeks of pregnancy who have undergone surgery of the digestive tract (food pipe, stomach, or bowel).
    • Probiotics may increase the proportion of 'good' bacteria in the bowel, but we do not know if this has any real benefit for the infant.

    What are probiotics?

    Probiotics are live bacteria that may offer health benefits when consumed. There is evidence that probiotics can prevent an inflammatory disease of the bowel (necrotising enterocolitis) and infection in premature babies.

    What are some of the risks of bowel surgery in babies?

    Surgery of the digestive tract increases the risk of infection, and infection can increase the length of hospital stay. Treatment with antibiotics (medicines that treat bacterial infection) and the stress of surgery can diminish the 'good' bacteria in the bowel.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if giving probiotics to babies born from 35 weeks of pregnancy who underwent stomach or bowel surgery could improve rates of infection, length of hospital stay, rates of death, feeding, and the proportion of 'good' bacteria in the stomach and bowel.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that randomly assigned babies to experimental treatment (probiotics) or a control, which could be placebo (a 'dummy' treatment that does not contain any medicine but looks or tastes the same as the medicine being tested) or no treatment.

    What did we find?

    We found one small study of 61 babies born from 35 weeks of pregnancy who had digestive tract surgery. Thirty babies received probiotics after the surgery, and 30 received a placebo.

    In the probiotics group, 13% of babies had a blood infection, compared with 19% in the placebo group. This difference is too small to be significant.

    On average, the babies in the probiotic group were receiving food into their stomach about 15 hours sooner than those in the placebo group. Again, this difference is not important.

    Among 39 infants who were tested, those in the probiotics group had considerably more 'good' bacteria in their bowel compared to the placebo group.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    We have little confidence in the evidence because it is based on a single study with few participants. The evidence is not strong enough to support giving probiotics routinely. Well‐designed and large studies are needed to determine the benefits and harms of giving probiotics to newborns after digestive tract surgery.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up to date to August 2023.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    This review provides low‐certainty evidence from one small randomised controlled trial (RCT) that the postoperative administration of probiotics compared to placebo may have little or no effect on risk of proven sepsis or time to full‐enteral feeding in neonates who have undergone gastrointestinal surgery.

    Probiotics may substantially increase the abundance of beneficial bacteria in the intestine of these neonates, but the clinical implications of this finding are unknown.

    We identified two ongoing studies that have not reported the gestational age of prospective study participants; we are unsure if these studies will be eligible for inclusion in future updates of this review.

    Implications for research

    There is a need for large RCTs to assess the safety and efficacy of probiotics in the postoperative management of term and near‐term neonates after gastrointestinal surgery.

    Future studies should be adequately powered to investigate the effects of any probiotic formulation (single‐ or multi‐species) that is approved for use in a neonatal population, compared with a placebo. Studies should include important short‐term outcomes such as mortality, sepsis, duration of hospital stay, time of full enteral feeding, and stool microflora. Moreover, the research plan should incorporate other core outcomes in neonatology ( Webbe 2020 ). Investigators should design parallel‐group RCTs (randomised either at the individual or cluster level) rather than cross‐over trials.

    Background

    The intestinal microflora plays an important role in human health by providing a barrier against colonisation of pathogens, facilitating important metabolic functions, stimulating immune system development, and maintaining intestinal motility ( Guarner 2001 ; Lundell 2011 ). A variety of dynamic host and environmental factors affect the development of intestinal microflora. Gastrointestinal surgery may alter this complex ecosystem. Lundell 2011 found that adults undergoing colorectal surgery who received probiotics had improved intestinal microbial population, significantly decreased incidence of further infectious complications, shorter postoperative hospital stay, and shorter period of antibiotic administration. One systematic review that included 34 randomised controlled trials (RCTs) found probiotics to be safe and effective nutritional adjuncts for reducing postoperative complications in adults who had elective abdominal surgery ( Chowdhury 2020 ). With this in mind, we considered it worthwhile to explore the potential benefits of probiotics in neonates undergoing gastrointestinal surgery.

    Description of the condition

    Neonates undergo surgical intervention for congenital defects of the abdominal wall and intestine, and less frequently for acquired conditions such as necrotising enterocolitis (NEC). Most of these conditions are either diagnosed in the antenatal period or shortly after birth. Managing these conditions requires variable duration of inpatient stay, drug therapies, and appropriate feeding strategies. Neonates undergoing gastrointestinal surgery are susceptible to bacterial sepsis. The infectious complications in the postoperative period may originate from the intestinal microflora; this hypothesis is known as the 'gut origin of sepsis' ( Assimakopoulos 2018 ; Guarner 2001 ; Nieuwenhuijzen 1996 ). Postoperative sepsis occurs due to disruption of the gut barrier from surgical trauma, increased intestinal permeability, gut microbial imbalance, and immunological compromise of the host ( Correia 2012 ).

    The foetal gut is sterile, and development of the intestinal microflora begins immediately and rapidly after birth ( Ezaki 2012 ; Hill 2017 ). The establishment of gut microflora is a complex process. Several extrinsic factors could influence the acquisition of the intestinal microflora; these include the bacterial load of the environment; the mode of delivery; medication; and host‐related factors such as anatomical development of the intestinal tract, birth weight, peristalsis, immune responses, microbial interaction, and mucosal receptor ( Aguilar‐Lopez 2021 ; Fanaro 2003 ). Systemic antibiotics are used in the postoperative period to prevent or manage infections. However, antibiotics have a significant impact on the intestinal colonisation pattern of the neonate ( Eck 2020 ; Fanaro 2003 ; Fouhy 2012 ).

    Delayed initiation of enteral feeding and variable lengths of management with total parenteral nutrition (TPN) are not uncommon in newborns undergoing surgery. A neonatal piglet model of TPN showed selective growth of mucolytic bacteria including Clostridium perfringen s ( Deplancke 2002 ). Parenteral nutrition and delayed oral feeding may be associated with delayed establishment of stable microflora and dysbiosis of microflora ( Cerdó 2022 ; Fanaro 2003 ).

    Description of the intervention

    Probiotics are live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a significant health benefit on the host ( FAO/WHO Working Group 2002 ; Nair 2013 ). Commonly used probiotics include strains of Lactobacillus , Bifidobacterium , Streptococcus salivarius , and Saccharomyces boulardii ( Schanler 2016 ). The main functions of probiotics are related to increasing intestinal motility, suppressing pathogenic microorganism activity, improving intestinal barrier function, and modulating the immune response ( Correia 2012 ; Ezaki 2012 ; Schanler 2016 ; Taft 2018 ). Different strains of probiotics may have different mechanisms of action and effectiveness ( Sherman 2009 ; van den Akker 2018 ).

    How the intervention might work

    The beneficial effects of probiotics could be due to the following factors ( Correia 2012 ; Jeppsson 2011 ; Nair 2013 ; Quigley 2006 ; Rolfe 2000Tan 2014 ).

    • Probiotics may antagonise the pathogenic bacteria by:

      • producing organic acids, hydrogen peroxide, bacteriocins, and defensin (from Paneth cells);

      • competing for substrate required for fermentation;

      • inhibiting adherence of enteric pathogens to mucosal surfaces;

      • enhancing secretion of mucus layer overlying the epithelial lining of the gut; and

      • obscuring receptor binding sites and directly binding to the pathogen.

    • Probiotics may modulate immune function by:

      • increasing activity of the natural killer cells;

      • inducing secretion of cytokine and immunoglobulin A; and

      • inhibiting nuclear factor‐κB activation, interferon‐γ, tumour necrosis factor‐α, interleukin (IL)‐12, and IL‐10.

    • Probiotics may promote barrier integrity by:

      • altering mucus and chloride secretion;

      • inhibiting pathogenic‐induced alteration of epithelial permeability; and

      • regulating enterocyte gene expression.

    Probiotics may reduce the incidence of sepsis and improve intestinal motility, thus reducing morbidity and mortality and improving enteral feeding in neonates in the postoperative period.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Probiotics may be beneficial in adults who undergo gastrointestinal surgery ( Chowdhury 2020 ; Correia 2012 ; Jeppsson 2011 ). Although studies have suggested that probiotics can reduce mortality and prevent NEC in preterm neonates ( Sharif 2020 ; Patel 2018 ; Sawh 2016 ), the role of probiotics administered after abdominal surgery in neonates born at term and near term is unclear. Areas of concern with probiotic use are the risk of sepsis from the probiotic strain itself and possible transfer of antimicrobial resistance from probiotic strains to more pathogenic bacteria in the intestinal microbiota ( Boyle 2006 ; Doron 2015 ).

    Objectives

    To evaluate the efficacy and safety of administering probiotics after gastrointestinal surgery for the postoperative management of neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included parallel‐group RCTs and quasi‐RCTs with individual or cluster allocation. Given the nature of the intervention, outcomes, and possibility of carryover effect, cross‐over trials were ineligible for inclusion in this review.

    Types of participants

    We included infants born from 35 weeks of gestation who had one or more gastrointestinal surgical procedures within 28 days of birth. Infants could be enroled at any time during the inpatient stay for the surgery. For the purpose of this review, we defined gastrointestinal surgery as any form of surgical intervention prescribed to treat a gastrointestinal pathology.

    Types of interventions

    Eligible studies evaluated oral administration of probiotics versus placebo or no intervention. We defined administration of probiotics as supplementation with any probiotic product, at any dosage and for any length of time, during the inpatient stay for gastrointestinal surgery.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic)

    • All‐cause neonatal mortality (within 28 days)

    • Time to full enteral feeds in days

    Secondary outcomes

    • Duration of hospital admission in days

    • All‐cause mortality prior to discharge from hospital

    • Pathogenic or non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora (as defined by the studies), assessed through faecal culture at any time during the study period

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The Cochrane Neonatal Information Specialist designed the search strategy with clinical input and from the review authors.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases without restrictions on language, publication year, publication type, or publication status.

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), via Wiley (Issue 8 of 12, July 2023; searched 15 August 2023)

    • Ovid MEDLINE (MEDALL; 1946–14 August 2023)

    • Embase, OVID (1974–14 August 2023)

    Searching other resources

    We searched the following clinical trials registries for ongoing or recently completed trials.

    • U.S. National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Registry ClinicalTrials.gov ( clinicaltrials.gov )

    • World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform ( trialsearch.who.int/ )

    We searched the reference lists of articles selected for inclusion in this review and any related systematic reviews to identify additional relevant articles.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    We entered search results into Covidence and removed duplicates. Two review authors (AT, KW) assessed the titles and abstracts of all remaining records and eliminated those that were clearly ineligible. We then retrieved the full‐text articles of all potentially eligible studies, and the same two review authors (AT, KW) independently assessed them against our Objectives and Criteria for considering studies for this review . We managed any disagreements by consensus.

    We recorded the reasons for exclusion of any studies that readers could plausibly assume met out eligibility criteria. We recorded the study selection process in sufficient detail to create a PRISMA flow diagram ( Moher 2009 ).

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (AT, ET) designed a data extraction sheet for study reports, and two review authors (AT, KW) used it to independently record the following features of each study.

    • Authors

    • Date and place of publication

    • Study design and number of study sites

    • Inclusion and exclusion criteria

    • Setting

    • Summary of participant characteristics

    • Summary of intervention and control conditions

    • Number of participants in each arm (including dropouts)

    • Adverse events

    • Outcome measurement and assessment time points

    • Data for assessing risk of bias ( Assessment of risk of bias in included studies )

    • Any relevant additional information reported by the study authors

    We planned to manage disagreements through discussion or by involving a third review author (WT) if necessary. Had we been unable to resolve disagreements in this way, we would have contacted the study author(s).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (AT, KW) independently assessed the risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) of all included trials using the Cochrane risk of bias tool RoB 1, which covers the following domains ( Higgins 2011 ).

    • Sequence generation (selection bias)

    • Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    • Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    • Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    • Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    • Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    • Any other bias

    We resolved any disagreements by discussion. Appendix 1 provides a more detailed description of the risk of bias assessment for each domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous data, we planned to present risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We planned to calculate the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs if the RD was statistically significant ( Higgins 2022 ).

    For continuous data, we calculated mean differences (MDs) when outcomes were measured in the same way between trials, and standardised mean differences (SMDs) to combine data from trials that measured the same outcome using different methods. We converted continuous data presented as medians and interquartile ranges (IQRs) to means and standard deviations (SDs) using the method suggested by Wan and colleagues ( Wan 2014 ).

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis in our review was the participating infant. However, if the unit of analysis was not the same as the unit of randomisation (e.g. in cluster‐randomised trials), we planned to adjust for clustering using the guidance given in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions ( Higgins 2022 ). If a study reported repeated observations (e.g. episodes of sepsis) in the same participant, we treated the data as dichotomous, counting each participant once.

    Dealing with missing data

    We intended to carry out analysis on an intention‐to‐treat (ITT) basis for all outcomes. We planned to contact the trial authors when we considered that missing data were 'not missing at random'. We planned to make explicit the assumptions of any methods used to deal with missing data.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We planned to explore clinical heterogeneity (participants, interventions, and outcomes) and methodological heterogeneity (study design, outcome measurement tools, and risk of bias) between the included trials. To explore statistical heterogeneity, we planned to visually inspect forest plots (direction and magnitude of effects and degree of overlap between CIs) and considered the Chi² statistic generated in forest plots (with significance set at P < 0.10). If we found heterogeneity, we planned to explore possible explanatory factors by conducting subgroup analyses. To quantify statistical heterogeneity, we planned to use the I² statistic, interpreting the values as follows ( Higgins 2022 ).

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important.

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity.

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity.

    • 75% to 100%: represents considerable heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Had we pooled more than 10 studies in a meta‐analysis, we would have created a funnel plot to inspect visually the risk of publication bias, whereby more pronounced asymmetry may indicate a substantial overestimation of the intervention effect ( Higgins 2022 ).

    Data synthesis

    We planned to synthesise the data using the fixed‐effect model where we assumed all effect estimates were estimating the same underlying intervention effect (true effect).We undertook cluster‐RCT analyses at the level of the individual while accounting for intracluster correlations in the data using methods recommended in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions ( Higgins 2022 ).

    We intended to identify clinical heterogeneity by examining variability in the participants, choice of the probiotic product, and outcomes of the studies included in the review.

    If studies used different scales for the same continuous outcome, we planned to calculate the SMD, assessing the impact of using the highest versus the lowest of the available SDs on the overall estimate of effect. If SDs were not reported, we planned to estimate the SD based on similar studies ( Higgins 2022 ).

    The study included in this review used medians for continuous variables because data were skewed. We used the formula proposed by Wan and colleagues to impute means and SDs from lower quartile, median, and upper quartile summary statistics ( Higgins 2022 ; Wan 2014 ).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Had we found sufficient data, we would have performed the following subgroup analyses of our primary outcomes.

    • Specific intestinal pathology (e.g. intestinal atresia, abdominal wall defect, or NEC)

    • Probiotic product (single microorganism or combination), given the strain‐specific effects

    • Type of feeding (breast milk versus formula)

    • Number of courses of antibiotics

    Sensitivity analysis

    If sufficient data were available, we planned to perform the following sensitivity analyses.

    • To determine the impact of risk of bias on the overall effect estimate, we planned to remove studies at high overall risk of bias from the analysis and compare the results ( Higgins 2022 ).

    • To determine the impact of heterogeneity on the overall estimate of effect, we planned to remove studies that contributed most to between‐study heterogeneity from the analyses and compare the results ( Higgins 2022 )

    This is an early‐stage review with one included trial that enroled neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation ( Rao 2022 ). In future updates, if we identify trials that enroled only term infants (born from 37 weeks' gestation), we will perform a sensitivity analysis by excluding Rao 2022 from the overall analysis.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook , to assess the certainty of evidence for the following (clinically relevant) outcomes ( Schünemann 2013 ).

    1. Proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic)

    2. All‐cause neonatal mortality (within 28 days)

    3. Time to full enteral feeds in days

    4. All‐cause mortality prior to discharge from hospital

    5. Pathogenic or non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora

    Two review authors (KW and AT) independently assessed the certainty of the evidence. We considered evidence from RCTs as high certainty to begin with, downgrading by one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based on design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of the evidence, precision of estimates, and presence of publication bias.

    We reported the certainty of the evidence in a summary of findings table created using GRADEpro GDT .

    The GRADE approach assigns one of the following four ratings to each body of evidence assessed.

    • High certainty: we are very confident that the true effect lies close to that of the estimate of the effect.

    • Moderate certainty: we are moderately confident in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be close to the estimate of the effect, but there is a possibility that it is substantially different.

    • Low certainty: our confidence in the effect estimate is limited; the true effect may be substantially different from the estimate of the effect.

    • Very low certainty: we have very little confidence in the effect estimate; the true effect is likely to be substantially different from the estimate of effect.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    Our searches of databases and other sources returned 472 records. After removing 97 duplicates, we screened 375 records and eliminated 369 that were clearly irrelevant. We reviewed six studies (either as full‐text articles or trial registration records). We included one study, excluded three studies, and identified two ongoing studies (with three references; JPRN‐UMIN000033805 ; Mugarab‐Samedi 2017 ). Figure 1 illustrates the study selection process in a PRISMA flow diagram.

    Included studies

    We included one study that enroled 61 neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation ( Rao 2022 ). The study aimed to evaluate gut dysbiosis in neonates with congenital gastrointestinal surgical conditions. The infants were randomised to receive either a triple‐strain bifidobacterial probiotic (30 infants) or a placebo (31 infants). The median gestational age was 38 weeks. Stool microbiota was analysed before supplementation, then one and two weeks following supplementation and again prior to discharge.

    Morinaga Milk Industry Co., Ltd, Japan, provided the trial supplements free of cost. Neonatal Research and Education, Neonatal Directorate, King Edward Memorial Hospital for Women, Western Australia provided funds for this study.

    The Characteristics of included studies table provides further details.

    Excluded studies

    Of the four studies excluded during full‐text review, readers could reasonably assume that the following three were eligible.

    • El‐Sawaf 2012 was not a neonatal study.

    • Murakami 2016 included 13 infants, of whom nine required surgery within three days of birth. Four of the surgical cases were randomly assigned to receive probiotics, and the other five were controls. Administration of probiotics did not lead to prevention of infection. Based on the results of the intestinal microflora, the study authors reported that surgical stress has a considerable effect on the intestinal microbiota. We excluded this study because one infant was born before 35 weeks.

    • Powell 2016 included 24 infants with gastroschisis who were born from 34 weeks' gestation. Administration of probiotics partially attenuated the participants' significant dysbiosis.

    Allocation

    Rao 2022 performed group assignment using a computer‐generated randomisation sequence in randomly ordered block sizes of two and four. The investigators ensured allocation concealment by using sequentially numbered individual boxes, each containing 30 identical sachets of trial supplements. We judged the study at low risk of selection bias.

    Blinding

    Investigators, clinical and non‐clinical outcome assessors, nursing staff, and parents were blinded to the allocation status of enroled neonates. We judged Rao 2022 at low risk of performance and detection bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    As stool samples were available for 39/61 infants and were available for analysis at two weeks after supplementation, we judged Rao 2022 at high risk of attrition bias.

    Selective reporting

    All outcomes prespecified in the protocol were reported in the publication.

    Other potential sources of bias

    We judged Rao 2022 at unclear risk of other bias: there was a higher rate of caesarean delivery in the placebo group, but a post‐hoc subgroup analysis according to mode of delivery showed a significantly higher abundance of Bifidobacteriaceae in the probiotic group, irrespective of the mode of delivery.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Summary of findings table ‐ Probiotics compared to placebo for neonates born from 35 weeks of gestation who have undergone gastrointestinal surgery

    Probiotics compared to placebo

    Primary outcomes
    Proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic)

    Probiotics may have little or no effect on risk of proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic) compared to placebo (OR 0.64, 95% CI 0.16 to 2.55; 61 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1 ; Figure 4 ).

    All‐cause neonatal mortality (within 28 days)

    Rao 2022 did not report 28‐day mortality.

    Time to full enteral feeds in days

    Probiotics compared to placebo may have little or no effect on time to full enteral feeds in days (mean 14.75 days in probiotics group versus mean 14.12 days in placebo group; MD 0.63 days, CI −4.02 to 5.28; 61 participants, low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2 ; Figure 5 ).

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 5
    Secondary outcomes
    Duration of hospital admission

    Probiotics compared to placebo may have little or no effect on duration of hospital admission (mean 34.12 days in probiotics group versus mean 25.62 days in placebo group; MD 8.5 days, 95% CI −0.03 to 17.03; 61 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    All‐cause mortality prior to discharge from hospital

    No infants in Rao 2022 died prior to discharge.

    Pathogenic or non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora

    Probiotics compared to placebo may substantially increase the abundance of beneficial non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora (Bifidobacteriaceae) two weeks after supplementation (mean 38.9 in probiotic group versus mean 0.71 in placebo group; MD 38.22, 95% CI 28.40 to 48.04; 39 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4 ).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Evidence from one small RCT with 61 participants contributing data to primary outcomes of this review showed that probiotics administered to term and near‐term neonates after gastrointestinal surgery may have little or no effect on risk of proven sepsis (positive bacterial culture, local or systemic) and time to full enteral feeds in days compared to placebo ( Rao 2022 ).

    Rao 2022 did not report 28‐day all‐cause mortality, but no infants died during their stay in the neonatal intensive care unit. Probiotics may have little or no effect on duration of hospital stay. The abundance of non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora was much higher among neonates who received probiotics; however, the clinical benefit of increased Bifidobacteriaceae abundance is unknown in this population.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    This is an early stage Cochrane Review that includes only one small RCT ( Rao 2022 ). The evidence to address objectives of this review is limited given the small sample size. These results are not applicable to routine practice and should be interpreted with caution.

    Quality of the evidence

    We considered Rao 2022 at high risk of attrition bias and unclear risk of other bias because there were higher rates of caesarean delivery in the placebo group. Because the study included only 61 infants, the effect estimates for continuous variables have low precision (time to full enteral feeds in days, duration of hospital stay, and pathogenic or non‐pathogenic intestinal microflora). Imprecision was also an issue for outcomes with few events (proven sepsis). As a result, we downgraded the certainty of evidence for all outcomes by two levels for very serious imprecision ( summary of findings Table 1 ).

    Potential biases in the review process

    We used the standard methods of Cochrane Neonatal to conduct this systematic review. We applied no language restrictions, and we searched an appropriate range of sources to identify published and ongoing studies.

    We believe that the protocol for this review was implemented appropriately, and most differences between the protocol and the review were not a source of bias ( Differences between protocol and review ). However, the post‐hoc change to include infants born after 35 weeks of gestation (rather than 37 weeks of gestation) could be a potential source of bias. During the title and abstract screening phase, there were no marginal decisions around exclusion of studies.

    Though only one study was included in this review, we believe that our search strategy included all relevant reports. We formally assessed and reported risk of bias.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We identified one previous systematic review on the role of probiotics in neonates with gastrointestinal surgical conditions ( Rao 2018 ); it included two small RCTs that were ineligible for inclusion in this review. Rao 2018 found limited evidence and recommended adequately powered RCTs to address this issue. Similarly, we found that the evidence to routinely recommend probiotics in neonates with gastrointestinal surgical pathologies is limited.
