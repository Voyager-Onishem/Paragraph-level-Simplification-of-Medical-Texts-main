Surgical or radiological treatment for varicoceles in subfertile men

    • Background

    Varicoceles are associated with male subfertility; however, the mechanisms by which varicoceles affect fertility have yet to be satisfactorily explained. Several treatment options exist, including surgical or radiological treatment, however the safest and most efficient treatment remains unclear. 

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness and safety of surgical and radiological treatment of varicoceles on live birth rate, adverse events, pregnancy rate, varicocele recurrence, and quality of life amongst couples where the adult male has a varicocele, and the female partner of childbearing age has no fertility problems.

    Search methods

    We searched the following databases on 4 April 2020: the Cochrane Gynaecology and Fertility Group Specialised Register, CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, PsycINFO, and CINAHL. We also searched the trial registries and reference lists of articles.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) if they were relevant to the clinical question posed and compared different forms of surgical ligation, different forms of radiological treatments, surgical treatment compared to radiological treatment, or one of these aforementioned treatment forms compared to non‐surgical methods, delayed treatment, or no treatment. We extracted data if the studies reported on live birth, adverse events, pregnancy, varicocele recurrence, and quality of life.

    Data collection and analysis

    Screening of abstracts and full‐text publications, alongside data extraction and 'Risk of bias' assessment, were done dually using the Covidence software. When we had sufficient data, we calculated random‐effects (Mantel‐Haenszel) meta‐analyses; otherwise, we reported results narratively. We used the I2 statistic to analyse statistical heterogeneity. We planned to use funnel plots to assess publication bias in meta‐analyses with at least 10 included studies. We dually rated the risk of bias of studies using the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool, and the certainty of evidence for each outcome using the GRADE approach.

    Main results

    We identified 1897 citations after de‐duplicating the search results. We excluded 1773 during title and abstract screening. From the 113 new full texts assessed in addition to the 10 studies (11 references) included in the previous version of this review, we included 38 new studies, resulting in a total of 48 studies (59 references) in the review providing data for 5384 participants. Two studies (three references) are ongoing studies and two studies are awaiting classification.

    Treatment versus non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment

    Two studies comparing surgical or radiological treatment versus no treatment reported on live birth with differing directions of effect. As a result, we are uncertain whether surgical or radiological treatment improves live birth rates when compared to no treatment (risk ratio (RR) 2.27, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.19 to 26.93; 2 RCTs, N = 204; I2 = 74%, very low‐certainty evidence). Treatment may improve pregnancy rates compared to delayed or no treatment (RR 1.55, 95% CI 1.06 to 2.26; 13 RCTs, N = 1193; I2 = 65%, low‐certainty evidence). This suggests that couples with no or delayed treatment have a 21% chance of pregnancy, whilst the pregnancy rate after surgical or radiological treatment is between 22% and 48%. We identified no evidence on adverse events, varicocele recurrence, or quality of life for this comparison.

    Surgical versus radiological treatment

    We are uncertain about the effect of surgical versus radiological treatment on live birth and on the following adverse events: hydrocele formation, pain, epididymitis, haematoma, and suture granuloma. We are uncertain about the effect of surgical versus radiological treatment on pregnancy rate (RR 1.13, 95% CI 0.75 to 1.70; 5 RCTs, N = 456, low‐certainty evidence) and varicocele recurrence (RR 1.31, 95% CI 0.82 to 2.08; 3 RCTs, N = 380, low‐certainty evidence). We identified no evidence on quality of life for this comparison.

    Surgery versus other surgical treatment

    We identified 19 studies comparing microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment to any other surgical treatment. Microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment probably improves pregnancy rates slightly compared to other surgical treatments (RR 1.18, 95% CI 1.02 to 1.36; 12 RCTs, N = 1473, moderate‐certainty evidence). This suggests that couples with microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment have a 10% to 14% chance of pregnancy after treatment, whilst the pregnancy rate in couples after other surgical treatments is 10%. This procedure also probably reduces the risk of varicocele recurrence (RR 0.48, 95% CI 0.29, 0.79; 14 RCTs, N = 1565, moderate‐certainty evidence). This suggests that 0.4% to 1.1% of men undergoing microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment experience recurrent varicocele, whilst 1.4% of men undergoing other surgical treatments do. Results for the following adverse events were inconclusive: hydrocele formation, haematoma, abdominal distension, testicular atrophy, wound infection, scrotal pain, and oedema. We identified no evidence on live birth or quality of life for this comparison.

    Nine studies compared open inguinal surgical treatment to retroperitoneal surgical treatment. Due to small sample sizes and methodological limitations, we identified neither treatment type as superior or inferior to the other regarding adverse events, pregnancy rates, or varicocele recurrence. We identified no evidence on live birth or quality of life for this comparison.

    Radiological versus other radiological treatment

    One study compared two types of radiological treatment (sclerotherapy versus embolisation) and reported 13% varicocele recurrence in both groups. Due to the broad confidence interval, no valid conclusion could be drawn (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.16 to 6.20; 1 RCT, N = 30, very low‐certainty evidence). We identified no evidence on live birth, adverse events, pregnancy, or quality of life for this comparison.

    Authors' conclusions

    Based on the limited evidence, it remains uncertain whether any treatment (surgical or radiological) compared to no treatment in subfertile men may be of benefit on live birth rates; however, treatment may improve the chances for pregnancy. The evidence was also insufficient to determine whether surgical treatment was superior to radiological treatment. However, microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment probably improves pregnancy rates and reduces the risk of varicocele recurrence compared to other surgical treatments. High‐quality, head‐to‐head comparative RCTs focusing on live birth rate and also assessing adverse events and quality of life are warranted.

    • Surgery or radiological treatment for varicoceles in subfertile men

    Background

    A varicocele is a dilatation (enlargement) of the veins along the spermatic cord (the cord suspending the testis) in the scrotum. Dilatation occurs when valves within the veins along the spermatic cord fail and allow retrograde blood flow, causing a backup of blood. Surgical treatment involves the closing off of the vessels, typically with sutures or clips. Radiological treatment involves both embolisation, in which the vessel is blocked with small particles, or sclerotherapy, where an agent is administered that damages the vessels, causing them to shrink. The mechanisms by which varicoceles might affect fertility, or the mechanisms by which surgical or radiological treatment of varicoceles might restore fertility, have not yet been explained.

    Review question

    We reviewed the evidence for the effect of varicocele treatment on live birth, adverse events, pregnancy rate, varicocele recurrence, and quality of life in subfertile couples where the male has a varicocele, and the female partner of childbearing age has no fertility problems.

    Study characteristics

    We found 48 randomised controlled trials (a type of study in which people are assigned to one of two or more treatment groups using a random method) comparing treatment versus no treatment or versus a different treatment method in a total of 5384 men. The evidence is current to April 2020.

    Key results

    We are uncertain whether surgical or radiological treatment improves live birth rates when compared to no treatment. Treatment may improve pregnancy rates compared to delayed or no treatment. The evidence suggests that couples with no or delayed treatment have a 21% chance of pregnancy, whilst the pregnancy rate after surgical or radiological treatment is between 22% and 48%. Data were lacking on adverse events, varicocele recurrence, and quality of life.

    We are uncertain about the effect of surgical versus radiological treatment on live birth, pregnancy rate, varicocele recurrence, and the adverse event hydrocele formation. Data were lacking on quality of life for this comparison.

    Microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment probably improves pregnancy rates slightly compared to other surgical treatments. This suggests that couples with microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment have a 10% to 14% chance of pregnancy after treatment, whilst the pregnancy rate in couples after other surgical treatments is 10%. This procedure also probably reduces the risk of varicocele recurrence. This suggests that 0.4% to 1.1% of men undergoing microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment experience recurrent varicocele, whilst 1.4% of men undergoing other surgical treatments do. Results on adverse events were inconclusive. Data were lacking on live birth and quality of life.

    We are uncertain about the effects of open inguinal surgical treatment versus retroperitoneal surgical treatment on adverse events, pregnancy rates, or varicocele recurrence. Data were lacking on live birth and quality of life.

    We are uncertain about the effects of radiological treatment (sclerotherapy versus embolisation) on varicocele recurrence. Data were lacking on live birth, adverse events, pregnancy, and quality of life.

    Certainty of the evidence

    Our findings were inconclusive, as the certainty of the available evidence ranged from moderate to very low depending on outcome. More research is needed with live birth or pregnancy rate as the primary outcome.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Varicocele treatment, when compared to non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment, showed a beneficial effect on pregnancy rate amongst 1193 men with mixed semen analysis. Nine men would need to be treated for one to benefit.

    Microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment, when compared to other surgical treatment, showed a beneficial effect on pregnancy rate amongst 1473 men. Ten men would need to be treated for one to benefit. The same comparison showed a beneficial effect on varicocele recurrence rate amongst 1565 men. Fifteen men would need to be treated for one to benefit.

    The direct comparison of other treatment methods did not permit us to draw any valid conclusions about the superiority or inferiority of one intervention over another on pregnancy, varicocele recurrence, or adverse events. How any of these interventions impact quality of life is unclear, as this outcome was not measured in any study.

    Implications for research

    The ideal trial design for the 'treatment versus non‐surgical, delayed, or no treatment' comparison would be to compare a group of men undergoing the treatment procedure with the other group obtaining a sham surgical procedure. Any other design is potentially biased by the placebo effect of having had the operation performed. However, such a trial design would be difficult to obtain ethical committee approval, as it would put the control group at risk of surgical and anaesthetic complications without any possibility of benefit (Hargreave 1997). As all studies included in the current review mostly considered invasive treatment, blinding of the investigator, participant, or assessor to the procedure performed was rarely performed. However, the outcome measures of this review, live birth and pregnancy rate, were unambiguous. As a result, apart from the placebo effect, no other negative factors of the lack of blinding were presumed to have affected the conclusions drawn.

    Although a trial is the most optimal design to assess the efficacy of the 'treatment versus treatment' comparison, potential bias cannot be ruled out, particularly as the the studies were not blinded. However, as blinding was difficult due to the nature of the invasive treatments performed and as lack of blinding was unlikely to influence the outcomes assessed, all studies were graded at a low risk of bias for blinding. Nevertheless, studies that could implement blinding and mitigate potential bias would be ideal.

    The studies included in the current review of varicocele treatment were heterogeneous. This indicates the need for a large, properly conducted randomised controlled trial of varicocele treatment in men with varicocele and sperm defects, from couples with otherwise unexplained subfertility. However, we realise that it will become increasingly difficult to conduct such a study, as the introduction of in vitro fertilisation (IVF) and intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI) in the fertility clinic will make many men reluctant to take the risk of being allocated to the no‐treatment arm of such a study, whilst a robust treatment of proven effectiveness is readily available in the form of IVF/ICSI. The issue will be further compounded by the fact that as many couples today tend to delay their first pregnancy, they are likely to feel pressed for time to conceive once they have decided in their mid‐ to late‐thirties to seek professional help for their fertility issues. Nevertheless, in countries or couples where IVF/ICSI is not a feasible option, varicocele treatment will continue to be performed and contribute to clinical knowledge and research.

    In the future, authors should strive to report adverse events alongside outcomes, as this was poorly done in the studies assessed and has large implications on the overall success and practicality of the treatment of varicoceles.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    A varicocele results from the dilation and distention of the internal spermatic vein and pampiniform venous plexus within the spermatic cord in the scrotum. Most varicoceles are left‐sided due to the anatomy of the testicular drainage system, accounting for 85% to 90% of cases, with only 1% being right‐sided, and up to 30% being bilateral (Chiba 2016a; Jarow 2001; Skoog 1997; WHO 1991).

    Clinical diagnosis is accomplished primarily through physical examination in either adolescents presenting for a routine medical check‐up or adults evaluating their fertility status. Men are visually examined in both supine and standing positions, physically palpated, and perform a Valsalva manoeuvre, which involves attempting exhalation against a closed airway, that further distinguishes the dilation of the pampiniform plexus. Ultrasonography is also used as a tool to diagnose subclinical varicocele and to assess any discrepancies in testicular volume between the left and right testicle which could call for intervention (Chiba 2016a).

    Adolescent and adult varicoceles are graded following the World Health Organization (WHO) scale: WHO 0: only demonstrable by technical diagnostic methods; WHO I: only palpable/visible during Valsalva manoeuvre; WHO II: palpable when in upright position at room temperature; WHO III: visible when in upright position at room temperature (WHO 1991).

    The mechanism through which varicoceles impair male fertility remains uncertain. However, impaired blood drainage from the testis leading to increased venous pressure, oxidative stress, imbalances of testosterone, and an elevated testicular temperature in the seminiferous tubules have been proposed to have deleterious effects on spermatogenesis, semen quality, hormone production by Leydig cells, and the function of Sertoli cells (Chiba 2016a; Chiba 2016b; Ding 2012; Duran 2002; Jarow 2001; Kupis 2015; Lewis 2010; Skoog 1997; WHO 1991). Variation in testicular temperature is considered to have the most negative impact on spermatogenesis, which, in combination with oxidative stress and increased levels of reactive oxygen species, can induce apoptosis in spermatozoa. A genetic link may contribute to these aforementioned factors, as decreased levels of heat shock proteins, polymorphisms in the glutathione S transferase and nitric oxide synthase genes, and non‐physiological alterations in the genes and proteins of BCL2 associated X, apoptosis regulator (BAX ) and BCL2 apoptosis regulator (BCL2), have been associated with varicocele patients (Hassanin 2018). However, despite these findings, no theory can explain the exact effect of varicoceles on infertility, highlighting the complexity of the condition and the importance of assessing couple fertility status, varicocele grade, and semen quality on a case‐by‐case basis when selecting an appropriate varicocele treatment method that will best impact fertility (Jensen 2017).

    Many physicians accept occlusion of the spermatic vein as the treatment of choice for this condition. Although many techniques exist, due to the lower complication rates, microsurgical varicocelectomy, and particularly microscopic subinguinal varicocelectomy, has been proposed as the gold‐standard technique (Masterson 2019; Nieschlag 1993; Tatem 2017). However, evidence explaining the mechanisms by which varicocelectomy might restore fertility is lacking. Furthermore, the existence of a causal relationship between the specific distension of the pampiniform plexus (a network of many small veins found in the human male spermatic cord) and fertility impairment has been questioned.

    The treatment of varicoceles with the aim of increasing male fertility has been performed since 1952. Various techniques were developed in the following decades, and varicocele treatment has since been recognised as a means of helping treat male infertility (Marte 2018). In 1978 and 1992, assisted reproductive technologies (ARTs), namely in vitro fertilisation (IVF) and intracytoplasmic sperm injection (ICSI), were successfully introduced as additional measures to potentially increase fertility in subfertile couples. IVF may be undergone with additional hormonal ovarian stimulation and involves the fertilisation of ova by spermatozoa in culture media and later implantation of zygotes into the uterus. ICSI is an additional technique where, instead of thousands of spermatozoa being left to fertilise the ova, one single spermatozoon is directly injected into the ovum to ensure fertilisation (CDC 2017). Due to the direct path to pregnancy that IVF and ICSI offer subfertile couples, these methods have been more intensely pursued and researched in the last few decades. Although this has resulted in a lack of recent evidence on the treatment of varicoceles for subfertility, the disadvantages associated with ARTs, particularly the high costs, higher maternal and foetal complications, multiple‐gestation pregnancies, ovarian hyperstimulation, and increased birth defects, are notable reasons for why varicocele treatment remains a comparable treatment method (Pandey 2012; Pathak 2016; Revelli 2011).

    Furthermore, despite male infertility being linked to an increased risk of cancer and other health problems, the overall health of these men is typically disregarded in an attempt to create a viable pregnancy (Eisenberg 2015a; Eisenberg 2015b; Kolettis 2001; Walsh 2009; Walsh 2010). This is also demonstrated in major increases in rates of ICSI despite the availability of specific therapies, such as varicocelectomy, to treat the male aspect of infertility (Boulet 2015). ICSI pregnancies have also been reported to have a 1.5‐ to 4‐fold increase in genetic disorders, intellectual disabilities, and birth defects compared to IVF pregnancies (Amor 2008; Bonduelle 2002; Davies 2012; Gjerris 2008; Sandin 2013; Zhu 2006). Varicocelectomy thus provides an option to treat both the underlying male subfertility and associated health issues whilst aiming to produce a viable pregnancy and healthy baby.

    Due to the disadvantages, health risks, and costs associated with ARTs, the potential ability of varicocele treatment to both improve male infertility and underlying health problems and increase spontaneous pregnancies without the use of ARTs poses considerable benefits whilst addressing the same goals (Pathak 2016). Varicocele treatment thus presents a treatment option for couples who are unwilling or unable to undergo ARTs or who wish to undergo them whilst addressing the male health aspect.

    Emerging studies have also begun combining ARTs with varicocele repair, which may further increase pregnancy rates alongside improving male health. This review does not consider the use of ARTs with varicocele treatment, as these methods have different indications. ARTs present an option for treating subfertility mainly in the female partner, apart from the combination treatment with ICSI for the male partner, whilst varicocele treatment is an option if the male alone is subfertile. Nevertheless, studies combining ARTs and varicocele treatment, and studies comparing these methods directly, would be of interest for future research.

    Description of the intervention

    Surgical treatment

    Varicocelectomy refers to the surgical repair of a clinical varicocele which is palpable during physical examination. Several surgical techniques described and reviewed in the literature are used in clinical practice for the correction of varicoceles (Cassidy 2012; Choi 2013; Kupis 2015).

    Subinguinal varicocelectomy involves a 2‐centimetre traverse incision by the external inguinal ring to approach and deliver the spermatic cord and testis. As this surgical treatment generally uses a microscope, it is often referred to as microscopic subinguinal varicocelectomy. It is viewed as a highly difficult procedure, as there is a higher risk of damaging the arteries; nevertheless, it exposes the spermatic cord without requiring the dissection of abdominal muscles or fascia, resulting in fewer postoperative complications with lower hydrocele formation and reduced varicocele recurrences, thus rendering it the gold standard in this regard (Binsaleh 2007; Hou 2015; Tatem 2017). As microscopic subinguinal varicocelectomy is currently considered the gold standard and was performed in many surgical comparison studies, we chose it to be the main focus of a comparison due to its relevancy to clinical practice.

    Retroperitoneal varicocelectomy, also referred to as suprainguinal, highest entry point, Palomo, high ligation, or abdominal varicocelectomy, refers to an open varicocelectomy procedure and involves a medial inferior incision to the ipsilateral anterior superior iliac spine through the external and internal oblique fascia, after which the vein in question is ligated. This technique does not allow for the saving of the lymphatics, resulting in a high rate of postoperative hydrocele formation (Chan 2011). Further, the testicular artery and its periarterial network of fine veins may be ligated, leading to a lower recurrence rate, but a destruction of the testicular artery as well (Kass 1992). Laparoscopic varicocelectomy is a subsection of retroperitoneal varicocelectomy, however involves laparoscopic cameras for a more precise dissection of arteries and internal spermatic veins (Chiba 2016a).

    Inguinal varicocelectomy involves an incision over the inguinal canal, allowing for elevation of the spermatic cord and vessels for dissection and ligation with sutures or clips. The procedure can be performed conventionally open with or without a microscope. In conventional non‐magnified open inguinal varicocelectomy, also referred to as the Ivanissevich technique, the internal spermatic veins are ligated, which results in a lower rate of varicocele recurrence compared to the retroperitoneal technique (Tatem 2017). With the microsurgical technique, which involves the use of a microscope, the lymphatics can be spared, and the incidence of postoperative hydrocele formation is significantly reduced compared to the retroperitoneal or conventional technique.

    Radiological treatment

    Radiological interventions are also performed to treat varicoceles. These comprise varicocele embolisation, which is a frequently used technique that involves the blocking of one or more blood vessels or abnormal vascular channels using either a coil or a balloon, and may include the selective catheterisation and embolisation of the gonadal veins (Cassidy 2012). Depending on the location of the varicocele, different types of embolisation may be performed, including bilateral embolisation, unilateral left‐sided embolisation, and unilateral right‐sided embolisation (Cassidy 2012). Another radiological intervention is sclerotherapy, in which the spermatic vein is ligated after administration of a sclerosing agent, which causes damage to the endothelial cells of the intima of the vessels, causing them to shrink (Tauber 2006).

    The following two radiological treatment categories comprised the radiological treatment group: 

    1. Antegrade and retrograde embolisation

    2. Antegrade and retrograde sclerotherapy

    For the comparison of any surgical or radiological treatment with a control intervention, we included non‐surgical methods (treatment with counselling and with clomiphene citrate), delayed treatment, and no treatment as control methods.

    How the intervention might work

    The mechanism through which varicoceles impair male fertility remains uncertain. However, impaired blood drainage from the testis leading to increased venous pressure, oxidative stress, imbalances of testosterone, and an elevated testicular temperature in the seminiferous tubules have been proposed to have deleterious effects on spermatogenesis, semen quality, hormone production by Leydig cells, and the function of Sertoli cells. (Chiba 2016a; Chiba 2016b; Ding 2012; Duran 2002; Jarow 2001; Kupis 2015; Lewis 2010; Skoog 1997; WHO 1991). Through either the ligation of or blocking off the dilated veins, the aforementioned techniques strive to reverse these negative effects back to their physiological norm with the aim of restoring fertility. Varicocele treatment has thus been investigated and performed for several decades as a means of potentially improving fertility.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Varicoceles are present in 15% of the normal male population and up to 40% of men with infertility. Varicoceles are now recognised as the single most surgically correctable cause of male infertility (Ding 2012). There are a variety of surgical and radiological techniques with different advantages and disadvantages for the correction of clinical varicoceles. To date, there have been numerous recommendations for the ‘gold standard’ approach for correcting clinically diagnosed varicoceles amongst infertile males. From a clinical perspective, it is essential to establish which of these surgical or radiological methods yield the best results in terms of improving fertility outcomes, such as live birth rate and pregnancy, amongst couples unable to conceive due to male factor infertility.

    Conclusions regarding the true effect of varicocele treatment on live birth and pregnancy can only be derived from prospective studies with an unbiased comparison group: randomised controlled trials (RCTs). We therefore aimed to consolidate the evidence from RCTs on surgical and radiological treatment when evaluating the effects of varicocele repair on fertility status. This will permit the development of consistent and clear guidelines on treating varicocele‐related male infertility that can be put into effect worldwide.

    This review is an update and extension of the 2012 review, which compared any treatment (surgical/radiological) to non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment (Kroese 2012). This current review extends the scope of the previous review, and now compares different treatment methods directly with each other. The outcomes investigated were also expanded through the addition of quality of life as an outcome. This extension was made at the request of the Cochrane Gynaecology and Fertility Group to fit the requirements of an upcoming WHO report on the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of infertility, and to inform their recommendations on varicocele treatment.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the effectiveness and safety of surgical and radiological treatment of varicoceles on live birth, adverse events, pregnancy rate, varicocele recurrence, and quality of life amongst couples where the adult male has a varicocele, and the female partner of childbearing age has no fertility problems.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Published and unpublished randomised controlled trials (RCTs) were eligible for inclusion. We excluded non‐randomised trials, as they are associated with a high risk of bias. RCTs were eligible if they dealt with the surgical or radiological repair of varicocele amongst infertile couples, and if they contained a control group that received a different surgical repair or radiological treatment to the intervention group, a non‐surgical technique, delayed treatment, or no treatment. We excluded quasi‐randomised studies.

    Types of participants

    We included adult men (18 years or older) with a varicocele of any grade with either normal or abnormal semen analysis, who were part of a couple with otherwise unexplained subfertility (female partner of childbearing age with no fertility issues).

    The American Urological Association (AUA) and the American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) recommend varicocele treatment when the following conditions are fulfilled: infertile men attempting to conceive, a palpable varicocele, and abnormal semen parameters (Schlegel 2021). The European Association of Urology (EAU) guidelines also recommend treatment in adolescents with ipsilateral reduction in testicular volume (EAU 2018). Following these guidelines, men with a subclinical varicocele and normal semen analysis are not offered varicocele treatment and would be excluded from this review. To give this review a broad scope, we did not exclude men with a subclinical varicocele and normal semen analysis. However, in order to reflect the AUA/ASRM and EAU guidelines, we have presented a subgroup analysis only including men with a clinical varicocele and abnormal semen analysis. We recognise the bias this introduces in the results, as men with normal semen analyses or a subclinical varicocele, or both, are less likely to see improvement in live birth and pregnancy rates to the same extent as men with abnormal semen parameters and a clinical varicocele.

    Types of interventions

    We included the following interventions and comparisons.

    1. Treatment versus non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment

    2. Surgical versus radiological treatment

    3. Microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment versus other surgical treatment

    4. Radiological versus other radiological treatment

    Types of outcome measures

    We included studies that met the inclusion criteria described above, regardless of whether they reported on the following outcomes.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Live birth

    2. Adverse events

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Pregnancy as defined by trial authors

    2. Varicocele recurrence

    3. Quality of life (measured with a validated tool, e.g. 36‐item Short Form Health Survey (SF‐36))

    Semen quality was specified in the original review as a secondary outcome, but later seemed irrelevant in the presence of more clinically meaningful and patient‐relevant outcomes. It was therefore omitted in the 2012 update of the review, and again when updating and extending the current review.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We searched for all published and unpublished RCTs, without language restriction, and in consultation with the Cochrane Gynaecology and Fertility Group Information Specialist. The Information Specialist consulted the search strategy for previous reviews and adapted it to fit the expanded review by not limiting the search dates as initially planned for the update search. The search was able to capture all of the studies for the 'treatment versus no treatment' comparison, and for the new 'treatment versus treatment' comparison. An update search was run on 4 April 2020.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following electronic databases, trial registers, and websites:

    1. The Cochrane Gynaecology and Fertilty Group Specialised Register (ProCite platform), searched 4 April 2020 (Appendix 1);

    2. The Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL, The Cochrane Library Issue 4, 2020) via the Cochrane Register of Studies Online (CRSO) (Web platform), searched 4 April 2020 (Appendix 2);

    3. MEDLINE (Ovid platform), searched from 1946 to 4 April 2020 (Appendix 3);

    4. Embase (Ovid platform), searched from 1980 to 4 April 2020 (Appendix 4);

    5. PsycINFO (Ovid platform), searched from 1806 to 4 April 2020 (Appendix 5);

    6. CINAHL (EBSCO platform) (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature), searched 1961 to 4 April 2020 (Appendix 6);

    7. Web of Knowledge (Web platform) searched 4 April 2020 (Appendix 7);

    8. US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (Web platform) searched 4 April 2020 (www.clinicaltrials.gov);

    9. World Health Organization International Trials Registry Platform (WHO ICTRP) (Web platform) searched 4 April 2020 (www.who.int/trialsearch/Default.aspx).

    Searching other resources

    We handsearched the reference lists of included studies and systematic reviews retrieved by the search and contacted experts in the field to obtain additional data. We also handsearched Epistemonikos.org, ISI Web of Science, and OpenGrey for any additional titles that were not included in the previous searches.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Titles and abstracts retrieved by the search were dually and independently screened, meaning two reviewers independently reviewed each record, by a team of six review authors (EP, CO, SK, BN, GW, NM). We retrieved the full texts for all records deemed potentially relevant, and review authors EP, CO, and SK dually and independently screened these against the eligibility criteria. Any disagreements were resolved by consensus. Title, abstract, and full‐text screening was conducted in Covidence software (Covidence), and tracked in an EndNote X8 database (EndNote ). Studies requiring translation were sent to native speakers, who checked the study for eligibility. The selection process is documented in the PRISMA flow diagram (Moher 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    Four review authors (EP, CO, SK, NM) dually and independently extracted data from the eligible studies using a data extraction form in Covidence that was designed by the review authors (Covidence). Any disagreements were resolved by consensus. Data extracted included study characteristics and outcome data, as follows: method of randomisation; number of men randomised, excluded, and analysed (when reported); whether a single‐centre or multicentre study; whether a parallel or cross‐over design; nature of interventions; participant age (standard deviation), and eligibility criteria. We listed inclusion and exclusion criteria; blinding of treatment (virtually impossible in studies involving surgery); the use of sequential analysis or factorial design; the performance of a power calculation; duration of follow‐up; whether pregnancy was an outcome measure and, if so, how it was confirmed (if reported); and the source of any funding. For females, we recorded age and confirmation of non‐female factor infertility, when applicable. The data were exported from Covidence to RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2019), and presented in the Characteristics of included studies tables. Where studies had multiple publications, the main trial report was used as the reference, and additional details were derived from secondary papers. We corresponded with study investigators for further data on methods or results, or both, as required.

    In the preparation of the previous review, additional information was obtained from Dr. Nieschlag on the randomisation procedure in his studies (Nieschlag 1993; Nieschlag 1995/1998).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Four review authors (EP, CO, SK, NM) dually and independently assessed the included studies for risk of bias using the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' assessment tool based on the following domains (Higgins 2021): selection bias (random sequence generation and allocation concealment); performance bias (blinding of participants and personnel); detection bias (blinding of outcome assessors); attrition bias (incomplete outcome data); reporting bias (selective reporting); and other bias. Any disagreements were resolved by discussion. All judgements have been described fully and are presented in the Characteristics of included studies tables.

    Measures of treatment effect

    When three or more studies allowed for quantitative analysis, we used the numbers of events in the intervention and comparative groups of each study to calculate the Mantel‐Haenszel risk ratios (RRs) for dichotomous data. We presented 95% confidence intervals (CI) for all outcomes. We used RR as an effect measure as it is easier to interpret for decision makers and clinicians than odds ratios (OR). We reported the study results narratively when insufficient data were available. 

    We planned to pool continuous data using the mean difference (MD) if the outcome was measured employing the same scale across all studies; otherwise, we would use the standardised mean difference (SMD). We planned to compare final measurements instead of mean‐changes from baseline to postintervention. However, none of the included studies reported on the only continuous outcome of quality of life.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The primary analysis was per man (from a couple) analysed. We counted multiple live births/pregnancies per man as one live birth/pregnancy.

    Dealing with missing data

    We analysed the data on an intention‐to‐treat basis as much as possible (i.e. including all randomised participants in the analysis in the groups to which they had been randomised), and made attempts to obtain missing data from the original investigators. Live birth or pregnancy was assumed not to have occurred in couples without a reported outcome or couples who were lost to follow‐up. For other outcomes, only the available data were analysed. No imputation was undertaken.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We considered whether the clinical and methodological characteristics of the included studies were sufficiently similar for meta‐analysis to provide a clinically meaningful summary. We assessed statistical heterogeneity using the I2 statistic. An I2 measurement greater than 50% was considered indicative of substantial heterogeneity (Higgins 2021).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Due to the difficulty of detecting and correcting for publication bias and other reporting biases, we aimed to minimise their potential impact by ensuring a comprehensive search for eligible studies and by being alert to duplication of data. There is always the risk of publication bias. If there were 10 or more studies in a primary analysis, we planned to produce a funnel plot to explore the possibility of small‐study effects (a tendency for estimates of the intervention effect to be more beneficial in smaller studies).

    Data synthesis

    We analysed data using RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2019), and graded the certainty of evidence using GRADEpro GDT (GRADEpro GDT). Where three or more studies were sufficiently similar in terms of intervention, comparison, and outcome, we combined the data using Mantel‐Haenszel random‐effects model. We did not use fixed‐effect model because based on the variation in delivering surgical and radiological treatment, we could not assume one fixed effect in the included studies. We rated the certainty of the evidence using the GRADE approach, developed by the GRADE Working Group (Schünemann 2013).

    In the meta‐analysis graphs, an increase in the risks of a particular outcome, which may have been beneficial (e.g. pregnancy/live birth) or detrimental (e.g. adverse events), was displayed to the right of the centre line, and a decrease in the risks of an outcome to the left of the centre line.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Where data were available, we conducted a subgroup analysis with more strictly defined inclusion criteria, as follows: studies that included men with abnormal semen and clinical varicocele, and studies that reported live birth/pregnancy as a primary outcome.

    We planned that if we detected substantial heterogeneity, we would explore possible explanations for it via subgroup analyses. As such, we analyzed one subgroup concerning men with abnormal semen analysis and clinical varicocele, however didn’t do further subgroup analyses. We planned to take any statistical heterogeneity into account when interpreting the results, especially if there was any variation in the direction of effect.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to conduct sensitivity analyses for the primary outcomes to determine whether the conclusions were robust to arbitrary decisions made regarding eligibility and analysis. These analyses included consideration of whether the review conclusions would have differed if eligibility was restricted to studies without high risk of bias. However, due to the lack of blinding, nearly all studies were rated at high risk of bias, therefore we did not conduct this sensitivity analysis.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    Summary of findings tables were generated for the three main comparisons using GRADEpro GDT software and Cochrane methods GRADEpro GDT; Schünemann 2013). This table evaluated the overall certainty of the body of evidence for the review outcomes (live birth, adverse events, pregnancy, varicocele recurrence, quality of life), using GRADE criteria (study limitations (i.e., risk of bias), consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness and publication bias) for the main review comparisons (surgical treatment or radiological treatment versus another surgical or radiological treatment method, or versus a control method (non‐surgical method, delayed treatment, or no treatment)). Judgements about evidence certainty (high, moderate, low or very low) were made by two review authors (EP, BN) working independently, with disagreements resolved by discussion. Judgements were justified, documented, and incorporated into reporting of results for each outcome.

    Random sequence generation

All studies were rated at low risk of bias. In one study, blinding was done for outcome assessors (Al Said 2008).

    Primary outcomes
    1.1. Live birth

    We are uncertain whether surgical or radiological treatment improves live birth rates compared to no treatment.     Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We found that any treatment (surgical or radiological) may be more effective in increasing pregnancy rates than delayed or no treatment. Any treatment (surgical or radiological) was found to be more effective in increasing pregnancy rates in participants with abnormal semen parameters and clinical varicocele; however, we acknowledge that the findings of subgroups are merely observational. Our findings failed to offer valid evidence that any treatment (surgical or radiological) has a positive or negative effect on live birth. Evidence on varicocele recurrence, adverse events, or quality of life was lacking.

    When comparing surgical with radiological treatment, we could draw no valid conclusions that surgical treatment or radiological treatment was the superior method in improving the chance of live birth or pregnancy. The evidence on varicocele recurrence and adverse events (hydrocele formation, pain, epididymitis, haematoma, and suture granuloma) was also inconclusive. Evidence on quality of life was lacking for this comparison.

    When comparing surgical treatments with each other, microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment showed higher pregnancy rates and lower varicocele recurrence rates than other surgical treatments. However, given the inconclusive evidence, we are uncertain if the type of surgery affects live birth or adverse events. Evidence on quality of life was missing. Comparing open inguinal surgical treatment with retroperitoneal surgical treatment, we could draw no valid conclusions that one of these surgical approaches was the superior method in terms of live birth, pregnancy rates, varicocele recurrence rate, and adverse events. Evidence on quality of life was lacking for this comparison.

    The only study comparing radiological treatments reported solely on varicocele recurrence, and due to methodological limitations, we were unable to draw any firm conclusions about efficacy.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Only three of the 48 included studies reported on the primary efficacy outcome of live birth. These studies compared any surgical or radiological treatment versus non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment, and surgical treatment versus radiological treatment. The evidence base for the most patient‐relevant outcome is thus very limited. More studies (33) reported on pregnancy, which is a relevant outcome but could be inflated by miscarriages or still birth, and is ultimately not as patient relevant as live birth. In addition, most studies lacked definitions of pregnancy, leaving it unclear whether pregnancy referred to clinical pregnancy (sonographically proven) or early stages of pregnancy (only confirmed by a positive pregnancy test).

    The scope was expanded for the 2021 review due to the clinical relevance of the 'treatment versus treatment' comparison. We found that microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment was superior to other surgical treatment in terms of pregnancy rate and varicocele recurrence. The certainty of evidence was moderate. The results for pregnancy rates, varicocele recurrence, and adverse events for other surgical comparisons were inconclusive due to very broad confidence intervals. One study that compared treatments directly reported on live birth. No study reported on quality of life.

    Although the majority of included studies focused solely on couples with male factor infertility, older studies did not always specify that women had no known fertility problems. This should be considered when interpreting the findings.

    Quality of the evidence

    The clinical and statistical heterogeneity of the studies and the small numbers of participants should be taken into account, and the overall certainty of the evidence was rated mostly as low or very low for all outcomes (see summary of findings Table 1; summary of findings Table 2; summary of findings Table 3; summary of findings Table 4). Reasons for downgrading the certainty of evidence were mainly imprecision (small studies, broad confidence intervals), as well as inconsistency (high I2 as indicator for heterogeneity) and high risk of bias. A funnel plot for the comparison 'treatment versus non‐surgical, non‐radiological, delayed, or no treatment' and the outcome of pregnancy rate was not suggestive of publication bias. In the other meta‐analyses, fewer than 10 studies were included, so we did not conduct funnel plots. Consequently, the risk of publication bias cannot be ruled out.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The decision to include studies which included normozoospermic men from infertile couples is disputable (Breznik 1993; Ghanaie 2012; Nilsson 1979; Unal 2001; Yamamoto 1996), but was based on the decision to broaden the scope of the review and represent a wide number of men for whom varicocelectomy is deemed appropriate. Given their allocation by randomisation, these men were evenly distributed between the experimental group and the controls. Also, the decision to include studies that accepted men with subclinical varicocele is disputable (Breznik 1993; Grasso 2000; Nilsson 1979; Unal 2001; Yamamoto 1996). The AUA/ASRM and EAU state that normospermia and subclinical varicocele are no indication for surgical treatment (Schlegel 2021; EAU 2018). A literature search regarding the relationship between grade of varicocele and the response to varicocelectomy showed different outcomes, ranging from no improvement of semen analysis after treatment of subclinical varicocele to significant improvement. However, live birth or pregnancy rate was not an outcome in these studies. For this reason we performed a subgroup analysis that excluded these studies (Breznik 1993; Grasso 2000; Nilsson 1979; Unal 2001; Yamamoto 1996). Future updates of this review might consider altering the protocol alongside the guidelines of the AUA and EAU, only including men with abnormal semen analysis and clinical varicocele, as this patient group is more relevant for clinical practice and more likely to show a positive benefit from treatment.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    This review covers a long period of time. Particularly in the older studies, study methodology was far from perfect. Although the findings of our previous meta‐analyses are consistent with some non‐Cochrane Reviews (Baazeem 2011; Cocuzza 2008; French 2008; Marmar 2007), the current updated review suggests that there might be some value in varicocelectomy in selected men. A recent non‐Cochrane Review also found similar findings, exhibiting a significant difference in spontaneous pregnancy rates in men with clinical and subclinical varicocele and abnormal semen analysis (Kim 2013). However, this paper reported on the same papers included in the 2012 review (Kroese 2012).

    A recent non‐Cochrane Review comparing microscopic surgical treatment (subinguinal and inguinal) to laparoscopic treatment reported similar results, indicating that microscopic treatment results in lower recurrence rates and fewer adverse events (Wang 2020).

    One systematic review comparing treatment methods found no significant difference in spontaneous pregnancy rates, however found reduced rates of varicocele recurrence and hydrocele formation after microsurgical treatment compared to laparoscopic or open surgical treatment (Ding 2012). However, this review included data from two papers that reported their results for varicocele recurrence and adverse events per number of varicocele instead of per number of men, thus mixing the denominators. These papers were included in our review for the outcome pregnancy rate, which they reported per number of men, but data were not included for the other outcomes (Al Kandari 2007; Al Said 2008).

    Another systematic review comparing retroperitoneal treatment, microsurgical inguinal treatment, and microscopic subinguinal treatment found that microscopic inguinal surgical treatment resulted in statistically significantly higher spontaneous pregnancy rates when compared to high ligation and subinguinal treatment; however, the studies reporting this were not RCTs and were from the early 2000s (Schauer 2012). The comparison in our review focused on microscopic subinguinal surgical treatment, which is currently viewed as the gold standard. Schauer 2012 also reported higher spontaneous pregnancy rates in treatment groups compared to no treatment groups, which is in line with our results. However, these results are again subject to interpretation, given that this review contained studies that were not RCTs (Schauer 2012).

    Another systematic review comparing various treatments (laparoscopic treatment, retroperitoneal treatment, open inguinal and subinguinal treatments, microsurgical subinguinal and inguinal treatments, percutaneous venous embolisation, Tauber antegrade sclerotherapy, retrograde sclerotherapy, and no treatment) to one another found that inguinal microsurgical treatment, subinguinal microsurgical treatment, open inguinal treatment, and laparoscopic varicocelectomy led to statistically significantly higher pregnancy rates (Wang 2015). Subinguinal and inguinal microsurgical treatment also showed lower levels of varicocele recurrence. Furthermore, Tauber antegrade sclerotherapy and subinguinal microsurgical treatment led to a lower rate of hydrocele formation. However, this review contained studies that were not RCTs (Wang 2015).
