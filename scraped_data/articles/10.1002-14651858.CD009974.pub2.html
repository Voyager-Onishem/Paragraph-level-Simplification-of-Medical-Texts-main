Non pharmacological interventions for spasticity in multiple sclerosis

    • Background

    Spasticity is commonly experienced by people with multiple sclerosis (MS), and it contributes to overall disability in this population. A wide range of non pharmacological interventions are used in isolation or with pharmacological agents to treat spasticity in MS. Evidence for their effectiveness is yet to be determined. 

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness of various non pharmacological interventions for the treatment of spasticity in adults with MS.

    Search methods

    A literature search was performed using the Specialised Register of the Cochrane Multiple Sclerosis and Rare Diseases of the Central Nervous System Review Group on using the Cochrane MS Group Trials Register which among other sources, contains CENTRAL, Medline, EMBASE, CINAHL, LILACS, PEDRO in June 2012. Manual searching in the relevant journals and screening of the reference lists of identified studies and reviews were carried out. Abstracts published in proceedings of conferences were also scrutinised.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that reported non pharmacological intervention/s for treatment of spasticity in adults with MS and compared them with some form of control intervention (such as sham/placebo interventions or lower level or different types of intervention, minimal intervention, waiting list controls or no treatment; interventions given in different settings), were included.

    Data collection and analysis

    Three review authors independently selected the studies, extracted data and assessed the methodological quality of the studies using the Grades of Recommendation, Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE) tool for best‐evidence synthesis. A meta‐analysis was not possible due to methodological, clinical and statistical heterogeneity of included studies.

    Main results

    Nine RCTs (N = 341 participants, 301 included in analyses) investigated various types and intensities of non pharmacological interventions for treating spasticity in adults with MS. These interventions included: physical activity programmes (such as physiotherapy, structured exercise programme, sports climbing); transcranial magnetic stimulation (Intermittent Theta Burst Stimulation (iTBS), Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS)); electromagnetic therapy (pulsed electromagnetic therapy; magnetic pulsing device), Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS); and Whole Body Vibration (WBV). All studies scored 'low' on the methodological quality assessment implying high risk of bias. 

    There is 'low level' evidence for physical activity programmes used in isolation or in combination with other interventions (pharmacological or non pharmacological), and for repetitive magnetic stimulation (iTBS/rTMS) with or without adjuvant exercise therapy in improving spasticity in adults with MS. No evidence of benefit exists to support the use of TENS, sports climbing and vibration therapy for treating spasticity in this population.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is 'low level' evidence for non pharmacological interventions such as physical activities given in conjunction with other interventions, and for magnetic stimulation and electromagnetic therapies for beneficial effects on spasticity outcomes in people with MS. A wide range of non pharmacological interventions are used for the treatment of spasticity in MS, but more robust trials are needed to build evidence about these interventions.

    • Non pharmacological interventions for treatment of spasticity in Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

    Spasticity is a common and debilitating symptom, causing ‘stiffness’, ‘spasms’ or ‘tightness’ in the weakened arm or leg in people with multiple sclerosis (MS). Overall, spasticity is difficult to treat. Current treatments include various medications (such as botulinum toxin injections to relax the affected muscles) and non drug methods aiming to achieve functional goals for patients (and their caregivers) such as physiotherapy, magnetic stimulation, electromagnetic therapy, vibration therapy. In this review, nine studies evaluating various non drug treatments to treat spasticity in adult with MS were included, comprising a total of 341 participants. Results from these studies suggest that all included non pharmacological therapies have low level of evidence or no evidence in improving spasticity in people with MS. However, caution should be used in the interpretation of the results, due to the poor methodological quality of all the included studies. More research is needed to determine the usefulness of these interventions before they can be recommended as routine treatments.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    A range of non pharmacological interventions are used for the treatment of spasticity in people with MS. This review found only 'low level' evidence that physical activity given in conjunction with other interventions (such as BoNT, iTBS), magnetic stimulation and electromagnetic therapies have some beneficial effects on spasticity. Despite this, the evidence is inadequate for routine use of these treatments for treating spasticity in the MS population.

    Clinician involvement is needed to build evidence in spasticity outcomes from everyday clinical practice. The clinical applicability of findings of this review need to be confirmed in future well‐designed trials with bigger sample size and with longer‐term follow‐up.

    Implications for research

    In light of the review limitation mentioned above, future trials would need to consider the following.

    • Robust studies with low bias, appropriate sample sizes and power to detect expected differences.                         

    • Careful definition and selection of target patients.

    • Longer follow‐up (more than six months) to determine benefits of an intervention.     

    • Details of spasticity, localisation pattern, severity and impact on everyday function.                

    • Use of appropriate, responsive outcome measures for spasticity that focus on activity limitation and ‘active/passive’ functional improvement, and achievement of individual goals of treatment.                         

    • Demonstration of any differential treatment effect between single intensive interventions and multidisciplinary intervention.

    • Appropriate intervention protocol for therapy (such as dose/intensity and time).                           

    • Any adverse effects associated with the intervention.                        

    • Caregiver burden and their involvement in the programmes.

    • Cost‐utility of intervention                     

    Background

    Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic neurological disease characterised by patchy inflammation, gliosis and demyelination within the central nervous system (CNS). It affects approximately 2.5 million people worldwide (WHO 2004). The patterns of presentation in MS can vary. These include: ‘relapsing remitting’ MS (80%), characterised by exacerbations and remission, which may convert to a ‘secondary progressive’ form of MS with progressive disability acquired between attacks; ‘primary progressive’ MS (15%), where persons develop progressive disability from the onset; and ‘progressive relapsing’ MS (5%), where persons begin worsening gradually and subsequently start to experience discrete attacks. People with MS have a prolonged median survival time from the time of diagnosis (approximately 40 years) (Weinshenker 1989). Therefore, issues of progressive physical disability, psychosocial adjustment and social reintegration progress over time and have implications for the person with MS, their carers and treating clinicians in terms of healthcare access, provision of services and financial burden.

    The clinical manifestations of MS are variable and can involve visual loss, double vision, motor weakness, spasticity, ataxia, tremor, sensory loss, sphincter dysfunction and cognitive impairment. People with MS can present with various combinations of deficits such as physical (weakness, spasticity, sensory loss and ataxia), cognitive (memory), psychosocial, behavioural and environmental problems which limit activity and participation. Spasticity is a common physical symptom experienced by almost two‐thirds of MS patients (Rizzo 2004). Unlike static conditions (single episode of stroke and spinal cord injury), MS can have a fluctuating and often progressive course, making spasticity management more challenging.

    Description of the condition

    Spasticity in MS

    Spasticity is a common phenomenon in patients with upper motor neuron (UMN) disorders including MS. The pathophysiology of spasticity is complex and not fully understood. Spasticity has traditionally been defined as 'a motor disorder which is a component of the UMN syndrome, characterised by a velocity‐dependent increase in tonic stretch reflexes (muscle tone) with exaggerated tendon jerks, resulting from hyperexcitability of the stretch reflex' (Lance 1980). Another broader definition of spasticity is 'a disordered sensori‐motor control, resulting from an UMN lesion, presenting as intermittent or sustained involuntary activation of muscles' (Pandyan 2005). This definition suggests that 'spasticity’ can be interpreted as an umbrella term for an array of positive features of the UMN syndrome (Monaghan 2011). The key feature of spasticity is hyperexcitability of muscle stretch reflexes (Ansari 2009) due to an imbalance between excitatory and inhibitory spinal and supraspinal neuronal circuitries (Mukherjee 2010). Spasticity in MS is believed to be due to axonal degeneration or demyelination within specific descending tracts, or both, which leads to a disturbance of inhibitory interneuronal spinal network pathways.

    MS‐related spasticity may be generalised, focal (affecting a localised part of a limb) or multifocal (affecting more than one part of a limb or limbs). Spasticity results in stiffness and abnormal posturing of the limb due to net imbalance of forces between the agonist and antagonist muscles, affecting static joint position and dynamic limb movement (Sheean 1998). It may also affect the truncal musculature resulting in poor postural control. Spasticity has a direct correlation with disease progression, and resulting weakness and fatigability (negative features of the UMN syndrome), which often contribute to patient problems. Further, adaptive features may develop, including contractures and rheological changes in muscle, tendons and joints (O'Dwyer 1996), which can further exacerbate limb positioning, movement and function. The effect of muscle overactivity, weakness and soft tissue contractures on patient function should be assessed in order to ensure appropriate management.

    Spasticity‐related problems

    It is estimated that 60% to 90% of people with MS develop spasticity during their lifetime (Rizzo 2004), and it is a major contributor to overall disability (Beard 2003). In some instances, spasticity may be beneficial by counteracting muscle weakness, thereby increasing stability of the lower limb/s (Henze 2006). However, spasticity should be treated when it interferes with activity or the ability to provide care to the person with MS, such as hand hygiene. Inadequate treatment may result in pain, contractures and pressure sores, which may further impact on function (Beard 2003; Rizzo 2004).

    Spasticity‐associated problems can be classified according to the World Health Organization (WHO) International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) (WHO 2001) ‐ a framework used to describe the impact of disease at different levels. Spasticity‐related problems in people with MS may include:

    • impairments (problems with body structures or physiological function) such as restricted joint range of movement, loss of dexterity, abnormal limb postures and pain;

    • activity limitations including: (i) limitation in active limb use by the patient ('active' function) which can interfere with mobility and transfers, and independence with activities of daily living (ADL) such as feeding, grooming and bathing; (ii) difficulty with provision of care to an affected limb by the person with MS or their carer ('passive' function’), such as maintaining palmar hygiene or applying a splint or orthotic;

    • restrictions in ‘participation’ limiting societal roles relating to family, work and life situations.

    In addition to spasticity and the broader effects of the UMN syndrome described above, other MS‐related problems, such as fatigue, cognitive, psychosocial, behavioural and environmental issues, can contribute to overall disability. Furthermore, issues of impairment, activity limitation and restriction in participation progress over time (Khan 2007; Khan 2010) and are compounded by age‐related issues. Thus, management is complex requiring multiple treatment approaches (Pfleger 2010; Pfleger 2010a). Understanding the many challenges faced by people with MS, and spasticity in particular, can have considerable impact due to the increased demand for health care.

    Spasticity management

    The key to spasticity management is achievement of individualised, patient‐centred goals that are set collaboratively with patients, their carers and rehabilitation team in a functional context. Goals may be focused on reducing symptoms or impairments, or improving activity level (active and passive function), participation and quality of life (QOL). Elimination or avoidance of triggers which may initiate or enhance spasticity (such as urogenital infections, constipation, pain) and prevention of complications (such as contractures and pressure sores) may also be important (Henze 2006; Rekand 2010). 

    Non pharmacological interventions used in isolation and/or pharmacological agents are the mainstay in the management of spasticity, and rarely is there a place for surgical intervention (Stevenson 2010). Pharmacological treatments may include antispasmodic medications such as baclofen, diazepam, dantrolene and tizanidine (Beard 2003; Shakespeare 2003; Stevenson 2010). They have limited effectiveness and are associated with various systemic side effects, such as drowsiness, cognitive impairment and muscle weakness. Other treatments, such as intrathecal baclofen via a programmable implanted pump (Harned 2011), are invasive and substantially more expensive. Their use is usually restricted to people with severe disabling spasticity (Beard 2003; Henze 2006). Botulinum toxin A (BoNT) has been favoured as the pharmacological treatment of choice for the management of focal spasticity not responding to non pharmacological therapy (De Paiva 1999).

    A wide range of non pharmacological interventions for spasticity management are currently used, including: physical interventions, therapeutic ultrasound, electromagnetic therapies, acupuncture and orthotics (splints, garments). Often treatments are used in combination, encompassing a multidisciplinary rehabilitation approach.

    Description of the intervention

    In this review all non pharmacological interventions for spasticity in people with MS were included, which included:

    • uni‐disciplinary therapy, e.g. physiotherapy or occupational therapy only; 

    • individual non pharmacological treatment modalities or physical interventions that may form a component of a rehabilitation programme (described below);

    • multidisciplinary rehabilitation programmes involving the provision of a co‐ordinated programme by a specialised team of health professionals, delivered by two or more disciplines (medical, nursing, physiotherapy, occupational therapy, orthotists and others), primarily aimed at reducing spasticity.

    The usual elements of a multidisciplinary rehabilitation programme are (Steins 2002):

    • an individualised, patient‐centred plan formulated by the patient and rehabilitation team;

    • goals derived and prioritised through a multidisciplinary process, where goals are specific, measurable, achievable, realistic and timely (Wade 2010);

    • patient participation required to achieve the goals with improvement in the patient’s personal potential as a result;

    • outcomes demonstrating improvement in one or more domains of the ICF.

    Individual treatment modalities or physical interventions used in uni‐disciplinary or multidisciplinary rehabilitation settings may include (but are not limited to): stretching; neurodevelopmental treatment; static positioning; orthotics (splints, garments); casting; strengthening exercises; Transcutaneous Electric Nerve Stimulation (TENS); hydrotherapy; thermotherapy (infra‐red heat); cryotherapy (cold); shock waves; vibratory stimulation (whole‐body vibration); electromyography (EMG) biofeedback; Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS); therapeutic ultrasound; continuous passive motion robotics; acupuncture; psychological and educational interventions.

    How the intervention might work

    Physical interventions for the treatment of spasticity are thought to have some beneficial effects (Lockley 2006; Stevenson 2010) and are assumed to have some or all of the following effects (Lockley 2006):

    • minimising changes in the viscoelastic properties of the connective tissue;

    • altering the neural patterns of spasticity or spasms;

    • maintaining levels of function for the individual.

    Some non pharmacological interventions for spasticity have been studied: e.g. repetitive training of isolated movements was found to reduce spasticity in a paretic hand (Buetefisch 1995); cryotherapy (cooling) (Mecomber 1971) and hydrotherapy (Kesiktas 2004) were reported to exhibit a transient beneficial effect on elevated muscle tone; hydrotherapy has been suggested to reduce spasticity and the need for baclofen in patients with spinal cord injury (Kesiktas 2004). On the other hand, TENS and vibration stimulation (whole‐body vibration) may have no additional benefit for spasticity in people with MS (Miller 2007; Schyns 2009).

    The effectiveness of pharmacological treatments which include antispasmodic medications such as baclofen, diazepam, dantrolene and tizanidine and BoNT have been explored (Beard 2003; De Paiva 1999; Shakespeare 2003; Stevenson 2010). Overall effectiveness of multidisciplinary rehabilitation interventions in neurological conditions such as MS (Khan 2007), acquired brain injury (Turner‐Stokes 2011) and stroke (Langhorne 2011) have been documented. There are, however, no systematic reviews to highlight the effectiveness of non pharmacological interventions (uni‐disciplinary, multidisciplinary or both) for spasticity management in people with MS.  Although recent guidelines and consensus statements on spasticity management advocate various non pharmacological approaches (Haselkorn 2005; MS Australia 2009), the evidence is based mostly on isolated studies, narrative reviews or expert opinions.  

    Why it is important to do this review

    Spasticity is a common and disabling phenomenon experienced by people with MS, which impacts on patients' function (mobility, ability for self‐care and everyday living activities, psychosocial life) and requires long‐term management. A wide range of non pharmacological interventions are currently being used to manage spasticity, but the benefit and harms associated with these interventions in people with MS have not been established.

    A systematic evaluation of the existing evidence is therefore required to guide treating clinicians in spasticity management. 

    Objectives

    This review aimed to investigate the effectiveness and safety of non pharmacological interventions for the management of spasticity in people with MS in improving patient outcomes.

    Specific questions addressed by this review included the following.

    • Are non pharmacological interventions (uni‐disciplinary and/or multidisciplinary rehabilitation) effective in minimising impairment, activity limitation and participation restriction in people with MS with spasticity?

    • What non pharmacological interventions (uni‐disciplinary and/or multidisciplinary rehabilitation) are effective in reducing spasticity in people with MS, and in which setting?

    • Are non pharmacological interventions (uni‐disciplinary and/or multidisciplinary rehabilitation) effective in minimising the burden of care and improving quality of life in people with MS with spasticity?

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that compared a non pharmacological therapy to treat spasticity in people with MS with a control situation were included (see Types of interventions for detail on the control situations).  

    Types of participants

    Adults (18 years or over) with MS and spasticity affecting upper or lower limbs or both, and generalised, focal or multifocal spasticity were included. All participants had a confirmed diagnosis of MS based on validated criteria (Mc Donald 2001; Polman 2005; Poser 1993). All subgroups of the disease such as relapsing remitting, primary progressive and secondary progressive MS were included (Lublin 1996).

    Types of interventions

    All modalities of non pharmacological interventions aimed at reducing spasticity in people with MS were considered; these included the following:

    • uni‐disciplinary therapy, e.g. physiotherapy or occupational therapy only, and individual treatment modalities or physical activity programmes (as described in Description of the intervention);

    • multidisciplinary rehabilitation programmes. In this review, multidisciplinary rehabilitation is defined as any co‐ordinated therapy programme delivered by two or more disciplines (occupational therapy, physiotherapy, exercise physiology, orthotics, other allied health and nursing) in conjunction with medical input (neurologist or rehabilitation medicine physician) that aims to achieve patient‐centred goals related to reducing spasticity.

    The settings of rehabilitation programmes included the following:

    • outpatient or day treatment settings, which were located within private or public hospitals, community rehabilitation centres or specialist rehabilitation centres;

    • home‐based settings, in the patients' own homes and local community;

    • inpatient rehabilitation settings where care was delivered 24 hours per day, including specialised medical rehabilitation units or hospital wards.

    Rehabilitation programmes and interventions are individualised, thus treatments provided can be variable and the actual content of rehabilitation care may vary from patient to patient. Therefore, we included any study that stated or implied rehabilitation provided it satisfied the definition as stated above and compared it to a control situation.

    Control situations included: no treatment; placebo/sham; and other interventions, for example, lower‐level or different types of intervention such as 'routinely available local services' (e.g. medical care or physiotherapy only), 'minimal intervention' (such as 'information only'), waiting list conditions, or interventions given in different settings (ambulatory, inpatient or home) and lower‐intensity or different variants of intervention (e.g. lower dosage/intensity, different mode of delivery).

    Concomitant pharmacological/surgical interventions were accepted, if administered in the same way in both the control and the treated groups. Surgical and pharmacological interventions provided in isolation for spasticity management were not considered in this review.

    Types of outcome measures

    Diverse outcomes were identified, given the varied presentations of spasticity‐related problems and goals of treatment related to MS severity.

    Primary outcomes

    Primary outcomes reflected the level of activity limitation and impairment according to the ICF (WHO 2001), which included the following:

    • reduction in spasticity [e.g. the Ashworth (Ashworth 1964) or Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS) (Bohannon 1987; Ansari 2009)];

    • improvement in upper limb functioning and (upper limb‐related) ADL: passive function or ease of care [e.g. Leeds Arm Spasticity Impact Scale (Bhakta 1996), Disability Assessment Scale (Brashear 2002), Arm Activity measure (Ashford 2010); active motor function [e.g. Motor Activity Log (MAL) (Van der Lee 2004) or Action Research Arm Test (ARAT) (Lyle 1981)];

    • improvement in mobility, including tests of walking speed, balance and gait pattern [e.g. Timed Up And Go (TUAG) (Podsiadlo 1991), Barthel index (BI) (Mahoney 1965), 10‐metre walk test (Green 2002)];

    • any adverse events that had resulted from the intervention. We defined serious adverse effects as those events that were life‐threatening or required prolonged hospitalisation. 

    The measures of achievement of intended goals for treatment (e.g. goal attainment scaling or other measure of goal achievement) were also included. The assimilation of individualised goals for treatment was increasingly used as an overall measure of outcome for trials of rehabilitation. Various outcomes measures were expected, hence specific outcome measures did not form part of the inclusion criteria for this review.

    Secondary outcomes

    These included specific outcomes that reflect:

    • symptoms or impairments, e.g. pain (measured by verbal scores, visual analogue scales), spasm frequency (Penn Spasm Frequency Scale (Penn 1989), joint range of movement (using MAS (Ansari 2009; Bohannon 1987), Tardieu scale (Ansari 2008; Mehrholz 2005)), involuntary movements;

    • restriction in participation and impact on carers: impact of spasticity in people with MS [e.g. Multiple Sclerosis Spasticity Scale (MSSS‐88); Hobart 2006), reduction of carer strain and burden (e.g. Caregiver Strain Index (CSI); (Robinson 1983), quality of life (e.g. MS Quality of Life (MSQOL 54); Vickrey 1997)].

    • outcomes that reflected service utilisation, including costs of care, length of hospital admissions, readmission and extent of use of services.

    Timing of outcome measures

    The outcome time points were either short‐term (up to three months) or long‐term (greater than three months) from the start of the intervention.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We conducted a systematic search to identify all relevant published and unpublished RCTs. No language restrictions were applied to the search.

    Electronic searches

    The Trials Search Co‐ordinator searched the Cochrane Multiple Sclerosis and Rare Diseases of the Central Nervous System Working Group on using the Cochrane MS Group Trials Register (20 June 2012) which among other sources, contains CENTRAL, MEDLINE, EMBASE, CINAHL, LILACS, PEDRO. The search terms are listed in Appendix 1 (Appendix 1). For the search strategy used to search the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) please see: Appendix 2.

    In addition, we searched the Clinical trials registries (http://clinicaltrials.gov) and The WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) search portal (http://apps.who.int/trialsearch/Default.aspx) for all prospectively registered and ongoing trials.

    Information on the Specialised Register and details of search strategies used to identify trials can be found in the 'Specialised Register' section within the Multiple Sclerosis and Rare Diseases of the Central Nervous System Group module.

    Searching other resources

    An expanded search was performed to identify articles potentially missed through the database searches and articles from ‘grey literature' from 1996 to June 2012. This included:

    • handsearches of reference lists of all retrieved articles, texts and other reviews on the topic;

    • handsearches of the most relevant journals related to MS and spasticity research and treatment (such as, but not limited to: Archives of Physical Medicine and Rehabilitation ; Journal of Rehabilitation Medicine ; Journal of Neurology ; Journal of Neurology, Neurosurgery and Psychiatry , Clinical Rehabilitation , Neurology , Physical Therapy , Multiple Sclerosis and others);

    • searches using the related articles feature (via PubMed);

    • searches of ProQuest Dissertations and Theses;

    • searches of Web of Science for citation of key authors;

    • searches of SIGLE (System for Information on Grey Literature in Europe);

    • contacting local and foreign experts for further information (such as the Cochrane MS Group, key authors of publications in this review);

    • contacting authors and researchers active in this field.

    The following websites were also searched for ongoing and unpublished trials:

    • Current Controlled Trials (http://www.controlled‐trials.com);

    • UK Clinical Research Network Portfolio Database (http://public.ukcrn.org.uk/search/).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (BA, LLM) independently screened and short listed all abstracts and titles of studies identified by the search strategy for appropriateness based on the selection criteria. The review authors (BA, FK, LLM) independently evaluated each study from the shortlist of potentially appropriate studies for inclusion or exclusion. The full text of the article was obtained for further assessment to determine if the trial met the inclusion/exclusion criteria. A final consensus decision was made by discussion amongst all the review authors, if no consensus was met about the possible inclusion/exclusion of any individual study. We planned to submit the full article to the editorial board for arbitration, where there was still no consensus agreement regarding the inclusion/exclusion of a study, however, this was not required. Review authors were not be masked to the name(s) of the author(s), institution(s) or publication source at any level of the review.

    Further information about the method of randomisation or a complete description of the interventions from the trialists was sought when required.

    Data extraction and management

    Four review authors (BA, FK, LLM, MD) independently extracted the data from each study that met the inclusion criteria, using a standardised data collection form. A final check was made by the fifth review author (DW). We contacted the primary authors of potentially eligible studies to provide data and clarification, where adequate data were not reported. All studies that met the inclusion criteria were summarised in the 'Characteristics of included studies' table provided in the Review Manager (RevMan) 5 software developed by The Cochrane Collaboration (RevMan 2011) and included details on design, participants, interventions and outcomes.

    The following information from individual studies was reported:

    • publication details;

    • study design, study setting, inclusion/exclusion criteria, method of allocation, risk of bias;

    • patient population, for example, age, type of MS, disease duration, disability (according to Kurtzke's Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS) score; Kurtzke 1983));

    • details on spasticity (severity and affected side);

    • details of intervention;

    • outcome measures;

    • withdrawals, length and method of follow‐up and number of participants followed up.

    Further details were requested from the main author of the manuscript or studies where detailed data were missing.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Four review authors (BA, FK, LLM, MD) independently assessed the methodological quality of the included studies using the Cochrane 'Risk of bias' tool (Higgins 2011). We assessed the sequence generation, allocation concealment, blinding of participants, therapists and outcome assessors, incomplete outcome data and selective outcome reporting. A judgement of ‘yes’ indicated a low risk of bias, ‘no’ indicated a high risk of bias, and ‘unclear’ indicated either unclear or unknown risk of bias. See Table 1.

    Measures of treatment effect

    All data were entered and analysed in RevMan 5 (RevMan 2011). A quantitative analysis was not possible due to clinical heterogeneity; the use of diverse methods, interventions and outcome measures; and insufficient data available. If studies had been available, we would have calculated risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for dichotomous data and differences in means or standardised differences in means (SMD) with 95% CIs for continuous data. We would also have calculated for each outcome of interest, summary estimates of treatment effect (with 95% CIs for each comparison). We qualitatively summarised the studies in the Characteristics of included studies, presented the results of individual studies in the Table 3 and described the results in the Discussion section below.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The intended appropriate unit of analysis was type, intensity and setting of non pharmacological intervention, however, this was not possible. We analysed the methodological quality of RCTs using the GRADE system for existing evidence (Higgins 2011). The GRADE approach is applicable to all types of studies. The three levels of quality using the GRADE approach and the four levels of grading the body of evidence of included studies are shown in Table 1 and Table 2.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted the primary authors of potentially eligible studies to provide data and clarification, where required. We excluded studies with fatal flaws (for instance, withdrawals by more than 40% of the patients, nearly total non adherence to the protocol, or very poor or non adjusted comparability in the baseline criteria) (Khan 2007; Turner‐Stokes 2011; van Tulder 1997; Van Tulder 2003).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We followed statistical analysis as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).  As mentioned above, it was not possible to conduct a comprehensive quantitative analysis owing to the variability of interventions and methods used, and the type of available data reported in each study.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Publication bias was minimised by sourcing unpublished data where possible (Egger 1998). We contacted the authors for the full data set or the reason for not publishing the data, where data were not reported or published in full.

    Data synthesis

    For reasons mentioned above, data were not pooled for a quantitative analysis. If studies had been available, we would have attempted a quantitative analysis provided there was clinical homogeneity and the data in each study allowed for such an analysis. We would also have calculated a weighted treatment effect across trials using the Cochrane statistical package Review Manager 5 (RevMan 5) and expressed the results as risk ratios (RRs) with 95% CIs and risk differences (RDs) with 95% CIs for dichotomous outcomes and mean differences (MDs) and 95% CIs for continuous outcomes. We would have initially used a fixed‐effect model and approximate Chi2 tests for heterogeneity to assess outcome data for compatibility with the assumption of a uniform risk ratio (P > 0.10). In the presence of significant heterogeneity (P < 0.10), random‐effects meta‐analysis would have been used instead.

    We have highlighted the strength of study findings, discussed gaps in current literature and identified future research directions in the Discussion section.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We were unable to perform subgroup analysis for the following subgroups owing to the lack of available data.

    1. Type of MS (relapsing remitting, progressive)

    2. EDSS (< 6, > 6)

    3. Duration of follow‐up of the participants (≤ three months; > three months)

    4. Affected site, i.e. upper limb, lower limb or both

    5. Type of non pharmacological (uni‐disciplinary and/or multidisciplinary rehabilitation) intervention (i.e. inpatient, ambulatory care)

    Sensitivity analysis

    We were not able to perform a sensitivity analyses. If studies had been available, and heterogeneity existed across trials, sensitivity analyses would have been conducted by omitting trials with a high risk of bias as described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Included studies

    In total, nine RCTs (Giovannelli 2007; Lappin 2003; Miller 2007; Mori 2010; Mori 2011; Nielsen 1996; Richards 1997; Schyns 2009; Velkonja 2010) involving a total of 341 participants (301 included in analyses), fulfilled the inclusion criteria for this review (see Characteristics of included studies table). A majority of the included studies were conducted in Europe; three in Italy, Giovannelli 2007; Mori 2010; Mori 2011; two in United Kingdom, Miller 2007; Schyns 2009; and one each in Slovenia, Velkonja 2010; and Denmark Nielsen 1996, while two were conducted in United States (Lappin 2003; Richards 1997). The various non pharmacological interventions evaluated in the included studies consisted generally of physical activity programmes in isolation or concomitant with other interventions (pharmacological therapy or other non pharmacological intervention) and contemporary interventions.

    • Four studies (Giovannelli 2007; Mori 2011; Schyns 2009; Velkonja 2010) evaluated various physical activity programmes. Of these, three studies compared these programmes in conjunction with other interventions: one study evaluated the effects of physiotherapy in combination with botulinum toxin type A (BoNT) injection (Giovannelli 2007), one explored the effects of an exercise programme in combination with Intermittent Theta Burst Stimulation (iTBS) (Mori 2011), and another study evaluated the effects of whole body vibration (WBV) with or without an exercise programme (Schyns 2009). One trial compared two contemporary physical activity programmes with each other: i.e. sports climbing with yoga (Velkonja 2010).

    • Three studies evaluated the effectiveness of repetitive magnetic stimulation: two iTBS (Mori 2010; Mori 2011) and one Repetitive Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (rTMS) (Nielsen 1996).

    • Two studies investigated the effectiveness of electromagnetic therapy: one Pulsed Electromagnetic Therapy (Lappin 2003); and one magnetic pulsing device (Enermed) (Richards 1997).

    • One study each evaluated the impact of Transcutaneous Electric Nerve Stimulation (TENS) (Miller 2007) and WBV (Schyns 2009).

    The review authors are unaware of any on‐going RCTs in this area.

    The study inclusion criteria varied between trials. All trials included participants with definite MS, however, only five trials specified the commonly used clinical criteria: three trials used the clinical criteria of Poser (Lappin 2003; Nielsen 1996; Richards 1997), two the McDonald (Mori 2010; Mori 2011), and was unspecified in remaining four trials (Giovannelli 2007; Miller 2007; Schyns 2009; Velkonja 2010). Most studies included all types of MS, while two studies included only participants with relapsing‐remitting MS (Mori 2010; Mori 2011) and one (Giovannelli 2007) only participants with secondary progressive MS.

    All studies required some form of disability/impairment scale score as an entry criteria between specified values. These included: Ashworth Scale score, MAS (Giovannelli 2007; Miller 2007; Nielsen 1996), EDSS scores (Mori 2010; Mori 2011; Velkonja 2010), Hauser Ambulation Index (Schyns 2009) and MS Symptom Rating Form (Lappin 2003).

    The recruitment time period was not reported in any of the studies. The follow‐up periods varied between trials with most being immediately after treatment or up to two months. Only one trial reported long‐term follow‐up of up to eight to 20 months, but this was restricted only for evaluation of patient‐reported symptoms using subjective questionnaires (Miller 2007). For details of assessment time points for each trial refer to summary of findings in Table 3.

    All included studies reported at least one spasticity‐related outcome: group mean differences in EDSS, MAS pre‐treatment to post‐treatment, subjective ratings of improvement in individual symptoms. See Characteristics of included studies for details of clinical outcomes evaluated in each trial. Adverse events were reported only in three studies (Lappin 2003; Richards 1997; Schyns 2009).

    Excluded studies

    Ten studies were excluded after appraisals of the full report. See Characteristics of excluded studies table. The primary reasons for exclusion were: three were comparative, but not RCTs (Chiara 1998; Sosnoff 2009; Szecsi 2009); four studies did not use spasticity as a specific outcome measure (Castro‐Sanchez 2012; Farenelli 1999; Huges 2009; Livesley 1992); and three were only abstracts of RCTs presented in various conferences (Bernhardt 2011; Leocani 2012; Shaygannejad 2011). To date, none of these abstracts have been published as full accounts and despite multiple attempts to contact the authors, detailed data were not obtained.

    The methodological quality of the nine included trials was 'low'. All trials had substantial flaws in their methodological design with a high risk of bias related to their randomisation procedure; blinding of patients, therapists and outcome assessors; and outcome analysis.

    Allocation

    Concealment of allocation prior to entry to the study was described only in two studies (Lappin 2003; Richards 1997). Although all included studies stated the procedure to be randomised, methods of randomisation were reported in three studies only (Giovannelli 2007; Lappin 2003; Schyns 2009).

    Blinding

    Blinding of study participants and treating personnel were adequately described only in three studies (Lappin 2003; Nielsen 1996; Richards 1997), was not carried out in four studies (Giovannelli 2007; Miller 2007; Schyns 2009; Velkonja 2010) and unclear in one study (Mori 2010). One study (Mori 2011) partially blinded the participants (only two of the three groups in which participants were allocated were blinded). None of the studies attempted to test the success of patient blinding by questionnaire. Blinding of participants and treating personnel can be challenging in rehabilitation, but blinding outcome assessors is possible. Only five studies took measures to blind outcome assessors (Giovannelli 2007; Lappin 2003; Mori 2010; Richards 1997; Schyns 2009).

    Incomplete outcome data

    Eight of the nine included studies provided a description of participant withdrawals and losses to follow‐up. No participants withdrew or were lost to follow‐up in two studies (Mori 2010; Mori 2011), while one study (Velkonja 2010) did not report any information in this regard. Intention‐to‐treat analysis was not performed in any of the trials. In those studies with losses to follow‐up or withdrawals, participants who withdrew or did not continue the study were excluded from the analysis, and no information was available for these participants.

    Selective reporting

    All included studies reported pre‐specified (primary and secondary) outcomes.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Eight studies had small sample sizes (< 40 participants). Only Lappin et al (Lappin 2003) had adequate sample size (N = 117). A majority of the studies had short‐term follow‐ups and follow‐ups were restricted to immediate post‐treatment assessments. Two studies had differences in baseline characteristics amongst the two groups (Miller 2007; Nielsen 1996).

    Effects of interventions

    As mentioned earlier, a meta‐analysis was not performed due to heterogeneity of included studies. The included studies used a range of non pharmacological approaches to treat spasticity in people with MS, as well as various measures assessing other MS‐related symptoms. Outcome measures which were not directly related to treating spasticity e.g. cognition, bladder function, fatigue were excluded. A summary of the findings of the included trials is presented below. See Table 3 for summary of results and conclusions reported by the authors of the individual studies.

    Physical activity programme

    Four studies (Giovannelli 2007; Mori 2011; Schyns 2009; Velkonja 2010) evaluated the impact of different types of physical activity programmes (including structured physiotherapy, exercise programme and sports climbing). Three studies evaluated these programmes in conjunction with other interventions: BoNT (Giovannelli 2007), iTBS (Mori 2011) and vibration therapy (Schyns 2009).

    One study (Giovannelli 2007) investigated whether additional physiotherapy increases BoNT effects in reducing spasticity in people with MS. This trial included 38 people with secondary progressive MS affected by focal spasticity. The intervention group received BoNT and additional daily physiotherapy for 15 days, encompassing passive or active exercise and stretching regimens. The control group received only BoNT. The treatment group compared with the control group, showed a significant decrease in spasticity, measured by MAS score: at week two (2.73 versus 3.22), week four (2.64 versus 3.33) and week 12 (2.68 versus 3.33), (P < 0.01 for all time points). There was also a significant difference in MAS score between baseline and end of 12 weeks follow‐up in favour of the treatment group (mean difference (MD) (%) –0.95 (26.1) versus –0.28 (7.7), P < 0.01). The combined treatment was also more effective in reducing spasticity symptoms as measured by visual analogue scale at week four (6.95 versus 5.50, P < 0.01).

    One double‐blind, sham‐controlled trial (Mori 2011) investigated whether the combination of iTBS and a programme of exercise therapy improves motor disability in people with MS. Thirty participants were randomised into three different groups: iTBS plus exercise therapy, sham stimulation plus exercise therapy, and iTBS alone. The outcomes assessed before and after two weeks of treatment include: spasticity or tone using MAS and 88 items Multiple Sclerosis Spasticity Score questionnaire (MSSS‐88), fatigue using Fatigue Severity Scale (FSS), ADL using Barthel index (BI) and health‐related quality of life (HRQOL) using 54 items Multiple Sclerosis Quality of life inventory (MSQOL‐54). There was a significant improvement in MAS scores in the stimulated leg (2.1 ± 0.4 before treatment; 1.3 ± 0.4 after treatment; P < 0.05), MSSS‐88 (74.3 ± 11.4 before treatment; 53.2 ± 10.9 after treatment; P < 0.001), FSS (39.5 ± 4.2 before treatment; 31.6 ± 4.6 after treatment; P < 0.05), BI (92.5 ± 2.4 before treatment; 95.0 ± 1.85 after treatment; P < 0.05), and MSQOL‐54 physical health composite (59.7 ± 2.7 before treatment; 64.8 ± 2.7 after treatment; P < 0.05) scores after treatment in the iTBS plus exercise therapy group but not in the sham stimulation plus exercise therapy group. There was also a significant reduction of the MAS score in the iTBS group (3.3 ± 0.8 before treatment; 1.6 ± 0.8 after treatment; P < 0.05), while other measures of MS‐related disability were not affected by this treatment.

    Velkonja et al (Velkonja 2010) investigated the effects of two different contemporary aerobic physical activities: sports climbing and yoga (both compromising a series of stretching techniques, implementation of which demands body control and planning of complex movements) on spasticity, cognitive impairment, mood change and fatigue in people with MS. This trial included 20 participants with relapsing remitting or progressive MS randomly allocated to both groups for a 10‐week intervention period. There were no significant improvements in spasticity after both interventions (measured using MAS). In the sports climbing group there was a significant reduction in EDSS pyramidal functions score (25% reduction post‐treatment, P = 0.046), while in the yoga group there was a significant increase in selective attention performance (17% improvement post‐treatment, P = 0.005). There was also a significant reduction in fatigue in the sports climbing group (32.5%, P = 0.015), while yoga had no effect.

    One trial (Schyns 2009), which investigated the effects of the combination of vibration therapy with or without exercise programme, is summarised below under the 'Vibration therapy' subheading.

    Repetitive magnetic stimulation

    Three studies investigated the effects of repetitive magnetic stimulation on spasticity in people with MS: two iTBS (Mori 2010; Mori 2011) and one rTMS (Nielsen 1996).

    One trial (Mori 2011) investigated the effects of combination of iTBS and a programme of exercise therapy (summarised above under 'Physical activity programme' subheading).

    One trial (Mori 2010) evaluated the effectiveness of two weeks of daily sessions of iTBS in people with MS with lower limb spasticity. Twenty participants were pseudo‐randomised to either real or sham iTBS protocol. The compound motor action potentials and H reflex amplitude ratio (a reliable neurophysiological index of spinal excitability) and the MAS for spasticity were evaluated by blinded raters before and after the stimulation protocols. Compared with the sham control group, there was a significant reduction in MAS score from baseline in the intervention group at both one week after stimulation (9.86 ± 3.13 versus 7.14 ± 4.03; P < 0.05) and two weeks after the end of stimulation protocol (9.86 ± 3.13 versus 6.86 ± 3.20, P < 0.05). Similarly, participants receiving real iTBS showed a significant reduction in compound motor action potentials and H reflex amplitude ratio (74.6% ± 0.15%, P < 0.05 ) one week after the stimulation and persisting up to two weeks after the end of stimulation protocol (82.3% ± 0.22%, P < 0.05). There were no significant effects for sham stimulation.

    One double‐blind placebo‐controlled study (Nielsen 1996) evaluated the effect of rTMS on spasticity in 38 people with MS. Participants were given one session of 16 stimuli of 25Hz over the leg motor area and one session of five minutes rTMS twice daily for seven consecutive days. At day one post‐treatment: MAS score improved significantly in the treatment group compared with the control group (mean change ‐3.3 ± 4.7 AU versus 0.7 ± 2.5 AU, P = 0.003); self‐score of ease of daily day activities improved significantly in both groups (P < 0.05 for both groups), but there was no difference between the groups. The threshold of the stretch reflex also increased significantly in the treatment group compared with the control group (4.3 ± 7.5deg/s versus ‐3.8 ± 9.7deg/s, P = 0.001). At day eight post‐treatment, there was no statistically significant difference in clinical spasticity score or self‐score of ease of daily day activities between the two groups, however, the threshold of the stretch reflex remained improved significantly in the treatment group compared to the control group (4.4 ± 7.5deg/s versus ‐1.8 ± 8.5deg/s, P = 0.028). At day 16 post‐treatment, none of the scores remained statistically significant between the two groups.

    Electromagnetic therapy

    Two studies evaluated the effectiveness of electromagnetic therapy: one a pulsed electromagnetic therapy (Lappin 2003); and one magnetic pulsing device (Enermed) (Richards 1997).

    One multi‐site, double‐blind, placebo‐controlled, cross‐over trial (Lappin 2003) evaluated the effects of a pulsed electromagnetic therapy on MS‐related fatigue, spasticity, bladder control, and overall QOL. This trial included 117 participants, who received four weeks of the active and placebo treatments separated by a two weeks washout period. There was significantly greater improvement in fatigue (P = 0.04) and QOL (P = 0.03) in the active device group compared with the control group. There were no significant differences in treatment effects for bladder control (P = 0.26) or disability composite (P = 0.77). The results for spasticity were mixed, indicating no significant difference in the treatment effects on daily diary ratings, however, muscle spasm/spasticity measured using the MSQLI measure at the end of each sessions showed statistically significant differences (P = 0.04). The three scales (fatigue, pain and spasticity) used to create the quality of life index (QLI) showed moderate inter‐correlations (r = 0.32 to 0.60).

    One double‐blind trial (Richards 1997) evaluated clinical and subclinical effects of a magnetic pulsing device (Enermed) (with frequency of the magnetic pulse of 4‐13 Hz range) on disease activity in people with MS. Thirty people with MS randomly received either an active Enermed device (intervention group) or a similar magnetically inactive device (control group) on preselected sites between 10 and 24 hours a day for two months. There was a significant improvement in the performance scale (PS) combined rating for bladder control, cognitive function, fatigue level, mobility, spasticity, and vision in the treatment group compared with the control group (‐3.83 ± 1.08 versus ‐0.17 ± 1.07, change in PS scale, P < 0.005). There was also a significant change between pre‐treatment and post‐treatment in alpha EEG magnitude during the language task.

    Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS)

    One single‐blind, cross‐over trial (Miller 2007), evaluated the effectiveness of TENS on spasticity in people with MS. Thirty‐two people with MS were randomly allocated into two groups: two weeks of 60 minutes (Group 1) and eight hours daily (Group 2) of TENS applications (100 Hz and 0.125 ms pulse width). There were no statistically significant differences in the Global Spasticity Score following either 60 minutes or eight hours daily of TENS (P = 0.433 and 0.217, respectively). However, compared to the short 60 minutes application, eight hours application time led to a significant reduction in muscle spasm (P = 0.038) and pain (P = 0.008).

    Vibration therapy

    One trial (Schyns 2009) evaluated the effectiveness of a Whole Body Vibration (WBV) programme on tone, muscle force, sensation and functional ability in MS. Sixteen participants were randomly allocated into two groups: Group 1 received four weeks of WBV plus exercise three times per week, two weeks of no intervention and then four weeks of exercise alone three times per week, while Group 2 received the two treatment interventions in the reverse order to Group 1. the exercise programme had positive effects on muscle force and well‐being, but there was insufficient evidence that the addition of WBV provided any further benefit. There was no difference in MAS score in either intervention. However, for each group, the combination of WBV and exercises showed significant reduction on MSSS‐88 sub‐scales of muscle spasms (P = 0.02) and pain (P = 0.036). No statistically significant changes were observed for other MSSS‐88 components (ADL, social functioning, stiffness, gait, body movement and emotional health). There were some improvements in functional abilities measured by 10‐metre walk and Timed Up and Go Test, which were statistically not significant (P = 0.56; P =0.70, respectively).

    Discussion

    A wide range of non pharmacological interventions are used for the treatment of spasticity in people with MS. The interventions investigated in this review include: physical activity programmes, magnetic stimulation therapies, electromagnetic therapies and vibration therapies. The studies generally were of poor quality design and were underpowered. There was 'low level' of evidence for non pharmacological interventions such as physical activities given in conjunction with other interventions (such as BoNT, iTBS); magnetic stimulation and electromagnetic therapies as beneficial for spasticity in people with MS. There was no evidence of benefit for the use of other non pharmacological interventions (such as TENS, sports climbing, vibration therapy) in treating spasticity in this population.

    Summary of main results

    The nine included studies evaluated a range of non pharmacological interventions for treatment of spasticity in people with MS. The included trials were heterogeneous in terms of: type of intervention, outcome measures, and study quality. Hence, quantitative synthesis was not possible and a qualitative synthesis of 'best evidence' was provided.

    There is 'low level' evidence for:

    • addition of active physiotherapy after BoNT injection in reducing spasticity up to 12 weeks;

    • iTBS as a single intervention or in combination with exercise therapy reduced spasticity after two weeks of treatment;

    • short‐term benefits of rTMS for improved spasticity, functional abilities and stretch reflex thresholds;

    • short‐term beneficial effects of pulsing magnetic fields for spasticity, bladder control, cognitive function, fatigue level, mobility, and vision. 

    There is no evidence of benefit in the reported studies that TENS, sports climbing, and WBV (with or without an exercise programme) provide any additional improvements in spasticity in people with MS.

    The quality of evidence is reduced by the limited number of studies and the methodological weakness identified (underpowered, had high‐risk of bias with small sample size, short follow‐up periods, lack of rigorous methodology and variable use of outcome measures. Subgroup analysis for type of MS, disease severity (EDSS scores); disease duration and affected site i.e. upper limb, lower limb or both was not possible due to lack of data.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Despite a wide search strategy only nine RCTs evaluating different forms of non‐pharmacological interventions were included. The three abstracts identified were not published as full studies and we were not able to get the further information from the primary authors. We are not aware of any other ongoing or unpublished studies.

    The included studies used a variety of outcome measures for spasticity, the most common being the MAS. These outcome measures are summarised in Characteristics of included studies. Due to the limited number of studies and quality of the existing studies, many aspects of non pharmacological interventions for MS spasticity remain unproven. This is consistent with the published guidelines for spasticity management (Haselkorn 2005; MS Australia 2009) and a recent review of spasticity management in stroke population (Demetrios 2012).

    Studies where spasticity was measured incidentally; i.e. primary goal of the study was not to reduce spasticity, may have been missed or not reported.

    Quality of the evidence

    The nine included trials were rated as 'low' for methodological quality due to substantial flaws in their methodological design with a high risk of bias related to randomisation procedure, blinding protocols and outcome analysis. Further, all studies failed to give sufficient detail to assess their potential risk of bias in some areas of methodological quality domains, and correspondence was not received from the authors (Figure 2). The limitations include:

    • lack of reporting of definition of spasticity;

    • high risk of selection bias as allocation concealment was described in only two studies (Lappin 2003; Richards 1997);

    • lack of description of randomisation procedure, reported in only three studies (Giovannelli 2007; Lappin 2003; Schyns 2009);

    • high risk of performance bias due to: non‐blinding of the study participants and treating personnel; described only in three studies (Lappin 2003; Nielsen 1996; Richards 1997); only five studies took measures to blind outcome assessors (Giovannelli 2007; Lappin 2003; Mori 2010; Richards 1997; Schyns 2009); none of the studies attempted to test the success of patient blinding by questionnaire;

    • most studies were underpowered with small sample sizes;

    • generalisability of the results is limited, as most studies recruited participants from a single centre with strict inclusion and exclusion criteria;

    • lack of an intention‐to‐treat analysis protocol;

    • lack of longer‐term follow‐up to detect effect of intervention;

    • difficulty controlling for patients' personal and other confounding factors, which influence patient‐therapist interaction, compliance, and delivery of therapy, thus impacting on outcomes, such as, patient motivation and self‐efficacy, comorbidity and activity level outside of therapy programmes, (not assessed in any of the studies).

    Potential biases in the review process

    The conclusions from this review are limited due to the small number of studies of poor methodological quality that investigated a range of non pharmacological interventions as described above. In addition, the review authors recognise a number of limitations in the methodology of the review itself, and the completeness of the retrieved literature.

    1. We were not able to rule out some degree of selection bias from the literature search (Van Tulder 2003), given that the search strategy principally encompassed the cited literature, despite the extended range of terms that were used to capture the widest possible selection of the relevant literature.

    2. We were not able to rule out publication bias as we cannot exclude the possibility that there have been negative trials that have not reached the published literature (Egger 1998).

    3. Reference bias (Goetzsche 1987) is a further possible confounder, as our search strategy included searching of reference lists within the relevant papers for other possible articles missed in our electronic searches.

    We therefore welcome contact from any readers who are aware of important high quality studies that would meet the criteria for this review, but are so far not included.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    The findings of this review highlight the existing gaps in literature and emphasise the importance, albeit finding some support for non pharmacological interventions for spasticity in MS. These findings are consistent with existing reviews for the management MS symptoms (Beard 2003; Rietberg 2007; Stevenson 2010).
