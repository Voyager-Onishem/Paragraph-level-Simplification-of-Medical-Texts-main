Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) for rhabdomyolysis

  • Xiaoxi Zeng
  • Ling Zhang
  • Taixiang Wu
  • Ping Fu
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 15 June 2014 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD008566.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی
  • Français

    • Background

    Rhabdomyolysis is a condition that is characterised by the breakdown of skeletal muscle tissue and leakage of intracellular myocyte contents into circulating blood. Rhabdomyolysis can lead to acute kidney injury (AKI) and is a potentially life‐threatening condition. Studies have indicated that continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) may provide benefits for people with rhabdomyolysis by removing potentially damaging myoglobin and stabilising haemodynamic and metabolic status.

    Objectives

    We aimed to: i) assess the efficacy of CRRT in removing myoglobin; ii) investigate the influence of CRRT on mortality and kidney‐related outcomes; and iii) evaluate the safety of CRRT for the treatment of people with rhabdomyolysis.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Renal Group's Specialised Register to 6 January 2014 through contact with the Trials' Search Co‐ordinator using search terms relevant to this review. We also searched China National Knowledge Infrastructure (from 1 January 1979 to 16 April 2013) and the Chinese Clinical Trials Register (to 16 April 2013).

    Selection criteria

    All randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs that investigated clinical outcomes of CRRT for people with rhabdomyolysis were included.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two authors independently assessed studies for inclusion and extracted data. We derived risk ratios (RR) for dichotomous data and mean differences (MD) for continuous data with 95% confidence intervals (CI). Methodological risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane risk of bias tool.

    Main results

    Of the three included studies (101 participants), one evaluated continuous arteriovenous haemodialysis and two investigated continuous venovenous haemofiltration; all included conventional therapy as control.

    We found significant decreases in myoglobin in patients among whom CRRT therapy was initiated on days four, eight, and 10 (day 4: MD ‐11.00 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐20.65 to ‐1.35; Day 8: MD ‐23.00 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐30.92 to ‐15.08; day 10: MD ‐341.87 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐626.15 to ‐57.59) compared with those who underwent conventional therapy.

    Although CRRT was associated with improved serum creatinine, blood urea nitrogen, and potassium levels; reduced duration of the oliguria phase; and was associated with reduced time in hospital, no significant differences were found in mortality rates compared with conventional therapy (RR 0.17, 95% CI 0.02 to 1.37). The included studies did not report on long‐term outcomes or prevention of AKI.

    Overall, we found that study quality was suboptimal: blinding and randomisation allocation were not reported by any of the included studies, leading to the possibility of selection, performance and detection bias.

    Authors' conclusions

    Although CRRT may provide some benefits for people with rhabdomyolysis, the poor methodological quality of the included studies and lack of data relating to clinically important outcomes limited our findings about the effectiveness of CRRT for people with rhabdomyolysis.

    There was insufficient evidence to discern any likely benefits of CRRT over conventional therapy for people with rhabdomyolysis and prevention of rhabdomyolysis‐induced AKI.

    • Is continuous renal replacement therapy beneficial for people with rhabdomyolysis?

    Rhabdomyolysis is a potentially life‐threatening condition where damaged muscle tissue breaks down quickly, and products of damaged muscle cells are released into the bloodstream. Of these products, a protein called myoglobin is harmful to kidney health and can lead to acute kidney injury. There is some evidence to suggest that continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) may provide benefits for people with rhabdomyolysis.

    We searched the literature published before 6 January 2014, and after assessment, included three small studies that involved 101 participants. Our analysis found that although CRRT showed limited advantages over conventional treatment to improve some aspects of kidney function and muscle tissue loss, we found no significant benefits in reducing risk of death.

    The small body of available evidence demonstrated poor methodological quality, and was insufficient to enable us to make any robust conclusions about the effectiveness of CRRT for people with rhabdomyolysis. Larger and better designed studies would be needed to investigate if CRRT is beneficial for people with rhabdomyolysis.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Data from three small RCTs indicated that although mortality and adverse events did not differ significantly among patients treated with CRRT and conventional therapy, CRRT may be more effective in removing myoglobin and modifying parameters related to muscle lysis and kidney function. Poor methodological quality and absence of some clinically important measurements from the included studies were significant limitations. We were unable to conclude whether or not CRRT is a safe and effective option to treat people with rhabdomyolysis.

    Implications for research

    High quality RCTs are needed to evaluate the use of CRRT to treat people with rhabdomyolysis and prevent AKI among this patient population.

    Future studies should ensure that parameters influencing outcomes such as surgical interventions, sepsis, and multiple organ dysfunction syndrome are well considered when defining inclusion and exclusion criteria. Study authors should also ensure that clinically‐relevant outcomes such as mortality, kidney function recovery (in terms of glomerular function and other parameters), myoglobin clearance rate, correction of pathophysiological processes involved in rhabdomyolysis (i.e. muscle cell lysis and electrolyte disturbances); and adverse events are clearly documented and reported. Future studies should also assess prevention or improvement of AKI in people with rhabdomyolysis that are attributable to CRRT, and influences of CRRT mode, dose, frequency, timing of initiation, and duration on kidney function.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Rhabdomyolysis is a clinical and biochemical syndrome in which intracellular myocyte content leaks into extracellular fluid and the bloodstream after rapid breakdown (lysis) of skeletal muscle tissue (Huerta‐Alardin 2005; Vanholder 2000). The aetiology of skeletal muscle injury in rhabdomyolysis is diverse, including direct muscle injury, muscle overuse, ischaemia, infection, metabolic disorders, inflammatory myopathies, toxins, and drugs (Khosla 2003). In the USA, the incidence of rhabdomyolysis is about 2 per 10,000 person‐years (Nicholas 2007). Between 13% and 50% of people with rhabdomyolysis develop acute kidney injury (AKI) (Bosch 2009). It has been estimated that rhabdomyolysis may account for 7% to 10% of all AKI in the USA (Bagley 2007). Up to 85% of people with major traumatic injuries have some degree of rhabdomyolysis (Huerta‐Alardin 2005).

    Clinical presentation of rhabdomyolysis can vary significantly. About half of adult patients present with muscle weakness, muscle pain (myalgia), and dark urine (Gabow 1982; Watemberg 2000). Rhabdomyolysis is indicated by elevated serum creatine kinase levels greater than 1000 U/L (Mannix 2006; Melli 2005), which is more than five times the upper limit of normal (Gabow 1982). Urine dipstick tests are often positive for blood with either no or few red blood cells seen under microscopy (Vanholder 2000). Presence of elevated serum and urine myoglobin levels may be helpful indicators for diagnosing rhabdomyolysis (Hunter 2006). Other diagnostic indicators include electrolyte disturbances, such as hypocalcaemia and hyperkalaemia, and increased serum phosphorous. Increased blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, hyperuricaemia, and high anion acidosis may also be observed in people with rhabdomyolysis‐induced AKI (Vanholder 2000). 

    Description of the intervention

    Rhabdomyolysis management aims to treat the underlying cause and prevent kidney dysfunction by counteracting the potentially toxic effects of myoglobin.

    The cornerstone of treatment is expansion of extracellular volume. Early and vigorous intravenous volume replacement prevents both hypovolaemic shock and rhabdomyolysis‐induced AKI (Better 1990). Sodium bicarbonate therapy has been recommended in some settings, although benefits have not yet been established. Therapy involves administering normal saline and sodium bicarbonate as fluid is replenished in people with rhabdomyolysis, especially those with metabolic acidosis (Bosch 2009). Where urinary flow is sufficient, fluid therapy can be complemented by administration of mannitol (a sugar alcohol) (Huerta‐Alardin 2005; Vanholder 2000). Definitive benefits of furosemide (a diuretic) for rhabdomyolysis have not been demonstrated (Bosch 2009).

    Renal replacement therapy (RRT) is indicated when uraemic encephalopathy, deteriorating kidney function, uncontrolled hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and fluid overload occur due to rhabdomyolysis and AKI (Vanholder 2000). RRT modes include continuous RRT (CRRT) and intermittent RRT. CRRT mechanisms for solute removal involve haemodialysis (diffuse, small solute transport), haemofiltration (convective small and medium solute transport), or a combination of both. CRRT enables continuous removal of solutes and gradual correction of fluid overload (Paganini 2007). Some CRRT modes, such as continuous haemofiltration and haemodiafiltration, may be beneficial for people with rhabdomyolysis because they can remove circulating myoglobin and other nephrotoxic metabolites (Ronco 2005).

    How the intervention might work

    Many people with rhabdomyolysis experience low blood pressure and shock due to bloodstream fluid depletion. Incidence of sepsis, inflammatory processes and multiple organ dysfunction syndromes are high in people with crush syndrome, a class of rhabdomyolysis caused by traumatic compression of muscles (Erek 2002). Measures should be taken to prevent further kidney damage or tubular obstruction, altered intra‐renal haemodynamics, and tubular cell dysfunction caused by myoglobin for people with rhabdomyolysis. Myoglobin (molecular weight 17,000 Da), which is compatible with convective removal (Amyot 1999; Nicolau 1998; Ronco 2005), can be transported to the filtrate by continuous haemofiltration and haemodiafiltration (Bastani 1997; Bellomo 1991; Berns 1991; Nicolau 1996; Winterberg 1990). Compared with intermittent haemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis, CRRT is better able to maintain stable haemodynamic and homeostasis status, and remove myoglobin and inflammatory mediators (Baldwin 2007; De Vriese 1999;Ronco 2002). CRRT may therefore be a better choice for blood purification than intermittent RRT in the management of people with rhabdomyolysis. 

    Why it is important to do this review

    Despite its theoretical advantages over other forms of RRT for rhabdomyolysis, the measurable benefits, appropriate dose and timing of CRRT initiation remain controversial. Moreover, it has been argued that the need for continuous anticoagulation is disadvantageous, especially among traumatised patients (Vanholder 2000). The absence of reviews that have assessed CRRT safety and efficacy for rhabdomyolysis prompted us to evaluate the available evidence to establish its benefits and harms for people with rhabdomyolysis.

    Objectives

    We aimed to investigate the following for the treatment of people with rhabdomyolysis.

    • Assess the efficacy of CRRT in removing myoglobin

    • Investigate the influence of CRRT on mortality and kidney‐related outcomes

    • Evaluate the safety of CRRT.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (RCTs in which allocation to treatment was obtained by alternation, use of alternate medical records, date of birth or other predictable methods) investigating the clinical outcomes of CRRT for rhabdomyolysis, irrespective of blinding, publication status, or language.

    Types of participants

    People of any age or sex who had been diagnosed with rhabdomyolysis attributable to any cause were eligible for inclusion. Diagnostic criteria for rhabdomyolysis included:

    • elevated serum creatine kinase (> 1000 U/L or more than five times the upper limit of normal);

    • positive urine dipstick test for blood with the absence of red blood cells in the urine sediment; and

    • presence of elevated serum and urine myoglobin levels, creatinine and BUN; hypocalcaemia, hyperkalaemia, and acidosis.

    We excluded patients with chronic kidney disease and those who had received RRT before randomisation.

    Types of interventions

    The following interventions were eligible for inclusion.

    • CRRT alone or combined with supportive therapies such as hydration, alkalisation, and diuretics

    • Continuous venovenous haemofiltration (CVVH), continuous arteriovenous haemofiltration (CAVH), continuous venovenous haemodiafiltration (CVVHDF), continuous arteriovenous haemodiafiltration (CAVHDF), continuous venovenous haemodialysis (CVVHD) and continuous arteriovenous haemodialysis (CAVHD) for rhabdomyolysis

    • Control treatments could be supportive therapies alone, such as hydration, alkalisation, and diuretics, or supportive therapies with intermittent haemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis.

    Adequate descriptions of timing for initiation, mode, dose, CRRT membrane type, dialysis type, and details of supportive therapies were required to support subgroup and sensitivity analyses when possible.

    Studies investigating other forms of extracorporeal blood purification, such as plasmapheresis or hybrid RRT, were excluded.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • All‐cause mortality

    • Length of survival

    • Length of hospital stay

    • Prevention or improvement of AKI (yes/no); recovery from AKI (yes/no).

    Secondary outcomes

    • Severity of skeletal muscle cell injuries: serum myoglobin and creatine kinase levels; myoglobin and creatine kinase clearance rates

    • Kidney function measures: glomerular filtration rate (GFR), creatinine, BUN and uric acid levels

    • Electrolyte measures: serum potassium, sodium, calcium and phosphate levels

    • Inflammatory process measures: cytokines, C‐reactive protein and white blood cell counts

    • Other laboratory examinations related to disease progression, such as haemoglobin, aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine transaminase (ALT), and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) levels

    • Symptom improvement

    • Adverse events: cardiovascular instability (e.g. arrhythmia, hypotension and shock); bleeding events; catheter infection or sepsis; or others such as hypothermia, thrombocytopenia, hypoglycaemia or hypophosphataemia

    • Health‐related quality of life.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Renal Group's Specialised Register to 6 January 2014 through contact with the Trials' Search Co‐ordinator using search terms relevant to this review. The Cochrane Renal Group’s specialised register contains studies identified from the following sources.

    • Monthly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL)

    • Weekly searches of MEDLINE OVID SP

    • Handsearching of renal‐related journals and the proceedings of major renal conferences

    • Searching of the current year of EMBASE OVID SP

    • Weekly current awareness alerts for selected renal journals

    • Searches of the International Clinical Trials Register (ICTRP) Search Portal and ClinicalTrials.gov.

    Studies contained in the Specialised Register are identified through search strategies for CENTRAL, MEDLINE, and EMBASE based on the scope of the Cochrane Renal Group. Details of these strategies, as well as a list of handsearched journals, conference proceedings and current awareness alerts, are available in the specialised register section of information about the Cochrane Renal Group.

    See Appendix 1 for search terms used in strategies for this review.

    Searching other resources

    • Chinese Clinical Trials Register (ChiCTR) for unpublished and ongoing studies (from 1 January 1979 to 16 April 2013)

    • China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI) (up to 16 April 2013).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    The search strategy described was used to obtain titles and abstracts of studies that could be relevant to the review. Two authors (ZX, ZL) independently assessed study eligibility. Excluded studies were listed with reasons for exclusion, and studies that might include relevant data were retained initially. The same authors independently assessed retrieved abstracts, and if necessary the full text, to determine studies eligible for inclusion. Disagreements were resolved by discussion or arbitration with a third author (WT). We planned to contact study authors if information about methodology or data were unclear or missing.

    Data extraction and management

    Two authors (ZX, ZL) independently extracted data using standard data extraction forms which were pilot tested (Higgins 2011). If necessary, studies reported in languages other than English or Chinese would have been translated before assessment. We collected data on demographic and clinical characteristics of participants, methods, interventions, outcomes and other information to inform assessment of bias and conflicts of interest, such as sources of support. Where more than one publication of one study existed, we planned to group reports together and only the publication with the most complete data would have been included. Where relevant outcomes were only published in earlier versions, these data were used. Any discrepancy between published versions would have been highlighted. If data from the published studies were insufficient or missing, we planned to correspond with the original authors for additional information and for clarification of any ambiguous descriptions or missing data.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    The following items were independently assessed by two authors using the risk of bias assessment tool (Higgins 2011) (see Appendix 2).

    • Was there adequate sequence generation (selection bias)?

    • Was allocation adequately concealed (selection bias)?

    • Was knowledge of the allocated interventions adequately prevented during the study (detection bias)?

      • Participants and personnel

      • Outcome assessors

    • Were incomplete outcome data adequately addressed (attrition bias)?

    • Are reports of the study free of suggestion of selective outcome reporting (reporting bias)?

    • Was the study apparently free of other problems that could put it at a risk of bias?

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous data (all‐cause mortality and adverse events), results were expressed as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (95% CI). For continuous data (length of hospitalisation, laboratory data, myoglobin and creatine kinase clearance rates), we reported mean differences (MD) or standardised mean differences (SMD) with 95% confidence intervals (95% CI).

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cross‐over and cluster RCTs were not included in this review. Although we planned to meta‐analyse studies with multiple intervention groups to create a single pair‐wise comparison where clinically appropriate (Higgins 2011), none of the included studies investigated more than two interventions. Repeated observations from more than one time point were considered as different outcomes, and were compared in separate analyses.

    Dealing with missing data

    Attempts were made to contact the original authors of all included studies for missing data, but we received a response from only one investigator (Zeng 2008). Data obtained were included in the analysis. The principal investigator of an ongoing trial (NCT00391911) replied that although a pilot study had been completed, the manuscript was not yet ready for publication.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    Heterogeneity was analysed using a Chi² test on N‐1 degrees of freedom, with an alpha of 0.10 used for statistical significance and with the I² test (Higgins 2003). I² values of 25%, 50% and 75% corresponded to low, medium and high levels of heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We planned to assess reporting biases by constructing funnel plots. The small number of included studies meant that this was not feasible (Higgins 2011).

    Data synthesis

    Data were pooled using the random‐effects model.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to perform subgroup analysis to investigate potential sources of heterogeneity, such as demographic and clinical characteristics of participants, interventions and study quality. Heterogeneity among participants related to rhabdomyolysis pathogens, age, and comorbidities. Heterogeneity in interventions related to mode, dose, and duration of haemodialysis. The following subgroup analyses were planned.

    1. Cause of rhabdomyolysis: physical and non‐physical

    2. CRRT mode: CAVH/CVVH, CAVHDF/CVVHDF, CAVHD/CVVHD

    3. Dose, timing of initiation and/or duration of therapy.

    We also planned to make comparisons among mode, dose, timing for initiation, and duration of CRRT therapy to identify optimal CRRT prescribing for rhabdomyolysis if adequate data were available.

    Sensitivity analysis

    Proposed a priori subgroup analysis was planned to explore clinical heterogeneity. If an adequate number of studies had been included in this review, we planned to perform sensitivity analysis by repeating the meta‐analysis after removing studies assessed at high risk of bias.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    The search strategy described identified 14 references; we found another 75 references from Chinese language databases. After removing duplicates and irrelevant articles, 14 studies remained. Of these, NCT00391911 was classified as an ongoing study, and El Wakil 2005 is awaiting classification (we attempted to contact the investigators of El Wakil 2005, but had not received response at the time of publication of this review). We assessed 14 full‐text studies, and after exclusions, included three studies in our analysis. All included studies were published in Chinese language.     Included studies

    See Characteristics of included studies.

    The three included studies (Dong 2005; Wang 2008; Zeng 2008) involved 101 participants (male/female: 69/32; age range: 18 to 67 years). The studies were small: sample size ranged from 22 to 43 participants.

    Dong 2005 (36 participants) and Wang 2008 (43 participants) included people diagnosed with crush syndrome who had histories of being involved in traffic accidents, physical abuse and non‐traumatic compression. These participants had abnormal urine sediment or serum myoglobin findings, presented with acute kidney dysfunction (such as oliguria or anuria), and elevated serum BUN and creatinine levels. Zeng 2008 enrolled 22 participants with rhabdomyolysis related to traffic accidents, drug use, severe infection or exercise‐related injuries. Diagnosis was based on abnormal urine test results, creatine kinase elevation, and development of AKI. 

    Wang 2008 compared the effect and safety of conventional therapy (including surgical intervention when necessary) with conventional therapy plus CAVHD (12 to 24 hours/d; dialysis fluid volume 2000 to 3000 mL/h; blood flow volume 150 to 200 mL/min; with heparin as anticoagulant therapy).

    Dong 2005 and Zeng 2008 investigated conventional therapy (including surgical interventions and intermittent haemodialysis as appropriate) and conventional therapy plus CVVH (10 to 24 h/d; replacement fluid volume 2000 to 3000 mL/h; blood flow volume 120 to 200 mL/min; with heparin/low molecular weight heparin as anticoagulant therapy).

    Excluded studies

    See Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Eleven studies were excluded: seven were not RCTs (Fu 2010; Liao 2006; Meng 2011Peng 2007; Timokhov 1997; Uchino 2005; Warren 2002), and four were investigated the wrong intervention (Dilek 2000; Nakamura 2000c; Peltonen 2007; Wakabayashi 1994).

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Risk of bias was assessed as high: blinding and allocation concealment methods were not reported in the included studies. All other reporting was suboptimal. Summary and assessment of risk of bias are depicted in Figure 2 and Figure 3.

    Allocation

    Although all included studies claimed that participants were randomised to intervention and control groups, none provided adequate descriptions of allocation sequencing generation methods or allocation concealment. Our assessment of information provided by the authors lead us to conclude that Zeng 2008 was a quasi‐RCT.

    Blinding

    Most reported outcomes were laboratory‐tested parameters, and therefore, were unlikely to be influenced by blinding. However, all studies reported length of hospital stays, which may have been affected by subjective opinions of healthcare providers involved in the studies. No blinding was reported in the included studies.

    Incomplete outcome data

    None of the studies described follow‐up and drop‐outs and withdrawals were not reported. Wang 2008 and Zeng 2008 listed the numbers of participants who were assessed at study end. We assumed that results of all included participants were reported in both studies.

    Selective reporting

    None of the included studies reported details of the study protocols or prespecified outcomes. There were insufficient data available to enable an assessment of selective reporting to be made.

    Other potential sources of bias

    The planned construction of funnel plots could not be undertaken because of the small number of included studies. This may influence valid interpretation of real asymmetry from chance (Higgins 2011).

    Wang 2008 did not report baseline data for intervention and control group participants.

    None of the studies indicated if conventional therapies were comparable between study and control groups.

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings for the main comparison

    All‐cause mortality

    Wang 2008 reported compared with conventional therapy, CAVHD did not significantly influence mortality (Analysis 1.1 (1 study, 43 participants): RR 0.17, 95% CI 0.02 to 1.37).

    Length of hospital stay

    All studies reported length of hospital stay. Among patients treated with CAVHD (Analysis 1.2.1 (1 study, 43 participants): MD ‐8.20 (days), 95% CI ‐12.80 to ‐3.60) or CVVH (Analysis 1.2.2 (2 studies, 58 participants): MD ‐9.69 (days), 95% CI ‐14.16 to ‐5.22; I² = 28%) hospital stays were significantly shorter than conventional therapy.

    Serum myoglobin and creatine kinase levels

    Dong 2005 reported that serum myoglobin was significantly lower on day 4 (Analysis 1.3.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐11.00 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐20.65 to ‐1.35) and day 8 (Analysis 1.3.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐23.00 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐30.92 to ‐15.08) among CVVH group participants compared with the control group. Zeng 2008 reported that both serum myoglobin (Analysis 1.3.3 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐341.87 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐626.15 to ‐57.59) and creatine kinase (Analysis 1.4 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐1726.42 (μg/L), 95% CI ‐3135.38 to ‐317.46) were significantly decreased on day 10 of treatment among intervention group participants. 

    Kidney function measures

    None of the studies reported whether CRRT influenced incidence of myoglobin‐induced AKI in people with rhabdomyolysis, nor percentages of participants who had complete or partial recovery of kidney function.

    Zeng 2008 reported that participants who underwent CVVH experienced shorter oliguria periods than those who received conventional therapy alone (Analysis 1.5 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐11.10 (days), 95% CI ‐13.29 to ‐8.91). Serum creatinine (Analysis 1.6.3 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐160.92 (μmol/L), 95% CI ‐295.95 to ‐25.89) and BUN (Analysis 1.7.3 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐4.94 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐8.03 to ‐1.85) were measured on day 10; both were lower in intervention group participants.

    Dong 2005 reported that serum creatinine was significantly lower in CVVH group participants compared with the control group on day 4 (Analysis 1.6.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐24.00 (μmol/L), 95% CI ‐37.57 to ‐10.43) and day 8 (Analysis 1.6.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐76.00 (μmol/L), 95% CI ‐88.09 to ‐63.91). Similar results were observed in serum BUN level on day 4 (Analysis 1.7.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐6.80 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐7.94 to ‐5.66) and day 8 (Analysis 1.7.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐8.50 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐9.48 to ‐7.52).

    Electrolyte measures

    Dong 2005 reported significantly lower serum potassium levels on day 4 among CVVH group participants (Analysis 1.8.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐0.50 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐0.73 to ‐0.27) and day 8 (Analysis 1.8.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐0.50 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐0.81 to ‐0.19). Zeng 2008 reported the CVVH group had lower potassium levels than control group participants on day 10 of treatment (Analysis 1.8.3 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐0.77 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐1.31 to ‐0.23).

    Dong 2005 reported no significant difference in serum calcium either on day 4 (Analysis 1.9.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD 0.30 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐0.21 to 0.81) or day 8 (Analysis 1.9.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD 0.40 (mmol/L), 95% CI ‐0.06 to 0.86).

    Other tests of disease progression

    Zeng 2008 measured ALT and AST on Day 10. Participants in the CVVH group had significantly lower ALT (Analysis 1.10 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐48.62 (IU/L), 95% CI ‐87.84 to ‐9.40) and AST (Analysis 1.11 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐178.37 (IU/L), 95% CI ‐328.70 to ‐28.04) levels than other study participants. Dong 2005 and Zeng 2008 reported white blood cell count data; counts were significantly lower among CVVH group participants than control group participants on day 4 (Analysis 1.12.1 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐2.30 (× 109/L), 95% CI ‐2.48 to ‐2.12), day 8 (Analysis 1.12.2 (1 study, 36 participants): MD ‐3.10 (× 109/L), 95% CI ‐3.33 to ‐2.87), and day 10 (Analysis 1.12.3 (1 study, 22 participants): MD ‐3.90 (× 109/L), 95% CI ‐7.01 to ‐0.79). 

    Adverse effects

    Dong 2005 reported adverse effects. Incidence of arrhythmia (Analysis 1.13.1 (1 study, 36 participants): RR 0.67, 95% CI 0.21 to 2.18), shock (Analysis 1.13.2 (1 study, 36 participants): RR 0.63, 95% CI 0.14 to 2.81) and gastrointestinal haemorrhage (Analysis 1.13.3 (1 study, 36 participants): RR 0.98, 95% CI 0.40 to 2.40) did not differ significantly between supportive therapy plus CVVH and supportive therapy alone.

    With the exception of reporting CRRT modalities relating to length of hospital stay, insufficient data reporting meant that further subgroup analyses could not be performed. Sensitivity analyses were not undertaken because of the small number of low quality included studies.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included three small, low quality studies (101 participants) in this review. These limitations meant that pooled analysis could be performed for length of hospital stay only. Although our analysis indicated that CRRT might offer some advantages over conventional therapy for people with rhabdomyolysis in terms of removing myoglobin, modifying parameters related to kidney dysfunction, and muscle lysis, CRRT was not found to reduce either mortality or rates of adverse events.

    The limitations of the included studies did not enable us to derive conclusive evidence about CRRT for people with rhabdomyolysis. Furthermore, some clinically significant parameters were not addressed by the included studies, such as length of survival, prevention of AKI, and kidney outcomes. The included studies did not provide sufficient demographic data and clinical characteristics of patients, particularly age, aetiology, timing and dosage of CRRT.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    This review presents analysis of the available clinical evidence concerning CAVHD and CVVH for people with rhabdomyolysis. The evidence was suboptimal in terms of completeness and applicability.

    Primary outcomes for analysis were not addressed adequately by the included studies. Mortality was reported in only one study that compared CAVHD with conventional therapy (Wang 2008). All‐cause mortality data were not available for patients treated with other forms of CRRT, notably CVVH, which is the most frequently used form of CRRT for critical AKI care (Susantitaphong 2011). None of the studies evaluated CRRT to prevent AKI, overall kidney function recovery or length of survival. Follow‐up in all studies was insufficient to determine kidney function outcomes. Adverse events were reported in only one study.

    Conventional therapy was provided for participants in both arms of the included studies. Fasciotomy was performed when necessary (as defined by the studies). Because surgical intervention can significantly influence survival and kidney function outcomes (Better 1990; Sever 2002), the effectiveness of CRRT could not be attributed with confidence.

    Information on divergent age, sex, and aetiological factors were not provided to enable subgroup analyses. Insufficient data reporting meant that CRRT dose, timing and duration could not be analysed.

    It is noteworthy that all studies included Chinese participants, which limited applicability of the evidence to other ethnic or racial groups.

    Quality of the evidence

    Low methodological quality and the small number of included studies meant that robust conclusions could not be derived concerning efficacy and safety of CRRT for rhabdomyolysis.

    Reporting of allocation concealment, blinding, and allocation sequencing generation methods were omitted from all studies. All included studies were published in Chinese journals, where such omissions have been found to be a common methodological flaw. Wu 2009 reported that only 6.8% of studies that were claimed to be RCTs in Chinese journals applied adequate randomisation methodologies.

    The included studies did not provide sufficient information to assess if they were free of selective reporting (Dong 2005; Wang 2008; Zeng 2008).

    Other methodological flaws were lack of reporting of drop‐out rates and reasons for withdrawal (Dong 2005), and omission of baseline comparisons between study and control group participants (Wang 2008).

    Potential biases in the review process

    Although efforts were made to identify relevant studies, it is possible that unpublished studies exist. The limited number of included studies meant that we were unable to construct funnel plots to explore reporting biases. Our searches did not identify protocols for any of the included studies, and hence, we were unable to assess selective outcome reporting.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Our search did not identify any other reviews that evaluated CRRT effectiveness and safety for people with rhabdomyolysis. Case reports and basic research addressing CRRT for people with rhabdomyolysis and possible mechanisms for its effectiveness have been published.

    Although adequate fluid resuscitation with urinary alkalinisation remains the mainstay of treatment for people with rhabdomyolysis (Schetz 1999), CRRT use may increase because of its theoretical ability to remove myoglobin and stabilise haemodynamic status. However, controversy persists about whether CRRT can effectively remove myoglobin. A series of case reports indicated that CRRT showed some efficacy in clearing myoglobin (Amyot 1999; Bellomo 1991; Schenk 2001; Zhang 2012). Positive results were reported using high‐flux filters (Cruz 2011) and high ultrafiltration volume (Bosch 2009; Naka 2005; Ronco 2005). Less positive outcomes were reported elsewhere: the sieving coefficient of myoglobin was reported at only 0.15 (Nicolau 1996), and the half‐life of serum myoglobin did not differ significantly among patients receiving conservative therapy and those undergoing CVVHDF (Mikkelsen 2005). We found that serum myoglobin levels were decreased in patients treated with CRRT compared with those who received conventional therapy. 

    Some of our findings did not agree with the results of previous reviews. We found that CRRT improved levels of serum creatinine, BUN, hyperkalaemia and reduced duration of the oliguria period, but earlier reviews demonstrated no statistical differences in recovery of kidney function between patients with AKI who underwent CRRT and those treated with intermittent RRT (Bagshaw 2008; Pannu 2008; Rabindranath 2007). However, because the earlier studies did not provide subgroup data for rhabdomyolysis and our review was unable to report on all primary outcomes, attempted comparison of data sets was futile. 

    In their previous meta‐analyses Bagshaw 2008 and Zarbock 2009 indicated that CRRT can provide greater haemodynamic stability and fewer arrhythmic complications. However, Dong 2005 reported that CRRT did not significantly reduce the incidence of adverse events such as arrhythmia and shock. This may warrant further investigation.
