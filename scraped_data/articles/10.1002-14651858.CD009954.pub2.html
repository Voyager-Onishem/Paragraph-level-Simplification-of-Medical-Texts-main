Cochrane Database of Systematic reviews Review - Intervention

Cervical ripening before first trimester surgical evacuation for non‐viable pregnancy

  • Kylie Webber
  • Rosalie M Grivell
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 10 November 2015 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD009954.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Medications or mechanical dilators are often used to soften and dilate the cervix prior to surgical evacuation of the uterus for non‐viable pregnancy, or miscarriage. The majority of miscarriages occur in the first trimester. The aim of cervical ripening is to reduce the possibility of injury to the uterus and cervix and improve the surgical ease of the procedure. Cervical ripening agents can have adverse effects and it is uncertain as to whether these risks outweigh the benefits of their use.

    Objectives

    To systematically review the benefits and harms of using cervical ripening agents prior to surgical evacuation of non‐viable pregnancy prior to 14 weeks' gestation.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group's Trials Register (30 April 2015) and reference lists of retrieved papers.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials (published in full‐text form, or as abstracts only), which assessed the use of pharmacological or mechanical agents to ripen the cervix in women undergoing dilation and curettage or vacuum aspiration for non‐viable pregnancy at less than 14 weeks' gestation were eligible for inclusion. Cluster‐randomised controlled trials and trials using a cross‐over design were not eligible for inclusion.

    Unpublished randomised controlled trials and quasi‐randomised trials would have been eligible for inclusion but none were identified.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed the studies for inclusion, assessed risk of bias and carried out data extraction. Data were checked for accuracy.

    Main results

    We included nine trials with 469 women. A diverse set of medications and regimens were studied in these trials, making the comparisons available for meta‐analysis limited. The comparisons draw data from six trials with 383 participants. All trials were relatively small and had several aspects of unclear risk of bias with few of this review's outcomes reported. Due to this, no data from three trials were able to be used despite them meeting inclusion criteria.

    We carried out four comparisons: isosorbide mononitrate or dinitrate compared with misoprostol; misoprostol compared with placebo; chemical dilation (use of medications) compared with mechanical dilation; and any cervical preparation compared with placebo.

    None of the included studies reported data on the review's primary outcome: cervical or uterine injury (perforation, laceration, creation of a false passage).

    No clear difference was shown between isosorbide compounds and misoprostol for the outcome need for manual cervical dilation (average risk ratio (RR) 0.76, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.10 to 5.64; three trials, 150 women; Tau² = 2.11; I² = 69%), however the data were heterogenous. In terms of adverse effects, misoprostol was associated with more vomiting (RR 0.11, 95% CI 0.01 to 0.85; two trials, 120 women), however there were no clear differences between isosorbide compounds and misoprostol in relation to other reported adverse effects (headache, nausea or hypotension). The dosing regimens differed in terms of dose, number of administrations and route of administration in the different trials. Mechanical (Dilapan‐S hygroscopic) dilators performed similarly to chemical dilators in a single trial (65 women) that measured difficulty in cervical dilation, excessive bleeding and adverse effects.

    Misoprostol was shown to be more effective than placebo for cervical ripening (reduced need for manual cervical dilation ) (RR 0.14, 95% CI 0.08 to 0.26; one trial, 120 women), and surgical time was reduced when misoprostol was used (mean difference (MD) ‐3.15, 95% CI ‐3.59 to ‐2.70; one trial, 120 women). However, compared to placebo, misoprostol, was associated with more abdominal pain (RR 29.00, 95% CI 1.77 to 475.35; one trial, 120 women), although no clear differences in the risk of other adverse effects (nausea, vomiting, headache or fever) were observed between groups.

    There was no clear differences between chemical dilation and mechanical dilators for the outcomes: difficulty in cervical dilation , excessive bleeding or adverse effects.

    Compared with placebo, any cervical preparation reduced the need for manual cervical dilatation (average RR 0.25, 95% CI 0.07 to 0.89; two trials, 168 women; Tau² = 0.67; I² = 81%), and reduced surgical time (MD ‐2.55, 95% CI ‐3.67 to ‐1.43, two trials, 168 women; Tau² = 0.63; I² = 96%).

    None of the included trials reported on the review's other secondary outcomes, including: injury to bladder or bowel, miscarriage/preterm birth in a subsequent pregnancy, analgesia use after administration of ripening agent but before surgery, or analgesia use after surgery.

    Authors' conclusions

    This review found no evidence to evaluate cervical ripening prior to first trimester surgical evacuation for miscarriage for reducing the rate of cervical or uterine injury, however, this may be because these outcomes are very rare. Cervical preparation was shown to reduce the need for manual cervical dilatation compared with placebo.

    Misoprostol and isosorbide mononitrate and dinitrate were similarly effective in ripening the cervix, however there was more vomiting with misoprostol. Mechanical (Dilapan‐S hygroscopic) dilators performed similarly to chemical dilators.

    The nine studies included in this review were small and the methodological quality of the trials was mixed, and for the most part, not well‐described; thus any conclusions drawn from the data included in this review must be treated with caution. Consequently, large, high‐quality trials are required to determine whether the benefits of this treatment outweigh the risks. Further research should be powered to assess the rate of cervical and uterine injury between interventions. Future research should also guide clinicians in deciding whether the benefits of reduced manual cervical dilatation outweigh the risks of adverse effects associated with these agents (nausea, vomiting, headache, fever, diarrhoea and pain). Women's satisfaction and outcomes of future pregnancies should also be assessed.

    • Cervical ripening before first trimester surgical evacuation for non‐viable pregnancy

    What is the issue?

    Using medications or devices to soften and dilate the cervix can reduce the amount of manual dilation required before the uterus is surgically emptied of a non‐viable pregnancy, or miscarriage. The purpose of this cervical ripening is to reduce the possibility of injury to the uterus and cervix and make the procedure easier to perform, although the ripening agents can have side effects.

    Why is this important?

    A large number of gynaecologists are already using cervical ripening techniques before surgical evacuation of the uterus for miscarriages. It is important to assess whether this is an effective and safe practice.

    What evidence did we find?

    Our search for studies (on 30 April 2015) identified nine randomised controlled studies involving 469 women, but only six trials (involving 383 women) contributed data towards this review. The studies included small numbers of women and were not generally of high quality.

    None of the included studies reported data on cervical or uterine injury, which are rare events.

    Cervical preparation, either using mechanical or chemical dilation with misoprostol, was shown to reduce the need for manual cervical dilatation and surgical time compared with placebo (two trials, 168 women).

    Misoprostol was associated with more abdominal pain than placebo but no clear differences were observed in the risk of other adverse effects such as nausea, vomiting, headache or fever.

    Misoprostol and isosorbide mononitrate and dinitrate were similarly effective in ripening the cervix and reducing the need for manual cervical dilation (three trials, 150 women). Misoprostol caused more vomiting but with no clear differences in other reported adverse effects (headache, nausea or hypotension). The dosing regimens differed in terms of dose, number of administrations and route of administration in the different trials. Mechanical (Dilapan‐S hygroscopic) dilators performed similarly to chemical dilators in a single trial (65 women) that measured difficulty in cervical dilation, excessive bleeding and adverse effects.

    The medications or devices used did reduce the need for manual dilation of the cervix, but were associated with some side effects. These include abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, fever, headache and diarrhoea. The particular agents that we compared were misoprostol, isosorbide mononitrate, isosorbide dinitrate and Dilapan‐S hygroscopic dilators. No agent performed significantly better than another.

    What does this mean?

    The nine studies included in this review were small and the methodological quality of the trials was varied. For the most part, the study methods were not well‐described; thus any conclusions drawn from the data included in this review must be treated with caution. Large, high‐quality trials are required to understand whether easier dilation means there is less risk of injury to the woman's uterus or cervix during the surgery, or if there are any effects on future pregnancies.

    Implications for practice

    No data are available from these studies to assess whether cervical ripening prior to first trimester surgical evacuation for miscarriage reduces the rate of cervical or uterine injury, most likely because these outcomes are very rare and the studies were not adequately powered. Cervical preparation reduces the need for manual cervical dilatation compared with placebo.

    Misoprostol and isosorbide mononitrate and dinitrate were similarly effective in ripening the cervix, however there were more gastrointestinal side effects (vomiting) with misoprostol. Dilapan‐S hygroscopic dilators performed similarly to chemical dilators.

    Implications for research

    There are only small studies of mixed methodological quality investigating the use of cervical preparation in first trimester non‐viable pregnancy evacuation. Further research should be powered to assess the rate of cervical and uterine injury between interventions. Future research should also guide clinicians in deciding whether the benefits of reduced manual cervical dilatation outweigh the risks of adverse effects associated with these agents (nausea, vomiting, headache, fever, diarrhoea and pain). Patient satisfaction and outcomes of future pregnancies should also be assessed.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Pregnancy failure, most commonly known as miscarriage, affects 10% to 20% of clinically recognised pregnancies. One in four women are likely to experience a miscarriage in their lifetime (Alberman 1992). The majority (approximately 80%) of failed pregnancies occur in the first trimester, with the rate of pregnancy loss dropping considerably after 14 weeks' gestation (Alberman 1992). Dilation and curettage, vacuum aspiration or suction curettage are commonly employed techniques used to empty the uterus of the products of conception following the failure of a pregnancy.

    Dilation and curettage has been used since 1943 as the primary method of active management of miscarriage in early pregnancy. It accounts for up to 75% of emergency gynaecological procedures (Creinin 2001). This procedure can be performed in an operating theatre or in outpatient clinics using a variety of analgesic regimens from general anaesthesia to a local anaesthetic cervical block. It involves the dilation of the cervix with a series of standard diameter rods, followed by removal of the contents of the uterus with a curette, often attached to a vacuum suction device or aspiration catheter.

    It is a simple procedure, but carries a small chance of significant complications. The most common complications include post‐procedural infection (0% to 10%), incomplete evacuation (2% to 3%), haemorrhage (less than 1%), uterine perforation, cervical injury and death (rare) (Creinin 2001).

    Cervical and uterine injuries are uncommon but can cause serious and ongoing problems including haemorrhage, prolonged surgical time, bladder or bowel injury, cervical incompetence, future preterm birth or late miscarriage.

    Description of the intervention

    Many gynaecologists have become accustomed to using an agent to soften (or ripen) the cervix prior to dilation and curettage in order to reduce the risk of cervical injury and uterine perforation from forceful cervical dilatation. Commonly used agents include mechanical and pharmacological dilators. The mechanical dilators are devices such as Foley catheters, laminaria, Lamicel and Dilapan‐S which are placed in the cervix and use physical pressure or osmosis to expand the cervical canal.

    Chemical agents for cervical ripening include misoprostol, gemeprost, mifepristone, oxytocin, or sodium nitroprusside. Misoprostol and gemeprost are prostaglandin E1 analogues, which cause smooth muscle contractions in the uterus and softening of the cervix (Tang 2007). Sodium nitroprusside softens the cervix without causing uterine contractions, possibly by nitric oxide mediated breakdown of cervical connective tissue (Facchinetti 2000). Mifepristone is a progesterone antagonist, which is thought to cause collagen remodelling and uterine contractions, resulting in cervical softening (Tang 2006).

    How the intervention might work

    It is hypothesised that ripening the cervix prior to mechanical dilation at the time of suction curettage may reduce physical trauma to the uterus and cervix and therefore reduce procedural complications. It may also reduce the pain experienced by women having the procedure performed under local anaesthetic.

    The woman may experience some side effects of treatment with mechanical or pharmacological agents. All agents may cause abdominal pain and vaginal bleeding as they soften and dilate the cervix. Misoprostol and gemeprost can cause diarrhoea, shivering, nausea, vomiting, increased body temperature or rashes (Fiala 2007; MIMS 2010). Sodium nitroprusside can cause hypotension, headache, vomiting and palpitations (Facchinetti 2000; MIMS 2010). Mifepristone can cause malaise, dizziness, chills, fever, headache, diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, skin rashes, and urticaria (Martindale 2010).

    Why it is important to do this review

    It is important to determine whether the commonly employed technique of pre‐operative cervical ripening is safe and effective. This review will complement eight other existing Cochrane reviews that focus on the management of first and second trimester non‐viable pregnancy and also termination of pregnancy (induced abortion) in the first and second trimesters of pregnancy (Kapp 2010; Kulier 2011; Kulier 2001; Lohr 2008; May 2008; Newmann 2010; Renner 2009; Say 2002). There remains a knowledge gap regarding whether cervical ripening agents have a different effect on pregnancies that have failed in the first trimester and pregnancies which are viable at the time of termination. This review aims to address that gap.

    Objectives

    To systematically review the benefits and harms of using cervical ripening agents prior to surgical evacuation of a non‐viable pregnancy prior to 14 weeks' gestation.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (including those published in abstract form only) which assessed the use of pharmacological or mechanical agents to ripen the cervix in women undergoing dilation and curettage or vacuum aspiration for non‐viable pregnancy at less than 14 weeks' gestation were eligible for inclusion.

    Quasi‐randomised trials would have been eligible for inclusion but none were identified. We would have included unpublished trials but none were identified. We planned to exclude studies using a cluster‐randomised or cross‐over design.

    Types of participants

    Women undergoing dilation and curettage or vacuum aspiration for non‐viable pregnancy at less than 14 weeks' gestation.

    Types of interventions

    We planned to compare cervical ripening agents including misoprostol, mifepristone, isosorbide mononitrate, gemeprost, and laminaria versus no cervical ripening agent or placebo.  We planned to compare cervical ripening agents with each other and also compare different regimens of the same agent.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Cervical or uterine injury (perforation, laceration, creation of a false passage) as defined by trial authors.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Need for manual cervical dilatation

    2. Surgical time

    3. Excessive bleeding prior to procedure, as defined by trial authors

    4. Excessive bleeding during or after the procedure, as defined by trial authors

    5. Unplanned pregnancy expulsion

    6. Uterine infection as defined by the need for post operative antibiotic treatment

    7. Injury to bladder or bowel

    8. Miscarriage/preterm birth in a subsequent pregnancy

    9. Adverse effects of cervical ripening agents

    10. Satisfaction and emotional well being of the woman

    11. Analgesia use after administration of ripening agent, but before surgery

    12. Analgesia use after surgery

    Search methods for identification of studies

    The following methods section of this review is based on a standard template used by the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group’s Trials Register by contacting the Trials Search Co‐ordinator (30 April 2015).

    The Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group’s Trials Register is maintained by the Trials Search Co‐ordinator and contains trials identified from:

    1. monthly searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL);

    2. weekly searches of MEDLINE (Ovid);

    3. weekly searches of Embase (Ovid);

    4. monthly searches of CINAHL (EBSCO);

    5. handsearches of 30 journals and the proceedings of major conferences;

    6. weekly current awareness alerts for a further 44 journals plus monthly BioMed Central email alerts.

    Details of the search strategies for CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase and CINAHL, the list of handsearched journals and conference proceedings, and the list of journals reviewed via the current awareness service can be found in the ‘Specialized Register’ section within the editorial information about the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group.

    Trials identified through the searching activities described above are each assigned to a review topic (or topics). The Trials Search Co‐ordinator searches the register for each review using the topic list rather than keywords.

    Searching other resources

    We searched reference lists of published papers for any additional potential studies.

    We did not apply any language or date restrictions.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Both review authors independently assessed for inclusion all the potential studies we identified as a result of the search strategy. We resolved any disagreement through discussion.

    Data extraction and management

    We designed a form to extract data. For eligible studies, two review authors extracted the data using the agreed form. We resolved discrepancies through discussion. Data were entered into Review Manager software (RevMan 2014) and checked for accuracy.

    When information regarding any of the above was unclear, we attempted to contact authors of the original reports to provide further details.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Both review authors independently assessed risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Handbook) (Higgins 2011). Any disagreement was resolved by discussion.

    (1) Random sequence generation (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups.

    We assessed the method as:

    • low risk of bias (any truly random process, e.g. random number table; computer random number generator);

    • high risk of bias (any non‐random process, e.g. odd or even date of birth; hospital or clinic record number);

    • unclear risk of bias.   

     (2) Allocation concealment (checking for possible selection bias)

    We described for each included study the method used to conceal allocation to interventions prior to assignment and assessed whether intervention allocation could have been foreseen in advance of, or during recruitment, or changed after assignment.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation; consecutively numbered sealed opaque envelopes);

    • high risk of bias (open random allocation; unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes, alternation; date of birth);

    • unclear risk of bias. 

    (3.1) Blinding of participants and personnel (checking for possible performance bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind study participants and personnel from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We considered that studies were at low risk of bias if they were blinded, or if we judged that the lack of blinding would be unlikely to affect results. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for participants;

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias for personnel.

    (3.2) Blinding of outcome assessment (checking for possible detection bias)

    We described for each included study the methods used, if any, to blind outcome assessors from knowledge of which intervention a participant received. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes.

    We assessed methods used to blind outcome assessment as:

    • low, high or unclear risk of bias.

    (4) Incomplete outcome data (checking for possible attrition bias through withdrawals, dropouts, protocol deviations)

    We described for each included study, and for each outcome or class of outcomes, the completeness of data including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We stated whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total number of randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. We assessed methods as:

    • low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data; missing outcome data balanced across groups);

    • high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups; ‘as treated’ analysis done with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (5) Selective reporting bias

    We described for each included study how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found.

    We assessed the methods as:

    • low risk of bias (where it is clear that all of the study’s pre‐specified outcomes and all expected outcomes of interest to the review have been reported);

    • high risk of bias (where not all the study’s pre‐specified outcomes have been reported; one or more reported primary outcomes were not prespecified; outcomes of interest are reported incompletely and so cannot be used; study fails to include results of a key outcome that would have been expected to have been reported);

    • unclear risk of bias.

    (6) Other bias (checking for bias due to problems not covered by (1) to (5) above)

    We described for each included study any important concerns we have about other possible sources of bias.

    (7) Overall risk of bias

    We made explicit judgements about whether studies were at high risk of bias, according to the criteria given in the Handbook (Higgins 2011). With reference to (1) to (6) above, we planned to assess the likely magnitude and direction of the bias and whether we consider it was likely to impact on the findings. We planned to explore the impact of the level of bias through undertaking sensitivity analyses ‐ seeSensitivity analysis. 

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we present results as summary risk ratio with 95% confidence intervals. 

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we used the mean difference. We planned to use the standardised mean difference to combine trials that measured the same outcome, but used different methods.  

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cluster‐randomised trials and cross‐over trials

    We planned to exclude cluster‐randomised and cross‐over trials.

    Multi‐arm studies

    For multi‐arm studies, we used methods described in the Handbook (Higgins 2011) to overcome possible unit‐of analysis errors, by combining groups to make a single pair‐wise comparison (where appropriate).

    Dealing with missing data

    For included studies, we noted levels of attrition. We aimed to explore the impact of including studies with high levels of missing data in the overall assessment of treatment effect by using sensitivity analysis, however this was unnecessary as there were no missing data.

    For all outcomes, we carried out analyses, as far as possible, on an intention‐to‐treat basis, i.e. we attempted to include all participants randomised to each group in the analyses, and all participants were analysed in the group to which they were allocated, regardless of whether or not they received the allocated intervention.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We assessed statistical heterogeneity in each meta‐analysis using the T², I² and Chi² statistics. We regarded heterogeneity as substantial if an I² was greater than 30% and either a T² was greater than zero, or there was a low P value (less than 0.10) in the Chi² test for heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    In future updates, if there are 10 or more studies in the meta‐analysis, we will investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We will assess funnel plot asymmetry visually. If asymmetry is suggested by a visual assessment, we will perform exploratory analyses to investigate it.

    Data synthesis

    We carried out statistical analysis using the Review Manager software (RevMan 2014). We used fixed‐effect meta‐analysis for combining data where it was reasonable to assume that studies were estimating the same underlying treatment effect: i.e. where trials were examining the same intervention, and the trials’ populations and methods were judged sufficiently similar. If there was clinical heterogeneity sufficient to expect that the underlying treatment effects differed between trials, or if substantial statistical heterogeneity was detected, we used random‐effects meta‐analysis to produce an overall summary, if an average treatment effect across trials was considered clinically meaningful. The random‐effects summary was treated as the average range of possible treatment effects and we discussed the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. If the average treatment effect was not clinically meaningful, we would not have combined trials. Where we use random‐effects analyses, the results are presented as the average treatment effect with 95% confidence intervals, and the estimates of T² and I².

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We identified substantial heterogeneity, and had planned to investigate it using subgroup analyses and sensitivity analyses. We had planned to consider whether an overall summary was meaningful, and if it was, use random‐effects analysis to produce it, however we were unable to do this due to lack of primary outcome data.

    We had planned to carry out the following subgroup analyses:

    1. mechanical versus pharmacological cervical ripening agents;

    2. pregnancies which had a fetal pole identified at ultrasound versus those that did not.

    However, none of our trials reported on our primary outcome or whether a fetal pole was identified or not, so no subgroup analyses were performed. Planned subgroup analysis will be carried out in future updates, if appropriate, and we will assess subgroup differences by interaction tests available within RevMan (RevMan 2014). We will report the results of subgroup analyses quoting the χ2 statistic and P value, and the interaction test I² value

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to carry out sensitivity analysis to explore the effects of trial quality assessed by allocation concealment and other risk of bias components, by omitting studies rated as inadequate (high risk of bias or unclear risk of bias) for these components, however no studies reported our primary outcome so sensitivity analyses were not performed. We will carry out planned sensitivity analysis in future updates, if appropriate.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    See: Figure 1.

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 1

    Study flow diagram.

    The search of the Cochrane Pregnancy and Childbirth Group's Trials Register retrieved 18 reports relating to 16 studies. We have included nine studies (10 reports), and have excluded five. Two studies (three reports) are in the Studies awaiting classification section awaiting translation into English prior to assessment for eligibility.

    Included studies

    We included nine studies (involving 469 women) (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005; Facchinetti 2001; Hidalgo 2010; Jabir 2009; Phusaanantakul 2010; Ragusa 1994).

    Design

    All nine included studies were randomised controlled trials.

    Two of the studies (Chen 2008 and Facchinetti 2001) were three‐armed trials, Jabir 2009 included four arms and the remainder of the trials compared two arms.

    Sample sizes

    All of the nine included studies were relatively small with 120 (Jabir 2009) being the greatest number of participants. The remaining eight studies included 65 or fewer women.

    Setting

    The trials were carried out in Mexico (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005), Germany (Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005), Italy (Facchinetti 2001; Ragusa 1994), Venezuela (Hidalgo 2010), Iraq (Jabir 2009), and Thailand (Phusaanantakul 2010). They were performed in hospitals (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; David 2003; David 2005; Hidalgo 2010; Jabir 2009; Phusaanantakul 2010; Ragusa 1994); for two trials, the setting was not described (Chen 2008; Facchinetti 2001).

    Participants

    Women in the first 14 weeks of gestation with a non‐viable pregnancy were included in all studies. Other criteria varied and included no history of hypotension (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; Phusaanantakul 2010); no evidence of cervical change (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; David 2003; David 2005; Hidalgo 2010); no vaginal bleeding (Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005); no previous cervical surgery (Chen 2008); and no uterine contractions (Hidalgo 2010).

    Interventions

    The surgical intervention was defined as suction evacuation under general anaesthesia in Phusaanantakul 2010; the other trials merely specified that the women were scheduled for surgical evacuation of the uterus.

    A diverse set of medications and regimens were studied in the included trials, making the comparisons available for meta‐analysis limited. Isosorbide mononitrate is the active metabolite of isosorbide dinitrate, and therefore their effects on cervical tissue are likely to be similar (MIMS 2010). They were therefore included in the same comparison group for meta‐analysis.

    Three trials compared isosorbide mononitrate or dinitrate with misoprostol. Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005 compared 80 mg/1.5 mL intracervical isosorbide dinitrate gel with 400 mcg/1.5mL intracervical misoprostol gel solution every three hours for a maximum of four doses. David 2005 compared 40 mg isosorbide mononitrate vaginal tablets with 200 mcg misoprostol vaginal tablets, however a presumed typographic error in the methods stated that the misoprostol was gemeprost. These data have been included in the misoprostol comparisons. Hidalgo 2010 compared 40 mg vaginal isosorbide mononitrate with 200 mcg vaginal misoprostol every four hours up to 24 hours prior to uterine curettage.

    Only the study by Chen 2008 compared chemical dilators with mechanical dilators. This was a three‐armed trial which compared 80 mg vaginal isosorbide mononitrate mixed with sterilised hydrogel, 200 mcg vaginal misoprostol dissolved in sterilised hydrogel and Dilapan‐S hygroscopic cervical dilators.

    Two studies, Jabir 2009 and Phusaanantakul 2010 compared a cervical ripening agent with placebo. Jabir 2009 was a multi‐arm study which compared 400 mcg vaginal misoprostol and 400 mcg oral misoprostol with each other and against oral and vaginal placebo. Phusaanantakul 2010 compared 20 mg vaginal isosorbide mononitrate with placebo.

    David 2003 compared 1 mg gemeprost vaginal suppository with 0.5 mg nitroglycerin vaginal gel. Ragusa 1994 compared single with double instillations of 0.5 mg intracervical prostaglandin E2 gel (Cerviprost‐Organon). Facchinetti 2001 compared 5 mg of a 2% nitroprusside‐based gel (Nitrogel) into the cervix, with 10 mg Nitrogel and with placebo.

    Outcomes

    The primary outcome of most of the studies was the clinical assessment of cervical dilatation intraoperatively. Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005, Chen 2008, David 2003, Hidalgo 2010, Jabir 2009, Phusaanantakul 2010 and Ragusa 1994 assessed this with the largest diameter Hegar's dilator able to pass through the cervix with minimal resistance. David 2003 and David 2005 included an "ease of mechanical dilatation" score. Facchinetti 2001 collected information regarding the cervical length on ultrasound and also the force in Newtons required for mechanical dilatation with a dinamometre.

    All of the papers assessed the known adverse effects of the medications, including pain, with the exception of Facchinetti 2001, which measured blood pressure only. Chen 2008, Jabir 2009, Phusaanantakul 2010, and Ragusa 1994 attempted to assess bleeding complications. Jabir 2009 and Phusaanantakul 2010 assessed surgical time and Phusaanantakul 2010 included a patient satisfaction score. Facchinetti 2001 measured the blood levels of nitrates/nitrites.

    Excluded studies

    Firouzabadi 2012, Dunn 2008, and Surita 1997 were excluded as they included participants with second trimester pregnancy losses, and Hernandez‐Valencia 2003 as it was not clear if the trial was randomised. Dee 2009 assessed the medical management of miscarriage rather than surgical management. For more information see Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    All trials were relatively small and had several aspects of unclear quality with few outcomes reported. See Figure 2 and Figure 3 for a summary of 'Risk of bias' assessments.

    Randomisation and allocation methods were not further described in many studies, nor whether participants, clinicians and/or outcome assessors were blinded. Overall, there is significant risk of selection and performance bias in the studies included. There was no loss to follow‐up in any of the studies as assessment of cervical dilatation and immediate procedure‐related complications are collected in the same patient encounter as administration of the cervical ripening agent. Selective reporting bias remains unclear.

    Allocation

    Three trials were assessed as being at low risk for selection bias. Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005, Hidalgo 2010 and Phusaanantakul 2010 used a computerised random number generator to allocate women to groups and described sealed opaque envelopes containing the treatments.

    Methods used to generate the random sequence and conceal allocation were incompletely described in the Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005; Facchinetti 2001; Ragusa 1994 trials, and they have therefore been judged to be at unclear risk of selection bias.

    Jabir 2009 allocated from two envelopes, one with pieces of paper containing placebo or misoprostol and the second for the route of administration. This system likely increased the risk of selection bias and has therefore been assessed as high risk.

    Blinding

    Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005 described identical appearing micro vials and that the surgeon reporting outcomes was blinded, so this trial was at low risk of performance and detection bias. Facchinetti 2001 described the study as "double blind", however provided no further detail, and thus this trial was judged to be at unclear risk of performance and detection bias; Phusaanantakul 2010 et al also described their trial as "double blind", however provided further detail of the placebo used, and of blinded clinicians assessing outcome data, and thus this trial was judged to be at low risk of performance and detection bias

    The participants were blinded in Hidalgo 2010 and Jabir 2009, however it is not known if the study personnel including outcome assessors were blinded, and thus risks of performance and detection bias were judged to be unclear. No detail of blinding was provided in Ragusa 1994, which was therefore judged to be at unclear risk of both performance and detection bias. In Chen 2008 they mixed the misoprostol and isosorbide mononitrate with sterilised hydrogel to assist with blinding, however the appearance of third intervention, Dilapan‐S, was unable to be concealed; risk of performance bias was judged as low. It is unclear who reported the outcome in this trial, and thus risk of detection bias was judged as unclear.

    The David 2003 and David 2005 interventions differed and it was considered unlikely/unfeasible that the participants/study personnel would have been blinded; thus, the trials were judged to be at high risk of performance bias. Neither trial detailed whether it was possible to blind outcome assessors; thus, the risk of detection bias was judged to be unclear.

    Incomplete outcome data

    There was no loss to follow‐up in eight of the nine of the included studies (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005; Hidalgo 2010; Jabir 2009; Phusaanantakul 2010; Ragusa 1994); thus, the risk of attrition bias was judged as low. In Facchinetti 2001, there was insufficient information provided to assess attrition bias.

    Selective reporting

    There was insufficient information available to confidently assess reporting bias across all of the included studies (i.e. with no access to trial protocols) (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; Chen 2008; David 2003; David 2005; Facchinetti 2001; Hidalgo 2010; Jabir 2009; Phusaanantakul 2010; Ragusa 1994).

    Other potential sources of bias

    Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005 was judged to be low risk of other bias, with comparable groups at baseline, and no other obvious sources of bias identified.

    In the other eight trials, largely, there was insufficient detail of methodology provided to confidently assess other potential sources of bias. David 2005 stated that misoprostol was used in the title of the paper, however a presumed typographical error in the methods states "either 200 mcg gemeprost (Cytotec, Pfizer, Germany) or 40 mg isosorbide mononitrate (Mono Mack, Mack, Illertissen, Germany) was applied". We have included these data in the misoprostol versus isosorbide mononitrate comparisons.

    The standard deviations reported by Phusaanantakul 2010 for surgical time are extremely small and do not seem plausible. The trial authors were unable to be contacted to clarify, so the times have been included as stated in the publication.

    Effects of interventions

    Primary outcome

    Cervical or uterine injury

    Our primary outcome of cervical or uterine injury was not reported on by any of our nine included trials; we presume such injury may not have occurred in any of the trial participants as the authors commented on there being no serious adverse events.

    Secondary outcomes

    Isosorbide mononitrate or dinitrate compared with misoprostol (comparison 1)
    Need for manual cervical dilatation

    There was no difference in these medications for need for manual cervical dilatation prior to uterine curettage (average risk ratio (RR) 0.76, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.10 to 5.64; three trials, 150 women;Tau² = 2.11; I² = 69% (Analysis 1.1)). There was substantial heterogeneity between the three included studies. This is likely to be because these studies were conducted in different populations, using different protocols including time interval to surgery. Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005 used 80 mg isosorbide dinitrate endocervical gel versus 400 mcg misoprostol endocervical gel every three hours, using up to four doses. They found that the misoprostol group was more likely to need manual cervical dilatation after 12 hours than the isosorbide mononitrate group. David 2005 used a single dose of 200 mcg misoprostol tablet or 40 mg isosorbide mononitrate intravaginally and assessed the need for manual cervical dilatation after a minimum of three hours post treatment. The authors found that there was a non‐significant trend towards isosorbide mononitrate patients requiring more manual cervical dilatation. Hidalgo 2010 compared 40 mg isosorbide mononitrate with 200 mcg misoprostol vaginally every four hours up to 24 hours and found that the number of participants with a successfully ripened cervix was similar in both groups. These regimens are sufficiently different in terms of dosing, number of administrations and route of administration that a conclusion regarding which medication is more effective is difficult to draw, which also contributes to the substantial heterogeneity.

In the other two trials, only one woman suffered from hypotension from the isosorbide mononitrate group of Hidalgo 2010 (RR 3.00, 95% CI 0.13 to 70.83, two trials, 90 women (Analysis 1.5)).

    Other regimens
    Need for manual cervical dilatation

    Two doses of prostaglandin E2 gel was no more effective than one dose for cervical ripening (Ragusa 1994), with one out of 16 women having cervical dilatation < 5 mm in the two doses group as compared to three out of 16 in the one dose group. David 2003 made a comment that cervical dilation was more difficult in the nitroglycerin group (P < 0.01) and significantly smaller Hegar’s dilators could be used (P < 0.05), but no numbers were supplied in this brief communication.

    Excessive bleeding prior to procedure

    Vaginal bleeding occurred in three participants given nitroglycerin in the David 2003 study and nine given gemeprost (P = 0.05, n = 30).

    Unplanned pregnancy expulsion

    Intracervical products of conception were found in nine women given gemeprost by the David 2003 group, but none given nitroglycerin (P = 0.05, participants = 30).

    Adverse effects

    In Jabir 2009, 400mcg of oral misoprostol was more likely to cause abdominal pain that 400 mcg of vaginal misoprostol. Oral misoprostol increased the adverse effects of nausea, vomiting, headache, abdominal pain and diarrhoea when compared with placebo, however vaginal misoprostol did not (Jabir 2009). Pelvic pain occurred in five participants from the David 2003 study, four who were exposed to gemeprost and one who was given nitroglycerin (participants = 30). None of the participants of this trial complained of headache, hypotension or tachycardia (David 2003).

    The secondary outcomes of injury to bladder or bowel, miscarriage/preterm birth in a subsequent pregnancy or analgesia use were not commented on by any of the trial authors.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    Our primary outcome of cervical or uterine injury was not reported on in any trial, and we presume this may mean it did not occur as no serious adverse events were reported by authors. This suggests such injuries are rare. Therefore, the effectiveness of cervical preparation before first trimester surgical evacuation of non‐viable pregnancy in reducing cervical or uterine injury remains unknown.

    Meta‐analysis of three trials (150 women) (Arteaga‐Troncoso 2005; David 2005; Hidalgo 2010) suggests that isosorbide mononitrate/dinitrate and misoprostol were similarly effective in ripening the cervix prior to uterine curettage. Misoprostol was associated with more vomiting than isosorbide preparations in two of the trials, however there was no clear difference between groups for nausea, headache or hypotension.

    When misoprostol was compared with placebo (in one trial of 120 women, Jabir 2009), it was effective in ripening the cervix and reducing surgical time. There was significantly more abdominal pain when misoprostol was used, and a possible increase in nausea, vomiting, fever, headache and diarrhoea. This trial, of 120 women was likely underpowered to detect clear differences that exist in these relatively infrequently occurring outcomes.

    Chemical dilators (misoprostol and isosorbide mononitrate) performed similarly to Dilapan‐S, a hygroscopic mechanical dilator, when excessive bleeding during the procedure, difficulty in dilating the cervix and adverse effects were compared (in one trial of 60 women (Chen 2008)).

    Cervical preparation of any kind decreased the need for manual cervical dilatation when compared with placebo (in two trials of 168 women (Jabir 2009; Phusaanantakul 2010)).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The trials included in this review tested a large variety of cervical ripening agents both against each other and placebo. These included isosorbide mononitrate, isosorbide dinitrate, prostaglandin E2 intracervical gel, gemeprost, vaginal nitroglycerine, Dilapan‐S hygroscopic dilators and oral and vaginal preparations of misoprostol. Dosing and routes of administration were varied; and the Jabir 2009 trial suggests that route of administration is important with regards to adverse effects. Finding useful comparisons was therefore difficult and our primary question of whether cervical ripening before first trimester surgical evacuation of non‐viable pregnancy reduces the rate of cervical or uterine injury remains unanswered.

    There was also very limited information available regarding most of our secondary outcomes.

    We can not draw any conclusions regarding bleeding, unplanned pregnancy expulsion, uterine infection, injury to bladder or bowel or analgesia use. Only one trial (Phusaanantakul 2010) reported data regarding satisfaction and emotional well being of women.

    Information regarding miscarriage or preterm birth in a subsequent pregnancy would be challenging to collect due to the obvious time lag between the initial failed pregnancy and a subsequent one. This was not reported in any of these trials. If cervical preparation had an effect on a subsequent pregnancy it would be of significant interest to women, and remains an important clinical question.

    Quality of the evidence

    The methodological quality of the included trials was mixed, and for the most part, methodology was not well‐described; thus any conclusions drawn from the data included in this review must be treated with caution.

    Potential biases in the review process

    Isosorbide mononitrate is the active metabolite of isosorbide dinitrate, and therefore their effects on cervical tissue are likely to be similar (MIMS 2010), however as they were not compared head‐to‐head in a randomised trial there remains a possibility that their effects are different. They were included in the same comparison group for meta‐analysis in order to compare these compounds with misoprostol in a meaningful way.

    We conducted an extensive search, but it remains possible that studies may have been missed. If we identify any such studies in future searches, we will assess them for potential inclusion in an update of this review.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    There are a number of other reviews addressing the safety and efficacy of cervical preparation medications for the purpose of termination of pregnancy (Kapp 2010; Kulier 2011; Kulier 2001; Lohr 2008; May 2008; Newmann 2010; Renner 2009; Say 2002). They agree with our data in that they also conclude that these medications are not associated with serious adverse outcomes, but have a variety of more minor adverse effects. The rate of uterine perforation or cervical injury is also very low in these data, and no conclusions are drawn regarding the gestation at which their benefits outweigh the risks of adverse effects.

    Kapp 2010 concluded that cervical preparation decreases the length of surgical abortion. Mifepristone 200 mg, osmotic dilators and misoprostol 400 mcg vaginally or sublingually were found to be the most effective methods of cervical preparation in this review on first trimester surgical abortion. This is an equivalent meta‐analysis to this review, but participants had unwanted viable pregnancies. It is interesting to note that 51 studies met inclusion criteria for the Kapp 2010 review, making their meta‐analysis more robust than ours.

    Our review does not answer the question of whether cervical ripening agents have a different effect on pregnancies that have failed in the first trimester and pregnancies which are viable at the time of termination as the numbers are too small to draw conclusions on this.
