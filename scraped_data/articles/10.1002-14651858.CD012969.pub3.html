Treatment of dental and orthodontic complications in thalassaemia

    • Background

    Thalassaemia is a quantitative abnormality of haemoglobin caused by mutations in genes controlling production of alpha or beta globins. Abnormally unpaired globin chains cause membrane damage and cell death within organ systems and destruction of erythroid precursors in the bone marrow, leading to haemolytic anaemia. The life‐long management of the general health effects of thalassaemia is highly challenging, and failure to deal with dental and orthodontic complications exacerbates the public health, financial and personal burden of the condition. There is a lack of evidence‐based guidelines to help care seekers and providers manage such dental and orthodontic complications. This review aimed to evaluate the available evidence on methods for treating dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia to inform future recommendations. This is an update of a Cochrane Review first published in 2019.

    Objectives

    To assess different methods for treating dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register in September 2022, and we searched nine online databases and trials registries in January 2022. We searched the reference lists of relevant articles and reviews and contacted haematologists, experts in fields of dentistry, organisations, pharmaceutical companies and researchers working in this field.

    Selection criteria

    We searched for published or unpublished randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that evaluated treatment of dental and orthodontic complications in individuals diagnosed with thalassaemia, irrespective of phenotype, severity, age, sex and ethnic origin.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently screened the 37,242 titles retrieved by the search. After deduplication, we identified two potentially relevant RCTs. On assessing their eligibility against our inclusion and exclusion criteria, we excluded one and included the other.

    Main results

    We included one parallel‐design RCT conducted in Saudi Arabia and involving 29 participants (19 males, 10 females) with thalassaemia. It aimed to assess the effectiveness of photodynamic therapy as an adjuvant to conventional full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling for the treatment of gingivitis. The average age of participants was around 23 years.

    There is very low‐certainty evidence from this trial that full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling plus photodynamic therapy compared to full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling alone may improve gingival index score and bleeding on probing after 12 weeks in people with thalassaemia.

    We found no studies that assessed other interventions for the various dental or orthodontic complications of thalassaemia.

    Authors' conclusions

    Although the included study showed greater reduction in gingivitis in the group treated with full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling plus photodynamic therapy, the evidence is of very low certainty. The study had unclear risk of bias, a short follow‐up period and no data on safety or adverse effects. We cannot make definitive recommendations for clinical practice based on the limited evidence of a single trial. Future studies will very likely affect the conclusions of this review.

    This review highlights the need for high‐quality RCTs that investigate the effectiveness of various treatment modalities for dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia. It is crucial that future trials assess adverse effects of interventions.

    • Treatment of dental and orthodontic problems in thalassaemia

    What is thalassaemia?

    Red blood cells make a pigment known as haemoglobin which carries oxygen around the body. In people with thalassaemia, the haemoglobin is not normal, due to defects (mutations) in two specific types of genes. This leads to the condition being classified as either alpha or beta thalassaemia. About 5% of the world population carry the mutation that causes the alpha globin gene to function only partially or not at all; the carrier rate for the beta globin gene is about 1.5%. Both forms of thalassaemia are mainly found in the belt of countries stretching from Sub‐Saharan Africa, through the Mediterranean region and the Middle East, to South and South‐East Asia. These disorders are increasingly found in many other parts of the world due to people moving from country to country.

    How does thalassaemia cause dental and orthodontic problems?

    When people inherit two copies of the mutated gene, the defective haemoglobin in the red blood cells does not release oxygen normally into the body. Defective cells build up in the body's organs and bone marrow cells, causing tissue damage and cell death, which leads to a decrease in red blood cells (anaemia). The lack of oxygen due to anaemia can stop organs from working normally, and people often need blood transfusions to correct the decrease in red blood cells. The body tries to compensate for the anaemia naturally by making more red blood cells, which causes expansion of the bone marrow spaces. In the skull, cheekbones and jawbones, this expanding bone marrow causes abnormal bony swellings, which can result in jaw deformity and teeth not being in their correct position (known as malocclusion). Severe changes to the face and jaw lead to difficulties with speech, eating and appearance. These visible features can be distressing for people with thalassaemia and have a negative impact on their quality of life.

    To survive, people with thalassaemia have to focus on dealing with the serious impact of anaemia on their general health and on managing complications related to the long‐term treatment of the condition. Consequently, they may neglect dental problems like tooth decay, gum disease and infections, and ordinary dental problems can become more severe and require advanced treatment. Before dental practitioners start any kind of dental treatment in people with thalassaemia, they need to consider both the underlying condition and the effects of the resulting anaemia or its treatment. Dental treatment may be particularly risky in people with thalassaemia who have had their spleen removed, as this can make them more prone to infections. There are no guidelines describing the best treatment plan, due to a lack of information in the scientific literature. 

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if any intervention was better than another to: 

    1. improve how teeth are aligned;

    2. reduce the severity of any other dental complications;

    3. reduce unwanted effects of treatment;

    4. reduce pain;

    5. reduce infections after dental treatment using preventive antibiotics;

    6. improve quality of life; or

    7. reduce the effects on professional and academic functions (e.g. loss or change of job, number of days off work or school).

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that evaluated any treatment for dental or orthodontic problems in people with thalassaemia. We summarised the results and rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and sizes. 

    What did we find?

    We found only one study suitable for inclusion in our review. This trial was conducted in a clinical setting in Saudi Arabia and enroled 29 people with thalassaemia. One group of participants received treatment with a light stimulation device in the infected areas of the gums in addition to professional full‐mouth cleaning, and the other group received professional full‐mouth cleaning only.

    Main results

    Full‐mouth cleaning plus light stimulation may reduce some aspects of gum inflammation more than full‐mouth cleaning alone. The study did not report any of our other outcomes of interest listed above.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    The study did not examine the effects of treatment beyond 12 weeks; nor did it examine unwanted effects of treatment. For these reasons, we have little confidence in the evidence. We cannot make definitive clinical recommendations based on this one study.

    How up to date is the evidence?

    The evidence is current to January 2022.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is very low‐certainty evidence based on one small randomised controlled trial (RCT) that photodynamic therapy used as an adjuvant to full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling may be effective in treating gingivitis in people with thalassaemia. This evidence is based on a single trial with a small sample size of people aged 15 years and older. Considering the unclear risk of bias, single source of evidence and absence of data on people younger than 15 years of age, we cannot define clear implications for practice based on this evidence. Future trials on this topic will very likely affect the conclusions of this review.

    Additionally, there is no evidence available to determine the effectiveness of interventions for other oral diseases in people with thalassaemia. This renders clinical practice devoid of evidence‐based guidelines to treat oral health conditions in people with thalassaemia.

    Implications for research

    This Cochrane Review illustrates the need for well‐designed, adequately powered, properly executed and reported RCTs to assess the effectiveness of treatments for dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia. It is important that such trials include data on adverse effects of interventions.

    Background

    For an explanation of clinical terms, refer to the glossary (Appendix 1).

    Description of the condition

    Haemoglobinopathies are a group of Mendelian recessive conditions in which the structure and synthesis of haemoglobin is defective; this results in chronic anaemia when found in the homozygous or compound heterozygous states. The two major subgroups are sickle cell disease and the thalassaemia syndromes. The most common inherited disorder of haemoglobin is alpha thalassaemia (α‐thalassaemia); around 5% of the global population are carriers, and approximately one million people are affected by the various α‐thalassaemia syndromes worldwide (Taher 2013). Beta thalassaemia (β‐thalassaemia) is also highly prevalent, with 80 to 90 million people reported to be carriers across the world (1.5% of the global population), approximately half of whom come from South‐East Asia (Taher 2013). At least 56,000 individuals are affected by thalassaemia every year, including at least 30,000 who need regular transfusions to survive and 5500 who die perinatally due to transfusion‐dependent α‐thalassaemia (Modell 2008). Haemoglobin disorders, such as the thalassaemias, have lost their regional specificity and are now a global concern (Weatherall 2010). While they occur primarily in the belt of countries stretching from Sub‐Saharan Africa through the Mediterranean region and the Middle East to South and South‐East Asia, they also occur in many other parts of the world owing to the global movement of populations (Taher 2013).

    Thalassaemia is a quantitative abnormality of haemoglobin and is caused by mutations that result in reduced production of either α or non‐α chains. In the α‐thalassaemias, the disruption of one or both alleles on chromosome 16 results in reduced or absent production of α‐globin chains. Deletions of the α‐genes are the most common mutations causing α‐thalassaemia and depending on the number of deletions, the possible resulting conditions are:

    1. a clinically asymptomatic silent carrier state (deletion of one of the four α‐globin genes);

    2. α‐thalassaemia minor with mild anaemia (deletion of two α‐globin genes); 

    3. haemoglobin H disease with mild to moderate chronic haemolytic anaemia (deletion of three α‐globin genes); or

    4. hydrops fetalis (deletion of all four α‐globin genes), a condition that, if untreated in utero, leads to in‐utero or neonatal death. 

    In the β‐thalassaemias, mutations in the genes controlling β‐chain production on chromosome number 11, cause no (β0) or reduced (β+) β‐chain production, leading to four clinical syndromes:

    1. β‐thalassaemia minor (one affected gene), with mild, microcytic, hypochromic anaemia;

    2. β‐thalassaemia major (homozygous or compound heterozygous state), with severe anaemia leading to dependence on transfusions;

    3. β‐thalassaemia intermedia, where symptoms of severity vary between major and heterozygote β‐thalassaemia; or

    4. a silent carrier state, with no clinical manifestation of the disease despite the presence of genetic changes (Colledge 2010; Park 2012; Rodak 2012).

    Transfusion dependence is another method used to differentiate the various thalassaemia phenotypes, with severity divided into two categories: transfusion‐dependent thalassaemia (TDT), where lifelong regular blood transfusions are required for survival (e.g. in β‐thalassaemia); and non‐transfusion‐dependent thalassaemia (NTDT), where individuals do not require lifelong regular blood transfusions to survive, although they may require occasional or even frequent transfusions in certain clinical settings and for defined periods of time. The term NTDT includes some β‐thalassaemia intermedia, haemoglobin E/β‐thalassaemia (mild and moderate forms) and α‐thalassaemia intermedia (haemoglobin H disease; Taher 2013).

    The unpaired globin chains produced in the thalassaemias are insoluble and incapable of releasing oxygen normally. These unpaired chains accumulate and precipitate into almost every organ system and also in erythroid precursors in the bone marrow, causing membrane damage and cell death, thus leading to ineffective erythropoiesis. Chronic anaemia causes increased gastrointestinal absorption of iron, causing systemic complications such as cardiac failure and multiple endocrine abnormalities (Park 2012; Rodak 2012).

    In lower‐income countries, improved hygiene, nutrition and public health services contribute to reduced levels of infant and childhood mortality, which means more infants with haemoglobinopathies (including thalassaemia) are surviving and presenting for treatment later in life (Weatherall 2001). Because thalassaemia is a life‐threatening disorder, managing the systemic condition alone becomes the priority for affected people, and health issues that are non‐life‐threatening and chronic in nature (e.g. dental problems) can be neglected both by the affected individuals and non‐oral healthcare providers. However, the continued neglect of minor oral health issues may aggravate the underlying medical condition, precipitate serious complications and require advanced or complex treatment strategies (Drew 1997; Ricerca 2009). For instance, early diagnosis and management of orofacial deformities are critical, because increased erythroid hyperplasia or extramedullary haematopoiesis over the years worsens malocclusion. Routine blood transfusions, which are essential for survival in some cases, can cause higher iron deposition in the gingiva and teeth, resulting in tooth discolouration. People with thalassaemia who have undergone a splenectomy are at high risk of infection due to bacteraemia following dental procedures (AlDallal 2016; Capellini 2014; Einy 2016).

    In people with thalassaemia, the pathogenesis, range and treatment of dental and orthodontic problems acquire different dimensions. The term dental complications covers all oral infections, diseases and conditions affecting the teeth, jaws, orofacial region and associated structures (e.g. dental caries, periodontal disease, odontogenic infections, osteomyelitis and cellulitis). Common dental complications occurring in people with thalassaemia include pallor of the gingiva and mucous membranes, caries, infections, pain and swelling of parotid glands, atrophic candidiasis, discolouration of teeth, reduction in tooth size and dental arch dimensions, delay in development and eruption of dentition, short root size, cortical erosion and trabecular enlargement (Capellini 2014; Goldfarb 1983; Hattab 1999; Hattab 2001; Mehdizadeh 2008; Poyton 1968; Van Dis 1986).

    The term orthodontic complications covers craniofacial deformities, jaw abnormalities, malocclusion or any other condition necessitating orthodontic treatment and adjunctive procedures, including orthognathic surgery. Common orthodontic complications are class II malocclusion, flaring and spacing of maxillary anterior teeth, increased overjet and open‐bite. Consequent to the increased iron absorption in thalassaemia, compensatory erythroid hyperplasia, which occurs in facial and cranial bones, gives rise to appearances variously termed 'facies thalassaemia' or 'Cooley's facies'. The appearance is characterised by craniofacial features such as prominent frontal and parietal bones, a depressed bridge of the nose, protruding cheekbones and canting of eyes (Drew 1997; Hattab 2012). These craniofacial manifestations and deformities lead to speech, eating and emotional difficulties. Factors that may influence these deformities are severity of anaemia, age, duration of clinical symptoms, splenectomy and timing of therapeutic blood transfusion and splenectomy (Drew 1997; Logothetis 1971).

    The visible features described above can cause distress in people with thalassaemia. Moreover, in people with chronic conditions such as thalassaemia, the life‐long management of the condition may also generate symptoms of anxiety and depression, depending on the perceived impact of the condition on functioning, the intensity of the medical regimen and the individual's ability to control the condition. In people with thalassaemia, increased symptoms of both anxiety and depression are associated with poorer functional health and well‐being (Capellini 2014; Mednick 2010). In this context, maintaining good oral health is very important, as oral health status affects the psychological aspects of oral health‐related quality of life (OHRQoL) more than functional aspects (Motallebnejad 2014).

    Description of the intervention

    Any treatment recommended as part of routine dental and orthodontic care must be augmented or modified in people with thalassaemia to accommodate their specific needs. Early and conservative management of dental caries is recommended to prevent disease progression and resultant complications, including those arising from complex treatment such as endodontic therapy. In tooth extractions or minor surgeries, it is important to use appropriate surgical management techniques, and prophylactic antibiotic coverage is recommended to prevent the development of osteonecrosis of the jaw (AlDallal 2016; Akintoye 2012; Barasch 2011; Hellstein 2011).

    The orthodontic treatment of facial deformity and malocclusion in people with thalassaemia improves their appearance, occlusion, function and oral health (Zahrowski 2009). Functional and extra‐oral appliances can correct dentofacial deformity in younger people (Einy 2016). Some studies have proposed hypertransfusion or even therapeutic transfusion to decrease the severity of craniofacial deformity in growing children (Drew 1997; Habibzadeh 2005; Kaplan 1964; Logothetis 1971; Park 2012; Saxon 1998). Hypertransfusion refers to transfusion regimens instituted early after diagnosis to prevent clinical symptoms caused by anaemia; they are aimed at maintaining a trough haemoglobin level of 10 g/dL to promote normal growth and development through childhood until the second decade of life. Furthermore, research suggests that low doses of radiation and administration of myelosuppressive agents (e.g. hydroxyurea) can restrict postoperative medullary expansion and thus prevent the recurrence of facial deformity (Drew 1997; Habibzadeh 2005; Park 2012; Saxon 1998).

    How the intervention might work

    In people with thalassaemia, dental treatment should be tailored according to the general health of the individual and the possible impact of dental procedures on their pre‐existing systemic condition (AlDallal 2016; Capellini 2014). A conservative approach and early intervention for managing dental caries may help to arrest disease in its early stages and eliminate the need for complex treatment such as root canal therapy or tooth extractions. This is more important in people receiving bisphosphonate therapy to treat thalassaemia‐related bone loss, which contraindicates the extraction of teeth due to the elevated risk of osteonecrosis (Akintoye 2012; Terpos 2010). However, if extractions or minor surgeries are unavoidable, prophylactic antibiotic coverage is recommended before carrying out the treatment. This is to prevent infections secondary to bacteraemia caused by the invasive dental procedures in susceptible and immunocompromised individuals with thalassaemia (AlDallal 2016; Akintoye 2012; Barasch 2011; Capellini 2014; Hellstein 2011).

    Early diagnosis and orthodontic treatment help to intercept and correct craniofacial deformities, thus rehabilitating occlusion and restoring function. Orthodontic treatment using fixed or removable appliances corrects malocclusion by moving teeth through alveolar bone. However, thinning of cortical bone may hasten tooth movement in this population, so the orthodontist must closely monitor tooth movements at shorter intervals, both clinically and radiographically, and use lighter forces than usual (Capellini 2014; Einy 2016). Hypertransfusion, or therapeutic transfusion, reduces reactive medullary hyperplasia, thus helping to arrest or reduce the severity of craniofacial deformity in growing children (Drew 1997; Habibzadeh 2005; Kaplan 1964; Logothetis 1971; Park 2012; Saxon 1998). In advanced stages, thalassaemia‐induced facial deformity necessitates surgery (Drew 1997; Park 2012). People with thalassaemia who undergo surgical interventions may require blood transfusions; this decision of whether to include this procedure should always be taken in consultation with a haematologist and be based on the type of thalassaemia and haemoglobin levels (Drew 1997; Taher 2013). Correction of haemoglobinopathy‐related deformities through surgical procedures (e.g. maxillary anterior segmental osteotomy, vertical maxillary reduction, resection and recontouring of the maxilla) removes the excessively proliferated marrow and disfigured bony structure and re‐establishes normal naso‐maxillary architecture and shape; and transverse, sagittal and vertical maxillary dimensions. As a result, these surgeries can restore form and function in the craniofacial area. Partial or complete prosthesis may also be used to cover up extreme facial deformities if surgery is not feasible (Drew 1997; Park 2012).

    Why it is important to do this review

    There is a lack of evidence‐based clinical guidelines and research data on dental treatment in people with haemoglobinopathies. Not even the comprehensive guidelines by the Thalassemia International Foundation (TIF) include guidance on managing oral health complications in people with thalassaemia (Taher 2013). Consequently, dental healthcare practitioners lack information, awareness and training in this area, and may refuse to treat people with thalassaemia for fear of intraoperative and postoperative complications.

    Managing the serious forms of thalassaemia over an individual's lifetime is extremely expensive and can be compounded by having to deal with infections and complications secondary to inadequate or inappropriate dental care (Weatherall 2001). Providing long‐term, comprehensive healthcare to these people requires considerable public health resources and medical expertise, hence this issue being labelled an emerging global health burden. Developing effective, accurate and timely treatment modalities for dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia will not only help to control lifetime treatment costs, but also significantly improve the quality of life of those affected (Weatherall 2001). With all these factors in mind, we aim to identify clinically‐proven treatment strategies to provide the best dental and orthodontic care for people with thalassaemia. This is an update of a previously published review (Mulimani 2019).

    Objectives

    To assess different methods for treating dental and orthodontic complications in people with thalassaemia.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered only randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs for inclusion.

    We did not consider cross‐over trials to be eligible because the severity of the condition may change with a preceding treatment.

    Types of participants

    We included trials that enroled people diagnosed with thalassaemia, irrespective of phenotype, severity, age, sex and ethnic origin.

    Types of interventions

    We included trials with one or more of the following interventions compared with placebo, no treatment or an alternative treatment for managing dental and orthodontic complications:

    1. different types of orthodontic appliances (e.g. fixed, removable, functional, orthopaedic);

    2. application of light forces, non‐extraction mechanics and modification of treatment according to bone and systemic conditions during orthodontic therapy;

    3. techniques to manage craniofacial deformity (e.g. surgical procedures, maintaining haemoglobin levels with early and regular transfusions, prostheses, radiation, administration of myelosuppressive agents such as hydroxyurea);

    4. techniques for caries management (e.g. minimally invasive techniques (such as remineralisation), arresting active lesions and restorations with minimal cavity design, and invasive techniques (such as root canal treatment));

    5. prophylactic antibiotics (e.g. penicillins, amoxicillin) during treatment of dental complications;

    6. management of dental pain using analgesics (e.g. non‐steroidal anti‐inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)) and alternative techniques (e.g. acupressure, relaxation, reiki); or

    7. any intervention to treat dental or orthodontic complications not listed above.

    We used the term dental complications to cover all oral infections, diseases and conditions affecting the teeth, jaws, orofacial region and associated structures. Examples are dental caries, periodontal disease, odontogenic infections, osteomyelitis and cellulitis.

    We used the term orthodontic complications to cover craniofacial deformities, jaw abnormalities, malocclusion or any other condition necessitating orthodontic and its adjunctive treatment procedures, including orthognathic surgery.

    Types of outcome measures

    We had planned to assess the outcomes at the following condition‐dependent time points: one week to six months for acute or initial stage complications (acute infections, caries, cysts, gingivitis, acute adverse events or episodes) and over six months for chronic and long‐term conditions (malocclusion, periodontal disease, tumours, bone loss or long‐standing adverse effects such as chronic nerve damage).

    Primary outcomes

    1. Resolution or reduction in severity of malocclusion (e.g. as measured by class of malocclusion, cephalometric analysis, malocclusion indices)

    2. Resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication (e.g. dental caries, periodontal disease, cysts, tumours), assessed by relevant measures

    3. Adverse effects related to treatment (e.g. worsening of systemic condition, impairment or loss of oral function, root resorption, nerve damage, bone loss)

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Resolution or reduction in severity of pain (e.g. as measured by visual analogue scale (VAS), dichotomous scale or Likert scale for pain assessment)

    2. Occurrence of systemic infections after dental treatment under antibiotic prophylaxis

    3. Quality of life (physical and social function, ability to fulfil roles, life satisfaction) as measured using a validated scoring system (e.g. 36‐Item Short Form Health Survey (SF‐36; RAND Health Care), the Profile of Mood Status (POMS; McNair 1971), Child Health Questionnaire‐Parent Report Form (Drotar 2006))

    4. Effects on professional and academic functions (e.g. loss or change of job, number of days off work or school)

    Search methods for identification of studies

    We searched for all relevant published and unpublished trials without restrictions on language, year or publication status.

    Electronic searches

    We searched for relevant trials in the Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group's Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register using the strategy 'dental AND (thalassaemia OR haemoglobinopathies general)'.

    The Haemoglobinopathies Trials Register is compiled from electronic searches of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; updated with each new issue of the Cochrane Library) and weekly searches of MEDLINE. Unpublished work is identified by searching the abstract books of five major conferences: the European Haematology Association conference, the American Society of Hematology conference, the British Society for Haematology Annual Scientific Meeting, the Caribbean Health Research Council Meetings and the National Sickle Cell Disease Program Annual Meeting. For full details of all searching activities for the register, see the relevant section of the Cochrane Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Group website.

    We checked the bibliographies of included trials and any relevant systematic reviews for further references to relevant trials.     Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (PM and RC in 2019, PM and ABL in 2022) independently screened the records identified by the searches. We obtained full‐text articles of all potentially relevant records to assess their eligibility. We identified and excluded duplicates, and collated multiple reports of the same trial, so that each trial, rather than each report, was the unit of interest in the review. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram (Figure 1).

    Data extraction and management

    We adapted a form designed and piloted by the Cystic Fibrosis and Genetic Disorders Review Group to include the outcomes in our review and used this to record extracted data, including trial design, setting, participant characteristics, intervention details and outcome data. Two review authors (PM, PK) extracted the data independently. We resolved any discrepancies through discussion. We then entered the data into the Review Manager 5 software and checked for accuracy (RevMan 2014).

    We planned to assess the outcomes at different time points, depending on the condition: one week to six months for acute or initial‐stage complications (acute infections, caries, cysts, gingivitis, acute adverse events or episodes) and over six months for chronic and long‐term conditions (malocclusion, periodontal disease, tumours, bone loss or long‐standing adverse events like chronic nerve damage). Since the included study assessed interventions for gingivitis, the relevant time points were one week to six months. However, we reported only the 12‐week follow‐up values in the analysis, although the trial authors had also reported six‐week values. Because gingivitis often progresses to periodontitis through destruction of periodontal tissue, it is essential to confirm that therapeutic interventions are effective for longer time periods. If interventions were effective at six weeks but not at 12 weeks, their lack of efficacy would be adequately reflected at the 12‐week time point, irrespective of what happened at six weeks.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (PM, ABL) independently assessed the risk of bias of the included trials using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions(Higgins 2017). We resolved disagreements by discussion.

    Random sequence generation (selection bias)

    For each included trial, we described the method used to generate the allocation sequence in sufficient detail to allow an assessment of whether it should produce comparable groups. We judged methods at low risk of bias (any truly random process, such as random number table or computer random number generator), high risk of bias (any non‐random process, such as odd or even date of birth, hospital or clinical record number) or unclear risk of bias (insufficient information).

    Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    For each included trial, we described the method used to conceal the allocation to interventions prior to assignment and assess whether this allocation could have been foreseen in advance of or during recruitment, or changed after assignment. We judged methods at low risk of bias (e.g. telephone or central randomisation, consecutively numbered sealed opaque envelopes), high risk of bias (open random allocation, unsealed or non‐opaque envelopes, alternation or date of birth) or unclear risk of bias (insufficient information).

    Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    For each included trial, we described the methods used, if any, to blind participants and personnel to the intervention a participant received. We judged a trial at low risk of bias if participants were blinded, or if we considered that the lack of blinding was unlikely to affect results. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes. We judged the methods at low, high or unclear risk of bias separately for participants and trial personnel.

    Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    For each included trial, we described the methods used, if any, to blind outcome assessors to the intervention a participant received. We assessed blinding separately for different outcomes or classes of outcomes. We judged the methods used to blind outcome assessment at low, high or unclear risk of bias.

    Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    For each included trial and for each outcome or class of outcomes, we described the completeness of data, including attrition and exclusions from the analysis. We stated whether attrition and exclusions were reported and the numbers included in the analysis at each stage (compared with the total randomised participants), reasons for attrition or exclusion where reported, and whether missing data were balanced across groups or were related to outcomes. We assessed trials as being at low risk of bias (e.g. no missing outcome data or missing outcome data balanced across groups), high risk of bias (e.g. numbers or reasons for missing data imbalanced across groups, or as‐treated analysis with substantial departure of intervention received from that assigned at randomisation) or unclear risk of bias.

    We considered up to 20% missing data for each outcome to be acceptable.

    Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    For each included trial, we described how we investigated the possibility of selective outcome reporting bias and what we found. We judged the methods at low risk (where it is clear that the publication reports all prespecified outcomes and all expected key outcomes), high risk (where the publication does not report all prespecified outcomes, or one or more reported primary outcomes were not prespecified, or outcomes of interest are reported incompletely and so cannot be used, or the trial does not include results of an expected key outcome) or unclear risk.

    Other bias (bias due to problems not covered by the other domains of the tool)

    For each included trial, we described any important concerns we had about other possible sources of bias. We assessed whether each trial was free of other problems that could put it at risk of bias and allocated judgements of low, high or unclear risk.

    Overall risk of bias

    We made explicit judgements about whether trials were at high risk of bias, according to the criteria provided in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2017). With reference to the individual points listed above, we also assessed the likely magnitude and direction of the bias and whether we consider it was likely to impact the findings.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We entered the trial's outcome data into the data tables in Review Manager 5 to calculate the treatment effects (RevMan 2014). If we had encountered dichotomous data, we would have used risk ratios (RRs). We reported the mean difference (MD) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for the single continuous outcome. If we had identified multiple continuous outcomes, we would have ensured that higher scores for continuous outcomes had the same meaning for the particular outcome, explained the effect direction, and reported where the direction was reversed, if necessary. We displayed the results of the only relevant outcome in a forest plot (see Data collection and analysis). In future updates, if we identify any results that cannot be expressed in forest plots, we will present the data in an additional table or report results narratively.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We considered cross‐over trials ineligible for inclusion because the severity of the condition could change with the first treatment procedure.

    We identified no cluster‐randomised trials in our searches. If we identify any in future updates, we will adjust the sample sizes using an estimate of the intracluster correlation coefficient (ICC) derived from the trial (if possible), or from another source. If we use ICCs from other sources, we will report this and conduct sensitivity analyses to investigate the effect of variations in the ICC. If we identify both cluster‐RCTs and individually randomised trials, we plan to synthesise the relevant information. We will consider it reasonable to combine the results from both types of trial if there is little heterogeneity between the trial designs, and the choice of randomisation unit (individuals in RCT versus clusters in cluster RCT) is unlikely to influence the effects of the intervention. We also intend to acknowledge heterogeneity in the randomisation unit and perform a sensitivity analysis to investigate the effects of the randomisation unit (Higgins 2022). 

    Dealing with missing data

    Missing data were not a problem in the trial we included. In future updates of this review, we will explore the impact of including trials with high levels of missing data (if any) in the overall assessment of treatment effect through a sensitivity analysis. For all outcomes, we will carry out analyses (as far as possible) on an intention‐to‐treat basis (i.e. we will attempt to include all participants randomised to each group in the analyses, and analyse all participants in the group to which they were allocated, regardless of whether they received the allocated intervention). The denominator for each outcome in each trial will be the number randomised minus any participants with missing outcome data. We will also perform sensitivity analyses, substituting alternative decisions or values for assumptions such as intention‐to‐treat approach or excluding participants with missing outcome data, to assess how sensitive results are to reasonable changes.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We included only one RCT in this update. If we include more trials in future updates, we will assess clinical heterogeneity by considering any differences between trials in design or participant characteristics.

    We will assess statistical heterogeneity in each meta‐analysis by visually examining forest plots for clear differences in results and using the Tau², I² and Chi² statistics. For the interpretation of the I² values, we will use the following guidelines (Higgins 2017):

    1. 0% to 40%: might not be important;

    2. 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity;

    3. 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity; and

    4. 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    We will regard heterogeneity as substantial if the I² statistic is greater than 50% and either the Tau² statistic is greater than zero or the P value of the Chi² test for heterogeneity is less than 0.10.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    If we include at least 10 trials in a future meta‐analysis, we will investigate reporting biases (such as publication bias) using funnel plots. We will assess funnel plot asymmetry visually and perform exploratory analyses to investigate possible asymmetry as detailed in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Page 2022).

    Data synthesis

    We carried out statistical analysis using Review Manager 5 (RevMan 2014). For future meta‐analysis of multiple trials, we will use a fixed‐effect meta‐analysis for combining data where it is reasonable to assume that trials are estimating the same underlying treatment effect (i.e. where trials are examining the same intervention, and the trial populations and methods are judged sufficiently similar). If there is sufficient clinical heterogeneity to suggest different underlying treatment effects between trials, or if we detect substantial statistical heterogeneity with a clinically meaningful average treatment effect across trials, we will use a random‐effects meta‐analysis to produce an overall summary. We will treat the random‐effects summary as the average range of possible treatment effects, and we will discuss the clinical implications of treatment effects differing between trials. If the average treatment effect is not clinically meaningful, we will not combine trials. If we use a random‐effects analysis, we will present the results as the average treatment effect with 95% CIs and the estimates of the Tau² and I² statistics.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    If we identify substantial heterogeneity (as defined above) in future updates, we will investigate the causes using subgroup analyses. Where an overall summary is meaningful, we will use a random‐effects analysis to produce it. We intend to carry out the following subgroup analyses:

    1. different types of thalassaemia;

    2. different ages: children (under 18 years) versus adults (18 years and over); and

    3. severity of anaemia (mild, moderate, severe).

    We will assess subgroup differences using interaction tests available within Review Manager 5 (RevMan 2014). We will report the results of the subgroup analyses quoting the Chi² statistic and P value, and the interaction test I² value.

    Sensitivity analysis

    If we include more trials in future updates, we will perform sensitivity analyses for aspects of the review that might affect the results (e.g. risk of bias). We will analyse the primary outcome separately for trials with a low risk of bias and high or unclear risk of bias for allocation concealment. We will explore the impact of including trials with high levels of missing data in the overall assessment of treatment effect, and we will assess how sensitive results are to reasonable changes in the assumptions we make on missing data, such as intention‐to‐treat approach or excluding participants with missing outcome data. We will carry out a sensitivity analysis to explore the effects of random‐effects analyses for outcomes with statistical heterogeneity. We will also conduct sensitivity analyses to investigate the effect of variations in any ICCs we have used.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a summary of findings table with the following key outcomes as listed under Types of outcome measures:

    1. change in class of malocclusion;

    2. resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication: dental caries (measured by reduction in Decayed, Missing due to caries and Filled Teeth in the permanent teeth (DMFT) index);

    3. resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication: gingivitis (measured by bleeding on probing (BoP));

    4. resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication: periodontitis (measured by clinical attachment loss);

    5. resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication: oral infections (measured by reduction in or resolution of lesions such as periapical cysts, abscesses, or space infections);

    6. resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication: systemic infections (measured by number of episodes after treatment for dental complications under antibiotic prophylaxis); and

    7. any other adverse effects related to treatment.

    We used the five GRADE considerations (risk of bias, imprecision, inconsistency, indirectness and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the evidence for the prespecified outcomes with GRADEpro software, according to methods and recommendations described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (GRADEpro GDT; Higgins 2017; Schünemann 2022). We justified all decisions to downgrade or upgrade the certainty of the evidence using footnotes, and we added comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review where necessary.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    We screened 37,242 titles from the search results of nine databases and registries. We eliminated three duplicates and identified two unique RCTs as potentially eligible for inclusion in the review (Figure 1). One trial was only available via an ongoing trials registry and the trial authors did not respond to requests to provide further information; based on the available information, we excluded it (NCT03311243). After a full‐text analysis of the second RCT we agreed that it met all our eligibility criteria and included it in our review (Al Asmari 2020).

    Included studies

    We included one RCT that evaluated interventions for gingivitis in 30 participants with thalassaemia (providing data from 29 participants; Al Asmari 2020).

    Location and settings

    The included trial was a single‐centre parallel RCT conducted in a clinical setting in Saudi Arabia (Al Asmari 2020). We unsuccessfully attempted to contact the trial authors to establish whether the trial was conducted in a private practice or in their affiliated school of dentistry. The trial was conducted between June 2017 and December 2018 and the follow‐up period was 12 weeks.

    Participants

    After screening 89 individuals with a known history of thalassaemia who were receiving haematological care in a blood disorder centre, the trial authors randomised 30 participants to two groups (15 to each). One participant from the intervention group dropped out. We recorded the inclusion and exclusion criteria in the Characteristics of included studies table. The mean age of participants in the intervention group was 22.51 years, while the mean age of participants in the control group was 23.62 years. Of the 29 participants who completed the trial, 19 were male and 10 female (Al Asmari 2020).

    Interventions

    Group A received full‐mouth ultrasonic scaling (FMUS) with photodynamic therapy (PDT) and Group B received only FMUS (Al Asmari 2020).

    For FMUS, a dental practitioner used an ultrasonic scaler device to remove hard deposits around teeth. After the procedure, all participants were educated on oral hygiene measures (tooth brushing technique, dental flossing and use of mouth rinse). They received verbal reinforcement at each follow‐up appointment and toothpastes as an honorarium.

    In PDT, light interacts with a photosensitising agent in the presence of oxygen to destroy abnormal cells. In Al Asmari 2020, the dental practitioner used a diode laser (HELBO TheraLite, Bredent Medical, Germany) around the teeth with the worst gingival scores (gingival index (GI) of 2 or more) after FMUS at baseline. The Characteristics of included studies table presents further details. The PDT session lasted 60 minutes on average.

    Outcomes

    Investigators assessed outcomes at baseline, at six weeks and at 12 weeks after the intervention. The outcomes assessed were plaque index (PI) at four sites per tooth, GI at four sites per tooth, probing depth at six sites per tooth, BoP at six sites per tooth, interleukin (IL)‐6 levels and tumour necrosis factor (TNF)‐α levels. The tables in the article present further details of measurements (Al Asmari 2020). Because IL‐6 and TNF‐α are surrogate and indirect measures of periodontal disease, we did not analyse these outcomes. 

    Excluded studies

    We excluded one trial that was available only as a registry entry (NCT03311243). It compared the effectiveness of non‐surgical plaque control measures for treating chronic periodontitis in two groups of participants. Although the trial authors described the trial as randomised, the participants in one group had β‐thalassaemia while those in the second group were healthy controls, and all participants received the same treatment. As our review aims to compare different interventions between groups of people with thalassaemia, this trial was not suitable for inclusion (NCT03311243).

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We judged the overall risk of bias of the trial as unclear. Because lack of reporting constituted a potential source of bias, we attempted to obtain additional information from the trial authors, but were unsuccessful. Therefore, we completed the risk of bias assessment based on available information in the published report. Figure 2 summarises the review authors' judgements about each risk of bias item.

    Allocation

    We considered the trial at low risk of bias related to sequence generation, as the investigators used a computer‐based randomisation program. However, the trial report did not describe the method of allocation concealment, so we considered the risk of bias for this domain to be unclear.

    Blinding

    The trial report did not mention blinding of participants, care providers or outcome assessors, although it would have been possible to blind participants at least. However, lack of blinding does not affect the objective primary outcome measures of the study, so we considered the study at low risk of bias for this domain.

    Incomplete outcome data

    One participant in the intervention group dropped out of the trial, and the report provided no reason. This level of attrition is within the standard or expected range, so we judged the trial at low risk of attrition bias.

    Selective reporting

    We judged the trial at low risk of reporting bias because the results section included all outcomes specified in the trial objectives.

    Other potential sources of bias

    The report did not describe details of participant recruitment (e.g. the population pool from which participants were recruited) or participant characteristics that are known to influence gingivitis (e.g. smoking, socioeconomic status). Therefore, we considered there was insufficient information to assess risk of bias from other sources, and we classified this domain as unclear.

    Effects of interventions

    Primary outcomes

    1. Resolution or reduction in severity of malocclusion 

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess resolution of reduction in severity of malocclusion.

    2. Resolution or reduction in severity of any other dental complication 

    We included GI, BoP, PI and probing depth as indicators of gingival or periodontal disease for analysis in our review. 

    In Al Asmari 2020, the investigators rated GI at four sites according to the Löe‐Silness index, with scores from 0 to 3 (0 = normal gingiva, 3 = severe inflammation; Loe 1963). The group that received PDT in addition to FMUS showed greater reduction from baseline in gingivitis at 12 weeks as measured by GI (MD 0.16, 95% CI 0.02 to 0.30; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1).

    Investigators measured BoP dichotomously (0 = bleeding absent, 1 = bleeding present) at six sites per tooth, within 15 seconds of probing, and the article reported the proportion of participants in each group with BoP (Al Asmari 2020). As with GI scores, there was a greater reduction in the number of participants experiencing BoP at 12 weeks in the FMUS plus PDT group that in the group receiving FMUS alone (MD 8.50, 95% CI 6.76 to 10.24; Analysis 1.2). 

    Investigators examined plaque levels visually and determined PI at four sites of the teeth using the Silness‐Löe index (0 = absence of plaque, 3 = marked accumulation of plaque; Silness 1964). There was no difference between groups in the change from baseline in PI at 12 weeks (MD 0.06, 95% CI −0.13 to 0.25; Analysis 1.3).

    Investigators assessed probing depth (mm) at six sites per tooth. There was no difference between the groups in the change from baseline at 12 weeks (MD −0.01, 95% CI −0.22 to 0.20; Analysis 1.4).

    3. Adverse effects of treatment 

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess adverse effects of treatment.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Resolution or reduction in severity of pain

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess reduction in pain severity.

    2. Occurrence of systemic infections after dental treatment under antibiotic prophylaxis

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess the occurrence of systemic infections after dental treatment under antibiotic prophylaxis.

    3. Quality of life

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess quality of life.

    Al Asmari 2020 did not assess effects on professional and academic functions.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This review update included one parallel RCT conducted in Saudi Arabia, which assessed the effectiveness of adjunctive PDT in addition to FMUS for treating gingivitis in individuals with thalassaemia. The trial reported one of the primary outcomes and none of the secondary outcomes of interest for this review.

    There is very low‐certainty evidence, based on 29 participants, that PDT plus FMUS compared to FMUS alone improves BoP in people with thalassaemia over 12 weeks. There was a similar effect on GI score, although we did not grade the certainty of the evidence for this outcome. The intervention had no effect on the periodontitis indicators of PI score or probing depth.

    We found no trials that assessed interventions for any other dental or orthodontic complications in people living with thalassaemia.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We identified only one relevant RCT for inclusion in this review. The evidence is provided is applicable only to people aged over 15 years with thalassaemia who are being treated for gingivitis. There is no evidence on the efficacy of other interventions used to treat any of the various dental complications of thalassaemia.

    Quality of the evidence

    We were only able to assess one outcome using the GRADE criteria (reduction in severity of gingivitis) and found the certainty of the evidence to be very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence because of an unclear risk of bias in the one included trial and due to imprecision, as the number of participants was low. We also downgraded because the trial only reported the outcome in people aged 15 years and older, and it is unclear whether the results would be applicable to a younger population (summary of findings Table 1).

    Potential biases in the review process

    To avoid potential bias, we conducted our review with meticulous care, following the peer‐reviewed and preapproved exhaustive methodology laid out in the protocol to eliminate arbitrariness or bias (Mulimani 2018). To strengthen the certainty of evidence generated, we chose to include only RCTs and eliminated other trial designs which are less methodologically rigorous and prone to bias.

    We searched the literature extensively for this Cochrane Review without language or date restrictions, and we applied appropriate inclusion and exclusion criteria to identify the trials which truly addressed the review question. Two review authors independently carried out steps in the review process such as screening and selection of trials, and we resolved any disagreements through discussion and consensus. Despite our best efforts, it is possible that our search missed relevant trials. However, we believe that the efforts made during the review process reflect the best possible status of available evidence for treatment of dental and orthodontic complications in thalassaemia. The current status is that there is still a dearth of appropriately designed, executed and reported RCTs to investigate treatment modalities for dental and orthodontic problems in people with thalassaemia to aid in clinical practice and application.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Studies in people who do not have thalassaemia have found PDT to be an effective adjuvant to conventional FMUS (Meimandi 2017; Park 2019). The trial included in our review suggests this intervention may also be effective for treating gingivitis in individuals with thalassaemia. However, more information is needed regarding adverse effects and long‐term benefits. These aspects are particularly important in a systemic, chronic health condition such as thalassaemia.
