    • Background

    Pharmacotherapies such as loop diuretics are the cornerstone treatment for acute heart failure (AHF), but resistance and poor response can occur. Ultrafiltration (UF) is an alternative therapy to reduce congestion, however its benefits, efficacy and safety are unclear.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of UF compared to diuretic therapy on clinical outcomes such as mortality and rehospitalisation rates.

    Search methods

    We undertook a systematic search in June 2021 of the following databases: CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, Web of Science CPCI‐S and ClinicalTrials.gov. We also searched the WHO ICTRP platform in October 2020.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that compared UF to diuretics in adults with AHF.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed trial quality and extracted data. We contacted study authors for any further information, and language interpreters to translate texts. We assessed risk of bias in included studies using Risk of Bias 2 (RoB2) tool and assessed the certainty of the evidence using GRADE.

    Main results

    We included 14 trials involving 1190 people. We included people who had clinical signs of acute hypervolaemia. We excluded critically unwell people such as those with ischaemia or haemodynamic instability. Mean age ranged from 57.5 to 75 years, and the setting was a mix of single and multi‐centre. Two trials researched UF as a complimentary therapy to diuretics, while the remaining trials withheld diuretic use during UF. There was high risk of bias in some studies, particularly with deviations from the intended protocols from high cross‐overs as well as missing outcome data for long‐term follow‐up. 

    We are uncertain about the effect of UF on all‐cause mortality at 30 days or less (risk ratio (RR) 0.61, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.13 to 2.85; 3 studies, 286 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). UF may have little to no effect on all‐cause mortality at the longest available follow‐up (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.73 to 1.36; 9 studies, 987 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    UF may reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation at 30 days or less (RR 0.76, 95% CI 0.53 to 1.09; 3 studies, 337 participants; low‐certainty evidence). UF may slightly reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation at longest available follow‐up (RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.79 to 1.05; 6 studies, 612 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    UF may reduce heart failure‐related rehospitalisation at 30 days or less (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.37 to 1.04; 2 studies, 395 participants; low‐certainty evidence). UF probably reduces heart failure‐related rehospitalisation at longest available follow‐up, with a number needed to treat for an additional beneficial effect (NNTB) of 10 (RR 0.69, 95% CI 0.53 to 0.90; 4 studies, 636 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence). 

    No studies measured need for mechanical ventilation. 

    UF may have little or no effect on serum creatinine change at 30 days since discharge (mean difference (MD) 14%, 95% CI −12% to 40%; 1 study, 221 participants; low‐certainty evidence). UF may increase the risk of new initiation of renal replacement therapy at longest available follow‐up (RR 1.42, 95% CI 0.42 to 4.75; 4 studies, 332 participants; low‐certainty evidence). 

    There is an uncertain effect of UF on the risk of complications from central line insertion in hospital (RR 4.16, 95% CI 1.30 to 13.30; 6 studies, 779 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). 

    Authors' conclusions

    This review summarises the latest evidence on UF in AHF. Moderate‐certainty evidence shows UF probably reduces heart failure‐related rehospitalisation in the long term, with an NNTB of 10. UF may reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation at 30 days or less and at longest available follow‐up. The effect of UF on all‐cause mortality at 30 days or less is unclear, and it may have little effect on all‐cause mortality in the long‐term. 

    While UF may have little or no effect on serum creatinine change at 30 days, it may increase the risk of new initiation of renal replacement therapy in the long term. The effect on complications from central line insertion is unclear. 

    There is insufficient evidence to determine the true impact of UF on AHF. Future research should evaluate UF as an adjunct therapy, focusing on outcomes such as heart failure‐related rehospitalisation, cardiac mortality and renal outcomes at medium‐ to long‐term follow‐up.

    • Fluid removal therapy in acute heart failure

    Review question

    What are the effects of ultrafiltration (UF), a fluid removal therapy, in acute heart failure (AHF)?

    Background

    AHF is a common condition where the heart does not pump effectively, causing fluid to accumulate in the lungs and body. This causes difficulty in breathing, damage to heart and kidneys, high readmission to hospital and high death rates. Usual care involves medications called diuretics to remove this excess fluid, but some people may become resistant to it. UF provides an alternative therapy to quickly remove fluid. A circuit removes blood from the patient, which is then filtered in a machine before going back to the patient. It requires monitoring, a large central line cannula (tube into a blood vessel) and a blood thinning medication. It is unclear whether UF is effective or safe in AHF.  

    Study characteristics

    We searched for studies comparing UF to usual care in people with AHF. We searched for all relevant studies up to June 2021 and found 14 studies with about 1200 people. People who were very sick and frail, such as those who have had heart attacks and infections, were often not studied.

    Certainty of the evidence

    We used a validated tool (GRADE) to assess how certain we were of our results. Overall, there was low‐certainty evidence from these studies because of variability in the study settings, conflicting results between studies and limitations in how the studies were designed. 

    Key results

    We are uncertain whether UF has an impact on death rates at a short‐term follow‐up, as there are not enough data. At the longest time of follow‐up, UF may have little effect on death rates. 

    UF may reduce readmission to hospital rates within the short and long term. In particular, readmission rates from heart failure are probably reduced by 31% when followed up over a longer time frame (up to one year). The benefit of reducing heart failure‐related rehospitalisation is seen in 1 of 10 people treated with UF.

    UF may increase the risk of needing long‐term dialysis. It is not clear whether UF affects kidney function at 30 days since hospital discharge, or if the risk of central line complications are greater with UF. No studies researched the effect of UF on needing mechanical breathing support, or its effect on cost and health economics. 

    More robust studies looking at UF in conjunction with current therapy are needed. These should focus on outcomes that are important to patients, such as heart failure‐related hospital readmission rates, cardiac death rates and kidney damage at medium to long term.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The primary implication for practice is that ultrafiltration (UF) probably reduces heart failure (HF)‐related rehospitalisation at the longest available follow‐up. Moderate‐certainty evidence from four randomised controlled trials (RCTs) suggest a large and clinically significant estimated effect size (number need to treat for an additional beneficial outcome = 10). UF may reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation in the short and long term, while the effect on all‐cause mortality is unclear at 30days or less, and may have little effect in the long term.  

    Given that HF‐related rehospitalisation is a major public health burden, this finding could suggest a role for UF in people with acute heart failure (AHF) similar to those enrolled in the included RCTs. RCTs were conducted in middle‐ to high‐income countries, including people with AHF with hypervolaemia who were not critically unwell. In this patient population, readmission due to inadequate decongestion is a common and ongoing problem, which would be expected to impose a quality of life burden on affected patients.  

    This has to be balanced with the risk of complications, since UF may increase the risk of initiating long‐term dialysis. The risk from central venous access in UF is uncertain, but should be considered by physicians and patients to engage in shared decision‐making regarding the initiation of UF. Since UF can be achieved through different types of venous access that are less invasive, this would be a point of consideration. 

    No prospective cost analysis of UF has been conducted. From a health economics and policy perspective, decision makers will have to weigh up healthcare utilisation costs of UF against potential benefits such as reduction in HF‐related rehospitalisations.  

    Overall, no strong recommendation for UF can be made at this time based on the uncertainty of the evidence presented, but in carefully selected cases it may be appropriate.

    Implications for research

    The certainty of the evidence for UF is significantly limited by the choice of endpoints in our included trials. For instance, while creatinine changes and weight changes are reported in nearly all studies, there was less consistent reporting of more patient‐centred outcomes such as HF‐related rehospitalisation, mortality, hospital length of stay and need for renal replacement therapy. There are a few likely reasons for this. 

    First, weight change and change in serum creatinine are relatively easier to measure as trial endpoints than clinical outcomes such as HF‐related mortality, rehospitalisation and others that require extended follow‐up in a rigorous manner outside the hospital. Such clinical outcomes were measured in the few large RCTs but generally absent from the smaller studies.  While there appears to be pragmatic barriers to further large RCTs with follow‐up of clinically meaningful outcomes, such studies are nevertheless needed.

    Second, certain paradigms in the understanding of AHF have changed over time. Previous studies chose serum creatinine at short follow‐up as an endpoint, which is now understood to not be a clinically significant outcome or a meaningful measure of adverse events. Many studies did not evaluate need for renal replacement therapy, an important patient‐centred outcome. Cardiac and HF‐specific outcomes were poorly studied, which may underestimate the true clinical effect of UF given it is not expected to alter the disease course but rather help with decongestion. This is relevant to the endpoints and duration of follow‐up in future clinical trials. 

    Last, UF is targeted as a complementary therapy to usual care to help improve diuretic responsiveness, rather than as an alternative option. Future studies should focus on this, as this will provide data that translate well into clinical practice.  

    Thus, changing understanding of AHF has potentially moved the goal posts for what an ideal primary trial endpoint should be when comparing UF to usual care.  Future studies should focus on clinical outcomes such as HF‐related rehospitalisation, cardiac mortality and renal outcomes at medium‐ to long‐term follow‐up. Studies should evaluate the benefit of UF as a complementary therapy, and further research of critically unwell patients may also be additive.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Heart failure (HF) is a clinical syndrome caused by any structural or functional (or both) impairment in the ability of the heart to fill or eject blood (Ponikowski 2016; Yancy 2013). This leads to reduced cardiac output or increased intracardiac pressures, or both, resulting in symptoms such as dyspnoea, fatigue and peripheral oedema. Acute heart failure (AHF) is any rapid deterioration of HF signs and symptoms requiring urgent therapy (Ponikowski 2016; Yancy 2013). AHF can be precipitated by a variety of factors, such as ischaemia, arrhythmia, non‐adherence to diet or medications, pneumonia/respiratory processes, hypertension and worsening renal function (Fonarow 2008). Numerous prognostic factors for AHF have been previously described (Hollenberg 2019), among them advanced age and decreased kidney function (estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) less than 60 mL/minute) (Hollenberg 2019; Rangaswami 2019). 

    AHF is a heterogeneous set of clinical syndromes, with several classification systems in place (Alla 2007). It can be divided into people with existing, chronic HF with an acute decompensation (65% to 75% of patients hospitalised), or first presentation de novo HF (Farmakis 2018; Gheorghiade 2005; Nieminen 2006). Patients can be classified according to left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF), as HF with reduced LVEF less than 40% (HFrEF), mid‐range LVEF 40% to 49% (HFmrEF), or HF with preserved LVEF of 50% or greater (HFpEF) (Farmakis 2018). Another useful way of classifying AHF is through clinical profiles, looking at the presence or absence of hypoperfusion (cold peripheries, oliguria, dizziness, altered mental state) or congestion (pulmonary congestion, orthopnoea, peripheral oedema, ascites) (Ponikowski 2016; Yancy 2013). This can guide initial treatment as well as provide prognostic information. 

    Treatment goals in AHF include improvement in symptoms, limiting cardiac and renal injury, and preventing early readmission. Reducing congestion is a key goal in AHF, given that most people present with these symptoms (Ambrosy 2014). Pharmacotherapies such as loop diuretics are the primary treatments, administered to over 90% of patients (Fonarow 2007; Llorens 2015). They reduce sodium re‐absorption, which in turn increases water excretion and hypotonic urine production (Casu 2015). Dosage is adjusted according to the individual's clinical status, which is monitored by evaluating the patient's signs and symptoms, such as reduction in dyspnoea or daily weight. In some patients, there is poor response to pharmacotherapy, or diuretic resistance develops (Felker 2010; ter Maaten 2015), leading to refractory central venous congestion. Despite their common use, diuretics do not reduce mortality, and are associated with neuro‐hormonal activation, worsening renal function, electrolyte abnormalities and arrhythmias (Palazzuoli 2017).

    Shortness of breath is rated as the most‐bothersome symptom in about 50% to 60% of people with AHF, compared to 29% reporting oedema as their most bothersome symptom (Omar 2016). In people with acute pulmonary oedema, non‐invasive ventilation to provide positive pressure has become more common since the early 2000's (Masip 2018). This has reduced risk of pneumonia compared to traditional endotracheal intubation (Carron 2013). However, in people with persisting respiratory failure, mechanical invasive ventilation may still be needed (Masip 2018). 

    Description of the intervention

    Ultrafiltration (UF) is the removal of isotonic plasma fluid from whole blood across a semipermeable membrane (haemofilter) in response to a transmembrane pressure gradient (Ronco 2008). The UF circuit removes blood from the patient via a venous line (typically a central venous catheter). A syringe pump drives blood across the haemofilter within the extracorporeal circuit. Isotonic plasma fluid is removed here from the blood down the pressure gradient, after which the blood is returned to the patient (Costanzo 2019a). Portable UF devices are now available that do not require high‐level care monitoring, and the rate and volume of fluid removal can be precisely controlled. Anticoagulation is needed for patients to prevent filter clotting (Costanzo 2019a; Kazory 2016).

    How the intervention might work

    UF allows for effective and rapid decongestion. By removing isotonic plasma fluid, greater concentration of sodium per volume can be extracted, compared to diuretic use where hypotonic urine is excreted. This fluid removal can occur in a precise manner by controlling the rate and volume. It is associated with a reduction of intracardiac pressures (right atrial and pulmonary wedge pressures), as well as increased cardiac output (Giglioli 2010; Marenzi 2001). It also reduces renal venous congestion and improves renal haemodynamics and response to diuretic therapy (Costanzo 2019a; Giglioli 2010; Kazory 2016). 

    We expect the intervention to impact short‐ and medium‐term outcomes rather than alter the HF disease course. There is some limited evidence of sustained beneficial effects months after UF use (Agostoni 1994; Kazory 2016), which may be due to reduced neurohormonal activation, improved renal haemodynamics or other theoretically beneficial physiological effects (Hartupee 2017; Kazory 2016; Rangaswami 2019).

    Why it is important to do this review

    AHF is widely prevalent across the globe and is associated with high mortality and morbidity. In‐hospital mortality rates range from 4% in US to 7% in European surveys (Farmakis 2018). It is also the leading cause of hospitalisation in individuals over the age of 65 years in high‐income countries, representing 1% to 2% of all admissions (Gheorghiade 2005; Storrow 2014). In the USA, HF results in one million emergency department presentations and accounts for 1% to 2% of healthcare resource utilisation, 75% of which is attributed to hospitalisations (Bundkirchen 2004). High rates of rehospitalisation are seen with AHF, with rates ranging from 22% to 30% at one to three months (Farmakis 2018).

    Despite the high prevalence and burden of disease, there have been limited advances in AHF therapy in recent years (Alexander 2015; Collins 2015; Kazory 2016; Ma 2019; Metra 2019; Wang 2017). UF has physiological and theoretical clinical appeal, however its risks and benefits over the current standard of care must be evaluated.

    The most significant adverse effect of UF is acute kidney injury. Removal of fluid via UF can cause significant haemodynamic shifts and therefore temporary renal hypoperfusion (Wehbe 2015). However, it is now recognised that transient rise in serum creatinine during AHF therapy is not necessarily indicative of acute kidney injury (Ahmad 2018), and may not be a meaningful marker of long‐term adverse outcomes (Coca 2016). Long‐term renal effects of UF are unclear, with retrospective and observational UF studies suggesting a risk of renal replacement therapy with dialysis (Liang 2006; Patarroyo 2012), while some randomised trials with shorter follow‐up have varied outcomes (Costanzo 2017).

    Other risks are associated with catheter access including infection and flow problems, extracorporeal circuit clotting, bleeding due to over anticoagulation and hypotension due to intravascular depletion, which may require vasopressor support (Costanzo 2017; Liang 2006). These risks have been inconsistently reported in previous studies (Shi 2019).

    The economic implications also need to be considered since UF devices are expensive with increased healthcare staff and resource utilisation (Costanzo 2019a; Kazory 2016). No prospective cost analysis of UF is available (Costanzo 2017), and the limited retrospective analysis is inconclusive and conflicting. One analysis suggests that UF is unlikely to reduce cost from a societal perspective (Bradley 2009), with another suggesting that despite the higher up‐front costs, UF reduces longer‐term costs through reduction in rehospitalisation rates (Costanzo 2019b).

    Current international guidelines advise that UF therapy may benefit people with diuretic resistance, but the quality of the evidence is low. The 2016 European Society of Cardiology guidelines recommend that UF may be considered for people with refractory congestion who fail to respond to diuretic‐based strategies (quality of evidence – class IIb, level B) (Ponikowski 2016). The 2013 American College of Cardiology/American Heart Association guidelines suggest that UF may be considered in people with obvious volume overload (class IIb, level B) or refractory congestion (class IIb, level C) (Yancy 2013). A key research recommendation from the 2014 National Clinical Guideline Centre is evaluating whether UF can provide more rapid and effective decongestion compared to diuretics (NICE 2014).

    This Cochrane Review aims to provide stronger‐quality evidence by comparing UF to usual care (UC) with diuretic therapy. It summarises the current evidence, evaluating the effect of UF on mortality, rehospitalisation rates and adverse renal outcomes. This can help guide clinical practice as well as identify gaps in current research.

    Objectives

    Primary objective

    • To assess the effects of UF compared to diuretic therapy alone on clinical outcomes such as mortality and rehospitalisation rates.

    Secondary objectives

    • To assess whether the effects of UF differ in people with existing HF (HFrEF, HFmrEF or HFpEF).

    • To assess whether the effects of UF differ in people with or without renal impairment at the time of admission (eGFR less than 60 mL/minute).

    • To assess whether the effects of UF differ in settings with early initiation of UF therapy (less than 24 hours since admission) compared to delayed initiation in refractory congestion.

    • To assess whether the effects of UF differ in UF with intravenous diuretics versus UF alone.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included parallel‐arm randomised controlled trials (RCTs). We excluded cross‐over RCTs given the acute nature of the illness. The search found no cluster RCTs. We included studies reported as full text, those published as abstract only, and unpublished data.

    Types of participants

    We included adults (aged 18 years or over) with a clinical diagnosis of AHF of any severity or due to any aetiology, as defined by the investigators. We excluded participants with pre‐existing dialysis dependence. 

    In trials with mixed populations, that is trials where some participants met the inclusion criteria and others did not, we attempted to include only the eligible participants if this information was reported separately or could be obtained from the trial authors. Otherwise, we included studies with a mixed population if the majority (greater than 50%) of participants meet the eligibility criteria.

    Types of interventions

    We included trials where the intervention was UF compared to UC (defined as any intervention strategy not including UF). There were no restrictions on the duration, dosage or administration of diuretic use. We included UF through extracorporeal circuits.

    Types of outcome measures

    Reporting one or more of the outcomes listed here in the trial was not an inclusion criterion for the review. Where a published report had not reported one of these outcomes, we attempted to access the trial protocol and contact the trial authors to ascertain whether the outcomes were measured but not reported. We included relevant trials that measured these outcomes but did not report the data at all, or not in a usable format, in the review as part of the narrative.

    In studies where a time point was not specified, we attempted to contact the trial authors to obtain participant‐level data. If this was not available, we categorised it as longest available follow‐up.

    We collected data on tertiary outcomes such as quality of life and economic costs where this information was available, commenting on these outcomes narratively in the Discussion section.

    Primary outcomes

    • All‐cause mortality – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • All‐cause rehospitalisation – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • HF‐related rehospitalisation – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Weight change at longest in‐hospital follow‐up.

    • Need for mechanical invasive ventilation – up to and including 30 days from discharge.

    • Hospital length of stay.

    • Cardiac mortality – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • Change in serum creatinine (% change from admission) – 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • New initiation of long‐term renal replacement therapy at longest available follow‐up.

    • Major bleeding (as defined by study authors), in‐hospital.

    • Complications of central line insertion such as pneumothorax, bleeding, central line‐associated bloodstream infection (as defined by study authors), in‐hospital.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    On 4 June 2021, we conducted a systematic search of the following bibliographic databases:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) in the Cochrane Library (Issue 5, 2021);

    • Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, MEDLINE Daily and MEDLINE (Ovid, 1946 to 3 June 2021);

    • Embase (Ovid, 1980 to 2021 week 21);

    • CPCI‐S (Conference Proceedings Citation Index‐Science) on the Web of Science (Clarivate Analytics, 1990 to 4 June 2021).

    We adapted the search strategy for MEDLINE (Ovid) (Appendix 1) for use in the other databases. We applied the Cochrane sensitivity‐maximising RCT filter to MEDLINE (Ovid) and adaptations of it to the other databases, except CENTRAL (Lefebvre 2019). We searched Web of Science CPCI‐S for relevant conference abstracts.

    We also searched for ongoing and unpublished trials through the US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov/) and the World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/) in June 2021.

    We searched all databases from their inception and imposed no restrictions on language of publication or publication status.

    Searching other resources

    We checked the reference lists of all included studies and any relevant systematic reviews identified for additional references to trials. We examined relevant retraction statements and errata for included studies. Where we needed participant‐level data for outcomes, we contacted authors to request missing data.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (MS, NH) independently screened titles and abstracts of all studies identified by the search for potential relevance, coding them as 'retrieve' (eligible or potentially eligible/unclear) or 'do not retrieve'. We resolved any disagreements by discussion.

    We retrieved the full‐text study reports, and two review authors (MS, NH) independently screened the full texts and identified studies for inclusion, and identified and recorded reasons for exclusion of the ineligible studies. We resolved any disagreements through discussion or by consulting a third review author (AFC) if necessary. We identified and excluded duplicates and collated multiple reports of the same study so that each study, rather than each report, was the unit of interest in the review. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram and Characteristics of excluded studies table (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    We used a data collection form for study characteristics and outcome data that was piloted on one study in the review. Two review authors (MS, NH or author support AZ) extracted the following study characteristics from the included studies.

    • Methods: study design, total duration of study, number of study centres and location, study setting and date of study.

    • Participants: number randomised, number lost to follow‐up/withdrawn, number analysed, mean age, age range, gender (% women), ejection fraction classification (% HFpEF, HFmrEF, HFrEF), AHF diagnostic criteria used for enrolment, elevated cardiac troponin above/below the 99th percentile (%), chronic kidney disease stage (% I and II, % III and IV), creatinine and eGFR (mean), systolic blood pressure (mean, earliest reported value in hospital course), inclusion criteria and exclusion criteria.

    • Interventions: intervention (UF type, UF duration in days, UF flow rate), comparison (loop diuretic type, loop diuretic % continuous versus bolus dosing, cumulative intravenous loop diuretic dose expressed in milligram furosemide equivalents), concomitant medications (i.e. loop diuretics used in UF group; adjunct diuretic medications in control group – metolazone, tolvaptan, acetazolamide, chlorothiazide) and excluded medications.

    • Outcomes: primary and secondary outcomes specified and collected, and time points reported.

    • Notes: funding for trial and notable conflicts of interest of trial authors.

    Two people independently extracted outcome data (review authors MS/NH with author support AZ), while author support AZ extracted characteristics of included studies. 

    We resolved any disagreements by consensus or by involving a third review author (AFC). One review author (AT) transferred data into Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2019). We double‐checked that the data had been entered correctly by comparing the data presented in the systematic review with the data extraction form. A second review author (NH) spot‐checked study characteristics for accuracy against the trial report.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MS/NH or ML/AFC) independently assessed risk of bias for each study using version 2 of the Cochrane risk of bias tool (RoB 2), outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Sterne 2019). We resolved any disagreements by discussion or by involving a third review author (AT) if needed. We assessed the risk of bias in the included studies according to domains of bias:

    • arising from the randomisation process;

    • due to deviations from intended interventions;

    • due to missing outcome data;

    • in measurement of the outcome and

    • in selection of the reported result.

    We assessed the risk of bias for the outcomes of the included trials, which are included in summary of findings Table 1. We did ROB 2 assessments for all our primary outcomes at the time point of up to 30 days, as well as at the longest available follow‐up, even though this latter time point was not planned for the summary of findings table in the Cochrane Review protocol (Srivastava 2020). This was done in discussion with content experts as this data might be clinically useful. We did additional ROB 2 assessments for weight change, to undertake sensitivity analysis given moderate heterogeneity.

    When conducting the risk of bias assessment, we quantified the effect of assignment to the interventions at baseline, regardless of whether the interventions were received as intended (the intention‐to‐treat effect).

    We used the signalling questions in the RoB 2 tool and rated each domain as 'low risk of bias', 'some concerns' or 'high risk of bias'. We summarised the risk of bias judgements across different studies for each of the domains listed for each outcome. The overall risk of bias for the result was the least favourable assessment across the domains of bias.

    When considering treatment effects, we considered the risk of bias for the studies that contributed to that outcome.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed dichotomous data as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) and continuous data as mean difference (MD) with 95% CIs. We entered data presented as a scale with a consistent direction of effect.

    We narratively described skewed data reported as medians and interquartile ranges.

    When an outcome event had the potential to occur more than once by a specified time point (e.g. rehospitalisation), we based analysis on the first event.

    When denominators differed between outcomes within a single study due to censoring, we used the denominators provided by the authors accounting for censoring. Censoring is a statistical concept that refers to the situation in which a patient could not experience a given outcome past a certain time (the time of censoring) because they were no longer in the study.  Examples of when censoring may occur include those in which a patient is lost to follow‐up (i.e. they left the study before the time point of interest for an outcome) or an event happened that precluded another outcome from occurring (e.g. a patient died at time X, so they could not experience rehospitalisation after time X and should not be included in the denominator).  

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the participant. In cases where a study reported that a participant received UF during more than one hospitalisation between enrolment and follow‐up, we contacted the authors to obtain individual participant data and calculated patient‐time at the unit of single hospitalisation with UF.

    If a study used more than two groups (specifically UF versus UF plus diuretic versus diuretic alone), we attempted to obtain participant‐level data from the study authors and analysed data as 'UF versus usual care'. To account for the potential bias introduced by grouping 'UF' with 'UF plus diuretic' groups (if a study was designed in such a way), we attempted to perform subgroup analysis as described in Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity; however, insufficient studies reported this.

    Given that we are interested in multiple clinically relevant time points for certain primary and secondary outcomes (e.g. 30‐day versus longest available follow‐up), we first determined if a given study had reported outcomes in line with the time points of interest in our Cochrane Review protocol (Srivastava 2020). If data were not primarily analysed in a given study for one or more time points of interest, we contacted the study authors for participant‐level data to be analysed at the time points in our Cochrane Review protocol.

    We did not anticipate other significant unit of analysis issues considering that we have excluded cross‐over, cluster, and before‐after designs. The intervention by its nature cannot be performed on multiple body parts.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted investigators or study sponsors to verify key study characteristics and to obtain missing numerical outcome data where possible (e.g. when a study was identified as abstract only). It was not possible to use the Review Manager Web calculator to calculate missing standard deviations (SDs) because additional data from the trial, such as CIs, MDs or P values necessary for methods outlined in Sterne 2019 were not available. Therefore, we imputed the SDs from one of the other studies and chose the highest value to be conservative, as this is likely to result in the widest CI (Sterne 2019). We explored the impact of including studies with imputed SDs in the overall assessment of results by a sensitivity analysis including only studies without missing data.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We inspected forest plots visually to consider the direction and magnitude of effects and the degree of overlap between CIs. We used the I² statistic to measure heterogeneity among the trials in each analysis, while acknowledging that there is substantial uncertainty in the I² value when there is only a few studies. If we identified greater than moderate heterogeneity (defined here as an I² greater than 60%) (Sterne 2019), we reported it, combined studies using a random‐effects model and explored possible causes by prespecified subgroup analyses. 

    Additionally, regardless of the I² statistic, if visual inspection of the forest plots revealed any between‐study statistically significant differences in effect direction or magnitude for a given outcome, we used a random‐effects model and explored possible causes using our prespecified subgroup analyses. We further commented on characteristics of any discordant studies that could contribute to the observed discrepancy in magnitude or direction (or both) of effect.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We created and examined a funnel plot to assess for small‐study biases where we pooled data from at least 10 studies for a given outcome. 

    Data synthesis

    We undertook meta‐analyses only where this was meaningful, that is if the treatments, participants and underlying clinical questions were similar enough for pooling to make sense.

    We used a fixed‐effect model, unless the I² statistic was greater than 60%, or our assessment of forest plots as described above suggested more than moderate heterogeneity, in which case we used a random‐effects model in addition to the fixed‐effect model and reported both results. We undertook this approach with the assumption that an I² statistic greater than 60% represented greater than moderate heterogeneity; however, we acknowledge that in certain circumstances the I² statistic may not be sufficiently accurate to detect if significant heterogeneity is present (e.g. small number of studies, variation in direction of effect). 

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We were unable to carry out meaningful subgroup analysis, due to poor reporting of study characteristics or insufficient number of studies (fewer than 10 studies for statistically significant subgroup analysis recommended) (Deeks 2020).

    For outcomes with moderate heterogeneity, we attempted subgroup analysis for early versus late initiation of UF to determine if this reduced heterogeneity. 

    If enough data were available, we planned other subgroups such as UF with diuretics versus UF alone, LVEF (i.e. HFpEF versus HFrEF versus HFmrEF) and eGFR on admission.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We conducted a sensitivity analysis for the weight change outcome due to moderate heterogeneity in the meta‐analysis.  If similar levels of precision were identified in this analysis, the outcome was reported narratively. A sensitivity analysis was conducted for percentage change in serum creatinine levels at longest available follow‐up to assess the impact of studies with imputed SDs (borrowed from another study in the meta‐analysis). Further sensitivity analyses were not conducted, as all studies were marked as low risk of bias for missing outcome data and none of the studies had a mixed population.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a summary of findings table using the following outcomes.

    • All‐cause mortality – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • All‐cause rehospitalisation – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • HF‐related rehospitalisation – up to and including 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • Change in serum creatinine (% change from admission) – 30 days from discharge, and longest available follow‐up.

    • New initiation of long‐term renal replacement therapy at longest available follow‐up.

    • Complications of central line insertion such as pneumothorax, bleeding, central line‐associated bloodstream infection (as defined by study authors), in‐hospital.

    In contrast to our Cochrane Review protocol (Srivastava 2020), we included the longest available follow‐up after the review was conducted for our primary outcomes (all‐cause mortality, all‐cause rehospitalisation and HF‐related readmission). This was done in discussion with content experts as these data might be clinically useful.

    We used the five GRADE considerations (study limitations assessed through overall risk of bias, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness and publication bias) to assess the certainty of the evidence as it related to the studies that contributed data to the meta‐analyses for the prespecified outcomes. We used the methods and recommendations described in Chapter 12 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Schünemann 2017), using GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT). We justified all decisions to downgrade the certainty of evidence of studies using footnotes and made comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review where necessary.

    We made judgements about evidence certainty (AT) with clarifications and discussion by involving two review authors (MS, NH). We justified, documented and incorporated judgements into the reporting of results for each outcome.

    We undertook post‐hoc analysis and completed risk of bias assessments and GRADE certainty for our primary outcomes at both time points. This was done in consultation with content experts when it was clinically useful. 

    We extracted study data, formatted our comparisons in data tables, and prepared a summary of findings table before writing the results and conclusions of our review.

    Included studies

    See the Characteristics of included studies table for details of the 14 included studies.  

    Study inclusion criteria generally focused on signs and symptoms of hypervolaemia, as well as hospital admission diagnosis, to determine the presence of AHF and suitability for the trial.  In most cases, this included a mix of both objective and subjective criteria (oedema, jugular venous distension, chest radiography, invasive haemodynamic measurements, patient‐reported symptoms and others), with no two studies having exactly the same inclusions.  Exclusion criteria in many, but not all, studies screened out people receiving more than one or two doses of intravenous diuretics before randomisation. Many studies, particularly the largest trials (UNLOAD, CARRESS and AVOID), further excluded people with ACS, recent coronary intervention, any signs of haemodynamic instability (i.e. patient subgroups with the highest mortality risk from AHF), or a combination of these. Two studies compared UF with diuretic use to UC (Hu 2020; Shen 2017), while the remaining studies withheld diuretics during UF administration.

    The proportion of women was approximately 10% to 30% in all studies except Sav 2017 (68% in UF group and 40% in UC group were women) and Hu 2020 (45% were women overall). Mean age for the included studies ranged from 57.5 years (Shen 2017 UC arm) to 75 years (Marenzi 2014 UF arm), with the mean of most studies being in the 60s. Studies were generally well‐balanced by age and gender between the UC and UF groups. Five studies reported patients' categorical LVEF (i.e. HFpEF versus HFrEF); all five had mostly people with HFrEF (Bart 2005; Chung 2010; Costanzo 2010; Hanna 2012). Five studies (242 participants) were set in Europe (Giglioli 2011; Marenzi 2014; Sav 2017; Seker 2016; Tabak'ian 2010), one study (10 participants) in South America (Stein 2011), two studies (234 participants) in Asia (Hu 2020; Shen 2017), and six studies (704 participants) in North America (Bart 2005; Bart 2012; Chung 2010; Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015; Hanna 2012). Most studies had industry funding, included a mix of single and multi‐centre studies, and durations ranging from a few months to years.

    Studies varied markedly by outcomes assessed. Unfortunately, we were unable to obtain unpublished data for any of the 14 included studies regarding outcomes not commented on in study reports. The most commonly reported outcomes were weight loss (13/14 studies), change in serum creatinine (8/14 studies) and all‐cause mortality (9/14 studies). 

    Time points of reporting for weight loss and change in serum creatinine varied markedly. Our protocol dichotomised most outcomes into 30 days or less from hospital discharge versus longest available follow‐up in anticipation of such time point heterogeneity.  When considered by this dichotomy, overall heterogeneity by using the I2 statistic was high for weight change but not for change in serum creatinine.  

    No studies reported the occurrence of participants requiring mechanical invasive ventilation for respiratory failure at any time point. No included studies reported cardiac mortality for the 30‐day or less time point, but two studies did report this outcome at later time points (i.e. longest available follow‐up). No studies reported percentage change in creatinine at 30 days. At least one included study reported all other primary and secondary outcomes for each of the time points specified in our protocol.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 19 studies during full‐text screening (see Characteristics of excluded studies table).  The reasons for exclusion in both stages were similar. Most study exclusions were for one of two reasons: the study was not a parallel group RCT of UF versus UC or the population studied consisted of outpatients with HF or both.  

    With regards to the second reason, the authors noted that by the European Society of Cardiology definition of AHF, being an outpatient does not explicitly exclude AHF (Ponikowski 2016). Namely, AHF is defined as signs and symptoms of HF requiring a patient to seek unscheduled care, and such unscheduled care can be in the form of outpatient services (e.g. a person with AHF who is treated in the emergency department and discharged without hospital admission). Nevertheless, the nature of UF as an intervention (prolonged timing, advanced venous access, anticoagulation, specialised personnel, etc.) is such that any clinical trial practically requires a person with AHF to be hospitalised for enrolment.  People with chronic HF being enrolled in the outpatient setting are thus not likely to be representative of our study question.

    We excluded three studies that met all inclusion criteria for our protocol, but, after contacting the study authors, were noted to have no people enrolled in the UF group (Baxter Healthcare 2008; Gevaert 2011; NCT00368641). 

    Studies awaiting classification

    Nine studies are currently awaiting classification (ChiCTR‐IOR‐15006111; ChiCTR2000035430; Koratala 2016; Mukharlimov 1985; NCT01140399; NCT01457053; NCT01863511; NCT03161158; Weinstein 2004; see Characteristics of studies awaiting classification table).  All were studies where further information was needed from the study authors to determine inclusion or exclusion, but in which the authors had not responded to email requests at the time of publication. None had retrievable full study reports (e.g. all were clinical trial registrations or abstract only, or both). There was no clear exclusion reason for any. Two had unclear methodology and were deemed to need a more comprehensive report of methods to clarify if they met the inclusion criteria (Koratala 2016; Mukharlimov 1985). The remaining seven appeared to meet the inclusion criteria but did not have reported results, most being clinical trials registered but terminated for poor enrolment. In each of those seven cases, we were able to discern that it was possible at least some participants were enrolled and thus could have unpublished data that can be added to the review when/if contact is possible with the authors.

    Ongoing studies

    We found no ongoing studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We assessed the included studies using the RoB 2 tool (Sterne 2019) for each of the outcomes included in summary of findings Table 1, and in the forest plots for these outcomes and the Risk of bias (tables). Detailed risk of bias assessments can be found as a supplemental file (RoB assessments).

    Overall, the risk of bias from the randomisation process and selection of reported results was low. There was minimal missing data for short‐term follow‐up, however there was bias from missing data when looking at longer term follow‐up (e.g. all‐cause rehospitalisation at longest available follow‐up). These missing data at long‐term follow‐up also influenced how that outcome was measured. Additionally, there was high risk of bias due to deviations from intended interventions, with crossover between the two arms noted in some studies. 

    While outcomes in many studies were at high risk of bias or had some concerns, in nearly all of these cases the bias would be expected to favour UC over UF.  Given that the comparison was UF versus UC and was unblinded, the particular issue with crossovers in the largest trials (Bart 2012; Costanzo 2015) led to participants with UF being treated as UC (but still analysed as UF, given an intention‐to‐treat analysis).  Towards the same effect, in studies such as CARRESS participants assigned to UC by protocol could have crossed over to UF (Bart 2012). It is unclear in how many such participants this occurred, since this specific type of crossover was protocol‐specified rather than an incidental protocol violation (the latter of which may also have occurred). 

    As another example in which bias may have been present, but ultimately would be expected to favour UC and not UF, participants in the CARRESS study were more likely to have an ischaemic cause of AHF in the UF group than the UC group (Bart 2012). Ischaemia as a precipitant of AHF is one of the strongest predictors of poor short‐ and long‐term outcomes, including mortality (Arrigo 2017; Fonarow 2008; Harrison 2019).

    Effects of interventions

    See: Summary of findings 1 Ultrafiltration (UF) compared to usual care (UC) for acute heart failure (AHF)

    There were few disagreements between review authors regarding study selection, data extraction or risk of bias assessments. All were resolved with consensus. For the comparison of UF to UC, the effects of intervention were pooled as MDs for continuous outcomes and RRs for dichotomous outcomes.  See details of the main results in the summary of findings Table 1.

    All‐cause mortality

    Three studies (286 participants randomised) reported all‐cause mortality at a follow‐up of 30 days or less (Bart 2005; Hu 2020; Shen 2017). We are uncertain about the effects of UF on all‐cause mortality at 30 days or less (RR 0.61, 95% CI 0.13 to 2.85; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1). Event rates were low in each arm (1/134 in UF group versus 3/152 in UC group).

    Nine studies (987 participants randomised) reported all‐cause mortality at longest available follow‐up, varying from 30 days in Bart 2005 to one year in Marenzi 2014 (Bart 2012; Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015; Hanna 2012; Hu 2020; Marenzi 2014; Seker 2016; Shen 2017; Stein 2011). UF may have little to no effect on all‐cause mortality at longest available follow‐up, but the CI also included appreciable benefit or harm (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.73 to 1.36; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2).

    All‐cause rehospitalisation

    Three studies (337 participants randomised) reported all‐cause rehospitalisation at 30‐day follow‐up or less (Chung 2010; Costanzo 2015; Hu 2020). UF may reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation at 30‐day follow‐up or less (RR 0.76, 95% CI 0.53 to 1.09; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.3).

    Six studies (612 participants randomised) reported all‐cause rehospitalisation at longest available follow‐up (Bart 2012; Chung 2010; Costanzo 2015; Hanna 2012; Hu 2020; Marenzi 2014). UF may slightly reduce all‐cause mortality at longest available follow‐up (RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.79 to 1.05; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4).

    Heart failure‐related rehospitalisation

    Two studies (395 participants randomised) reported HF‐related rehospitalisations at 30 days or less from discharge (Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015). UF may reduce HF‐related rehospitalisation at 30 days or less (RR 0.62, 95% CI 0.37 to 1.04; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.5).

    Four studies (636 participants randomised) reported HF‐related rehospitalisations at longest available follow‐up (Bart 2012; Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015; Marenzi 2014). UF probably reduces the rate of HF‐related rehospitalisations at longest available follow‐up (RR 0.69, 95% CI 0.53 to 0.90; moderate‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). This corresponded to an absolute risk reduction (ARR) of 10.9%, or a number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) of 10, for the prevention of HF‐related rehospitalisation in this population.

    Weight change 

    Thirteen studies (1126 participants randomised) reported weight change from baseline (Bart 2005; Bart 2012; Chung 2010; Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015; Giglioli 2011; Hanna 2012; Hu 2020; Marenzi 2014; Sav 2017; Shen 2017; Stein 2011; Tabak'ian 2010). The certainty of the evidence was very low due to moderate heterogeneity, wide CIs, high imprecision and methodological limitations. We are uncertain whether UF affects weight change (MD 1.74 kg, 95% CI −0.65 to 4.12; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.7). 

    The initial fixed‐effect model resulted in a high level of heterogeneity (I2 = 96%), which persisted when a random‐effects model was applied (I2 = 96%).  A sensitivity analysis according to overall risk of bias was conducted due to the high heterogeneity in the meta‐analysis (as specified by our protocol). This sensitivity analysis returned similar levels of imprecision. However, removing Tabak'ian 2010 from the meta‐analysis substantially reduced the I2 statistic to 50% (MD 1.28 kg, 95% CI 0.34 to 2.22; 1087 participants randomised). Due to this study's poor methodological quality and small sample size (39 participants randomised), it is possible that the effect for this study was spuriously inflated (MD 8.18 kg, 95% CI 7.57 to 8.79), and led to the high heterogeneity when pooled with the other studies.  

    We attempted subgroup analysis for early versus late initiation of UF; however, this further increased the heterogeneity. Other planned subgroups had included UF with diuretics versus UF alone; however, only one study clearly allowed both UF and diuretics throughout the intervention (Marenzi 2014), therefore analysis for this subgroup was not performed. Clear subgroups by LVEF (i.e. HFpEF versus HFrEF versus HFmrEF) and eGFR on admission could not be done due to poor and inconsistent reporting. All the populations, interventions and outcome measurements were deemed reasonably similar. Therefore, in the absence of conclusive results from the sensitivity analysis and subgroup analysis, we have maintained the pooled random‐effects estimate and accounted for the inconsistency in the GRADE assessment.  

    Visual inspection of the funnel plot showed slight asymmetry (Figure 2). While this could be due to publication bias, there is no clear direction of effect. It is more likely this asymmetry is due to poor methodological quality leading to spuriously inflated effects in smaller studies (Stein 2011; Tabak'ian 2010).

    Hospital length of stay

    Five studies (341 participants randomised) reported hospital length of stay (Chung 2010; Costanzo 2010; Marenzi 2014; Seker 2016; Tabak'ian 2010). UF may have little to no effect on hospital length of stay between UF versus UC (MD −0.24 days, 95% CI −0.78 to 0.30; Analysis 1.8). Costanzo 2015 reported a non‐significant difference in length of stay as a median without reporting a mean or SD and was therefore unable to be pooled in the meta‐analysis (6 days with UF versus 5 days with UC; P = 0.106).     Cardiac mortality

    One study (146 participants randomised) reported cardiac mortality up to 30 days from discharge (Shen 2017). UF may have little to no effect for reducing cardiac mortality (RR 0.19, 95% CI 0.01 to 3.99; Analysis 1.9). 

    Two studies (367 participants randomised) reported cardiac mortality at longest available follow‐up (Costanzo 2015; Shen 2017). UF may have little to no effect for reducing cardiac mortality at the longest available follow‐up (RR 1.18, 95% CI 0.53 to 2.60; Analysis 1.10).

    One study (200 participants randomised) reported three deaths in the UF arm and five in the UC arm due to HF (Costanzo 2010). Deaths from other cardiac causes, such as myocardial infarction, were reported only for the control arm (one in the UC arm). Because of this, we could not pool the study results under cardiac mortality for either time point.

    Change in serum creatinine

    One study (221 participants randomised) reported change in creatinine at 30 days (Costanzo 2015). UF may have little or no difference in percentage change in serum creatinine levels at 30‐day follow‐up (MD 14%, 95% CI −12% to 4%).  

    Nine studies (764 participants randomised) reported a percentage change in serum creatinine levels at longest available follow‐up (Bart 2005; Bart 2012; Chung 2010; Costanzo 2015; Giglioli 2011; Hanna 2012; Hu 2020; Marenzi 2014; Sav 2017). UF may have little or no difference in percentage change in serum creatinine levels at the longest available follow‐up (MD 2%, 95% CI −11% to 15%; Analysis 1.12). Greater than 30% difference is clinically meaningful (Schmidt 2017). 

    We conducted a sensitivity analysis for the percentage change in serum creatinine levels at longest available follow‐up to assess the impact of studies with imputed SDs (borrowed from another study in the meta‐analysis). However, the sensitivity analysis showed that the overall result and conclusions were not affected by the studies with imputed SDs.  

    New initiation of renal replacement therapy (at longest available follow‐up)

    Four studies (322 participants randomised) reported the rates of new initiation of renal replacement therapy at longest available follow‐up (Costanzo 2010; Hanna 2012; Marenzi 2014; Seker 2016). UF may increase the risk of new initiation of renal replacement therapy (RR 1.42, 95% CI 0.42 to 4.75; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.13). Event rates were low in each arm (5/156 in UF group versus 4/166 in UC group). One study reported zero events in both arms (0/15 in UF group versus 0/15 in UC group) (Giglioli 2011). 

    Major bleeding in‐hospital

    One study (200 participants randomised) reported the rates of major bleeding (as defined by the study authors) (Costanzo 2010). There was no evidence of a difference in UF versus UC for reducing the rates of major bleeding (RR 0.14, 95% CI 0.02 to 1.14; Analysis 1.14). The definition of major bleeding was left to the reporting of bleeding events by the study authors per our protocol, and due to a lack of consistency in terminology across studies we chose to assess study‐defined major bleeding with bleeding events in other studies. We noted that this limits interpretation of this outcome, but was done given that multiple other studies reported no bleeding events. Five studies reported zero events in both arms (0/20 in UF group versus 0/20 UC group, Bart 2005; 0/15 in UF group versus 0/15 in UC group, Giglioli 2011; 0/40 in UF group versus 0/60 in UC group, Hu 2020; 0/27 in UF group versus 0/29 in UC group, Marenzi 2014; 0/10 in UF group versus 0/20 in UC group, Seker 2016). 

    Complications of central line insertion

    Six studies (779 participants randomised) reported the rate of in‐hospital complications related to central venous access (Bart 2005; Bart 2012; Costanzo 2010; Costanzo 2015; Hu 2020; Seker 2016). There is an uncertain effect of UF on the risk of central line complications (RR 4.16, 95% CI 1.30 to 13.30; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.15). The absolute increase in risk of a central line‐related complication, as defined by the study reports for UF versus UC, was 2.43% (number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) 42). Two studies reported zero events in both arms (0/15 in UF group versus 0/15 in UC group, Giglioli 2011; 0/27 in UF group versus 0/29 in UC group, Marenzi 2014). Hanna 2012 alluded to central line complications but no numbers were able to allow pooling of the data.

    No studies evaluated tertiary outcomes such as healthcare utilisation or undertook a cost–benefit analysis.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    There is an uncertain effect of UF on all‐cause mortality at 30 days or less due to small sample size. At the longest available follow‐up, UF may have little to no effect on all‐cause mortality.

    UF may reduce all‐cause rehospitalisation and HF‐related rehospitalisation at 30 days or less.  At the longest available follow‐up, UF probably reduces HF‐related rehospitalisations with an absolute risk reduction of 10.9%, and a NNTB of 10. 

    There is very low‐certainty evidence for the effect of UF on weight change, and its effect is unclear. We found moderate heterogeneity when measuring weight change, and inconsistent results were observed across the thirteen different studies. UF may have little to no effect on hospital length of stay from the five studies that researched this (Chung 2010; Costanzo 2010; Marenzi 2014; Seker 2016; Tabak'ian 2010). Cardiac mortality was not well researched. Two studies assessed cardiac mortality, finding that UF may have little to no effect on this outcome (Costanzo 2015; Shen 2017). No studies were found that evaluated need for mechanical ventilation. This could reflect that such acutely unwell patients have been excluded from the research cohort, or that this patient focused outcome has not been adequately researched.

    With regard to adverse effects, UF may increase the risk of new initiation of renal replacement therapy at longest available follow‐up. UF may have little to no effect on serum creatinine change at 30 days since discharge or at the longest available follow‐up. There is an uncertain effect of UF on complications from central line complications. Major bleeding events were rare across UF and UC group. Five studies reported zero events in both arms (Bart 2005; Giglioli 2011; Hu 2020; Marenzi 2014; Seker 2016). 

    No studies were found that looked at cost–benefit analysis. This is important given the significant healthcare and resource utilisation required for UF.

    We acknowledge that follow‐up times for outcomes assessed at longest available follow‐up (all‐cause mortality, all‐cause rehospitalisation, cardiac mortality, HF‐related rehospitalisation, change in serum creatinine and new initiation of long‐term renal replacement) were heterogeneous, varying from 48 hours to one year. However, we chose to assess outcomes at both 30 days or less and longest available follow‐up. Thirty days appeared to be the most reasonable time point as we expect UF to impact short‐ and medium‐term outcomes rather than modify the HF disease course (see How the intervention might work). Other meta‐analyses on the topic have also reported outcomes at the longest available follow‐up, which we have included (Wobbe 2021). 

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    This review summarises the current evidence of UF in AHF. It evaluates the effect of UF on various clinical outcomes, with very low‐ to moderate‐certainty evidence available. We could not perform meaningful subgroup analysis to look at which patient setting or population UF may be indicated in. This was because of limited studies as well as poor and inconsistent reporting of characteristics such as proportion of chronic kidney disease or HFrEF/HFmrEF/HFpEF. Additionally, many studies were performed before HFmrEF was introduced as a formal classification in 2016 by the European Society of Cardiology guidelines (Ponikowski 2016).

    A key limitation is that the studies in this review may have underestimated the true clinical benefit of UF. UF works to improve diuretic responsiveness, and should be a complimentary therapy to UC, rather than an alternative option. In many studies, such as the CARRESS‐HF and UNLOAD trials, loop diuretics were not allowed or were discontinued during UF treatment. It is likely that when the two strategies are used in association, a better clinical outcome could be demonstrated.

    Additionally, many studies focused on endpoints such as all‐cause mortality and readmission. This may not reflect the true effect of UF, since we do not expect UF to alter the disease course. Such outcomes may focus on unrelated events since people with AHF can be admitted for a variety of reasons.  HF‐related rehospitalisation and mortality are better endpoints for evaluating the impact of UF, however this was poorly reported with only two (HF‐related rehospitalisation) and four (mortality) studies. 

    With regards to adverse outcomes, many studies focused on serum creatinine change in hospital. Our understanding of AHF has evolved over time, and it is now recognised that transient rise in serum creatinine during AHF decongestion therapy is not necessarily indicative of acute kidney injury, but can represent a haemodynamic change in glomerular filtration rate (Ahmad 2018). Only four studies reported the rate at which patients required long‐term renal replacement therapy. This patient‐centred outcome would be a useful endpoint to assess in future studies more robustly.

    The results from this review can be applied to people with AHF who have symptoms of congestion, without haemodynamic instability or significant comorbidities. People with severe renal impairment, acute coronary syndrome, systemic infection or marked hypotension were often excluded from the research cohort. Results from this review cannot be applied to this patient subgroup, which have the highest mortality risk from AHF.

    All studies were performed in middle‐ and upper‐income countries, so caution should be applied when generalising results to low‐income countries.

    Quality of the evidence

    Overall, there was very low‐ to moderate‐certainty evidence from the studies. Our confidence in the estimates was reduced due to differences in the study settings, methodological limitations of the studies, significant variability in the study results and imprecise results with wide CLs that included no effect. 

    High risk of bias in the various study methodology impacts how confident we are drawing conclusions from the evidence found. The major methodological limitations came from deviations from intended protocol from crossovers, which mostly favoured UC over UF across all outcomes (see Risk of bias in included studies  for further detail). Bias is also introduced when evaluating some safety endpoints of complications of central line insertion or need for long‐term dialysis. Loss to follow‐up at longer time points is particularly important when evaluating need for long‐term dialysis, and the inherent unblinded nature of this intervention can bring in selection or measurement bias when assessing central line complications. 

    The study limitations as well as the marked heterogeneity between the studies contributed to the conflicting results found in the effects of UF. There were differences in selection of the study population and exclusion criteria, heterogeneity of the indications for the use of UF as well as the UF protocols implemented, and high variability in the conventional therapy used for the control group. This needs to be considered when interpreting the effects of intervention, and therefore we have downgraded our certainty of evidence. 

    Potential biases in the review process

    While our search strategy was comprehensive, there is a chance of publication bias affecting our review. A secondary outcome of weight change was the only outcome with more than 10 studies pooled, and was assessed for publication bias with a funnel plot. This showed slight asymmetry, which could be due to multiple reasons. This includes a non‐reporting bias as well as likelihood of small trials with poor methodological rigour tending to show a larger intervention effect (Egger 1997). 

    Within the eligible studies, there are nine awaiting classification, pending further information from authors. Seven of these studies appeared to meet criteria based on their trial records without any reported results. It is possible they have unpublished data that have not been included. At the data collection stage, no unpublished or individual‐level data could be obtained despite attempts to contact the authors. These unpublished data have the potential to introduce publication bias.  Future updates to this review will benefit greatly if such unpublished data are able to be obtained.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    UF may have little effect on all‐cause mortality in the long‐term, which is consistent with other reviews (Jain 2016; Shi 2019; Siddiqui 2017; Wobbe 2021). Most reviews noted a reduction in all‐cause rehospitalisation, and particularly HF‐related rehospitalisation with UF (Jain 2016; Kwok 2017; Shi 2019; Siddiqui 2017; Wobbe 2021). 

    There were conflicting results for weight loss in previous reviews. Some reported no or minimal difference (Jain 2016; Kwok 2017; Shi 2019) compared to others finding significant weight loss with UF (Chen 2015; Jain 2016; Siddiqui 2017; Wobbe 2021). These contradictory results were seen in our review, which is why we downgraded our evidence for the effect of UF on weight change to very low certainty. 

    At the longest available follow‐up, we found UF may have little to no impact on serum creatinine change. This is consistent with other reviews that found no significant differences in renal outcomes (Chen 2015; Jain 2016; Kwok 2017; Shi 2019; Siddiqui 2017; Wobbe 2021). However, our review found the effect of UF on new initiation of dialysis maybe increased at long‐term follow‐up.
