Laryngeal mask airway surfactant administration for prevention of morbidity and mortality in preterm infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome

    • Background

    Laryngeal mask airway surfactant administration (S‐LMA) has the potential benefit of surfactant administration whilst avoiding endotracheal intubation and ventilation, ventilator‐induced lung injury and bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD).

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of S‐LMA either as prophylaxis or treatment (rescue) compared to placebo, no treatment, or intratracheal surfactant administration via an endotracheal tube (ETT) with the intent to rapidly extubate (InSurE) or extubate at standard criteria (S‐ETT) or via other less‐invasive surfactant administration (LISA) methods on morbidity and mortality in preterm infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome (RDS).

    Search methods

    We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, and three trial registries in December 2022.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs), cluster‐ or quasi‐RCTs of S‐LMA compared to placebo, no treatment, or other routes of administration (nebulised, pharyngeal instillation of surfactant before the first breath, thin endotracheal catheter surfactant administration or intratracheal surfactant instillation) on morbidity and mortality in preterm infants at risk of RDS. We considered published, unpublished and ongoing trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently assessed studies for inclusion and extracted data. We used GRADE to assess the certainty of the evidence.

    Main results

    We included eight trials (seven new to this update) recruiting 510 newborns. Five trials (333 infants) compared S‐LMA with surfactant administration via ETT with InSurE. One trial (48 infants) compared S‐LMA with surfactant administration via ETT with S‐ETT, and two trials (129 infants) compared S‐LMA with no surfactant administration. We found no studies comparing S‐LMA with LISA techniques or prophylactic or early S‐LMA.

    S‐LMA versus surfactant administration via InSurE

    S‐LMA may have little or no effect on the composite outcome of death or BPD at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age (risk ratio (RR) 1.50, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.27 to 8.34, I 2 = not applicable (NA) as 1 study had 0 events; risk difference (RD) 0.02, 95% CI −0.07 to 0.10; I 2 = 0%; 2 studies, 110 infants; low‐certainty evidence). There may be a reduction in the need for mechanical ventilation at any time (RR 0.53, 95% CI 0.36 to 0.78; I 2 = 27%; RD −0.14, 95% CI −0.22 to −0.06, I 2 = 89%; number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) 7, 95% CI 5 to 17; 5 studies, 333 infants; low‐certainty evidence). However, this was limited to four studies (236 infants) using analgesia or sedation for the InSurE group. There was little or no difference for air leak during first hospitalisation (RR 1.39, 95% CI 0.65 to 2.98; I 2 = 0%; 5 studies, 333 infants (based on 3 studies as 2 studies had 0 events); low‐certainty evidence); BPD among survivors to 36 weeks' PMA (RR 1.28, 95% CI 0.47 to 3.52; I 2 = 0%; 4 studies, 264 infants (based on 3 studies as 1 study had 0 events); low‐certainty evidence); or death (all causes) during the first hospitalisation (RR 0.28, 95% CI 0.01 to 6.60; I 2 = NA as 2 studies had 0 events; 3 studies, 203 infants; low‐certainty evidence). Neurosensory disability was not reported. Intraventricular haemorrhage ( IVH) grades III and IV were reported among the study groups (1 study, 50 infants).

    S‐LMA versus surfactant administration via S‐ETT

    No study reported death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA. S‐LMA may reduce the use of mechanical ventilation at any time compared with S‐ETT (RR 0.47, 95% CI 0.31 to 0.71; RD −0.54, 95% CI −0.74 to −0.34; NNTB 2, 95% CI 2 to 3; 1 study, 48 infants; low‐certainty evidence). We are very uncertain whether S‐LMA compared with S‐ETT reduces air leak during first hospitalisation (RR 2.56, 95% CI 0.11 to 59.75), IVH grade III or IV (RR 2.56, 95% CI 0.11 to 59.75) and death (all causes) during the first hospitalisation (RR 0.17, 95% CI 0.01 to 3.37) (1 study, 48 infants; very low‐certainty evidence). No study reported BPD to 36 weeks' PMA or neurosensory disability.

    S‐LMA versus no surfactant administration

    Rescue surfactant could be used in both groups. There may be little or no difference in death or BPD at 36 weeks (RR 1.65, 95% CI 0.85 to 3.22; I 2 = 58%; RD 0.08, 95% CI −0.03 to 0.19; I 2 = 0%; 2 studies, 129 infants; low‐certainty evidence). There was probably a reduction in the need for mechanical ventilation at any time with S‐LMA compared with nasal continuous positive airway pressure without surfactant (RR 0.57, 95% CI 0.38 to 0.85; I 2 = 0%; RD −0.24, 95% CI −0.40 to −0.08; I 2 = 0%; NNTB 4, 95% CI 3 to 13; 2 studies, 129 infants; moderate‐certainty evidence). There was little or no difference in air leak during first hospitalisation (RR 0.65, 95% CI 0.23 to 1.88; I 2 = 0%; 2 studies, 129 infants; low‐certainty evidence) or BPD to 36 weeks' PMA (RR 1.65, 95% CI 0.85 to 3.22; I 2 = 58%; 2 studies, 129 infants; low‐certainty evidence). There were no events in either group for death during the first hospitalisation (1 study, 103 infants) or IVH grade III and IV (1 study, 103 infants). No study reported neurosensory disability.

    Authors' conclusions

    In preterm infants less than 36 weeks' PMA, rescue S‐LMA may have little or no effect on the composite outcome of death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA. However, it may reduce the need for mechanical ventilation at any time. This benefit is limited to trials reporting the use of analgesia or sedation in the InSurE and S‐ETT groups. There is low‐ to very‐low certainty evidence for no or little difference in neonatal morbidities and mortality. Long‐term outcomes are largely unreported.

    In preterm infants less than 32 weeks' PMA or less than 1500 g, there are insufficient data to support or refute the use of S‐LMA in clinical practice. Adequately powered trials are required to determine the effect of S‐LMA for prevention or early treatment of RDS in extremely preterm infants. S‐LMA use should be limited to clinical trials in this group of infants.

    • Laryngeal mask airway surfactant administration for prevention of morbidity and mortality in preterm infants with, or at risk of, respiratory distress syndrome

    Key messages

    – Surfactant administration via laryngeal mask may reduce the need for mechanical ventilation at any time when compared to surfactant administration via endotracheal tube.

    – Surfactant administration via laryngeal mask probably reduces the need for mechanical ventilation at any time compared to no surfactant administration.

    – Further trials are needed.

    What is a surfactant?

    Pulmonary surfactant is a natural substance that prevents the air sacs of the lungs (alveoli) from sticking together (collapsing) when the baby exhales (breathes out). Surfactant is often lacking in the lungs of newborn babies with respiratory distress syndrome.

    What is respiratory distress syndrome?

    Respiratory distress syndrome is a breathing disorder that occurs mainly in infants born before their due date (37 weeks' gestation). It is caused by a lack of surfactant.

    How is respiratory distress treated?

    The usual treatment involves providing breathing support and oxygen, and administering artificial surfactant directly into the newborn infant's lungs through the breathing pipe (trachea). This can be via a tube in the trachea (called an endotracheal tube) for infants on mechanical ventilation or via a thin tube or nebuliser (a machine that turns liquid medicine into a fine mist) for infants breathing for themselves whilst on pressure support to open the lungs (called continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP)). However, placing tubes in the infant's trachea is technically challenging and mechanical ventilation may damage the lungs. An alternative is to insert a tube called laryngeal mask airway into the infant's upper airway, which fits on the infant's larynx (the tube just above the trachea) and allows breathing support and administration of surfactant. This technique is thought to be less technically challenging.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out if surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway compared to dummy treatment (placebo), no treatment, or surfactant administration via an endotracheal tube or other less‐invasive methods given to premature infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome could improve survival. We also wanted to know if it reduced the risk of developing a chronic lung condition called bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) or duration of mechanical ventilation and other outcomes, such as any harmful effects.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that compared surfactant administration via laryngeal mask to no treatment, surfactant administration via an endotracheal tube or other less‐invasive methods in preterm infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome. We compared and summarised the results of the included studies and rated our confidence in the evidence based on factors such as study methods and size.

    What did we find?

    We identified eight studies that investigated this therapy in 510 premature infants. Surfactant administration via laryngeal mask may reduce the need for mechanical ventilation at any time when compared to surfactant administration via endotracheal tube. Furthermore, we found that surfactant administration via laryngeal mask probably reduces the need for mechanical ventilation at any time compared to no surfactant administration. Long‐term outcomes were not reported. No trial compared surfactant administered by laryngeal mask airway to surfactant administered by a thin catheter. Given the encouraging results from these trials, high‐quality trials of laryngeal mask airway surfactant administration in very preterm infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome are justified.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    We are not confident in the evidence because it was based on relatively few cases and few studies.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up to date to 1 December 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    In preterm infants of less than 36 weeks' gestation, rescue surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway (S‐LMA) may have little or no effect on the composite outcome of death or bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) at 36 weeks. However, it may reduce the need for mechanical ventilation at any time. This benefit is limited to trials reporting the use of analgesia or sedation in the INtubation‐SURfactant‐Extubation (InSurE) and surfactant administration via endotracheal (S‐ETT) groups. There is low‐ to very‐low certainty evidence for no or little difference in neonatal morbidities and mortality. Long‐term outcomes are largely unreported.

    In preterm infants at less than 32 weeks' gestation or less than 1500 g, there are insufficient data to support or refute the use of S‐LMA in clinical practice. Adequately powered trials are required to determine the effect of S‐LMA for prevention or early treatment of respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) in extremely preterm infants. S‐LMA use should be limited to clinical trials in this group of infants.

    Implications for research

    Adequately powered trials are required to determine the effect of S‐LMA for preventing or treating RDS in preterm and extremely preterm infants, particularly compared to other non‐invasive techniques, including thin catheter surfactant administration whilst on nasal intermittent positive pressure ventilation. Studies should measure short‐ and long‐term outcomes prespecified in this review. Given the evidence for prophylactic and early surfactant administration, trials should enrol infants early in the course of respiratory illness.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Respiratory distress syndrome (RDS) results from pulmonary surfactant deficiency and is an important cause of morbidity and mortality in preterm infants. Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and meta‐analyses have demonstrated the efficacy of surfactant therapy in both prevention and treatment of RDS. A wide variety of surfactant preparations have been studied. These include synthetic surfactants ( Soll 2000 ; Soll 2010 ), and surfactants derived from animal sources (natural surfactants) ( Seger 2009 ; Soll 2002 ). Although both synthetic and animal‐derived surfactant preparations are effective ( Seger 2009 ; Soll 2000 ; Soll 2002 ; Soll 2010 ), clinical trials suggest that animal‐derived surfactants may be more effective ( Ardell 2015 ), than protein‐free synthetic surfactants ( Tooley 1987 ). Furthermore, clinical trials have shown that prophylactic surfactants ( Soll 2000 ; Soll 2002 ; Soll 2010 ), and early surfactants ( Stevens 2007 ), are superior to the selective use of surfactants in preventing morbidity and mortality in preterm infants. They also suggest that a multiple‐dose strategy is superior to a single‐dose strategy ( Soll 2009 ). Protein‐containing synthetic surfactants have also been successfully tested ( Pfister 2007 ), although these preparations are not currently available for clinical use.

    However, despite the benefits of surfactants, many infants develop bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) and chronic lung disease (CLD). Although the aetiology of CLD in preterm infants is multifactorial ( Allen 2003 ), ventilator‐induced lung injury (VILI) remains one of the main implicated risk factors ( Clark 2000 ; Coalson 1999 ). Only a few resuscitative positive pressure ventilation (PPV) breaths may result in VILI ( Björklund 1997 ; Fleckonoe 2008 ; Grossmann 1986 ; O'Reilly 2008 ).

    Both surfactant prophylaxis and therapy require endotracheal intubation to facilitate surfactant administration. Although surfactant is an established effective intervention for either prevention or treatment of RDS ( Seger 2009 ; Soll 2002 ; Soll 2009 ; Soll 2010 ; Stevens 2007 ), endotracheal intubation and PPV, which follow, have adverse effects.

    Endotracheal intubation is a potentially traumatic procedure often performed without optimal pain management ( Sarkar 2006 ). It may be accompanied by significant haemodynamic instability, including hypoxia, bradycardia, blood pressure fluctuations and increased intracranial pressure ( Ghanta 2007 ; Marshall 1984 ). Intubation is inevitably associated with colonisation of the trachea, retained secretions resulting in collapse, differential aeration and high airflow resistance resulting in increased work of breathing, potentially leading to nosocomial pneumonia and sepsis ( Aly 2008 ; Young 2005 ). Intubation is associated with an inflammatory process which can lead to lung injury and BPD ( Young 2005 ). Current evidence suggests that PPV of immature, surfactant‐deficient lungs is harmful and may increase the likelihood of the development of BPD ( Björklund 1997 ; van Marter 2000 ). Björklund demonstrated that resuscitation of surfactant‐deficient immature lambs with as few as six breaths damages the lungs and blunts the therapeutic effect of subsequent surfactant replacement ( Björklund 1997 ). Grossmann 1986 showed similar results. Fleckonoe 2008 demonstrated that just six hours of ventilation is enough to cause marked airway epithelial injury in very preterm and near‐term foetal sheep. O'Reilly 2008 reported that ventilator‐induced injury extends to involve the conducting airways.

    One approach to surfactant administration is the InSurE (INtubation‐SURfactant‐Extubation) technique pioneered by Victorin ( Victorin 1990 ), and Verder ( Verder 1994 ). Evidence showed that early surfactant replacement therapy with prompt extubation to nasal continuous positive airway pressure (nCPAP) is associated with less need for mechanical ventilation, lower incidence of BPD and fewer air leak syndromes when compared with later selective surfactant replacement and continued mechanical ventilation with extubation from low ventilator support ( Stevens 2007 ). However, the limited data also showed the InSurE procedure to be associated with a trend for decreased cerebral oxygenation, higher cerebral oxygen extraction and decreased electrical brain activity ( Hellstrom‐Westas 1992 ; van de Berg 2010 ). The InSurE procedure may need to be repeated if the first dose of surfactant was not sufficiently effective ( Bohlin 2007 ), leading to an additional risk of brain damage.

    The main strategy used to avoid endotracheal intubation and PPV in premature infants is the application of nCPAP, or continuous distending pressure (CDP), immediately following birth ( Ho 2020a ; Ho 2020b ; Kamper 1999 ). Studies have suggested that CDP may lead to less CLD when compared to elective intubation, surfactant and PPV ( Aly 2001 ; De Klerk 2001 ). Similarly, nasal intermittent positive pressure ventilation (nIPPV) has shown a decreased need for intubation and mechanical ventilation. It also reduces the frequency of apnoea and the incidence of CLD without an increase in adverse effects ( Lemyre 2002 ; Lemyre 2017 ). Although CDP and nIPPV strategies avoid endotracheal intubation and PPV, they preclude surfactant administration, a standard and proven treatment for RDS. Furthermore, CDP, nIPPV and InSurE may fail in 25% to 50% of preterm infants ( Finer 2010 ; Kugelman 2007 ; Morley 2008 ; Reininger 2005 ), resulting in late surfactant administration, a strategy proven to be inferior ( Soll 2010 ; Stevens 2007 ).

    Description of the intervention

    Non‐invasive or minimally invasive methods of surfactant administration may potentially reduce the need for intubation and endotracheal surfactant administration. Possible strategies include:

    1. intra‐amniotic instillation ( Abdel‐Latif 2010 ; Petrikovsky 1995 );

    2. pharyngeal instillation ( Abdel‐Latif 2011a ; Kattwinkel 2004 );

    3. administration via a thin endotracheal catheter ( Abdel‐Latif 2021 ; Kribs 2007 );

    4. nebulised surfactant administration in spontaneously breathing infants ( Abdel‐Latif 2012 ; Jorch 1997 );

    5. administration via laryngeal mask airway (LMA) ( Abdel‐Latif 2011b ; Trevisanuto 2005 ).

    One Cochrane review found that LMA can achieve adequate ventilation during neonatal resuscitation in a time frame consistent with current guidelines ( Qureshi 2018 ), and one small RCT found no clinically significant difference between the LMA and endotracheal tube (ETT) when bag and mask ventilation was unsuccessful.

    This review focused on LMA surfactant administration (S‐LMA). An example of a protocol for S‐LMA involves positioning the LMA, followed by instilling the surfactant in two to four aliquots via the LMA ( Trevisanuto 2005 ). Each aliquot follows a brief PPV until the surfactant disappears from the LMA. Once the surfactant aliquots are complete, the LMA is removed, and the baby is placed on CDP and managed by standard protocol. S‐LMA is also known as surfactant administration through laryngeal or supraglottic airways (SALSA) ( Zapata 2022 ), and Catheter and Laryngeal Mask Endotracheal Surfactant Therapy (CALMEST) ( Vannozzi 2017 ).

    The LMA is a supraglottic device consisting of a curved plastic tube with an elliptical inflatable mask inserted blindly into the patient's posterior pharynx. The mask may be inflated in the hypopharynx creating an airtight seal around the upper oesophagus. It offers the possibility of rapid establishment of adequate ventilation and access to the airway without tracheal intubation, even when performed by relatively inexperienced personnel. Different types of LMA are available (i‐Gel, ProSeal, PA x press, CobraPLA, classic, Supreme, Ultimate, PRO‐Breathe, Unique, air‐Qsp and AuroOnce). Possible adverse effects of S‐LMA include hypoxia and bradycardia during administration, laryngospasm and malposition of the LMA, with a potential impact on the newborn.

    The practice of awake LMA insertion without premedication use remains heterogeneous. One evidence‐based statement from the French Society of Neonatology (SFN) recommended atropine use before LMA insertion, except in an immediate life‐threatening situation ( Durrmeyer 2023 ). There were no specific data regarding the use and types of premedication ( Durrmeyer 2023 ).

    How the intervention might work

    S‐LMA is designed to avoid endotracheal intubation yet potentially offers the benefits of early surfactant administration. Combining this strategy with antenatal steroid administration and CDP may offer synergy to treat RDS, avoiding both endotracheal intubation and PPV associated with lung injury that may lead to BPD. In one small case series, delivering surfactant via LMA was feasible, with no complications reported ( Brimacombe 2004 ; Trevisanuto 2005 ).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Despite substantial advances in neonatal intensive care, CLD results in a significant health burden to infants born at less than 32 weeks' gestation who receive mechanical ventilation. CLD results in substantial neonatal and infant morbidities and health resource utilisation ( Allen 2003 ). CLD is associated with chronic respiratory difficulties ( Doyle 2006 ; Kilbride 2003 ); prolonged and recurrent hospitalisation ( Chye 1995 ); neurodevelopmental disability including cerebral palsy, neurosensory and motor disability ( Hughes 1999 ; Majnemer 2000 ; Skidmore 1990 ); and poor cognitive outcome ( Hughes 1999 ). CLD significantly impacts the daily life of families that persists beyond the neonatal period ( Korhonen 1999 ). S‐LMA has the potential benefit of avoiding VILI and BPD.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of S‐LMA either as prophylaxis or treatment (rescue) compared to placebo, no treatment, or intratracheal surfactant administration via an endotracheal tube (ETT) with the intent to rapidly extubate (InSurE) or extubate at standard criteria (S‐ETT) or via other less‐invasive surfactant administration (LISA) methods on morbidity and mortality in preterm infants with or at risk of respiratory distress syndrome (RDS).

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We considered RCTs using randomisation or quasi‐randomisation of participants regardless of the allocation unit (individual or cluster) eligible for inclusion in this review. Published or unpublished studies were eligible for inclusion.

    Types of participants

    We included preterm infants (less than 37 weeks' gestation) with, or at risk for, RDS of any severity and any postnatal age.

    Types of interventions

    Intervention

    Surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway (S‐LMA) at any dose, using any surfactant (synthetic, animal‐derived or protein‐containing) and any type of LMA (Classic; ProSeal; i‐Gel; PA X press; CobraPLA) compared with placebo, no treatment, or intratracheally instilled surfactant via ETT or a minimally invasive method such as thin catheter surfactant.

    Where possible, we planned to perform separate comparisons for the following.

    1. Prophylactic surfactant defined as a treatment strategy in which the intent was to treat a preterm infant based on the risk of RDS (e.g. less than 27 weeks' gestational age (GA) or as defined by the study authors) within the first hour of life.

    2. Rescue surfactant defined as the treatment of an established disease (preterm infant less than 37 weeks' GA requiring respiratory support and having signs and symptoms of RDS).

    Comparisons

    We categorised trials by intervention in the comparator (control) group as below.

    1. S‐LMA versus surfactant administration via invasive methods.

      1. S‐LMA versus InSurE: for the controls, there was the intent to extubate soon after surfactant delivery ( Haberman 2002 ), that is, the InSurE procedure ( Reininger 2005 ; Victorin 1990 ).

      2. S‐LMA versus S‐ETT: control infants remained intubated after surfactant delivery, with delayed extubation after a period of mechanical ventilation and after meeting standard extubation criteria.

    2. S‐LMA versus LISA: infants in the control group received surfactant through less‐invasive methods such as thin catheter and continued on non‐invasive respiratory support (nCPAP, nIPPV, high flow (HF), variations thereof).

    3. S‐LMA versus no surfactant administration: infants in the control group continued non‐invasive respiratory support (nCPAP, nIPPV, HF, variations thereof) without surfactant administration.

    Types of outcome measures

    We have underlined below the critical outcomes that we have graded and presented in the summary of findings tables. We included many outcomes to account for extremely preterm and borderline preterm infants.

    Primary outcomes

    1. Death or bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD) (composite outcome), defined as the need for oxygen or respiratory support at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age (PMA) ( Shennan 1988 ).

    Secondary outcomes
    Measures of the safety of the surfactant administration procedure

    1. Failure to place laryngeal mask airway (LMA) or endotracheal tube (ETT) at the first attempt.

    2. Bradycardia during the intervention (heart rate less than 100 beats per minute (bpm) or as defined by study authors).

    3. Hypoxaemia during the intervention (oxygen saturation less than 80% or as defined by study authors).

    4. Apnoea (cessation of spontaneous breathing greater than 20 seconds) during the intervention.

    5. Surfactant reflux during the intervention.

    6. Postintervention gastric surfactant aspirated greater than 10% of surfactant dose.

    7. Postintervention intratracheal surfactant received.

    8. Number of doses of intratracheal surfactant received postintervention.

    For studies comparing laryngeal mask airway or other non‐invasive methods

    1. Need for positive pressure ventilation (PVV) during the intervention.

    2. Need for immediate intubation (within 15 minutes of intervention).

    Measures of respiratory support

    1. Mechanical ventilation within the first 72 hours of life.

    2. Mechanical ventilation within the first 72 hours or not ventilated but reached failure criteria.

    3. Mechanical ventilation at any time .

    4. Pulmonary air leak, any (during the first hospitalisation) .

    5. Pulmonary air leak requiring drainage (during the first hospitalisation).

    6. Duration of mechanical ventilation via an ETT (days; in survivors).

    7. Duration of any respiratory support (mechanical ventilation, nasal continuous positive airway pressure (nCPAP), high flow (HF)) (days; in survivors).

    8. Duration of oxygen therapy (days; in survivors).

    9. Postnatal systemic corticosteroid therapy for BPD prevention.

    Outcomes during hospitalisation

    1. BPD (physiological definition), evaluated where necessary by a room‐air challenge at 36 weeks' PMA for infants with borderline oxygen requirements ( Walsh 2004 ).

    2. BPD (clinical definition) among survivors to 36 weeks' PMA .

    3. Intraventricular haemorrhage (IVH), any grade ( Papile 1978 ).

    4. IVH, grade III and IV ( Papile 1978 ).

    5. Cystic periventricular leukomalacia (PVL).

    6. Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) requiring medical therapy.

    7. Necrotising enterocolitis (NEC), modified Bell stage 2 or greater ( Bell 1978 ; Walsh 1988 ).

    8. Spontaneous intestinal perforation.

    9. Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP), any grade.

    10. ROP, stage 3 or greater.

    11. Duration of hospitalisation (days).

    12. Neonatal death (all causes) during the first 28 days.

    13. Death (all causes) during the first hospitalisation .

    Postdischarge outcomes

    1. Oxygen therapy at home.

    2. Number of hospital readmissions with respiratory illness in the first two years.

    3. Parent‐reported wheeze in the first two years.

    4. Bronchodilator use in the first two years.

    5. Neurosensory disability in survivors; measured beyond one‐year PMA and defined as any of

      1. cerebral palsy by clinical examination or other means;

      2. developmental delay more than two standard deviations (SD) below the population mean on standardised testing;

      3. blindness (visual acuity less than 6/60);

      4. deafness (hearing impairment requiring amplification).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MEA and DAO) independently assessed the risk of bias (low, high or unclear) of all included trials using the Cochrane RoB 1 tool ( Higgins 2017 ), for the following domains.

    1. Sequence generation (selection bias)

    2. Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias)

    4. Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    5. Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    6. Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    7. Any other bias

    We resolved any disagreements by discussion. See Appendix 7 for a more detailed description of the risk of bias for each domain.

    Measures of treatment effect

    Dichotomous data

    For dichotomous data, we presented the results using risk ratios (RR) and risk differences (RD) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We calculated the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or, where relevant, the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) with 95% CIs if there was a significant reduction or increase in RD.

    Continuous data

    For continuous data, we used the mean difference (MD) when trials measured outcomes in the same way and standardised mean difference (SMD) when trials that measured the same outcome used different scales. Where trials reported continuous data as the median and interquartile range (IQR) and data passed the test of skewness, we converted the median to mean and estimated the SD as IQR/1.35.

    Unit of analysis issues

    We performed the primary analysis per individual randomised.

    We planned to abstract information on the study design and unit of analysis for each study, indicating whether the clustering of observations was present due to allocation to the intervention at the group level or clustering of individually randomised observations (e.g. people within the intensive care units). Available statistical information needed to account for the implications of clustering on the estimation of outcome variances was planned to be abstracted, such as design effects or intracluster correlations and whether the study adjusted results for the correlations in the data. In cases where the study did not account for clustering, we planned to ensure that appropriate adjustments were made to the effective sample size following Cochrane guidance ( Higgins 2022 ). Where possible, we planned to derive the intracluster correlation coefficient (ICC) for these adjustments from the trial itself or a similar trial. If an appropriate ICC was unavailable, we planned to conduct a sensitivity analysis to investigate the potential effect of clustering by imputing a range of values of ICC.

    If any trials had multiple arms that were compared against the same control condition to be included in the same meta‐analysis, we planned to combine groups to create a single pair‐wise comparison, select one pair of interventions and exclude the others. In the meta‐analysis and data synthesis, we planned only to include the first phase data from cross‐over trials.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted the original investigators when we identified important missing data (in the outcomes) or unclear data. We planned to describe the number of participants with missing data in the Characteristics of included studies table and the Results section. We planned to make explicit the assumptions of any methods used to deal with missing data. We planned to perform sensitivity analyses to assess how sensitive results were to reasonable changes in the undertaken assumptions. We planned to address the potential impact of missing data on the review's findings in the Discussion section.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We used Review Manager Web to assess the heterogeneity of treatment effects between trials ( RevMan Web 2022 ). We used two formal statistical approaches.

    1. We assessed the presence of statistical heterogeneity using the Chi 2 test for homogeneity (P < 0.1). Because this test has low power when the number of studies included in the meta‐analysis is small, we set the significance level at 10% probability ( Higgins 2022 ).

    2. The I 2 statistic describes the percentage of total variation across studies due to heterogeneity rather than sampling error and is thus a measure of the validity of data pooling for meta‐analysis. We graded the degree of heterogeneity as follows: 24% or less, no heterogeneity; 25% to 49%, low heterogeneity; 50% to 74%, moderate heterogeneity; and 75% or greater, high heterogeneity.

    When we noted evidence of apparent or statistical heterogeneity, we assessed the source of the heterogeneity using sensitivity and subgroup analyses to look for evidence of bias or methodological differences between trials.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We attempted to obtain the study protocols of all included studies and compare outcomes reported in the protocol versus those reported in the findings for each of the included studies. If we suspected reporting bias (see Characteristics of included studies table), we planned to contact the study authors to request further information. When this was not possible, and the missing data were thought to introduce serious bias, we planned to examine the impact of including/excluding such studies in the overall assessment of results by performing a sensitivity analysis.

    We planned to investigate non‐reporting (including publication) bias by assessing the funnel plot asymmetry visually and with Egger's test in meta‐analyses if data were available from at least 10 trials contributing events ( Egger 1997 ).

    Data synthesis

    We performed meta‐analyses using the standard methods of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions ( Higgins 2022 ). We carried out the analysis on an intention‐to‐treat (ITT) basis for all included outcomes. We analysed all participants in the treatment group to which they were randomised, regardless of the actual treatment received. For categorical outcomes, we calculated typical estimates of RR and RD, each with 95% CI; for continuous outcomes, we calculated the MD if outcomes were measured in the same way between trials and SMD to combine trials that measured the same outcome, but used different scales. We used a fixed‐effect model to combine data where it was reasonable to assume that studies were estimating the same underlying treatment effect. We analysed and interpreted individual trials separately when we judged meta‐analysis as inappropriate. We explained clinical heterogeneity based on the different study characteristics and subgroup analyses where appropriate.

    Given the fundamental difference in the therapeutic approaches for control infants (InSurE, S‐ETT, LISA and no surfactant administration), we analysed data from trials within these categories separately and did not pool data in a meta‐analysis.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We explored potential sources of clinical heterogeneity through the following subgroup analyses.

    1. Use of sedation and analgesia premedication (i.e. sedation and analgesia used versus none).

    2. GA (28 weeks or less (extremely preterm), 29 to 32 weeks (very preterm), 33 to 36 weeks (preterm)).

    3. Timing of surfactant administration (i.e. prophylaxis versus rescue). Here we defined prophylaxis trials as those in which surfactant treatment was administered soon after birth to infants at risk of RDS and rescue trials as those that used treatment with surfactant selectively in infants demonstrating features of RDS.

    Of these, we could not do a subgroup analysis concerning the timing of surfactant administration (i.e. prophylaxis versus rescue) as all included studies used rescue surfactant.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We explored methodological heterogeneity using sensitivity analyses. We performed sensitivity analyses by excluding trials of lower quality based on a lack of any of the following: adequate randomisation, allocation concealment and less than 10% loss to follow‐up. As the intervention is unlikely to be adequately blinded, we did not include blinding as a criterion in the sensitivity analyses.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook ( Schünemann 2013 ), to assess the certainty of the evidence for the following critical (clinically relevant) outcomes.

    1. Death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA

    2. Mechanical ventilation at any time

    3. Pulmonary air leak, any (during the first hospitalisation)

    4. BPD (clinical definition) among survivors to 36 weeks' PMA

    5. IVH grade III and IV

    6. Death (all causes) during the first hospitalisation

    7. Neurosensory disability in survivors; measured beyond one‐year PMA

    Two review authors (MEA and DAO) independently assessed the certainty of the evidence for each of the outcomes above. We considered evidence from RCTs as high certainty but downgraded the evidence one level for serious (or by two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of the evidence, precision of estimates and presence of publication bias.

    We used the GRADEpro GDT tool to create three summaries of findings tables to report the certainty of the evidence for the following comparisons.

    1. S‐LMA versus InSurE ( summary of findings Table 1 )

    2. S‐LMA versus S‐ETT ( summary of findings Table 2 )

    3. S‐LMA versus no surfactant administration ( summary of findings Table 3 )

    The GRADE approach results in an assessment of the certainty of a body of evidence as belonging to one of four grades.

    1. High certainty: further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the estimate of effect.

    2. Moderate certainty: further research is likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and may change the estimate.

    3. Low certainty: further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and is likely to change the estimate.

    4. Very low certainty: we are very uncertain about the estimate.

The description of all included studies is displayed in the Characteristics of included studies table and is summarised in Table 1 . Three studies were halted early before achieving their planned sample size ( Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Pinheiro 2016 ). Attridge 2013 was halted because of slow enrolment, which was thought to be due to clinicians' hesitation at the study centre to intubate and administer surfactant by the endotracheal route. Barbosa 2017 was stopped early due to an interim analysis showing both groups were equivalent. Pinheiro 2016 was stopped after a preliminary analysis needed towards the end of a co‐investigator fellowship training revealed essential differences between groups.

    The sample size of the studies ranged from 26 ( Attridge 2013 ), to 103 infants ( Roberts 2018 ). Two studies were multicentre ( Roberts 2018 ; Sadeghnia 2014 ), and six were single‐centre ( Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ).

    No trial enrolled infants at less than 27 weeks' gestation or less than 800 g bodyweight. The smallest infant allocated LMA surfactant in the trials weighed 1025 g ( Barbosa 2017 ). The enrolment criteria for weight in the included trials was greater than 800 g ( Gallup 2022 ), 1000 g or greater ( Barbosa 2017 ; Pinheiro 2016 ), 1200 g or greater ( Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ), 1250 g or greater ( Roberts 2018 ), 1800 g or greater ( Gharehbaghi 2018 ), and 2000 g or greater ( Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    All studies were conducted in high‐income (USA) and upper‐middle‐income (Iran and Brazil) countries.

    Given the fundamental difference in the therapeutic approaches for control infants above (InSurE, S‐ETT, LISA and no surfactant administration), we analysed data from trials within these categories separately and did not pool data in a meta‐analysis.

    Outcomes

    The included studies reported a wide range of outcomes, and the prevalence of these depended on the type of population studied. Since most of the included studies' population was preterm or borderline preterm infants, outcomes such as severe IVH and CLD were rare as these are more common among extremely preterm infants. No trial enrolled infants less than 27 weeks' gestation or less than 800 g. The mean GA and birth weight of each trial are summarised in Table 1 .

    Primary outcomes

    1. Four studies reported the composite outcome of death or BPD, defined as the need for oxygen or respiratory support at 36 weeks' PMA ( Attridge 2013 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ).

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Reporting of most secondary outcomes was incomplete.

    2. Continuous outcomes, including duration of respiratory support, oxygen therapy and hospitalisation, were incomplete and variably reported as means and SD, and non‐parametric data (medians and IQR or range). We converted non‐parametric data to parametric data where available for inclusion in the meta‐analysis.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 12 studies, including seven observational studies, two reviews (all ineligible study types), one RCT comparing LISA with nCPAP versus LISA with LMA‐assisted ventilation (ineligible comparator), one case report and one animal study (see Characteristics of excluded studies table).

    Studies awaiting classification

    No studies are awaiting classification.

    Allocation

    Seven studies performed and reported randomisation adequately (low risk of bias; Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ). One study was described as randomised but did not report on the randomisation method (unclear risk of bias; Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    Seven studies performed and reported allocation concealment adequately (low risk of bias; Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ). One study did not report on allocation concealment ( Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    Blinding

    Seven studies did not blind the intervention to parents, caregivers or study personnel (high risk of bias; Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ). Sadeghnia 2014 did not report blinding (unclear risk of bias).

    One study blinded the outcome assessors (low risk of detection bias; Gharehbaghi 2018 ). The other studies either did not blind assessors (high risk of detection bias; Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Pinheiro 2016 , Roberts 2018 ), or report on the outcome's blinding (unclear risk of detection bias; Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    Incomplete outcome data

    Seven studies reported all enrolled infants (low risk of attrition bias; Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ). One study reported on analysed infants, and it is unclear whether all enrolled infants completed the study (unclear risk of attrition bias; Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    Selective reporting

    Seven studies reported prespecified outcomes ( Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 , Roberts 2018 ). Two of the seven studies were registered retrospectively, two years after first enrollment. For this reason, we judged them at unclear risk of reporting bias ( Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ). We judged the other five studies at low risk of reporting bias.

    One study did not have a published protocol (unclear risk of reporting bias; Sadeghnia 2014 ).

    Other potential sources of bias

    Three studies were stopped early and enrolled fewer participants than the projected sample size (unclear risk of bias; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ). Three studies were stopped early and judged at high risk of bias because of loss of equipoise ( Gallup 2022 ); important differences between groups ( Pinheiro 2016 ); and reduction of the trial power and the true effect of the intervention was likely to have been smaller than the reported effect ( Roberts 2018 ) (see Characteristics of included studies table). One study had higher five‐minute Apgar scores in the LMA group (unclear risk of other bias; Sadeghnia 2014 ). One study was judged at low risk of bias ( Amini 2019 ).

    Five studies involving 333 infants compared S‐LMA versus InSurE ( Amini 2019 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Sadeghnia 2014 ; summary of findings Table 1 ).

    Primary outcomes
    Death or bronchopulmonary dysplasia (composite outcome) defined as the need for oxygen or respiratory support at 36 weeks' postmenstrual age

    S‐LMA may have little or no effect on the composite outcome of death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA compared with InSurE (RR 1.50, 95% CI 0.27 to 8.34; I 2 = not applicable as 1 study (50 participants) had 0 events in both groups; 2 studies, 110 participants; low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ).

    Subgroup analysis by use of sedation and analgesia: four studies reported the use of sedation and analgesia in the InSurE group but not in the S‐LMA group ( Amini 2019 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ). There may be little or no difference in death or BPD in infants born at 28 to 36 weeks' GA (RR 1.50, 95% CI 0.27 to 8.34; 1 study, 60 participants; Analysis 3.1 ; Pinheiro 2016 ).

    The available data did not permit the other planned subgroup and sensitivity analyses.

    Measures of the safety of the surfactant administration procedure

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included eight RCTs (510 newborn infants). We found no studies of prophylactic or early S‐LMA or studies comparing S‐LMA to LISA. Seven studies were considered at low risk of selection and attrition bias, but seven studies were unblinded.

    Comparison 1: surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway versus surfactant administration via endotracheal tube with the intent to rapidly extubate

    Five studies reporting outcomes for 333 infants compared S‐LMA versus InSurE. We found low‐certainty evidence that there may be little or no difference in the primary outcome of death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA. There may be a reduction in mechanical ventilation at any time with S‐LMA compared with InSurE (low‐certainty evidence). In subgroup analysis, the reduction in mechanical ventilation at any time was restricted to four studies enrolling 236 infants that used analgesia or sedation for the InSurE technique. No trial reported the use of analgesia or sedation for S‐LMA except for the use of sucrose. There may be little or no difference in air leak and BPD (clinical definition) among survivors to 36 weeks' PMA (low‐certainty evidence). One study reported no infants had an IVH grade III or IV, and no infant had cystic PVL, NEC, spontaneous intestinal perforation or ROP (50 participants). There was one death before hospital discharge in the InSurE group (3 studies, 203 participants). No study reported neurosensory disability in survivors measured beyond one year' PMA.

    The measures of safety of the surfactant administration procedure found there may be little or no difference in failure to place LMA/ETT at the first attempt, bradycardia, hypoxaemia (oxygen saturation less than 80%), apnoea and surfactant reflux during the procedure with S‐LMA compared to InSurE techniques.

    Comparison 2: surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway versus surfactant administration via endotracheal tube with extubation at standard criteria

    One study enrolling 48 infants compared S‐LMA versus S‐ETT ( Barbosa 2017 ). The study did not report the primary outcome of death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA. There is low‐certainty evidence that S‐LMA may reduce the use of mechanical ventilation at any time compared to S‐ETT. For other morbidities and mortality, there were either too few events, or the morbidities were not reported, to assess effectiveness or safety. Postdischarge outcomes were not reported.

    The measures of safety of the surfactant administration procedure were not reported. Comparing S‐LMA and S‐ETT, there may be little or no difference in postintervention intratracheal surfactant received. The number of doses of intratracheal surfactant received postintervention was not reported.

    Comparison 3: surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway versus surfactant administration via less‐invasive methods

    We did not identify any study for this comparison. With the increasing move towards gentle and non‐invasive ventilatory strategies, comparing these strategies is paramount.

    Comparison 4: surfactant administration via laryngeal mask airway versus no surfactant administration

    Two studies enrolling 129 infants compared S‐LMA versus no surfactant administration. Rescue surfactant could be used in both groups in both trials. There is low‐certainty evidence that there may be little or no difference in death or BPD at 36 weeks' PMA. There is probably a reduction in mechanical ventilation at any time in the S‐LMA group compared to no surfactant. There is low‐certainty evidence that there may be little or no difference in BPD (clinical definition) among survivors to 36 weeks' PMA in the S‐LMA group compared to no surfactant. One study reported no infants in either group with any IVH, IVH grade III or IV, cystic PVL or died. PDA, NEC, spontaneous intestinal perforation, ROP and duration of hospitalisation were not reported. Postdischarge outcomes were not reported.

    Data on the safety measures of the surfactant administration procedure were limited. One study reported failure to place LMA at the first attempt in 30% of infants in the S‐LMA group. There were limited data on hypoxaemia, bradycardia and apnoea during the procedure. Postintervention gastric surfactant aspirated greater than 10% of surfactant dose was reported in 8% and 28% of infants in the S‐LMA groups in the two studies. S‐LMA may reduce the need for postintervention intratracheal surfactant compared to no surfactant.

    Currently, there are two ongoing registered trials. One trial is comparing surfactant administration by either a supraglottic airway device or direct laryngoscopy in late preterm and term newborns on nCPAP ( ANZCTRN12619000995178 ). One trial is comparing LMA versus ETT for surfactant administration in RDS in preterm neonates of more than 30 weeks' gestation ( CTRI/2016/12/007540 ).

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We included eight RCTs (enrolling 510 newborn infants) in this review. For specific comparisons, no studies evaluated prophylactic S‐LMA; S‐LMA was not compared to other LISA techniques, including thin catheter surfactant or nebulised surfactant for infants on non‐invasive respiratory support.

    Five trials enrolling 333 infants compared rescue S‐LMA with InSurE technique in very preterm or preterm infants ranging from 800 g to 2000 g birth weight and from 27 weeks up to 37 weeks' gestation, or both. Most outcomes were incompletely reported except for mechanical ventilation at any time. Long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes were also not reported.

    One trial enrolling 48 infants born at 28 to 35 weeks' gestation with birth weight of 1000 g or greater compared rescue S‐LMA versus S‐ETT. The primary outcome of death or BPD was not reported. Most outcomes were incompletely reported, or there were too few events to assess safety and efficacy. Long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes were also not reported.

    Two studies enrolling 129 infants with birth weights greater than 1200 g and GAs 28 to 35 weeks compared rescue S‐LMA versus no surfactant administration. Rescue surfactant could be used in both groups in both trials. The primary outcome of death or BPD was not reported. Most outcomes were incompletely reported, or there were too few events to assess safety and efficacy. Long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes were also not reported.

    No trial enrolled infants at less than 27 weeks' gestation or less than 800 g. Furthermore, subgroup data on very low birth weight infants (between 1000 g and 1500 g), and preterm infants (28 to 32 weeks' gestation) were unavailable. Only a few infants at less than 32 weeks' gestation or less than 1500 g were likely recruited in the studies included in this review. Given this limitation, the results of this review do not apply to very low birth weight or preterm infants.

    The scarcity of data in very preterm and extremely preterm infants may be related to the unavailability of suitably small LMA devices. LMA size 1.0 is generally recommended for preterm infants at 1500 g birth weight ( Pinheiro 2016 ). Although they have been used in infants as low as 800 g birth weight ( Brimacombe 1999 ), data supporting their use is lacking. Size 0.5 LMA is currently unavailable for routine clinical use. Technological improvement in this area may encourage studies using LMA in infants of less than 1500 g.

    Various LMA devices were used. This review has not assessed the efficacy or safety of specific devices in subgroup analysis.

    Dosaging and specifics of surfactant administration are documented in the Characteristics of included studies table with trials administering 100 mg/kg to 200 mg/kg of surfactant, most commonly via a thin catheter inserted through the LMA and most commonly in several aliquots with or without intermittent positive pressure ventilation (IPPV) after aliquots, with device removal after administration then reinstitution of non‐invasive ventilation. The risk of surfactant leakage and non‐optimal fitting of LMA were not reported.

    Sedation or opioid analgesia, or both, was not used in the S‐LMA group but was used in four trials for the InSurE procedure. The analysis that found S‐LMA reduced mechanical ventilation at any time is restricted to trials that used sedation or opioid analgesia for the InSurE procedure.

    There was also incomplete reporting of measures of safety of surfactant administration, including failure rates of insertion on the first attempt, surfactant reflux, hypoxaemia, bradycardia and apnoea during the procedure, and postintervention gastric surfactant aspirated greater than 10% of surfactant dose indicating failure to administer some or all of the dose intratracheal.

    Quality of the evidence

    The risk of bias in the studies included in this review varied ( Figure 3 ), with seven trials assessed at low risk of selection and attrition bias ( Amini 2019 ; Attridge 2013 ; Barbosa 2017 ; Gallup 2022 ; Gharehbaghi 2018 ; Pinheiro 2016 ; Roberts 2018 ), and with one trial having an unknown risk of bias ( Sadeghnia 2014 ) ( Figure 4 ). Given the interventions included in this review are difficult to blind, we did not assess blinding as a potential source of bias in this review. However, most of the reported outcomes were objective, so at a lower risk of reporting bias.

    The certainty of the evidence, assessed according to GRADE, was moderate to very low. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence as shown in the summary of findings tables due to serious study limitations in many trials ( summary of findings Table 1 ; summary of findings Table 2 ; summary of findings Table 3 ).

    We did not perform all planned subgroup analyses, as subgroup data were limited or unavailable. As no trial enrolled infants at less than 27 weeks' gestation or less than 800 g birth weight and limited data were available for very low birth weight (1000 g to 1500 g birth weight) or preterm infants (28 to 32 weeks' gestation), the results may not apply to very low birth weight or very preterm infants.

    Given only eight RCTs enrolling 510 newborn infants were included in this review, with many outcomes under‐reported or reported in a small number of infants, often with few events, the review is underpowered to detect important differences for most outcomes studied.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The protocol was first published in 2010 ( Abdel‐Latif 2010b ), and the review in 2011 ( Abdel‐Latif 2011b ); since then, both the research questions about the use of S‐LMA and the methods used for Cochrane reviews have changed, and we have, therefore, updated our methods substantially. In addition, due to changes in the research questions about S‐LMA, we have subsequently had to change the comparison groups ( Figure 2 ). We separated comparison 1 (invasive surfactant administration) into two distinct methods of invasive surfactant administration: InSurE and S‐ETT.

    Furthermore, we added a new comparison for S‐LMA versus surfactant administration via less‐invasive methods (LISA) such as thin catheters.

    We also removed the subgroup analyses: type of LMA surfactant (animal‐derived, synthetic). We removed blinding as a criterion in the sensitivity analysis as the intervention is unlikely to be able to be adequately blinded.

    These post hoc changes might be considered potential biases in the review process.

    For the 2023 update, we searched additional databases: Epistemonikos and the Chinese language articles from China/Asia On Demand (CAOD).

    We included subgroup analyses even when only one study was available because weight and gestation groups are commonly used in guideline development.

    Some outcomes may only be relevant for certain groups within the study population. For instance, BPD is more relevant for extremely preterm infants (28 weeks' gestation or less) than preterm (33 to 36 weeks' gestation). Subgroup analyses were performed only for GRADE outcomes. Data was limited, particularly for infants under 32 weeks' gestation and 1500 g.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    One recent systematic review including six RCTs enrolling 357 infants assessed the efficacy of S‐LMA versus 'standard methods of surfactant delivery' ( Al Ali 2022 ). Al Ali 2022 reported that S‐LMA was associated with decreased oxygen requirement, reduced intubation and mechanical ventilation. They reported no differences between groups for death, BPD or pneumothorax. The review concluded LMA might be an effective alternative method of surfactant delivery. However, further high‐quality RCTs with larger sample sizes and including extreme preterm infants are needed to establish LMA as an alternative technique for surfactant delivery.

    Another review compared S‐LMA, pharyngeal instillation and aerosolisation methods, and broadly supports the findings of our review ( Devi 2022 ).

    In one network meta‐analysis, Bellos 2021 compared surfactant administration via thin catheters, laryngeal mask, nebulisation, pharyngeal instillation, intubation and surfactant administration followed by immediate extubation (InSurE) and no surfactant administration. Bellos 2021 included 16 RCTs and 20 observational studies (13,234 participants). Compared with InSurE, administration via a thin catheter was associated with significantly lower mortality rates, mechanical ventilation, BPD and PVL with moderate certainty of evidence and NEC with low certainty of evidence. There were no differences observed by comparing InSurE with the administration via a laryngeal mask, nebulisation or pharyngeal instillation.

    One Cochrane review of surfactant therapy via a thin catheter (S‐TC) in preterm infants with or at risk of RDS concluded that S‐TC compared with S‐ETT is associated with reduced risk of death or BPD, less intubation in the first 72 hours, and reduced incidence of major complications and in‐hospital mortality ( Abdel‐Latif 2021 ). S‐LMA was not a comparator in this review.

    Given there are no comparisons between S‐LMA and other non‐invasive methods of surfactant delivery, and the findings of our review and the other reviews above, further evaluation of S‐LMA to standard methods of surfactant delivery (particularly other non‐invasive methods) in high‐quality RCTs with larger sample size are indicated.

    As the placement of LMA can be learned more quickly and easily than intubation and has a higher success rate on the first attempt without the need for laryngoscopy ( Pejovic 2020 ; Wanous 2017 ), S‐LMA has been proposed as an ideal method for administering surfactants in lower‐ and middle‐income countries (LMIC) ( Zapata 2022 ). Three studies included in the review were from upper‐middle or middle‐income countries (Brazil, Iran). A quality improvement initiative in Jordan (LMIC country) showed that surfactant administration through laryngeal or supraglottic airways (SALSA) was feasible and more effective than matched retrospective control of InSurE ( Abu Leyah 2022 ). Larger trials are necessary to establish the safety and efficacy of SALSA in LMIC.
