Cochrane Database of Systematic reviews Review - Intervention

Treatment for dysphagia (swallowing difficulties) in hereditary ataxia

  • Adam P Vogel
  • Megan J Keage
  • Kerstin Johansson
  • Ellika Schalling
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 13 November 2015 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD010169.pub2
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Hereditary ataxias are a heterogeneous group of disorders resulting in progressive inco‐ordination. Swallowing impairment, also known as dysphagia, is a common and potentially life threatening sequel of disease progression. The incidence and nature of dysphagia in these conditions is largely unknown. The loss of an effective and safe swallow can dramatically affect the health and well‐being of an individual. Remediation of difficulties of eating and drinking is an important goal in the clinical care of people with hereditary ataxia.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of interventions for swallowing impairment (dysphagia) in people with hereditary ataxias.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Neuromuscular Disease Group Specialized Register, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), MEDLINE, EMBASE, CINAHL Plus, PsycINFO, and the Education Resources Information Center (ERIC) on 14 September 2015. We also searched Linguistics and Language Behavior Abstracts (LLBA), Dissertation Abstracts, and Trials Registries on 24 September 2015.

    Selection criteria

    We considered all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs that compared treatments for hereditary ataxia with placebo or no treatment. We only included studies measuring dysphagia.

    Data collection and analysis

    Three review authors (ES, KJ, MK) independently screened all titles and abstracts. In the event of any disagreement or uncertainty over the inclusion of a particular paper, the review authors planned to meet and reach consensus.

    Main results

    We identified no RCTs from the 519 titles and abstracts screened. We excluded papers primarily for not including participants with a hereditary ataxia (that is, being focused on other neurological conditions), being theoretical reviews rather than intervention studies, or being neither randomised nor quasi‐randomised trials.

    We identified five papers of various design that described treatment for dysphagia, or improvement to swallow as a by‐product of treatment, in people with hereditary ataxia. None of these studies were RCTs or quasi‐RCTs.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is an absence of any significant evidence supporting the use of any dysphagia intervention in hereditary ataxia. The lack of evidence highlights the critical need for well‐controlled treatment trials in the field.

    • Treatment for swallowing difficulties in hereditary ataxia

    Review question

    We reviewed the evidence for management of swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) in people with one of the many hereditary ataxias.

    Background

    Hereditary ataxias are a varied group of disorders that mainly affect a person's ability to co‐ordinate movement. Symptoms become worse over time. Hereditary ataxias can also affect eyesight, speech, thinking, and swallowing. Although swallowing problems are common in ataxia, we do not know enough about their nature and severity. The loss of an effective and safe swallow can dramatically affect health and well‐being. Improving difficulties of eating and drinking is an important goal in the clinical care of people with hereditary ataxia.

    We wanted to discover if any treatments for dysphagia benefit people with hereditary ataxias. People with swallowing problems can experience malnutrition (lack of adequate nourishment), dehydration (lack of fluids), and pneumonia secondary to aspiration (lung infection secondary to food or liquids entering the lungs), which can be fatal. Any treatments aiming to prevent these problems or improve swallowing were of interest in the review.

    Study characteristics

    We searched medical databases for randomised controlled trials (RCTs) or quasi‐RCTs of treatments for swallowing difficulties in hereditary ataxia. Well performed RCTs provide the best quality evidence. Quasi‐randomised trials allocate participants to treatment using methods that are not completely random, such as by date of birth, case record number, or day of the week. No studies met these pre‐specified inclusion criteria.

    Key results

    We found no RCTs or quasi‐RCTs of treatment for swallowing difficulties in hereditary ataxia.

    Conclusions

    The absence of high quality studies examining the efficacy of treatments for dysphagia in these disorders means than no conclusions can be drawn on the most appropriate treatment of dysphagia and highlights the urgent need for well controlled trials in the field.

    The evidence is current to September 2015.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    The absence of high quality evidence prohibits the development of any clinical recommendations specific to hereditary ataxia. Current best practice in dysphagia management lacks strong empirical evidence; however, postural modification (for example, reducing the impact of scoliosis), compensatory head positions (for example, chin tuck), adaption of alternative feeding equipment (for example, controlled flow drinking containers), and dietary modification (for example, changing the texture or consistency of foods to improve swallowing) remain the mainstay of clinical practice. Published clinical guidelines reviewed and provided details for the management of people with Friedreich ataxia (Corben 2014).

    Implications for research

    There is a critical absence of high quality evidence supporting dysphagia management in people with hereditary ataxias. Two significant barriers currently limit the initiation of well controlled and adequately powered intervention studies. The first relates to a limited knowledge base on the nature, severity, and progression of swallowing (dys)function in many hereditary ataxias. Without an adequate understanding of how symptoms manifest and then deteriorate over time, trials may select inappropriate therapeutic targets. The second challenge relates to designing and implementing appropriately powered trials. The disorders described in this review are rare, making multisite and multinational collaborative intervention studies necessary to secure adequate participant numbers. International collaboration in combination with a series of thorough characterisation studies encompassing the most prevalent hereditary ataxias will enable the initiation of targeted therapies for this under‐researched group of disorders.

    Background

    Hereditary ataxias are a heterogeneous group of disorders that commonly manifest as progressive inco‐ordination and can result in impairments in vision, speech, cognition, and swallowing (Klockgether 2011). The hereditary forms of degenerative ataxia arise from genetic mutations that can be autosomal dominant (for example, spinocerebellar ataxia; SCA) (Whaley 2011), or autosomal recessive (for example, Friedreich ataxia) (Schulz 2009). Over 30 autosomal dominant hereditary ataxias have been identified, with a prevalence in Europe between one and three per 100,000 people (Dürr 2010). SCA make up the majority of the dominant form of the disorder with a typical age of onset between the third and fourth decades of life (Paulson 2009). One Norwegian study found a prevalence of hereditary ataxia (autosomal dominant and autosomal recessive) of 1 : 15,000, with Friedreich ataxia affecting 1 : 666,000 people and ataxia telangiectasia 1 : 250,000 people (Erichsen 2009). Molecular data from people with Friedreich ataxia collated in other Western European countries suggest a higher prevalence, with a carrier rate of 1 : 60 to 1 : 90 (Epplen 1997), and an estimated prevalence of 1 : 29,000 (Cossée 1997). The incidence of dysphagia in these populations has been estimated via clinical cohort studies, primarily through neurological examination as part of the initial phenotype descriptive work. Broken down by subtype, dysphagia has been reported in 100% of people with SCA1 (Genis 1995; Rüb 2004; Rüb 2012), 36% to 74% of people with SCA2 (Cancel 1997; Dürr 1996; Schöls 1998), 46% to 74% of people with SCA3 (Dürr 1996; Jardim 2001; Schöls 1998), 8% to 65% of people with SCA6 (Schöls 1998; Takahashi 2004), 30% to 58% of people with SCA7 (David 1998; Johansson 1998), and 47% of people with SCA17 (Rolfs 2003). Despite known incidence figures for dysphagia in some of the hereditary ataxias, the nature and severity of swallowing impairment remains largely unknown. In the only prospective study to investigate swallowing impairment in people with Friedreich ataxia, 92% of participants presented with dysphagia (Vogel 2014a). We know from work in people with Friedreich ataxia and related populations that the severity and nature of dysphagia can dramatically affect the health and well‐being of an individual (Ekberg 2002; Vogel 2014a), making remediation of difficulties of eating and drinking a key aspect of the clinical care plan of people diagnosed with a hereditary ataxia.

    Description of the condition

    Difficulties eating and drinking caused by dysphagia can result in a series of deleterious health and social consequences, including extended mealtimes, reduced nutritional intake, restricted dietary options, social isolation, and aspiration (food and liquids entering the lungs), potentially leading to pneumonia (Ekberg 2002). Data on the nature and progression of swallowing difficulties in people with a hereditary ataxia are limited. There are no longitudinal studies that document the onset, nature, or progression of dysphagia in people with Friedreich ataxia or other hereditary ataxias. However, we know dysphagia in people with Friedreich ataxia is characterised by oral and pharyngeal impairment that can result in coughing or choking on liquids and solid foods and secretion management (Vogel 2014a; Vogel 2015), and that the severity of these difficulties correlate significantly with reductions in quality of life (Vogel 2014a). Anecdotal evidence suggests that dysphagia appears with advancing disease and that people with Friedreich ataxia frequently choke, require modified foods, and eventually need nasogastric tube (NGT) or gastrostomy feeding as a consequence of disease progression (Pandolfo 2009). Additional concerns include difficulties controlling solids or liquids in the mouth, impaired mastication, drooling, nasal regurgitation, and a sensation of food lodging in the throat. Swallowing difficulties may be exacerbated when there are co‐existent postural or hand‐to‐mouth co‐ordination difficulties.

    Description of the intervention

    Management of swallowing difficulties in hereditary ataxias typically aims to facilitate safe and adequate nutrition and hydration. Intervention may be beneficial at any stage of disease progression. The degenerative nature of hereditary ataxias dictates that therapy programmes change over time to cater for evolving patient needs. The type and efficacy of treatment can also vary depending on the cognitive functioning of an individual, as some interventions rely on self management. Management options can be roughly broken down by their mode of delivery, and can include both direct and indirect approaches as well as compensatory techniques. Direct intervention methods often involve the use of food or liquids during exercises or activities designed to improve swallowing function (for example, modified Valsalva (effortful) swallow) (McCulloch 1996). Indirect interventions include oral motor exercises designed to strengthen and improve the co‐ordination of underlying musculature, and modified diets that involve textures and consistencies designed to facilitate swallowing, as well as tactile‐thermal stimulation techniques (Sciortino 2003). Pharmacological treatments designed to improve swallowing indirectly by treating spasticity include intrathecal baclofen (Schapiro 2002), or botulinum toxin administered intramuscularly or subcutaneously (Davis 2000). Compensatory techniques and patient education on safe swallowing strategies can include postural modification and altered head position, as well as the use of utensils and equipment suitable for people with movement disorders (for example, cups with controlled flow lids, and modified seating).

    How the intervention might work

    Compensatory strategies and patient education are typically delivered during the early phases of disease progression. Environmental modifications, such as reducing distractions and promoting a focus on the task of swallowing (for example, eliminating talking during meals) are common strategies. Postural modification serves to improve the anatomical position of the airway and oesophagus in order to aid the movement of a bolus (ball) of masticated food to the stomach. Altering head position to improve the anatomical position of important structures in the pharynx (for example, the epiglottis) is thought to reduce the likelihood of matter entering the trachea and lungs. In particular, there is some evidence to suggest that adopting the chin‐down posture during a swallow effectively alters oropharyngeal dimensions (Welch 1993), and in some cases improves the swallow by reducing the depth of laryngeal penetration and residue (Logemann 2008), and reducing premature overspill. As symptoms and clinical signs become more prominent, specific therapeutic exercises and strategies are appropriate. Direct interventions such as the modified Valsalva (effortful) swallow are designed to improve the overall strength, tone, and co‐ordination of the muscles recruited for safe and effective swallowing by over‐exerting the processes involved in swallowing (utilising the principles of traditional physiotherapy). Non‐surgical medical treatment such as intrathecal baclofen or intramuscular or subcutaneous botulinum toxin can increase or decrease tone to enable more functional oropharyngeal swallowing physiology (Dressler 2010).

    Why it is important to do this review

    There is currently no empirically documented population‐specific evidence on the benefit of swallowing therapy for people with Friedreich ataxia or other hereditary ataxias. The evidence for the suggested therapies are based on diseases and disorders similar to, but wholly distinct from, hereditary ataxias (Corben 2014) (also see the following reviews: Parkinson's disease (Deane 2001); paediatric neurological impairment (Morgan 2012); long term progressive muscle disease (Hill 2004); oesophageal sphincter dysfunction (Regan 2014); nutritional support (Gomes 2015); and acute stroke (Bath 1999)). The likely presence of dysphagia in all people with Friedreich ataxia and potentially other hereditary ataxias necessitates the urgent development and evaluation of effective and confirmed therapies for ameliorating this aspect of these disorders.

    Objectives

    To assess the effects of interventions for swallowing impairment (dysphagia) in people with hereditary ataxias.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (studies in which participants are allocated by systematic methods such as alternate days, date of birth or case record number).

    Types of participants

    Participants of any age, sex, ethnicity, stage of illness, and degree of severity. We included only studies incorporating participants with a genetically confirmed diagnosis of a hereditary ataxia presenting with dysphagia.

    Types of interventions

    We included interventions administered for a minimum of one day and a maximum of 12 months. We considered any intervention or combination of interventions compared with no treatment, placebo, another treatment, or combination of treatments. Possible types of interventions for inclusion into this review included the following.

    1. Altered feeding routes, including percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG), NGT, and intravenous and subcutaneous feeding.

    2. Nutritional and fluid supplementation.

    3. Surgery, where the aim was to improve swallowing.

    4. Instrumentally based biofeedback approaches: interventions that use some form of instrumentation designed to provide visual or other forms of biofeedback (for example, fibreoptic endoscopic evaluation of swallow (FEES), kinematics, and electromyography).

    5. Pharmaceutical treatments.

    6. Clinician‐driven interventions using traditional drill exercises without instrumentation in the absence of feedback other than clinician verbal feedback (for example, exercises of the lips or tongue to improve lip seal, strength, range, or co‐ordination of the musculature supporting swallowing, and drill‐based exercise therapy where the aim was to improve the underlying mechanisms (muscular and physiological) associated with dysphagia).

    7. Tactile‐thermal stimulation.

    8. Modified diets (changes of viscosity or texture).

    Types of outcome measures

    Both standardised and non‐standardised swallowing‐specific outcome measures administered at a minimum of one day and a maximum of six months post intervention completion. We considered non‐swallowing‐specific outcome measures as secondary assessments.

    Primary outcomes

    1. The presence or absence of aspiration or penetration of fluid or food matter during swallowing, as determined by functional imaging techniques (for example, videofluoroscopy swallowing studies (VFSS)), and assessed using validated protocols such as the Penetration Aspiration Scale (PAS) (Rosenbek 1996).

    Secondary outcomes

    Secondary outcomes specific to swallowing included the following:

    1. reliance on supplementary feeding (for example, PEG or NGT);

    2. recurrence of lower respiratory tract infection (requiring treatment with antibiotics) over a defined period of the trial;

    3. self report of dysphagia, such as EAT‐10 (Eating Assessment Tool) (Belafsky 2008), and improvement in quality of life measures specific to dysphagia (for example, the Swallowing Quality of Life (SWAL‐QOL) (McHorney 2006));

    4. direct measurement of the physiological function of the oropharyngeal mechanism for swallowing, such as effectiveness of lip seal, jaw rhythmicity, or adequacy of laryngeal closure.

    Secondary outcomes that were not specific to swallowing sought to capture longer term effects of therapy, measured at the completion of therapy or later included:

    1. generic health measures (for example, Short Form 36 Health Survey (SF36) (Ware 1992);

    2. adverse effects;

    3. burdens (for example, demands on carers, frequency of tests, and restrictions on lifestyle);

    4. economic outcomes (for example, cost and resource use).

    Search methods for identification of studies

    On 14 September 2015, we searched the Cochrane Neuromuscular Specialized Register, the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (2015, Issue 8 in The Cochrane Library), MEDLINE (January 1966 to September 2015), EMBASE (January 1980 to September 2015), CINAHL Plus (January 1937 to September 2015), PsycINFO (January 1806 to September 2015), Educational Resources Information Center (ERIC) (January 1966 to September 2015), Linguistics and Language Behaviour Abstracts (LLBA) (1973 to September 2015), and ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global (1980 to September 2015). We searched clinical trials registries including the World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/) portal and clinicaltrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov), for ongoing trials. We checked all references in the identified trials to identify any additional published data. There were no language limitations.

    Electronic searches

    The detailed search strategies are in the appendices: Appendix 1 (Cochrane Neuromuscular Specialized Register), Appendix 2 (CENTRAL), Appendix 3 (MEDLINE), Appendix 4 (EMBASE), Appendix 5 (CINAHL Plus), Appendix 6 (PsycINFO), Appendix 7 (ERIC), Appendix 8 (LLBA), Appendix 9 (ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global), Appendix 10 (ICTRP), and Appendix 11 (ClinicalTrials.gov).

    Searching other resources

    We checked all references in the identified trials. We did not contact authors of published or unpublished trials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Three review authors (ES, KJ, MK) independently screened titles and abstracts and excluded reports that were irrelevant. There were no disagreements between review authors.

    Data extraction and management

    No studies met inclusion criteria for this review. Had we identified any studies that met the inclusion criteria, we planned to use the analyses in Appendix 12.

    Results

    Description of studies

    See Characteristics of excluded studies table.

    Results of the search

    Our search conducted up until 14 September 2015 identified 682 citations from the databases; 519 abstracts remained after we removed duplicates (see Figure 1). Out of the 519 abstracts, five studies appeared to meet selection criteria and we reviewed full‐text versions. We identified three additional studies within the reference lists of these five citations. Of the eight full‐text articles reviewed, we excluded three studies for not being RCTs or quasi‐RCTs (Alfonsi 2010; Freund 2007; Powell 2007), two as they were reviews (Lefton‐Greif 2011; Wraith 2009), and three studies were not relevant to this review. We excluded the remaining 514 studies because they did not include participants with a hereditary ataxia, they were not intervention studies, or they were not RCTs or quasi‐RCTs.

    We briefly described three excluded treatment trials and two reviews in Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Included studies

    No studies met inclusion criteria.

    Excluded studies

    Three studies of low methodological quality described the efficacy of treatments for dysphagia in hereditary ataxias. See Characteristics of excluded studies for reasons for exclusion and brief descriptions of the excluded treatment studies.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    We identified no controlled studies for this review.

    Effects of interventions

    No controlled studies met inclusion criteria. Therefore, it was not possible to determine the benefit or otherwise of any dysphagia intervention in hereditary ataxias.

    Discussion

    People with hereditary ataxia experience significant morbidity and early mortality. Dysphagia is a common symptom of hereditary ataxia (Lefton‐Greif 2011; Vogel 2014a), and can result in significant health and social consequences (Ekberg 2002). Our understanding of the severity and nature of dysphagia in such syndromes is limited; however, anecdotal evidence tells us that dysphagia is prevalent in this group of disorders. The present review sought to investigate the efficacy of dysphagia treatments in hereditary ataxia; however, we did not identify any RCTs or quasi‐RCTs meeting our inclusion criteria. There is a critical gap between current best practice in clinical care and the evidence available supporting clinical decision making.

    Summary of main results

    No studies met our inclusion criteria. The absence of well controlled studies in the field means that no conclusions can be drawn on the efficacy of treatments for dysphagia in hereditary ataxia. The only papers that exist are case reports and descriptive studies, which do not allow us to draw conclusions about effectiveness or relative effectiveness.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    No RCTs have been conducted in this field. There is no evidence supporting treatment for dysphagia specifically for hereditary ataxias. The available literature is incomplete and not applicable in a clinical setting.

    Quality of the evidence

    The current evidence available from single case studies or observational studies for treatment of dysphagia in hereditary ataxias is insufficient and does not inform clinical practice.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The review drew evidence from published work that may have excluded studies published in other forums. We did not contact any authors of existing studies.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    No other reviews have been published on this specific topic. Four related reviews exist on the efficacy of treatment for dysphagia in neurological populations: these include chronic muscle disease (Hill 2004, update Jones 2015 in press), oropharyngeal dysphagia in children with neurological impairment (Morgan 2012), non‐pharmacological therapies for dysphagia in Parkinson's disease (Deane 2001), and the allied health interventions for people with Huntington's disease (Bilney 2003). All studies concluded that there was insufficient evidence to make recommendations on appropriate clinical practice. The reviews also echo the need for appropriately powered and controlled treatment trials in dysphagia for progressive neurological disorders. One Cochrane review on treatment for speech rehabilitation reported that there is insufficient and low or very low quality evidence from either RCTs or observational studies to determine the effectiveness of any treatment for speech disorders in any of the hereditary ataxias (Vogel 2014b).
