Oxygenation during the apnoeic phase preceding intubation in adults in prehospital, emergency department, intensive care and operating theatre environments

    • Background

    Apnoeic oxygenation is the delivery of oxygen during the apnoeic phase preceding intubation. It is used to prevent respiratory complications of endotracheal intubation that have the potential to lead to significant adverse events including dysrhythmia, haemodynamic decompensation, hypoxic brain injury and death. Oxygen delivered by nasal cannulae during the apnoeic phase of intubation (apnoeic oxygenation) may serve as a non‐invasive adjunct to endotracheal intubation to decrease the incidence of hypoxaemia, morbidity and mortality.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of apnoeic oxygenation before intubation in adults in the prehospital, emergency department, intensive care unit and operating theatre environments compared to no apnoeic oxygenation during intubation.

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. The latest search date was 4 November 2022.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs that compared the use of any form of apnoeic oxygenation including high flow and low flow nasal cannulae versus no apnoeic oxygenation during intubation. We defined quasi‐randomization as participant allocation to each arm by means that were not truly random, such as alternation, case record number or date of birth. We excluded comparative prospective cohort and comparative retrospective cohort studies, physiological modelling studies and case reports.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard Cochrane methods. Our primary outcomes were 1. hospital stay and 2. incidence of severe hypoxaemia. Our secondary outcomes were 3. incidence of hypoxaemia, 4. lowest recorded saturation of pulse oximetry (SpO2), 5. intensive care unit (ICU) stay, 6. first pass success rate, 7. adverse events and 8. mortality. We used GRADE to assess certainty of evidence.

    Main results

    We included 23 RCTs (2264 participants) in our analyses. Eight studies (729 participants) investigated the use of low‐flow (15 L/minute or less), and 15 studies (1535 participants) investigated the use of high‐flow (greater than 15 L/minute) oxygen. Settings were varied and included the emergency department (2 studies, 327 participants), ICU (7 studies, 913 participants) and operating theatre (14 studies, 1024 participants). We considered two studies to be at low risk of bias across all domains.

    None of the studies reported on hospital length of stay. In predominately critically ill people, there may be little to no difference in the incidence of severe hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 80%) when using apnoeic oxygenation at any flow rate from the start of apnoea until successful intubation (risk ratio (RR) 0.86, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.66 to 1.11; P = 0.25, I² = 0%; 15 studies, 1802 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    There was insufficient evidence of any effect on the incidence of hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 93%) (RR 0.58, 95% CI 0.23 to 1.46; P = 0.25, I² = 36%; 3 studies, 489 participants; low‐certainty evidence). There may be an improvement in the lowest recorded oxygen saturation, with a mean increase of 1.9% (95% CI 0.75% to 3.05%; P < 0.001, I² = 86%; 15 studies, 1525 participants; low‐certainty evidence). There may be a reduction in the duration of ICU stay with the use of apnoeic oxygenation during intubation (mean difference (MD) ‒1.13 days, 95% CI ‒1.51 to ‒0.74; P < 0.0001, I² = 46%; 5 studies, 815 participants; low‐certainty evidence). There may be little to no difference in first pass success rate (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.93 to 1.08; P = 0.79, I² = 0%; 8 studies, 826 participants; moderate‐certainty evidence). There may be little to no difference in incidence of adverse events including oral trauma, arrhythmia, aspiration, hypotension, pneumonia and cardiac arrest when apnoeic oxygenation is used. There was insufficient evidence about any effect on mortality (RR 0.84, 95% CI 0.70 to 1.00; P = 0.06, I² = 0%; 6 studies, 1015 participants; low‐certainty evidence).

    Authors' conclusions

    There was some evidence that oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation may improve the lowest recorded oxygen saturation. However, the differences in oxygen saturation were unlikely to be clinically significant. This did not translate into any measurable effect on the incidence of hypoxaemia or severe hypoxaemia in a group of predominately critically ill people. We were unable to assess the influence on hospital length of stay; however, there was a reduction in ICU stay in the apnoeic oxygenation group. The mechanism for this is unclear as there was little to no difference in first pass success or adverse event rates.

    • In adults, does oxygen therapy during the non‐breathing period before intubation prevent complications?

    What is intubation?

    Intubation is a medical procedure that involves insertion of a tube into the breathing passage during the course of anaesthesia before surgery or in life‐saving emergency situations such as road‐side trauma or in times of critical illness. The breathing tube serves as a portal for assisted ventilation.

    Why might oxygen therapy help?

    During the time when a person stops breathing after going to sleep ready for an operation before an oral breathing tube is inserted low blood oxygen levels (saturations) can occur. This potentially can lead to devastating complications such as a heart attack, stroke or death. Passively providing oxygen via the nose can provide oxygen deep into the lungs and might help prevent low oxygen saturations and the associated complications.

    What did we want to find out?

    Does oxygen therapy delivered by small tubes into the nose reduce the incidence of low and critically low oxygen levels in the blood during the time it takes for the medical team to insert the breathing tube whilst the person is not breathing? Does this then prevent the incidence of associated complications and change the length of stay in both the hospital and intensive care unit?

    What did we do?

    We searched medical databases for randomized controlled trials (a type of study where people are randomly assigned to treatment groups) of adults (aged years or older) that compared giving oxygen therapy with giving no oxygen therapy during the period between when they stopped breathing and intubation.

    What did we find?

    We found 23 studies with 2264 participants. The studies were conducted in intensive care units and operating theatres in countries around the world. Pharmaceutical companies contributed funding to some studies.

    Main results

    Compared with no oxygen therapy, oxygen therapy improved the lowest recorded blood oxygen levels slightly (by about 2%; 15 studies, 1525 participants), and reduced the duration of intensive care unit stay by about one day (5 studies, 815 people).

    Oxygen therapy had no effect on the incidence of critically low oxygen levels in the blood during the non‐breathing period in predominately critically ill people (15 studies, 1802 people).

    There was no effect on the occurrence of complications during intubation (10 studies, 997 participants), or in the success rate of first attempt at intubation (8 studies, 826 participants).

    None of our included studies reported the effect on hospital length of stay.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    Although we did find some benefits of oxygen therapy, we have low to moderate confidence in the results. This was mainly because the doctors in many studies were aware if the participants were receiving extra oxygen and there were differences between the groups of participants that we could not account for.

    What next?

    It is unlikely that oxygen therapy during the non‐breathing period provides much benefit to all participants in any of the measured intubation‐related outcomes. Further studies might focus on length of intensive care stay and any plausible reasons for its effect, or on which groups of participants it is more helpful to.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    Our evidence is current to 4 November 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    We were unable to assess the influence of oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation on hospital length of stay. We found that the use of oxygen therapy during the apnoeic phase preceding intubation did not alter the incidence of severe hypoxaemia. The studies investigating the incidence of severe hypoxaemia were predominantly in critically ill people and the certainty of the evidence was low, limiting our certainty about these results. In a subgroup of people without respiratory failure, we found some evidence of reduction in severe hypoxaemia, which suggests that there may be some benefit in people who are physiologically healthy, non‐obstructive and without significant respiratory comorbidities.

    We found some evidence that the use of oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation increases the lowest recorded oxygen saturation by a modest amount. This is unlikely to be clinically significant and resulted in little to no difference in the incidence of hypoxaemia (oxygen saturation (SpO2) less than 93%). We found some evidence of a small reduction in intensive care length of stay; however, the mechanism for this is unclear with little to no effect on first pass intubation success or adverse event rates.

    This systematic review focused on the use of apnoeic oxygenation prior to tracheal intubation and did not assess the use of apnoeic oxygenation where intubation was not planned or performed. This includes specific endoscopic, laryngeal and pharyngeal procedures, which are performed under apnoeic conditions. The outcomes of this review cannot be applied to clinical practice in those procedures.

    Implications for research

    The results presented in this systematic review were limited by the range of outcomes assessed by the individual studies and low‐to‐moderate certainty of evidence. We were unable to assess the primary outcome of hospital length of stay as no studies reported on this. However, the results presented showed no differences in hypoxaemia or adverse events. Therefore, it is unlikely that there are any plausible mechanisms for hospital length of stay to be different with apnoeic oxygenation during intubation. There may be some benefit in studies being performed specifically to investigate this outcome given the small difference in intensive care unit length of stay observed. Future research should aim to follow low risk of bias methodology, in particular focusing on blinding of personnel and outcome assessors, which is achievable with ingenuity.

    The evidence presented in this review suggests that benefit may be seen in people with physiologically normal lungs. Therefore, suggested future studies could include people at risk of difficult intubation that would require protracted apnoea times.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Endotracheal intubation is an essential procedure for the establishment of a definitive airway in a number of clinical settings, including prehospital, emergency department (ED), intensive care unit (ICU) and operating theatre (OT) environments. The Royal College of Anaesthetists and Difficult Airway Society's Fourth National Audit Project (NAP4) suggests that endotracheal intubation is used in 38.4% of general anaesthetics in the UK (Woodall 2011). Endotracheal intubation involves the placement of a cuffed endotracheal tube into the trachea, typically through the mouth, to enable oxygenation, ventilation and prevention of aspiration. Prolonged attempts at endotracheal intubation are associated with hypoxaemia, unplanned admission to the ICU and death (Caplan 1990; King 1990; Rose 1994). Hypoxaemia in this context is a potentially catastrophic complication and can lead to dysrhythmia, hypoxic brain injury and death (Caplan 1990; Davis 2008; Mort 2004).

    Preoxygenation is a widely accepted technique for prolonging the 'safe apnoea time' (Weingart 2012), the period during the intubation procedure during which the patient remains apnoeic without hypoxaemia. Preoxygenation can be less effective in critically ill people due to the effects of shunt physiology, increased metabolic demand, anaemia and decreased cardiac output (Drummond 1984; Farmery 1996; Mort 2005). In the NAP4, nearly 20% of all airway incidents occurred in the ICU, and ineffective preoxygenation in critically ill people was suggested as one possible contributor to this (Woodall 2011).

    Description of the intervention

    Apnoeic oxygenation aims to prevent hypoxaemia via the application of various oxygen delivery techniques during the apnoeic phase of intubation (Weingart 2012). Various protocols have been employed with nasal cannulae, including humidified or non‐humidified oxygen, as well as a range of oxygen flow rates (Binks 2017a; Binks 2017b; Holyoak 2017; White 2017). Low‐flow nasal cannulae (LFNC) are traditionally used in people requiring minimal oxygen supplementation. These consist of lightweight tubing attached to wall oxygen and split into two prongs at the end. These nasal prongs are positioned in the nostrils and flows of 1 L/minute to 6 L/minute of dry oxygen are traditionally titrated in spontaneously breathing people (O'Driscoll 2008). Oxygen can be delivered via LFNC at up to 15 L/minute in the setting of apnoeic oxygenation (Binks 2017a). High‐flow nasal cannulae (HFNC) are used as a non‐invasive delivery system for up to 100% humidified oxygen and are capable of delivering higher oxygen flow rates of up to 70 L/minute or more (Corley 2017; Mir 2017b). HFNC systems are generally comprised of a flow generator, air–oxygen blender and humidifier, with the heated humidified gas being delivered via a wide‐bore heated tube to nasal prongs.

    How the intervention might work

    During apnoeic oxygenation, the continuous oxygen supply is theorized to continue through the upper airways and into the alveoli via an aventilatory mass flow effect (Lyons 2019; Weingart 2012). This process is dependent on patency between the oropharyngeal airspaces and the lungs. During apnoea, alveolar capillary blood continues to absorb oxygen from the alveoli in the absence of lung movement, leading to a net subatmospheric alveolar pressure. This forms a pressure gradient to facilitate movement of additional administered oxygen into the alveoli (Weingart 2012). Preoxygenation by breathing pure oxygen prior to the period of apnoea assists this process, as this eliminates alveolar nitrogen. The persistence of alveolar nitrogen in the lungs during apnoea decreases the pressure gradient for oxygen transfer, as oxygen is absorbed from the alveoli (Kolettas 2014; Lyons 2019).

    Other proposed mechanisms that facilitate apnoeic oxygenation include cardiogenic oscillations, dead space flushing and positive airway pressure. Cardiogenic oscillations are measurable changes to airway pressure secondary changes in intrathoracic pressure from heart motion on the lung parenchyma or pulmonary blood flow (Lyons 2019; Mackenzie 1990; Rudlof 2010). The use of apnoeic oxygenation may also enhance flushing and washout of gases from anatomical dead spaces (Möller 2017). Apnoeic oxygenation, particularly via HFNC, may also produce a positive distension pressure within the bronchioles and alveoli, which may reduce the extent of atelectasis on induction of anaesthesia (Humphreys 2017).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Respiratory complications of endotracheal intubation have the potential to lead to significant adverse events including dysrhythmia, haemodynamic decompensation, hypoxic brain injury and death (Davis 2008; Mort 2004). Apnoeic oxygenation may serve as a non‐invasive adjunct to endotracheal intubation to decrease the incidence of hypoxaemia, morbidity and mortality.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of apnoeic oxygenation before intubation in adults in the prehospital, emergency department, intensive care unit and operating theatre environments compared to no apnoeic oxygenation during intubation.

    Primary objective

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of oxygenation compared with no oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation on length of hospital stay and peri‐intubation severe hypoxaemia, in adults in prehospital, ED, ICU and OT environments. Severe hypoxaemia was a reduction in saturation of pulse oximetry (SpO2) to less than 80%.

    Secondary objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of oxygenation compared with no oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation on the incidence of hypoxaemia (SpO2 below 93%), lowest recorded SpO2, ICU length of stay, first‐attempt intubation success rate and adverse event rate during intubation (e.g. cardiac arrhythmia), in adults in prehospital, ED, ICU and OT environments.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included all randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs that compared apnoeic oxygenation to no apnoeic oxygenation prior to endotracheal intubation. We defined quasi‐randomization as a controlled clinical trial where the method of allocation was known but was not considered strictly random; such as by alternation, case record number or date of birth. We excluded physiological modelling studies and case reports.

    Types of participants

    We included trials involving adults aged 18 years and older who required intubation in the prehospital, ED, ICU and OT environments.

    Types of interventions

    We included studies that compared the use of any form of apnoeic oxygenation (including HFNC (more than 15 L/minute) and LFNC (less than 15 L/minute)) versus no apnoeic oxygenation during intubation.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Hospital stay (days)

    • Incidence of severe hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 80%) from start of apnoea until successful intubation

    Secondary outcomes

    • Incidence of hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 93%) from start of apnoea until successful intubation

    • Lowest recorded SpO2 between the start of intubation and recovery from anaesthesia (%)

    • ICU stay (days)

    • First pass success rate (proportion of successful intubation on first laryngoscopy attempt)

    • Adverse events, including:

      • pharyngeal or dental trauma during intubation

      • cardiac arrhythmias and cardiac arrest during intubation

      • aspiration pneumonia (as defined by the study authors) within 24 hours of procedure and

      • other serious adverse events reported by study authors

    • Mortality (in‐hospital or 30‐day, as defined by study authors)

    We screened all included studies to identify relevant post hoc outcomes.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We performed a comprehensive search of electronic databases for articles using key words, synonyms and subject headings that relate to apnoeic oxygenation and intubation on 4 November 2022. We used controlled vocabulary specific to each database. We used the Cochrane Highly Sensitive Search Strategy for the identification of RCTs (Lefebvre 2011). Two review authors (CT and RV) independently performed screening. We did not impose restrictions on language or publication status.

    We searched the following databases from inception for published trials:

    • Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) (Issue 10 2022; Appendix 1);

    • MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 4 November 2022; Appendix 2);

    • Embase Ovid (1947 to 4 November 2022; Appendix 3);

    • Institute for Scientific Information Web of Science (1900 to 4 November 2022; Appendix 4); and

    • CINAHL EBSCO (1937 to 4 November 2022; Appendix 5).

    We also searched the following major clinical trial registers:

    • World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (apps.who.int/trialsearch/);

    • ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov/); and

    • ISRCTN registry (www.isrctn.com/).

    Searching other resources

    We performed a citation search of all included studies as well as any relevant systematic reviews on apnoeic oxygenation. We performed a search of Open Grey for grey literature, and Google Scholar for additional trials. Studies available as abstracts only were checked for availability as full‐length publications and, where unavailable, we contacted study authors to request any missing information.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methods and followed Cochrane guidelines, as outlined in the following sections.

    Selection of studies

    We used Covidence software to collate search results, remove duplicates, and record screening decisions and exclusions at each stage. Two review authors (CT and RV) independently screened the title, abstract, or both, of each reference identified by our search, to determine studies to be assessed further. Two review authors (CT and RV) then independently assessed the full text of any potentially relevant studies for eligibility or recorded reasons for exclusion. This was facilitated using the standardized digital screening, inclusion and exclusion tools. We resolved any disagreements by discussion until we achieved a consensus, or a third review author (LW) gave their judgement prior to proceeding at each stage.

    We recorded the process and present a PRISMA flow diagram (Moher 2009). For all full‐text articles screened and excluded, we recorded the reason for exclusion (see Excluded studies and Characteristics of excluded studies table).

    Data extraction and management

    Through Covidence, we used a standardized digital data‐extraction sheet. Two review authors (LW and TM) independently extracted information regarding trial design, population, setting, comparator, intervention, assessment of methodology, outcome data and any additional relevant information. We resolved any disagreements by discussion and consensus.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (LW and TM) independently assessed methodological quality using Cochrane's RoB 1 tool for assessing the risk of bias for RCTs (Higgins 2019a). There are seven specific measurements covered by this tool, and the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions describes how each domain should be assessed to reach a judgement of low, high or unclear risk of bias. If a study was judged at low risk in all domains, then it was deemed at low risk of bias overall. The criteria included:

    • random sequence generation;

    • allocation concealment;

    • blinding of participants and personnel;

    • blinding of outcome assessment;

    • incomplete outcome data;

    • selective reporting; and

    • other sources of bias.

    We resolved disagreements by discussion and consensus. To assess selective reporting, we screened the clinical trial databases ISRCTN (www.isrctn.com) and ClinicalTrials.gov for protocols of all included studies. We created a risk of bias summary, as well as risk of bias judgements for individual studies in the Characteristics of included studies table.

    Measures of treatment effect

    For dichotomous outcomes, we calculated risk ratios (RRs) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs). For continuous outcomes, we calculated mean differences (MDs) with 95% CIs. When appropriate, we calculated the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB), or the number needed to treat for an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) to aid clinical decision‐making.

    In circumstances where individual studies presented data as medians and ranges, if appropriate we estimated means and standard deviations (SD) utilising the methods described by Hozo 2005. When this was required, we performed a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of our estimates and highlighted it in the results to inform the integration of these estimates into the meta‐analysis.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The participant was the standard unit of analysis for our outcomes.

    One included study had three arms and we compared only the apnoeic oxygenation group with the control group, as the results in the third arm were not relevant to this review (Moon 2019).

    We did not identify any cluster‐randomized or cross‐over trials that were eligible for inclusion.

    The only analysis where participants were at risk of being counted twice was of adverse events; to avoid this, we analyzed data by type of adverse event and not as a composite outcome.

    There were no studies with multiple measured time points that required resolution.

    Our decisions were made in line with the recommendations in Chapter 23 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2019b).

    Dealing with missing data

    Where required, two review authors (LW and TM) contacted study authors or organizations to request missing data or clarification regarding unclear data. We outlined any missing data in the results and explained missing data in the discussion, and the extent to which the overall results may have been affected. There were no unreported SDs.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We investigated clinical heterogeneity via meta‐regression, identifying trends within subgroups of the clinical population (see Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity).

    We assessed statistical heterogeneity with a combination of visual assessment of forest plots and the Chi² test where there were sufficient studies included to do so. We used the I² test for each outcome (Higgins 2019a). We judged I² statistics as follows:

    • 0% to 40%: might not be important;

    • 30% to 60%: may represent moderate heterogeneity;

    • 50% to 90%: may represent substantial heterogeneity; and

    • 75% to 100%: considerable heterogeneity.

    The presence of heterogeneity served as a prompt to explore the reasons underlying it, as well as ensure the data included were correct and studies remained appropriate for inclusion. We elaborated in our discussion on how we managed results with heterogeneity and on factors that may have contributed to substantial or moderate heterogeneity.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    Where possible, if there were more than 10 included studies, we performed a visual assessment of a funnel plot to assess reporting bias.

    We attempted to minimise the impact of reporting bias, and assess its potential impact, by searching clinical trial databases for the results of registered trials that were completed but not yet published (i.e. www.isrctn.com and ClinicalTrials.gov). We also assessed trial protocols of studies included to assessed for any deviation between planned and reported primary and secondary trial outcomes.

    Data synthesis

    We analysed data using Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020), and RStudio (Version 4.0.2, R Core Team, Vienna, Austria, 2020; RStudio 2020) for meta‐regression using their 'meta' package (version 5.1‐1). We applied the fixed‐effect Mantel‐Haenszel model to analyses, preferring to present a fixed‐effect meta‐analysis alongside data about clinical and statistical heterogeneity. We expected a small number of studies, and sought a more equal weighting between studies rather than overweighting of large studies and underweighting of small ones that a random‐effects model can risk.

    If there was excessive clinical and significant heterogeneity (Chi² P value below 0.05) and the fixed‐effect model was inappropriate, we used the inverse variance method to apply a random‐effects model.

    We assumed statistical significance to correspond to an alpha of less than 0.05. We presented data for outcome measures in forest plots with 95% CIs reported.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Where there were sufficient data for each outcome, we performed subgroup analyses by meta‐regression to investigate heterogeneity. In particular, we explored the effect of setting of intubation (prehospital, ED, ICU and OT), since this is likely to be a cause of heterogeneity. Indications for intubation and the skill of the clinician differ significantly between each of these settings (Binks 2017a; Holyoak 2017).

    We also assessed whether there was a significant effect of flow rate, as a categorical (i.e. low flow (15 L/minute or less) versus high flow (more than 15 L/minute)) and continuous variable. The ability for oxygen to reach the alveoli is likely to be altered by flow rates and may impact the efficacy of apnoeic oxygenation (Holyoak 2017). In clinical practice, different equipment is used to deliver oxygen at less than 15 L/minute and greater than 15 L/minute and whether there is an effect between these two groups is relevant to clinical practice, in addition to any effect from flow rate as a continuous variable.

    Last, we assessed if there were particular participant factors that may have affected this outcome (e.g. obesity or people with respiratory failure). People with obesity and those with respiratory failure have significant alterations in their respiratory anatomy and physiology that are likely to alter the efficacy of apnoeic oxygenation (Ramachandran 2009). We defined obesity as a body mass index greater than 30 kg/m2.

    These factors were meta‐regressed in a random‐effects model using R (RStudio 2020). This model used a Knapp‐Hartung modification with residual restricted maximum likelihood to measure between‐study variance (Tau2) (Higgins 2004). We performed a meta‐regression in lieu of subgroup analysis to standardize methodology between covariate comparisons, rather than to alter the comparison based on the number of levels within the covariate under investigation (Appendix 6).

    Sensitivity analysis

    We performed a sensitivity analysis to explore the influence of risk of bias assessments (see Assessment of risk of bias in included studies). This allowed for assessment of the robustness of our conclusions. To perform the sensitivity analysis, we restricted the analysis to studies that were at low risk overall as defined in Assessment of risk of bias in included studies, and to the primary outcomes.

    We also performed a sensitivity analysis by excluding studies that estimated means and SDs from medians and ranges to assess the robustness of our estimates.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We presented the following outcomes in a summary of findings table: hospital stay, incidence of severe hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 80%), incidence of hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 93%), lowest recorded SpO2 (%), ICU stay (days), first pass success rate, adverse events and mortality.
    We used the eight GRADE considerations (study limitations, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, publication bias, magnitude of effect, possible confounders and dose–response gradient) to assess the certainty of the body of evidence, in accordance with guidance in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Guyatt 2008; Higgins 2019a). We used GRADEpro GDT software to perform this task (GRADEpro GDT). All decisions to upgrade or downgrade our assessment of the certainty of the evidence were described as footnotes to the summary of findings table.

    Six studies assessed mortality (Caputo 2017a; Frat 2019; Guitton 2019; Jaber 2016; Semler 2016; Vourch 2015).

Meta‐regression analyses were consistent with the overall results showing little to no difference in high‐flow and low‐flow apnoeic oxygenation (P = 0.344), setting of intubation (ED/ICU/OT) (P = 0.974) or non‐respiratory failure (P = 0.170).

    Discussion

    This review assessed the effect of oxygenation compared with no oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation on length of hospital stay and incidence of severe hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 80%), in adults in prehospital, ED, ICU and OT environments.

    Secondary outcomes included incidence of hypoxaemia (SpO2 less than 93%), lowest recorded SpO2, ICU length of stay, first‐attempt intubation success rate, adverse events and mortality.

    Summary of main results

    We included 23 RCTs with 2264 participants in the analyses for this systematic review.

    No studies assessed the effect on hospital length of stay.

    Apnoeic oxygenation at any flow rate may not alter the incidence of severe hypoxaemia from the start of apnoea until successful intubation. Meta‐regression analysis showed a difference between participants with and without pre‐existing respiratory failure. For people without respiratory failure there may be a decrease in incidence of severe hypoxaemia if apnoeic oxygenation is used. The CIs for this outcome were wide, and still included results that would be clinically relevant. The certainty of the evidence for this outcome was downgraded due to heterogeneity and risk of bias of included studies.

    Apnoeic oxygenation improved the lowest recorded oxygen saturation by an MD of 1.9%. There was significant heterogeneity in this result, which could not be accounted for by obesity, oxygen flow rates, setting of intubation or presence of pre‐existing respiratory failure. This result is unlikely to be clinically significant or affect patient outcomes. The certainty of evidence of the evidence was low; downgraded due to heterogeneity and risk of bias of included studies.

    Apnoeic oxygenation during intubation may decrease the duration of ICU stay (MD −1.13 days). The mechanism by which this may be mediated is unclear given the lack of demonstrable effect in severe hypoxaemia or adverse events. The certainty of the evidence for this outcome was downgraded due to heterogeneity and risk of bias of included studies.

    There may be no little or no difference in first attempt intubation success or the rate of adverse events. There was insufficient evidence of any effect on mortality; however, the CIs were wide and included potentially clinically significant effects.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Overall, the evidence gained from this review suggests that there may be minimal clinical benefit associated with treatment with oxygenation during the apnoeic period prior to intubation. The included RCTs allowed for analysis of low‐flow and high‐flow oxygenation, as well as oxygenation in specific subgroups of people (people with obesity and respiratory failure), and settings (ICU and OT). No studies investigated the effect of its use on the outcome duration of hospital stay.

    We note the evidence on meta‐regression analysis that participants who were intubated for a reason other than respiratory failure may derive benefit from oxygenation during the apnoeic period, by avoiding severe hypoxaemia. We believe this is physiologically plausible, as for oxygenation by diffusion to be effective a patent upper airway and adequate ventilation perfusion (V/Q) matching need to be present and V/Q mismatch is a common pathophysiological factor in most causes of respiratory failure.

    We also note the low‐certainty evidence that oxygenation in the apnoeic phase of intubation reduced the ICU length of stay. This is of significant interest and relevance to this intervention and its applicability to clinical practice. However, we believe this result should be interpreted with caution. There was no evidence of differences between groups we assessed by meta‐regression; with such modest effects of the intervention on other clinical surrogate endpoints it seems unclear how this reduction in ICU length of stay might have been mediated. Further studies could focus on determining any causative link between the use of oxygenation in the apnoeic period and reductions in ICU length of stay.

    There were insufficient data available to assess its effect in women undergoing obstetric procedures and intubated patients in the prehospital and ED environments. The outcome of severe hypoxaemia was predominantly assessed in critically ill people and, therefore, should not be applied to the OT environment.

    It is important to note there was no difference in adverse events with the use of the intervention.

    Applicability

    There is low‐certainty evidence to support the use of oxygenation during the apnoeic phase of intubation, particularly in people being intubated for reasons other than respiratory failure. However, the questionable clinical significance of modest effect sizes likely limits its integration into routine practice. This is important given that the costs of routine usage of apnoeic oxygenation are significant. None of the studies in the current review present any evidence of harm cause by the intervention.

    This systematic review did not examine the use of apnoeic oxygenation without subsequent intubation, for example in endoscopic sedation and thus these results cannot be applied to those settings.

    Certainty of the evidence

    The evidence presented in this review is of low certainty. Only two were deemed high quality RCTs (Jaber 2016; Moon 2019), with the other 21 were low‐quality RCTs.

    Seven studies provided insufficient information about the nature of sequence generation (Frat 2019; Ricottilli 2019; Rosen 2021; Sjoblom 2021; Tremey 2020; Wu 2020; Zodda 2018).

    Due to the nature of the intervention, 20 studies did not blind personnel and outcome assessors to the intervention. There was insufficient information provided in one study to determine whether blinding was adequate (Baraka 2007). However, the two high‐quality trials (i.e. overall low risk of bias) did demonstrate that with ingenuity, blinding of personnel and outcome assessors is possible (Jaber 2016; Moon 2019).

    This combined with other factors, particularly unexplained heterogeneity not addressed by factors analysed by meta‐regression, resulted in low to moderate confidence (GRADE) in the core outcomes presented.

    As such, additional studies are likely to change our confidence in the results presented.

    Potential biases in the review process

    As outlined in our methods and in line with the Cochrane's guidelines, we used a detailed search strategy with independent assessments of study eligibility, data extraction and risk of bias, which aimed towards minimising error and the introduction of bias into the review process.

    There was no potential for bias related to post hoc sensitivity analyses, as all analyses were prospectively determined during the protocol phase of the review.

    In the two outcomes where use of funnel plots was valid, both demonstrated significant skew. Whilst there are numerous potential reasons for funnel plot skew, we did not identify any methodological, true heterogeneity or artefactual reasons that might have explained the degree of small‐study effects that the funnel plots demonstrated. It remains possible there is a degree of publication bias present in the papers included in this review or the skew may have occurred by chance. We considered downgrading GRADE scores on the basis of this skew, but given we are not confident this is due to publication bias elected not to do so.

    We were unable to perform a sensitivity analysis based on risk of bias as only one study was deemed at low risk of bias. Therefore, it is difficult to interpret how this skew may have affected analyses, but it may indicate effects were overestimated.

    Three of the five review authors were involved in previous systematic reviews on similar, related topics. Only six of the 20 studies included in the present meta‐analysis were included in any of the previous reviews. Authors of the present review were not involved in any of the included or excluded clinical trials.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Oxygenation during the apnoeic phase preceding intubation has been the subject of numerous systematic reviews (Binks 2017a; Binks 2017b; Chaudhuri 2020; e Silva 2017; Pavlov 2017; Tan 2018; White 2017). The results presented in earlier reviews were largely derived from retrospective, non‐randomized trials (Binks 2017a; Binks 2017b; e Silva 2017; Tan 2018; Pavlov 2017; White 2017). The most recent systematic review included only 10 RCTs and included those preoxygenated with non‐invasive ventilation prior to intubation (Chaudhuri 2020). As such, the population was more likely to be dominated by very ill participants, and indeed most participants in the review were in ICU.

    With the exception of Binks 2017a and Holyoak 2017, all other reviews demonstrated no effect on the incidence of our critical outcome, severe hypoxaemia. The other meta‐analysis of RCTs by Chaudhuri 2020 assessed overlapping outcomes with our review, showing no difference in the incidence of adverse events, ICU length of stay and 28‐day mortality. Of the studies that measured lowest recorded SpO2, all showed slightly larger differences of 2.17% to 3.28% in favour of apnoeic oxygenation (Binks 2017a; Binks 2017b; Holyoak 2017; e Silva 2017; Tan 2018). There was a varying effect on first pass intubation success, with improvement in success rate being found in meta‐analyses with higher numbers of retrospective studies (Binks 2017a; e Silva 2017; Holyoak 2017; White 2017). This was potentially related to bias in the operators performing the intubation in these unblinded retrospective studies along with publication bias.

    Previous systematic reviews found varying effects on the incidence of hypoxaemia; however, the definition of hypoxaemia also varied between reviews with SpO2 parameters ranging from 90% to 95%. Similarly, only reviews with high proportions of retrospective cohort studies demonstrated an effect on incidence of hypoxaemia (Binks 2017b; e Silva 2017; Holyoak 2017; Tan 2018; White 2017). The other meta‐analysis of RCTs by Chaudhuri 2020 found no effect on severe hypoxaemia. No reviews demonstrated any effect of oxygenation during the apnoeic period on peri‐intubation adverse events or mortality (Binks 2017a; Binks 2017b; Chaudhuri 2020; e Silva 2017; Pavlov 2017; Tan 2018; White 2017).

    In summary, the findings of this systematic review with meta‐analysis are largely in keeping with those of previous reviews. The deviations in reported results can be explained by the exclusion of retrospective studies in our review and variation in outcome definitions.
