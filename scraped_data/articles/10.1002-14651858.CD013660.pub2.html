Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) for apnoea of prematurity

    • Background

    Apnoea of prematurity (AoP) is defined as a pause in breathing for 20 seconds or longer, or for less than 20 seconds when accompanied by bradycardia and hypoxaemia, in a preterm infant. An association between the severity of apnoea and neurodevelopmental delay has been reported. Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) is a form of non‐invasive ventilatory assistance that has been shown to be relatively safe and effective in preventing and treating respiratory distress among preterm infants. It is less clear whether CPAP treatment is safe and effective in the prevention and treatment of AoP.

    Objectives

    1. To assess the effects of CPAP on AoP in preterm infants (this may be compared to supportive care or mechanical ventilation).
    2. To assess the effects of different CPAP delivery systems on AoP in preterm infants.

    Search methods

    Searches were conducted in September 2022 in the following databases: Cochrane Library, MEDLINE, Embase, and CINAHL. We also searched clinical trial registries and the reference lists of studies selected for inclusion.

    Selection criteria

    We included all randomised and quasi‐randomised controlled trials (RCTs) in which researchers determined that CPAP was necessary for AoP in preterm infants (born before 37 weeks). Cross‐over studies were also included, provided sufficient data were available for analysis.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used the standard methods of Cochrane and Cochrane Neonatal, including independent assessment of risk of bias and extraction of data by at least two review authors. Discrepancies were resolved by involvement of a third author. We used the GRADE approach to assess the certainty of evidence for the following outcomes: 1) failed CPAP; 2) apnoea; 3) adverse effects of CPAP.

    Main results

    We included four single‐centre trials conducted in Malaysia, Spain, Germany, and North America, involving 138 infants with a mean/median gestation of 26 to 28 weeks. Two studies were parallel‐group RCTs and two were cross‐over trials. None of the studies compared CPAP with supportive care. All trials compared one form of CPAP with another. Two compared a variable flow device with ventilator CPAP, one compared two different variable flow devices, and one compared a variable flow device with bubble CPAP. Interventions were administered for periods ranging between six and 48 hours, with pressures between 4 and 6 cm H2O. We assessed all trials as having a high risk of bias for blinding of participants and personnel, and two studies for blinding of outcome assessors. We found a high risk of a carry‐over effect in two studies where the washout period was not adequately described, and a high risk of bias in a study that appeared to use an analysis method not generally accepted for cross‐over studies.

    Comparison 1. CPAP and supportive care compared to supportive care alone

    We did not identify any study for inclusion in this comparison.

    Comparison 2. CPAP delivered by different types of devices

    2a. Variable flow compared to ventilator CPAP

    Two studies were included in this comparison. We are very uncertain whether there is any difference in the incidence of failed CPAP, defined as the need for mechanical ventilation (risk ratio (RR) 0.16, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.01 to 2.90; 1 study, 26 participants; very low‐certainty). We are very uncertain whether there is any difference in the frequency of apnoea events (mean difference (MD) per four‐hour interval ‐0.10, 95% CI ‐1.30 to 1.10; 1 study, 26 participants; very low‐certainty). We are uncertain whether there is any difference in adverse events. Neurodevelopmental outcomes were not reported.

    2b. Variable flow compared to bubble CPAP

    We included one study in this comparison, but it did not report our pre‐specified outcomes.

    2c. Infant Flow variable flow CPAP compared to Medijet variable flow CPAP

    We are very uncertain whether there is any difference in the incidence of failed CPAP (RR 2.62, 95% CI 0.91 to 7.53; 1 study, 80 participants; very low‐certainty). The frequency of apnoea was not reported, and we do not know whether there is any difference in adverse events. Neurodevelopmental outcomes were not reported.

    Comparison 3. CPAP compared to mechanical ventilation

    We did not identify any studies for inclusion in this comparison.

    Authors' conclusions

    Due to the limited available evidence, we are very uncertain whether any CPAP device is more effective than other forms of supportive care, other CPAP devices, or mechanical ventilation for the prevention and treatment of AoP. The devices used in these studies included two types of variable flow CPAP device: bubble CPAP and ventilator CPAP. For each comparison, data were only available from a single study. There are theoretical reasons why these devices might have different effects on AoP, therefore further trials are indicated.

    • Continuous positive airway pressure for apnoea of prematurity

    Key messages

    We did not find any study that compared CPAP with supportive care for apnoea of prematurity.

    We found four studies comparing one type of CPAP to another. The studies were small and the care given by healthcare workers may have been influenced by knowing which treatment the baby was receiving. Therefore, we don’t know from these studies whether there is any difference between different types of CPAP for reducing apnoea in babies born too early.

    What is apnoea of prematurity?

    Babies born early (preterm) may experience prolonged pauses in their breathing. In a baby born too early, we call such pauses apnoea of prematurity. Apnoea may be due to immaturity of the mechanisms controlling breathing or to weakness of the muscles that keep their airway open during breathing. In severe apnoea the baby’s heart rate will slow and there will be a drop in the level of oxygen. If this happens too often it may affect the baby’s brain development. When babies have apnoea, supportive care is usually given. This may be in the form of stimulation, by stroking the baby or moving their limbs, providing oxygen, or both.

    What is CPAP?

    Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) is a form of breathing support, often referred to as a type of non‐invasive ventilation. The continuous pressure is applied usually through a nose piece, allowing the baby to continue to breathe by themselves but making breathing easier. This is probably because the positive pressure that is applied helps to keep the airway open.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out whether using CPAP in preterm babies might reduce apnoea or the need for a more invasive form of treatment such as mechanical ventilation, i.e. using a machine to do the breathing for the baby. We also wanted to know whether any particular method of applying CPAP was more effective.

    What did we do?

    We searched for studies that included preterm babies where the researchers thought CPAP was necessary for apnoea of prematurity. We included studies that compared CPAP with supportive care (such as stimulation, oxygen, or both) or studies that compared different types of CPAP.

    What did we find?

    We did not find any studies that compared CPAP with supportive care. We found four small studies, involving 138 babies. They compared two different forms of CPAP. There were three comparisons with only one study in each comparison and one study did not provide any useful data. The duration of CPAP in the four studies was between 4 and 48 hours. From these four studies, we do not know whether there is any difference between different forms of CPAP. This is because each comparison had only one study, the results were very imprecise because the studies were small, and the care given by the healthcare workers might have been influenced by their awareness of which treatment the baby was receiving.

    How up‐to‐date is the evidence?

    The evidence is up‐to‐date to 6 September 2022.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There are no randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) with other supportive methods such as kinaesthetic stimulation or oxygen therapy for apnoea of prematurity (AoP). As CPAP is already endorsed in clinical guidelines it is unlikely that any RCTs will be conducted to answer this question.

    Due to the limited available evidence from the four small studies included in this review, we are very uncertain whether any CPAP device is more effective than its comparator for the prevention and treatment of AoP. The devices included in these studies included two types of variable flow device, conventional bubble CPAP, and conventional ventilator CPAP, and for each comparison data were only available from a single study.

    Implications for research

    Although there are no trials comparing CPAP with kinaesthetic stimulation, oxygen therapy, or other supportive care, future trials are unlikely. This is because CPAP for AoP is endorsed in current clinical guidelines and is widely used. Nevertheless, further trials are indicated, particularly in low‐income settings where CPAP devices may not always be readily available.

    Since there are theoretical reasons why different CPAP devices might differ in their effects on AoP, further studies are justified in comparing different devices, particularly in extremely preterm infants who may be more likely to fail CPAP treatment. In low‐income settings, trials testing different technologies of low‐cost bubble CPAP against each other, as well as with variable flow devices, might be useful.

    We do not expect any further studies comparing CPAP with mechanical ventilation. We believe that this question is historical since mechanical ventilation is no longer standard management for apnoea of prematurity.

    The definition of AoP, although somewhat arbitrary, is now fairly widely accepted; future studies should therefore continue to use this definition, unless new further definitions become available.

    There are several ways of measuring apnoea. These include cardiorespiratory monitors, video cameras, nasal airflow devices, saturation monitors, impedance devices, or a combination of these. Since there is no standardised method, any method used in future trials needs to be applicable to clinical care and should be carefully described. In addition, the criteria for intervention, including the criteria for initiating CPAP and the criteria for initiating further treatment when CPAP fails, vary widely and therefore also need to be clearly and adequately described.

    Although blinding of the intervention is impractical, adherence to strict definitions for failed treatment would help reduce the impact of lack of blinding. Follow‐up studies looking at neurodevelopmental outcomes would be desirable. It would also be helpful if studies were done to determine the validity of methods used for the measurement of AoP, as well as the development of standard criteria for intervention.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Apnoea of prematurity (AoP) is widely defined as a pause in breathing for 20 seconds or longer, or for less than 20 seconds when accompanied by bradycardia and hypoxaemia, in a preterm infant (Eichenwald 2016). Incidences of apnoea are inversely related to gestational age, affecting almost all infants of 28 weeks’ gestation or less and typically resolving by 40 weeks' postmenstrual age (Henderson‐Smart 1981). An association between the severity of apnoea and neurodevelopmental delay has been reported (Janvier 2004; Pillekamp 2007).

    Traditionally, AoP has been classified into three groups: central, obstructive, and mixed apnoea. Central apnoea is caused by immaturity, or instability, of the respiratory feedback mechanism within the central nervous system (Eichenwald 2016). Obstructive apnoea is caused by closure of the upper airway, such as collapse or instability of pharyngeal tissue, nasal obstruction and, in some cases, neck flexion; the infant continues to breathe against the obstruction, thus obstructive apnoea is distinct from central apnoea due to the presence of chest motion. Mixed apnoea, the most common form, as its name suggests combines characteristics of both central and obstructive apnoea. Central apnoea can precede or follow obstructive apnoea.

    Description of the intervention

    Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) is a form of non‐invasive ventilatory assistance that is applied as constant pressure throughout the respiratory cycle, usually via the nostrils.

    CPAP has an established role in the management of respiratory distress in preterm infants (Ho 2020a; Ho 2020b; Subramaniam 2021), and it is used to facilitate weaning from mechanical ventilation (Davis 2003).

    Nasal CPAP can be applied via a binasal prong, a single nasopharyngeal prong, or a nasal mask. In the past, CPAP has been applied via face mask or by endotracheal tube (Davis 2001b; Rhodes 1973). Evidence suggests that the binasal prong is more effective than the single nasopharyngeal prong when used in weaning from mechanical ventilation (De Paoli 2008). Pressure, which is generated by a bubble circuit, a mechanical ventilator, or a CPAP driver, is generally set at a constant level, commonly between 4 and 8 cmH₂O (Bamat 2021; Eichenwald 2016). However, higher pressures have been used (Krouskop 1975).

    Infants at risk of AoP would usually be monitored with an apnoea or cardiorespiratory monitor, which might include oxygen saturation. For apnoeic episodes that are not clinically significant or frequent, simple interventions such as proper positioning and physical stimulation may be sufficient. In addition, oxygen therapy might be initiated. Blood transfusion is usually administered in the presence of anaemia. Methylxanthines might also be given to prevent or treat apnoea (Henderson‐Smart 2010a; Henderson‐Smart 2010b). They may be used prior to initiating CPAP, concurrently with CPAP, or as an additional treatment when CPAP fails (Eichenwald 2016). Methylxanthines (including theophylline, aminophylline, and caffeine) reduce apnoea and the need for mechanical ventilation. In addition, caffeine improves infant growth and long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes, and is less toxic (Henderson‐Smart 2010b).

    CPAP is typically considered after an episode of clinically significant apnoea or after frequent mild apnoeic episodes. The effectiveness of nasal intermittent positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV) compared to nasal CPAP for apnoea of prematurity has been studied. Trial results suggest that NIPPV may be more effective than nasal CPAP for preterm babies with severe or frequent apnoeic episodes (Lemyre 2017). If apnoea were deemed to be persistent, then endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation might be instituted.

    How the intervention might work

    In general, CPAP works by opening up collapsed airways, thus increasing functional residual capacity (FRC). This results in a decrease in ventilation perfusion (V/Q) mismatch, hence improving gas exchange within the lung (Rodriguez 2002).

    The mechanism of how CPAP prevents central apnoea might be described as stimulation of pulmonary stretch receptors in the lungs and sending of feedback to the respiratory centre of the brain, thus providing a respiratory drive (Hering‐Breuer reflex) (Speidel 1976). CPAP might also stabilise the chest wall, thus reducing inhibitory feedback from the chest wall reflexes during rapid eye movement (REM) sleep (Hagan 1977), and it may regularise respiration during periodic breathing (Kattwinkel 1975). CPAP might reduce the obstructive component of AoP by splinting the pharyngeal airway, thus improving the patency of the upper airway (Miller 1985).

    There are two main types of CPAP systems. The first consists of variable flow devices, which include flow drivers such as a specific nasal prong or mask. The second type, such as bubble CPAP systems and infant ventilators, consists of a continuous flow device (Courtney, 2007). Although all CPAP systems have their own specific properties, it is not clear whether there are any clinically important differences between them (De Paoli 2008). Nevertheless, they could vary in their effectiveness in preventing or treating AoP. For example, the bubbling mechanism in bubble CPAP affects a process described as facilitated diffusion caused by pressure oscillations from the bubbles (Pillow 2007). Animal studies suggest that measures of gas exchange are better when compared with ventilator CPAP (Huang 2008). Variable flow CPAP generates pressure at the airway proximal to the infant’s nares via a Bernoulli effect to maintain a constant pressure. During the expiratory phase in variable CPAP, the fluidic flip mechanism causes the flow to flip around to enable preterm babies to exhale with less effort (Klausner 1996), possibly making apnoea less likely. Variable flow CPAP also maintains a better uniform pressure level compared to continuous flow CPAP.

    CPAP may be less effective for premature babies of lower gestation and birth weight because these infants may have less mature respiratory systems and more compliant chest walls (Ammari 2005).

    When compared with invasive ventilatory methods, CPAP is generally free of adverse effects, the most common of which is trauma to the nares. This can range from mild oedema to septal necrosis (Fischer 2010). Pneumothorax has also been reported (Ho 2020b). The use of oxygen to treat AoP can result in prolonged hyperoxia, a potential cause of retinopathy of prematurity (van Zanten 2014).

    Why it is important to do this review

    CPAP has been shown in several reviews to be relatively safe and effective in preventing and treating respiratory distress among preterm infants, and in reducing the adverse effects associated with ventilation (Ho 2020a; Ho 2020b; Subramaniam 2021). In addition, CPAP has been compared with nasal intermittent positive pressure ventilation for the treatment of respiratory distress (Lemyre 2016; after extubation, Lemyre 2017), and for AoP (Lemyre 2017).

    However, it is important to determine whether CPAP is safe and effective for the prevention and treatment of AoP, and whether delivery by any particular CPAP device is superior.

    Objectives

    1. To assess the effects of CPAP on AoP in preterm infants (this may be compared to supportive care or mechanical ventilation).

    2. To assess the effects of different CPAP delivery systems on AoP in preterm infants.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised and quasi‐randomised controlled trials looking at treatment of AoP via CPAP. Cross‐over studies reporting short‐term outcomes such as apnoea frequency were also included, provided sufficient data were available for analysis.

    Types of participants

    We included trials of preterm infants (born before 37 weeks) in which the investigator had determined that CPAP is necessary for AoP. The criteria for determining whether CPAP is required were those defined by trial investigators.

    Types of interventions

    We included the following comparisons.

    1. CPAP and supportive care compared to supportive care alone.

    2. CPAP delivered by different types of devices (we compared one CPAP device versus any other, e.g. ventilator CPAP, bubble, or driver CPAP).

    3. CPAP compared to mechanical ventilation.

    Supportive care may include physical stimulation, the use of oxygen, or both. Blood transfusion may also be given in the presence of anaemia.

    For any of these comparisons, medication or another treatment for AoP could be used as a co‐intervention or as back‐up treatment for CPAP, provided it was used in both comparison groups.

    Trials comparing bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP) with conventional nasal CPAP were excluded because BiPAP is considered to be a form of nasal intermittent positive‐pressure ventilation (NIPPV), which is covered in another Cochrane Review (Lemyre 2017).

    Trials not including apnoea as an outcome were not included. Studies on CPAP used for respiratory distress were not included, even if apnoea was an outcome.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Failed CPAP: defined as any form of apnoea that is sufficiently frequent or severe as to require additional ventilator support, such as NIPPV or endotracheal intubation, or backup medical therapy such as a methylxanthine (for comparisons 1 and 2). The criteria used would be those defined by study authors (such as more than two apnoeic episodes within a four‐hour period requiring bag and mask ventilation)

    2. Apnoea

      1. Rates of apnoea and bradycardic events expressed as events per specified time period (apnoea with or without bradycardia or bradycardia alone)

      2. Differences in rates of apnoea (before and after treatment) expressed as events per hour (for all comparisons)

    Secondary outcomes

    • Cochrane Library (CENTRAL and CDSR) (2022, Issue 8), Wiley (6 September 2022);

    • Ovid MEDLINE(R) and Epub Ahead of Print, In‐Process, In‐Data‐Review & Other Non‐Indexed Citations, Daily and Versions 1946 to 2 September 2022;

    • CINAHL Complete (EbscoHost) (1975 to 06 September 2022).

    Searching other resources

    We searched the following trial registries in June and September 2022:

    • US National Library of Medicine (https://www.clinicaltrials.gov);

    • ISRCTN Registry (https://www.isrctn.com);

    • International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (https://trialsearch.who.int).

    Data collection and analysis

    We used the standard methods of Cochrane Neonatal.

    Selection of studies

    We identified and excluded duplicates. If we had found multiple reports of the same study we intended to collate them so that each study rather than each report was the unit of interest in the review. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram (Page 2021), along with Characteristics of included studies and Characteristics of excluded studies tables.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (EL and JWL) screened the title and abstract of all studies identified by the search strategy, and two review authors (JJL and IZG) independently assessed the full articles for all potentially relevant trials. We excluded studies that did not meet all inclusion criteria, and we have stated the reason for exclusion. We discussed any disagreements until consensus was reached. Two review authors independently extracted the data from the included studies (JJL and IZG). We used a standard data collection form, adapted for the purpose of collecting all specified outcome measures. We resolved discrepancies through discussion with a third review author (JJH).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (JJL and JWL) independently assessed the risk of bias (low, high, or unclear) of all included trials using the Cochrane risk of bias tool for the following domains (Higgins 2017).

    1. Sequence generation (selection bias).

    2. Allocation concealment (selection bias).

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias).

    4. Blinding of outcome assessors (detection bias).

    5. Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias).

    6. Selective reporting (reporting bias).

    7. Any other bias.

    We resolved any disagreements by discussion or by consultation with a third review author (JJH). See Appendix 6 for a more detailed description of risk of bias assessment for each of these domains.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We analysed treatment effects in individual trials using Review Manager 5.4 (Review Manager 2020), and we reported risk ratio (RR) and risk difference (RD) for dichotomous data, as well as mean difference (MD) for continuous data, with respective 95% confidence intervals (CIs). We would have determined the number needed to treat for an additional beneficial outcome (NNTB) or an additional harmful outcome (NNTH) if we had encountered analyses with a statistically significant difference in RD. If studies had reported rates of apnoea per infant per unit of time, we would have reported these as rate ratios.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis we used was the participating preterm infant in individually randomised trials and cross‐over trials. We considered each infant only once in an analysis. We included cross‐over studies if they measured only short‐term outcomes, such as apnoea frequency, and provided sufficient data for analysis. Significant effects on respiratory rate and oxygenation have been found to continue for up to six hours after discontinuation of CPAP (Abdel‐Hady 1998). Therefore, to avoid a carry‐over effect, we had planned to exclude studies with a washout period of less than six hours. If the washout period was unclear, we included these studies and judged them to be at high risk of bias for a carry‐over effect. We did not find any cluster‐randomised controlled trials (RCTs), but if we did encounter any, we would have followed the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2019).

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted the authors of two studies with unclear methods (Kattwinkel 1975; Telenko 1999), and in both instances the authors were not able to provide additional information.

    When data were missing and could not be derived, we approached analysis of missing data as follows.

    1. We would have contacted the original study investigators to request missing data when more than 20% of data were missing (Guyatt 2017).

    2. We would have standardised events reported to a commonly used period for mean apnoea rate.

    3. If different time periods were used for mean apnoea rate, we would have contacted the study authors and requested data for events per hour, along with standard deviation (SD).

    4. If this was not possible, we would have considered converting apnoea rates to events per hour by borrowing an SD from another similar study if appropriate.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    If we had been able to perform a meta‐analysis we would have assessed clinical heterogeneity by visually inspecting forest plots and statistical heterogeneity by using the I² statistic. We would have calculated the I² statistic for each RR analysis to quantify inconsistency across studies and to describe the percentage of variability in effect estimates that may be due to heterogeneity rather than to sampling error. We would have defined heterogeneity as follows: I² statistic less than 25%: none; I² statistic 25% to 49%: low; I² statistic: 50% to 74%: moderate; I² statistic 75% or greater: high (Higgins 2019). If we had detected 'high' levels of heterogeneity (I² statistic of 75% or greater), we would explore possible causes (e.g. differences in study design, participants, interventions, or completeness of outcome assessments).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We would have assessed publication bias by visually inspecting funnel plot asymmetry if our meta‐analyses had included at least 10 studies. If we had found significant asymmetry in the funnel plot, we would have reported this in the corresponding results.

    Data synthesis

    We used the statistical package, Review Manager 5 (Review Manager 2020). We would have used fixed‐effect meta‐analysis for combining data when it was reasonable to assume that studies were estimating the same underlying treatment effect (i.e. when trials were examining the same intervention, reported outcomes were sufficiently similar, and trial populations and methods were judged sufficiently similar). If rates were reported, we would have used generic inverse variance meta‐analysis to analyse rates of apnoea.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    If there were enough studies, we would have undertaken subgroup analyses according to:

    1. birth weight group: > 1500 g, 1001 g to 1500 g, and 1000 g or below;

    2. gestation group: 34 to 37, 28 to 33, or < 28 weeks' gestation;

    3. no apnoea medication versus concomitant medication;

    4. different CPAP interfaces (such as nasal mask, binasal prong, single nasal prong) (for comparisons 1 and 2); and

    5. different definitions of failed treatment (more severe apnoea versus less severe apnoea or backup treatment ventilation versus medication).

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to undertake sensitivity analyses to determine whether review findings were affected by including only studies using adequate methods (low risk of bias), defined as adequate randomisation and allocation concealment, blinding of intervention and outcome measurement, and less than 10% loss to follow‐up.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We used the GRADE approach, as outlined in the GRADE Handbook (Schunemann 2013), to assess the certainty of evidence for the following (clinically relevant) outcomes for each comparison.

    1. Failed CPAP: defined as apnoea that is sufficiently frequent or severe as to require additional ventilator support, including endotracheal intubation.

    2. Apnoea.

      1. Rates of apnoea and bradycardia expressed as events per hour.

      2. Differences in rates of apnoea (before and after treatment) expressed as events per hour.

    3. Adverse effects of CPAP (pneumothorax, gastrointestinal effects).

    4. Long‐term neurodevelopmental outcomes (neurodevelopment measured on a validated scale that measures cognitive, motor, or behavioural function, or blindness, deafness, or cerebral palsy, at about two years of age).

    Two review authors (JJH and IZG) independently assessed the certainty of evidence for each of the outcomes above. We considered evidence from RCTs as high certainty and downgraded the evidence by one level for serious (or two levels for very serious) limitations based upon the following: design (risk of bias), consistency across studies, directness of evidence, precision of estimates, and presence of publication bias. We used GRADEpro GDT to create summary of findings Table 1, summary of findings Table 2, summary of findings Table 3 and summary of findings Table 4 to report the certainty of evidence (GRADEpro GDT).

    The GRADE approach results in an assessment of the certainty of a body of evidence as one of four grades.

    1. High certainty: further research is very unlikely to change our confidence in the estimate of effect.

    2. Moderate certainty: further research is likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and may change the estimate.

    3. Low certainty: further research is very likely to have an important impact on our confidence in the estimate of effect and is likely to change the estimate.

    4. Very low certainty: we are very uncertain about the estimate.

    We excluded 865 records based on title/abstract and reviewed 16 full texts. We excluded 11 studies (Characteristics of excluded studies), and categorised one as 'awaiting classification' (Characteristics of studies awaiting classification). We included four studies in this review (Characteristics of included studies). See Figure 1.

    Included studies

    We included four studies including a total of 138 infants. All four studies compared two different forms of CPAP (Chua 2005; Gutierrez 2003; Pantalitschka 2009; Telenko 1999). Chua 2005 and Gutierrez 2003 were both two‐arm, parallel trials. Pantalitschka 2009 was a four‐period cross‐over study, but only two of the four interventions were relevant to this review. Each period was six hours. Since the total duration of the intervention was 24 hours there appears to be no washout period between each period. The two excluded interventions were both different forms of NIPPV. Telenko 1999 was a three‐period, two‐intervention, double cross‐over study with each period being six hours. Any washout period used was not stated. The study was reported only in abstract form. There were no usable outcome data in the abstract report. Dr Telenko was contacted but was not able to supply any further study information. Two studies were stopped early due to slow recruitment (Chua 2005; Pantalitschka 2009).

    The studies were set in Malaysia (Chua 2005), Spain (Gutierrez 2003), Germany (Pantalitschka 2009), and North America (Telenko 1999). One study was not funded (Chua 2005), and the remaining three studies did not report funding.

    Participants

    The total number of participants was 138 of which 122 had usable data, ranging from 16 to 80 in each study. The mean or median birth weight ranged from 900 g to 1100 g (range 480 g to 1540 g). The participant mean or median gestation was 26 to 28 weeks. The mean or median age at initiation of the intervention was between nine days and three weeks. One study did not report participants' age (Telenko 1999). Three studies excluded infants with risk factors for apnoea such as infection, electrolyte disturbance, and intraventricular haemorrhage. One study included infants with apnoea due to secondary causes (Gutierrez 2003).

    Interventions

    No study compared CPAP with supportive care, such as oxygen therapy.

    All four studies compared one form of CPAP with another. Chua 2005 and Telenko 1999 compared a variable flow device (Infant Flow (Electromedical Equipment) in Chua, and Aladdin Infant Flow (Hamilton Medical) in Telenko) with conventional ventilator CPAP. Pantalitschka 2009 compared underwater bubble CPAP with variable flow CPAP (Infant Flow (Electromedical Equipment)). Gutierrez 2003 compared two forms of variable flow devices (Infant Flow (Electromedical Equipment) with Medijet (Medin)). The CPAP pressures used were between 4 and 6 cm H2O.

    The duration of the intervention in Chua 2005 was 48 hours, six hours in Telenko 1999, six hours in Pantalitschka 2009, and 24 hours in Gutierrez 2003.

    For three studies all infants had received methylxanthines (one study each for aminophylline, caffeine, and theophylline), as prophylaxis prior to the initiation of the intervention. One study did not report use of methylxanthines (Telenko 1999).

    Outcomes

    Two studies reported our primary outcome, failed CPAP (Chua 2005; Gutierrez 2003). In both, the definition for failure was the need for intubation. One study did not state the criteria for intubation and one study stated that the decision was made by clinical judgement of the attending doctor.

    The other primary outcome, apnoea, was reported in all four studies. The definitions used included cessation of breathing for 20 seconds in three studies and a reduction in respiratory amplitude of 20% for more than 10 seconds in the fourth study. Definitions of desaturation were a fall to below 80% (two studies), 85%, or 88%. Bradycardia was defined as a fall in heart rate to below 80 or 100 beats per minute (two studies), a reduction of 30% below baseline in one study, and was not defined in one study.

    Three studies used data from the monitor memory to calculate apnoea rates. One study did not report how the data were collected. Rates of apnoea were reported in two studies (Chua 2005; Pantalitschka 2009), and one study reported the outcome as resolution of apnoea within 24 hours (Gutierrez 2003).

    The secondary outcomes reported were rate of apnoea with bradycardia (Chua 2005), bronchopulmonary dysplasia (oxygen dependency at 36 weeks) (Chua 2005), mortality (Chua 2005), pneumothorax (Chua 2005; Gutierrez 2003; Pantalitschka 2009), and gastrointestinal complications (abdominal distension, abdominal distension requiring cessation of feeds, vomiting) (Chua 2005; Gutierrez 2003).

    No study reported long‐term outcomes.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded 11 studies (Campbell 2006; Claassen 2019; Davis 1998; Davis 2001a; Gross 2020; Heiring 2015; Ishihara 2016; Kattwinkel 1975; NCT00187434; Yu 2016; Zaharie 2008). Four studies included CPAP for weaning from mechanical ventilation (Claassen 2019; Davis 1998; Davis 2001a; NCT00187434). Although some measured AoP as an outcome, this was part of the definition of the outcome failed extubation. Two studies compared CPAP with nasal IPPV including bilevel CPAP (Gross 2020; Ishihara 2016). One study compared CPAP with high flow nasal cannula (Campbell 2006). One study compared methylxanthine and placebo for AoP and both groups received CPAP (Yu 2016). One study compared prophylactic and treatment CPAP for respiratory distress syndrome (Zaharie 2008), and one study compared continuing CPAP versus weaning at 28 days of life (Heiring 2015). Kattwinkel 1975 compared apnoea rates before and after either kinetic stimulation or nasal CPAP. Dr Kattwinkel could not confirm that the patients were randomised to these two interventions.

    One further study is awaiting classification (Pantalitskcha 2006). We did not find any ongoing studies.

    Allocation

    Although all the studies were described as randomised, only one study clearly described adequate random sequence generation (Pantalitschka 2009), and only one study clearly described adequate allocation concealment (Chua 2005). We judged the remaining studies to be unclear.

    Blinding

    All studies were not blinded to caregivers, and we judged them to be at high risk of bias for blinding of the intervention. Pantalitschka 2009 described blinding of the outcome assessors whereas for Chua 2005 and Gutierrez 2003, both studies that used analysis of monitor recordings for the primary outcome, apnoea, it was unclear whether the outcome assessor was blinded to the intervention. Telenko 1999 did not describe blinding in the abstract report.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Three studies described complete outcome data (Chua 2005; Gutierrez 2003; Pantalitschka 2009), whereas the abstract report by Telenko 1999 did not give any information.

    Selective reporting

    One study was prospectively registered, and all prospectively registered outcomes were reported (Pantalitschka 2009). Chua 2005 was not prospectively registered, but all expected outcomes were reported, so we judged this to be at low risk of reporting bias. Gutierrez 2003 reported the primary outcome, apnoea, as resolution at 24 hours. We judged this study to be at unclear risk of reporting bias. Telenko 1999 did not give sufficient information in the abstract report to make a judgement.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Two studies, both cross‐over studies, did not adequately describe the washout period, and we judged both of these to be at high risk of bias for a carry‐over effect (Pantalitschka 2009; Telenko 1999). In addition, Pantalitschka 2009 appeared to use an analysis method not generally accepted for cross‐over studies.

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    There is no information from randomised controlled trials (RCTs) on the effect of continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) compared with supportive care on apnoea of prematurity (AoP). All four of the small studies we identified compared CPAP delivered by a variable flow device with another device (conventional ventilator CPAP (two studies), bubble CPAP (one study), and two different variable flow devices (one study)). From these four studies we are very uncertain whether any device is more effective than its comparator. There are no studies comparing CPAP with mechanical ventilation.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    We identified three of the four included studies by database searching and one study from other sources. This was an abstract of a thesis, the full text of which we obtained. One study had no funding and three studies did not state the source of funding, so we do not know whether there were any industry‐funded studies included. However, we have no reason to believe that if industry funding was involved, the funders had any influence on the results. We do not expect there to be other unpublished industry‐funded trials that are not available to us.

    Given that one study, a thesis, was identified from other sources, there is a possibility that there may be other unidentified theses. Therefore, although we carried out a comprehensive search, which, in addition to database searching, included discussion with experts in the field and examining the reference lists of included and excluded studies, as well as other related systematic reviews, there is a possibility that other unidentified studies, particularly theses, exist.

    The included studies were all more than 10 years old, suggesting that this research question is no longer current. Clinical practice guidelines recommend CPAP for apnoea of prematurity (Eichenwald 2016; NICE 2019), and therefore ethics boards may be unwilling to approve further studies, particularly comparing CPAP with supportive care. Nevertheless, this review shows that the evidence from randomised controlled trials is lacking.

    The devices included in these studies included two types of variable flow devices, conventional bubble CPAP and conventional ventilator CPAP. For each comparison, data were only available from a single study. There are theoretical reasons why these devices might have different effects on AoP, therefore this review shows that evidence is still lacking on whether any device is more effective than any other. There were no studies for two of our comparisons: CPAP compared with supportive care and CPAP compared with mechanical ventilation.

    A number of outcomes could not be reported. We received correspondence from two authors and neither could provide any further information.

    We did not attempt to look at the effect of different levels of CPAP pressure on AoP. Another Cochrane Review has looked at different CPAP levels and included apnoea as a secondary outcome, but none of the included studies reported this outcome (Bamat 2021).

    Quality of the evidence

    We judged the evidence for all the available outcomes as very low‐certainty. The reasons for downgrading included risk of bias (lack of blinding and in some comparisons cross‐over studies with potential carry‐over effect), imprecision due to data being taken from a single small study and wide confidence intervals incorporating both a benefit and a harm. We did not find any evidence of publication bias.

    Potential biases in the review process

    One potential bias in the review process was that one review author (JJH) was involved in one of the included studies, as the supervisor of the study author's thesis (Chua 2005). JJH was not involved in the data extraction for this study.

    In addition, another bias could have arisen when we excluded one study because we could not confirm whether it was an RCT. This was the only study that would have provided information for Comparison 1, CPAP versus supportive care (Kattwinkel 1975).

    In one study we found a discrepancy between the results reported in the English abstract and the main text, which was in Spanish. We used the data from the main text. Some of the results were reported as percentages, so we derived absolute numbers from these (Gutierrez 2003).

    We also included one cross‐over study that may have had no washout period. We had planned to exclude cross‐over studies with washout periods of less than six hours. However, rather than exclude the study we judged it to be at high risk of bias (Pantalitschka 2009).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We could not find any other systematic reviews assessing the effect of CPAP on AoP. A Cochrane Review evaluating nasal CPAP for extubation included AoP as part of their primary outcome, extubation failure, but did not report apnoea as a separate outcome; in addition, none of the included studies in this review reported apnoea as a separate outcome (Davis 2003). Two systematic reviews have compared nasal intermittent positive pressure (NIPPV) with nasal CPAP for AoP. A Cochrane Review included two small RCTs and the authors were not able to draw any conclusions from these about the effects of the two interventions on AoP (Lemyre 2017). A further systematic review included 18 interventional studies and found no difference in the frequency of apnoea, although a post hoc analysis of 10 studies that reported the incidence of any apnoea resulted in a reduction of this with NIPPV (Sabasi 2021)
