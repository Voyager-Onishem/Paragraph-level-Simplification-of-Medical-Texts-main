Surgical interventions for bilateral congenital cataract in children aged two years and under

    • Background

    Congenital cataracts are lens opacities in one or both eyes of babies or children present at birth. These may cause a reduction in vision severe enough to require surgery. Cataracts are proportionally the most treatable cause of visual loss in childhood, and are a particular problem in low‐income countries, where early intervention may not be possible. Paediatric cataracts provide different challenges to those in adults. Intense inflammation, amblyopia (vision is obstructed by cataract from birth which prevents normal development of the visual system), posterior capsule opacification and uncertainty about the final trajectory of ocular growth parameters can affect results of treatment. Two options currently considered for children under 2 years of age with bilateral congenital cataracts are: (i) intraocular lens (IOL) implantation; or (ii) leaving a child with primary aphakia (no lens in the eye), necessitating the need for contact lenses or aphakic glasses. Other important considerations regarding surgery include the prevention of visual axis opacification (VAO), glaucoma and the route used to perform lensectomy.

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness of infant cataract surgery or lensectomy to no surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts in children aged 2 years and under.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; which contains the Cochrane Eyes and Vision Trials Register; 2022, Issue 1); Ovid MEDLINE; Ovid Embase; the ISRCTN registry; ClinicalTrials.gov and the WHO ICTRP. The date of the search was 25 January 2022.

    Selection criteria

    We included all randomised controlled trials (RCTs) that compared infant cataract surgery or lensectomy to no surgery, in children with bilateral congenital cataracts aged 2 years and younger. This update (of a review published in 2001 and updated in 2006) does not include children over 2 years of age because they have a wider variety of aetiologies, and are therefore managed differently, and have contrasting outcomes.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methods expected by Cochrane. Two review authors extracted data independently. We assessed the risk of bias of included studies using RoB 1 and assessed the certainty of the evidence using GRADE.

    Main results

    We identified three RCTs that met our inclusion criteria with each trial comparing a different aspect of surgical intervention for this condition. The trials included a total of 79 participants under 2 years of age, were conducted in India and follow‐up ranged from 1 to 5 years. Study participants and outcome assessors were not masked in these trials.

    One study (60 children) compared primary IOL implantation with primary aphakia. The results from this study suggest that there may be little or no difference in visual acuity at 5 years comparing children with pseudophakia (mean logMAR 0.50) and aphakia (mean logMAR 0.59) (mean difference (MD) ‐0.09 logMAR, 95% confidence intervals (CIs) ‐0.24 to 0.06; 54 participants; very low‐certainty evidence), but the evidence is very uncertain. The evidence is very uncertain as to the effect of IOL implantation compared with aphakia on visual axis opacification (VAO) (risk ratio (RR) 1.29, 95% CI 0.23 to 7.13; 54 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). The trial investigators did not report on the cases of amblyopia. There was little evidence of a difference betwen the two groups in cases of glaucoma at 5 years follow‐up (RR 0.86, 95% CI 0.24 to 3.10; 54 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). Cases of retinal detachment and reoperation rates were not reported. The impact of IOL implantation on adverse effects is very uncertain because of the sparse data available: of the children who were pseudophakic, 1/29  needed a trabeculectomy and 8/29 developed posterior synechiae. In comparison, no trabeculectomies were needed in the aphakic group and 2/25 children had posterior synechiae (54 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). 

    The second study (14 eyes of 7 children under 2 years of age) compared posterior optic capture of IOL without vitrectomy versus endocapsular implantations with anterior vitrectomy (commonly called 'in‐the‐bag surgery'). The authors did not report on visual acuity, amblyopia, glaucoma and reoperation rate. They had no cases of VAO in either group. The evidence is very uncertain as to the effect of in‐the‐bag implantation in children aged under 1 year. There was a higher incidence of inflammatory sequelae: 4/7 in‐the‐bag implantation eyes and 1/7 in optic capture eyes (P = 0.04, 7 participants; very low‐certainty evidence). We graded the certainty of evidence as low or very low for imprecision in all outcomes because their statistical analysis reported that a sample size of 13 was needed in each group to achieve a power of 80%, whereas their subset of children under the age of 1 year had only 7 eyes in each group.

    The third study (24 eyes of 12 children) compared a transcorneal versus pars plana route using a 25‐gauge transconjunctival sutureless vitrectomy system. The evidence is very uncertain as to the effect of the route chosen on the incidence of VAO, with no cases reported at 1 year follow‐up in either group. The investigators did not report on visual acuity, amblyopia, glaucoma, retinal detachment and reoperation rate. The pars plana route had the adverse effects of posterior capsule rupture in 2/12 eyes, and 1/12 eyes needing sutures. Conversely, 1/12 eyes operated on by the transcorneal route needed sutures. We graded the outcomes with very low‐certainty because of the small sample size and the absence of a priori sample size calculation.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is no high level evidence for the effectiveness of one type of surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts over another, or whether surgery itself is better than primary aphakia. Further RCTs are required to inform modern practice about concerns, including the timing of surgery, age at which surgery should be undertaken, age for implantation of an IOL and development of complications, such as reoperations, glaucoma and retinal detachment. Standardising the methods used to measure visual function, along with objective monitoring of compliance with the use of aphakic glasses/contact lenses would greatly improve the quality of study data and enable more reliable interpretation of outcomes.

    • Which type of surgery is most effective for cataracts that are present in both eyes at or soon after birth in children up to the age of 2 years?

    Key messages
    We did not find enough high‐certainty evidence to decide which form of surgery was best for cataracts in both eyes, at or soon after birth (bilateral congenital cataracts). The three trials that we found all performed different types of surgery and therefore we could not compare them to one another.

    Future trials should investigate the best age to implant an artificial lens, complications caused by different methods of surgery, and whether newer types of surgery, such as ‘bag‐in‐the‐lens’ surgery, are better than traditional methods.

    What is a cataract?
    A cataract is when the normally clear lens inside the eye is cloudy and obscures vision. Some children develop cataracts in both eyes in the womb or soon after birth; these are called bilateral congenital cataracts. They are a major worldwide cause of childhood blindness, especially in low‐income countries.

    How is a cataract treated?

    Cataracts can affect visual development, so surgery is often recommended to remove the cloudy lens. Removing the cataract means that the eye cannot focus. This must be corrected by either replacing the affected lens with an artificial one, known as an intraocular lens, a contact lens or spectacles, or a combination of these. There is no worldwide agreement on which of these is best for children under 2 years of age.

    Surgery can lead to complications, such as reoperations, retinal detachment (when light‐sensitive tissue at the back of the eye, called the retina, pulls away from its normal position), and glaucoma. Glaucoma is a condition where the pressure in the eye gets too high. This can damage the optic nerve and cause blindness if untreated. Glaucoma is a lifelong risk after cataract surgery for children under 2 years of age, and can develop, whether or not an intraocular lens is implanted.

    The centre of the eye may become cloudy again after intraocular lens implantation. This causes blurred vision and requires laser treatment or further surgery. Different techniques to implant the intraocular lens can reduce reclouding. However, reclouding is a common complication when the cataract is removed, whether or not it’s replaced with an intraocular lens.

    Surgeons access the cataract from in front (anterior route) or behind (posterior route) the lens. The two routes have different rates of complications.

    What did we want to find out?
    We wanted to find out which surgical approach improves vision the most, or causes the fewest complications, or both, in children aged 2 years and under.

    What did we do? 
    We searched for trials that looked at different surgical approaches in children aged 2 years and under. We compared and summarised the results of the trials and rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and sizes.

    What did we find? 
    We found three trials that involved 90 children with bilateral congenital cataracts; of which 79 children were under the age of 2 years. The childen were followed up for 1 to 5 years after surgery. All three trials were conducted in India and compared different interventions.

    One study (60 children) compared implanting an intraocular lens during surgery with leaving the eye without a lens and managing the child's vision with a combination of spectacles and contact lenses. It reported no difference in the resulting vision or postoperative complications between the two groups. 

    One study (7 children under 2 years old) compared two surgical techniques for inserting an intraocular lens that can reduce the rate of reclouding of vision. They found no significant difference in their effectiveness, but they studied very few children.

    One study (12 children) used a smaller instrument to remove the cataract and compared two routes of removal: an anterior  approach (in front of the lens) and a posterior approach (behind the lens). The study reported no reclouding using either route and no significant difference in complication rates. 

    What are the limitations of the evidence?
    Our confidence in the evidence is very low because we found only a few trials, and they differed from each other. The three trials we included used different methods and measured different outcomes. The trials had a small number of participants and were all based in India. This means their results may not be applicable globally. 

    Study limitations include that one study (60 children) reported low compliance with the use of contact lenses after surgery. The other 2 studies (12 and 7 children respectively) did not specify in their methods all the outcomes they were going to measure, which means that their reporting may have been biased. Participants and outcome assessors were also not masked in these studies.

    How up to date is this evidence?
    This review updates our previous review. The evidence is up to date to 25 January 2022.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    Clear guidance regarding the most suitable surgical technique cannot be provided because we did not find any high‐certainty evidence in this review. The included trials highlight some of the difficulties in surgery for congenital cataract, particularly the significant postoperative complication of VAO.  The appropriate choice of surgery will depend on available resources, training, equipment and anaesthetic support for young children. Even allowing for that, there is little evidence regarding the best age for surgery, and whether IOL implantation is appropriate. 

    Implications for research

    This review has highlighted many gaps in the evidence surrounding the management of bilateral congenital cataracts in our population of interest: infants under the age of 2 years old. RCTs such as IATS have added to the evidence base in this age bracket, but only for unilateral cataract surgery, the results of which cannot be extrapolated to bilateral cataracts. An important clinical question this review looked to answer was whether primary IOL implantation was better than primary aphakia. We were unable to come to a conclusion on this because there is only uncertain evidence for the effectiveness of one type of surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts over another, or whether surgery itself is better than primary aphakia. 

     In particular, high‐level evidence is needed to address: 

    • the age(s) at which surgery should be undertaken, for maximum benefit and minimum risk;

    • when different surgical techniques may be indicated;

    • how surgery for the second eye should be managed;

    • appropriate age and circumstances for primary IOL implantation and its role in low‐resource countries;

    • methods for accurately calculating IOL power;

    • the threshold of vision deficit most likely to significantly benefit from surgery;

    • at what age(s) and in what situations anterior vitrectomy is indicated;

    • pathogenesis of secondary glaucoma;

    • risk factors for retinal detachment.

    Further RCTs are required to inform modern practice about concerns, including the timing of surgery, age at which surgery should be undertaken, age for implantation of an IOL and development of complications such as reoperations, glaucoma and retinal detachment. Standardising the methods used to measure visual function, along with objective monitoring of compliance with the use of aphakic glasses/contact lenses would greatly improve the quality of study data and enable more reliable interpretation of outcomes.

    Comparing outcomes is often confounded by the variation in, and unknown validity of methods to measure visual function; standardisation of this and objective monitoring of compliance with treatment would greatly improve the quality of study data and enable more reliable interpretation of outcomes.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Introduction

    Congenital cataracts are opacities of the lens of one or both eyes that are present at birth. They are a major cause of childhood blindness in the world (Taylor 1994). The rate of blindness from congenital cataract is much greater in low‐resource countries, probably because earlier intervention is possible in industrialised economies (Foster 1997).

    Obstruction of a child's vision by cataract from birth prevents normal development of the visual system; this is called amblyopia (Drews‐Botsch 2017). The outcome of treatment depends not only on the type of operation used to remove the cataract, but also on how soon after birth the cataract is detected and treated, and on the postoperative management of visual rehabilitation. It is generally accepted that the earlier the cataract surgery is carried out, the greater the likelihood of a good visual result (Birch 1996). On the other hand, earlier surgery is associated with more complications (Lloyd 2017).

    This review was concerned with surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts, which have a much greater impact on childhood blindness than unilateral cataracts, and have increased associated ocular anomalies, including microphthalmos, microcornea and anterior segment developmental anomalies. Unilateral cataracts are less common but present a difficult and different challenge because they are more likely to be associated with persistent foetal vasculature (Rahi 2000), and the likelihood of successful amblyopia treatment postoperatively is low (Neumann 1993).

    In this update of the review we changed the inclusion criteria to only include children 2 years old or under. This is because children older than 2 years have a wider variety of aetiologies, and are therefore managed differently, and have contrasting outcomes (Solebo 2020). 

    Epidemiology

    The reported incidence of congenital cataracts varies depending on both diagnostic criteria and geographical location. While incidence studies have been conducted in the UK, North America, Denmark, Sweden and Australia, none have been found based in low‐ to middle‐income countries (Sheeladevi 2016). The adjusted annual age‐specific incidence of new diagnosis of congenital and infantile cataract in the UK is 2.49/10,000 children (95% confidence interval (CI) 2.10 to 2.87) and at 5 years is 3.18/10,000 (95% CI 2.76 to 3.59), increasing to 3.46/10,000 by 15 years (95% CI 3.02 to 3.90) (Rahi 2001). While the incidence appears low, it is a significant, under‐reported and preventable cause of visual loss in childhood (Rahi 1999), that contributes many more person‐years of visual impairment than an adult cataract. 

    The reported prevalence of childhood cataract is lower in low‐ to middle‐income economies: 0.42 to 2.05/10,000 children compared with 0.63 to 13.6/10,000 in high‐income economies. However, interpretations of these estimates need to consider the wide variety of systems in place for early life eye disorder detection (Sheeladevi 2016).

    Description of the intervention

    Surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts is indicated if the cataracts are preventing normal visual development. Surgery involves removal of the natural lens of the eye, which is made of lens proteins enclosed within a clear membraneous capsule (Lloyd 2017). The cataract is removed by making a hole either in the front of the capsule or from behind it and removing the lens proteins. The two routes have different rates of complications (Raina 2016).  

    Removing the cataract removes a major element of the refractive power of the eye. The resultant refractive error must be corrected as soon as possible after the cataract is removed so that a normal visual stimulus and the potential for normal visual development are restored. Current options for correction of aphakia (absence of a lens in the eye) include intraocuar lenses (IOLs), contact lenses, spectacles or a combination of these (Birch 2005). If one eye is preferred for vision and the other shows a tendency to become amblyopic, patching of the preferred eye may be required. A more detailed evaluation of this treatment can be found in another published Cochrane Review on interventions for stimulus deprivation amblyopia (Antonio‐Santos 2020). More recently, the Infant Aphakia Treatment Study (IATS) has contributed to the evidence on the effect of patching on amblyopia (Drews‐Botsch 2016).

    Intraocular lenses are now being used more commonly in paediatric cataract surgery and may either be implanted at the time of the original surgery or planned for some time in the future (Solebo 2018). IOL implantation at the time of surgery reduces the power of spectacle or contact lens correction that is required. Controversy exists as to the suitability of very young children for implantation of an IOL that is expected to last a lifetime (de Faber 2017). There are also different opinions regarding the choice of IOL power for a growing eye (VanderVeen 2017).

    The posterior capsule (back wall of the capsule) is normally left intact in routine adult cataract surgery, which is thought to reduce the risk of complications including inflammation, glaucoma and retinal detachment. The posterior capsule is usually deliberately breached during paediatric cataract surgery because, in children, the posterior capsule rapidly opacifies after surgery, thereby obstructing vision again (visual axis opacification (VAO)). A primary posterior capsulotomy (making a central hole in the rear part of the lens capsule) may reduce the rate of VAO (Ashworth 2017), as may optic capture, where the implanted lens (IOL) is captured within the posterior capsulotomy (Raina 2002). Anterior vitrectomy (removal of the anterior part of the jelly of the eye behind the lens with a suction/cutting device) may also reduce the incidence of postoperative opacity formation, and primary posterior capsulotomy with anterior vitrectomy (as typically performed during in‐the‐bag surgery) is the preferred method of surgeons to reduce the rate of VAO in children under 6 years of age. But, some surgeons question the practice of routine anterior vitrectomy and suggest posterior optic capture without vitrectomy may be as effective at preventing VAO and avoids any unknown long‐term effects of vitrectomy (Gimbel 2004).

    Different approaches exist to access the lens in congenital cataract surgery and different instruments can be used. The 25‐gauge transconjunctival sutureless vitrectomy system was designed to reduce iatrogenic trauma at the surgical site by means of smaller incisions (Chee 2009). A pars plana or corneal approach can be taken to perform the lensectomy. Many surgeons prefer the corneal approach because they are more familiar with the anterior segment. The advantage of the pars plana approach is that it minimises the risk of trauma to the cornea and iris, but the tradeoff is an increased risk of removing too much capsule so that a secondary capsular IOL can not be supported (Wilson 2017).

    Glaucoma is a common complication after congenital cataract surgery, with a multicentre study reporting glaucoma‐related adverse events in 21% of cataract surgeries at 5‐year follow‐up (Solebo 2018). Postoperative inflammation is a risk factor for developing glaucoma and also needs to be considered in any surgical intervention (Grigg 2017).

    How the intervention might work

    We know that without surgery or with significant delay, poor vision and nystagmus (wobbly eyes indicating poor vision from birth) in bilateral visually‐significant cataracts is expected (Lambert 2017; Tomkins 2011). Timely and appropriate clearing of the congenital lens opacity and optically correcting their vision provides the child with a formed image on the retina. This will allow the visual area of the brain to develop and for the child's vision to be saved (Lambert 2017).

    Why it is important to do this review

    The aim of this review was to compare different surgical methods for the management of bilateral, visually‐significant congenital cataracts. A variety of techniques are available,  depending on geography, population and access to healthcare facilities. This review collates evidence on factors that have no agreed consensus, such as the timing of surgery, maintenance of a clear visual axis, implantation of an IOL and postoperative visual rehabilitation. It also aims to highlight gaps in this field of research for children under the age of 2 years, who have different visual outcomes to older children (Solebo 2020). 

    Objectives

    To assess the effectiveness of infant cataract surgery or lensectomy to no surgery for bilateral congenital cataracts in children aged 2 years and under.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) only in the review.

    Types of participants

    We included studies in which participants were children, aged 2 years or younger, with bilateral congenital cataracts. We excluded studies combining unilateral and bilateral cataracts where the results from the bilateral cases could not be extrapolated. In the original review, we included children under 15 years of age having surgery (Long 2008). We also wanted to include any studies that may have looked at the effect of delaying surgery. However, in this version of the review, we wish to concentrate on congenital cataracts that are visually significant in infancy, and so we are restricting our review to the above age limit.

    Types of interventions

    We included RCTs where infant cataract surgery or lensectomy (where the natural, crystalline lens is removed), was compared with no surgery. 

    As there are variations in lensectomy technique, we also included RCTs which compared one technique of lensectomy with another, such as whether an IOL was implanted or the eye left aphakic, the type of surgical approach and the type of IOL used, if any.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Visual acuity

      • Measured by a logMAR chart 1 year after surgery (the shortest time needed for postoperative results), or at longest postoperative follow‐up available

    The challenge of using early life visual acuity as an outcome measure is that 'normal' vision changes substantially over this age. This is why data from regular follow‐up through childhood are preferred. If other methods of reporting visual acuity are used, we will attempt to convert them to a logMAR scale.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Visual axis opacification (VAO): any method to assess the type and amount of opacification

    • Amblyopia: reduced visual acuity which cannot be improved after optical correction and is not attributable to a structural abnormality

    • Glaucoma

    • Retinal detachment

    • Reoperation rate

    Adverse events

    We included information on any adverse event occurring as a result of surgery.

    Follow‐up

    Regular follow‐up for congenital cataract cases is required, at least during childhood. For the purposes of our review, only sufficient follow‐up to gain postoperative results were required. We included all secondary outcomes that were available at 1 year postoperatively, or at longest follow‐up available.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    The Cochrane Eyes and Vision Information Specialist searched the following databases for randomised controlled trials and controlled clinical trials. There were no restrictions to language or year of publication. The date of the search was 25 January 2022.

    Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2022, Issue 1) (which contains the Cochrane Eyes and Vision Trials Register) in the Cochrane Library (searched 25 January 2022) (Appendix 1).
    MEDLINE Ovid (1946 to 25 January 2022) (Appendix 2).
    Embase Ovid (1980 to 25 January 2022) (Appendix 3).
    ISRCTN registry (www.isrctn.com/editAdvancedSearch; searched 13 January 2021) (Appendix 4).
    US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register ClinicalTrials.gov (www.clinicaltrials.gov; searched 25 January 2022) (Appendix 5).
    World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) (www.who.int/ictrp; searched 25 January 2022) (Appendix 6).

    We amended the inclusion criteria in 2009 from children aged up to 15 to children aged 2 years and below. We decided to retain the original search strategy to ensure we identified as many potentially relevant reports as possible. 

    Searching other resources

    We reviewed the bibliography of the included RCTs to see if there were any other studies on this topic.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (RS, LB) independently screened the titles and abstracts of the reports found by the electronic searches. We excluded any study that did not meet the inclusion criteria and we obtained the full‐text copies of reports that we judged to be possibly or definitely relevant. We documented reasons for exclusion for any study we subsequently rejected after viewing the full‐text copy. We documented the search results and selection process in a PRISMA flowchart (Liberati 2009).

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (RS, LB) independently extracted data from the included studies. We compared the extracted data and resolved differences by discussion. We collected information on study design (paired or parallel), participant characteristics such as age, details of the intervention and the outcomes they assessed. All data were managed in Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2022).

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (RS, LB) independently assessed each included trial for risk of bias according to the Cochrane RoB 1 tool and methods set out in Chapter 8 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2017). We assessed the following domains of bias.

    • Random sequence generation (sequence generation)

    • Allocation concealment (selection bias)

    • Masking of personnel (performance and detection bias)

    • Incomplete outcome data (attrition bias)

    • Selective reporting (reporting bias)

    • Other bias

    We graded each criterion as low risk of bias, high risk of bias and unclear. We recorded our assessments in the risk of bias tables.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We planned to calculate mean differences (MDs) for continuous variables such as visual acuity and VA and risk ratios (RRs) for dichotomous variables.

    Unit of analysis issues

    Cross‐over or cluster‐randomised trials are unlikely designs for this question. We included within‐person trials where a paired analysis was made, and we planned to extract effect measures from a paired analysis. In practice, none of the within‐person trials provided enough information for a paired analysis and, as both trials were very small, the data have been used as reported, which is a conservative assumption. We also included random allocation trials where both eyes of the same child were allocated to the same intervention but reported adjusted analysis where only one eye of each child was included.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted the trial investigators in instances of missing data. In the absence of a response within 2 weeks, we proceeded with the review with the available data only. We did not impute data for the purpose of this review.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    The broad scope of our review meant that there were significant differences, with all three of the included studies investigating different interventions. As a result, we reported each outcome using a qualitative (narrative) summary.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We could not use a funnel plot using standard error values to assess the risk of publication bias because the number of included trials was fewer than 10 (Page 2022).

    Data synthesis

    We did not conduct any meta‐analysis as we only included three trials, all for different aspects of surgical intervention. Due to methodological heterogeneity between the included trials, we have presented a narrative summary (McKenzie 2022). 

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    The included RCTs had substantial differences in clinical diversity. In total, seven outcomes were measured with high disparity in the number reported by each study.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We would have excluded trials with a high risk of bias in one or more domains from the results.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Initial search conducted for original published review

    The electronic searches for the original review (Long 2001) identified 104 reports of which we excluded 97 as they did not meet the inclusion criteria. We obtained full‐text copies of seven studies. We excluded five studies on methodological grounds (Ahmadieh 1999; Basti 1996; Basti 1999; Vasavada 1997; Vasavada 2000), and one after correspondence with the study authors (Thouvenin 1995). We included one study even though it included children with developmental cataract (Eckstein 1999); it did meet most of our inclusion criteria. 

    Electronic searches for subsequent updates

    2006 update: 158 reports were identified by the electronic searches. We excluded 149 reports that did not meet the inclusion criteria, and we obtained the full‐text copies of nine reports that were judged to be possibly or definitely relevant. We excluded six reports on methodological grounds on the basis of the published data alone (Bayramlar 2004; Hoyt 1982; Kugelburg 2005; Ram 2003; Saini 2003; Vasavada 2003). We included three trials that met the inclusion criteria and assessed these for methodological quality (Mullner 2003; Raina 2002; Vasavada 2001).

    2022 update: While our search strategy remained the same, our updated inclusion criteria has changed since the original search, and we are only including trials with children up to and including 2 years of age. This means that we have excluded the four trials in the original review (Eckstein 1999; Mullner 2003; Raina 2002; Vasavada 2001), as they no longer meet the inclusion criteria.

    The updated search was conducted in January 2022. The electronic searches yielded 1837 references (Figure 1). After we removed 615 duplicates, the Cochrane Information Specialist (CIS) screened the remaining 1222 records and removed 1078 references which were not relevant to the scope of the review. We screened the remaining 144 records. We obtained the full‐text copy of 12 trials which appeared to meet our inclusion criteria. We rejected seven of these (see the Characteristics of excluded studies table) and included three trials in the review.

    Included studies

    See Characteristics of included studies for further details of the included studies.

    Vasavada 2018

    Comparison: primary IOL implantation versus aphakia for bilateral cataract

    This study involved 60 children aged 2 years or younger undergoing bilateral cataract surgery. Participants were randomised to either receive a primary IOL implantation or to be left with primary aphakia. The aim was to determine which method produced the best visual acuity with the fewest complications. Postoperative follow‐up continued for 5 years. The complications assessed at every visit were glaucoma, visual axis opacification (VAO) and inflammation. Visual acuity was measured at each visit according to the logMAR scale.

    Kaur 2020

    Comparison: posterior optic capture of IOL without vitrectomy versus endocapsular implantation with anterior vitrectomy

    Participants had bilateral cataracts and were aged 5 years or under. Of the 18 participants, 7 were under the age of 1 year and had results reported separately. The aim was to compare the surgical outcomes of optic capture or in‐the‐bag IOL implantation. The first eye was randomised to one technique and the second was automatically randomised to the other group. Follow‐up was initially weekly, then monthly until 2 years postoperatively. The primary outcome was VAO, and a variety of data were collected on postoperative complications.

    Raina 2016

    Comparison: transcorneal versus pars plana route for lensectomy using the 25‐gauge transconjunctival sutureless vitrectomy system

    In this study, 12 participants under the age of 1 year with bilateral cataracts underwent surgery via the anterior transcorneal route in one eye and by the posterior pars plana route in the other. The choice of eye for each route was randomised, and the second surgery was between 1 and 3 weeks after the first. The aim was to compare the rate of complications between the two routes when using the 25‐gauge system. The primary outcome was not explicitly stated, but complications of surgery were assessed at 1 week, 1 month, 3 months and 1 year postoperatively.

    Allocation

    Vasavada 2018 was a well‐constructed trial. The investigators used equal group randomisation with sequenced, sealed envelopes for allocation concealment. We therefore assessed it to be at low risk of selection bias.

    Kaur 2020 stated that participants were randomised but did not report their method of randomisation. Raina 2016 also reported randomisation using a lottery system but did not specify how it was generated. We judged both trials to be of unclear bias for randomisation. However, both of these trials used a paired‐eye design, in which the two eyes of the same participant were concurrently allocated to the two different surgeries. So each participant was their own control. Overall, we judged their risk of selection bias to be low.

    Blinding

    We assessed all three trials to have unclear risk of performance bias. Part of the reason is that surgeons could not be masked to random assignments in the chosen trials. Another difficulty was masking participants to their group allocation because of notable differences in both treatment eyes. This was mentioned by Vasavada 2018 but it is unclear if attempts were made to mask the participants anyway. Neither Kaur 2020 nor Raina 2016 reported whether participants were masked to treatment group. However, because of the young age of the participants we judged that it is unlikely that not masking would have led to any significant bias.

    Vasavada 2018 took steps to mask surgeons by allocating treatment intraoperatively. The trial manager (an unscrubbed nurse) opened a sealed envelope to allocate which surgery was to be performed after the surgeon had completed the posterior continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis and anterior vitrectomy.

    We judged all three trials to have an unclear risk of detection bias. In one trial, assessment of outcome was judged by one of two examiners, it can be assumed they were not masked to the treatment allocation because of obvious differences between treatment and control eyes (Vasavada 2018). The other two trials did not report whether the personnel measuring outcomes were masked to treatment (Kaur 2020; Raina 2016).

    Incomplete outcome data

    We judged two trials to have low risk of attrition bias. It was specified in the protocol of one trial that only those participants that attended all 5 years of follow‐up appointments were included for analysis (Vasavada 2018). The number of participants that dropped out over this period was reported as 6 out of a total of 60, which we judged to be reasonable considering the extended follow‐up period. Raina 2016 reported results for all 12 of their participants in the final analysis, and so we judged this study to have low risk of attrition bias. Kaur 2020 did not include the results for 3 of the 18 participants that had surgery because of intraoperative complications that meant the procedure could not be completed as specified. We assessed this to have unclear risk of bias because the authors did not explain the characteristics of these participants.

    Selective reporting

    We assessed two trials to have unclear risk of bias because they did not prespecify the outcomes they were going to measure. Protocols or clinical trial registries were not publicly available for these trials (Kaur 2020; Raina 2016).

    One trial reported all the primary and secondary outcomes listed in its trial registry and subsequent methods; we judged this to have low risk of reporting bias (Vasavada 2018).

    Other potential sources of bias

    We identified no sources of other bias in one trial (Raina 2016).

    We classed two trials as unclear risk of bias (Kaur 2020; Vasavada 2018). Kaur 2020 did not specify how the anterior and posterior capsulorhexis size was measured in their methods.

    In Vasavada 2018, the investigators did not report the differences in ocular pathologies between the two eyes of each participant, such as microphthalmia or persistent fetal vasculature. This is relevant because statistical analysis was conducted based on the outcomes of the first operated eye, which was the one with the denser cataract or worse loss of vision. Although rare, the presence of other ocular pathologies may have influenced the decision of which eye was operated on first.

    Primary outcome: visual acuity

    In Vasavada 2018, at 5 years postoperative, there was no difference in visual acuity between the pseudophakic and aphakic groups. Mean logMAR visual acuity was 0.50 ± 0.33 in the IOL cohort compared with 0.59 ± 0.23 in those left aphakic (P = 0.79, mean difference (MD) ‐0.09 logMAR, 95% confidence interval (CI) ‐0.24 to 0.06; 54 participants; very low‐certainty evidence).     It is worth noting that there was a high rate of preoperative nystagmus in both groups (76% in pseudophakia and 72% in aphakia group). Although the difference was not statistically significant, it is likely that it limited improvement in visual outcome regardless of the treatment given.

    Discussion

    Effective surgical intervention is crucial to the successful management of visually significant congenital or early bilateral developmental cataracts. The three trials included in this review (Kaur 2020; Raina 2016; Vasavada 2018), examined different surgical techniques in an attempt to ascertain which approaches are more likely to maximise long‐term visual rehabilitation and minimise complications and the need for further intervention.

    Summary of main results

    Because the three trials included in this review compared the effects of different surgical procedures, we were unable to combine data in a meta‐analysis for any outcome targeted for this review. In addition, only one trial reported our primary outcome of postoperative visual acuity (Vasavada 2018). The study found no statistically significant difference in visual acuity for children with bilateral cataracts who were left aphakic compared to those with IOL implantations. While the study authors reported that there was a trend for pseudophakic eyes to recover faster, this is based on them being able to give documentable visual acuity at an earlier stage of their postoperative follow‐ups. But the lack of statistical power in this association prevented this review from concurring with this statement.

    Overall, limited data precludes us from making a conclusion on the optimal surgical intervention for bilateral congenital cataracts.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    The review question focussed on surgical interventions for bilateral congenital cataracts in children aged 2 years or under. This is because they have substantially different outcomes to older children and those with unilateral cataracts. We only identified three trials, all of which enrolled a small number of children, so they are not sufficient to address all the review objectives. Bilateral cataracts have a low prevalence, which can make it difficult to enrol participants in trials.

    We excluded studies with children over the age of 2 years, or studies that did not report separate findings for those under this age. This is because the implications of surgery for older children are comparatively well understood. Meanwhile, there is debate about the rate of postoperative complications in the under 2‐year age group.

    It is of note that the trials included in this review were all conducted in India, which may limit their general applicability. Practices in uptake and compliance with postoperative care can differ in the context of a resource‐poor setting. This was the main problem with the completeness of the Vasavada 2018 study, where there was limited reporting on the use of aphakic glasses and contact lenses for visual rehabilitation in surgeries where children were aphakic. Since our primary outcome was visual acuity, this may have affected results in favour of IOL implantation. A strength of this study is that it had a 5‐year follow‐up which represents good prediction for longer‐term outcomes. 

    The other two trials focussed on complications of surgery, which partly aligned with the secondary outcomes of our review. Kaur 2020 investigated two different techniques of IOL implantation to avoid VAO. The conclusions of the study are not directly applicable because they included children up to the age of 5 years. While they did separately report some outcomes for children under the age of 1 year, the small sample size limits its generalisability.

    Furthermore, the technique of posterior optic capture being researched in the Kaur 2020 study requires expertise and a long learning curve. All surgeries were performed by one surgeon and the results may not be replicable.

    The study investigators for Raina 2016 explored how outcomes differed between two surgical routes with a 25‐gauge vitrectomy system. The main problem with the applicability of their findings is that they also compared the usefulness of the 25‐gauge system with a 20‐gauge one without having a trial arm as a comparison. Also, many of the outcomes recorded concerned reporting on surgically‐induced astigmatism (demonstrating the benefits of the 25‐gauge system) and other complications which were not explicitly secondary outcomes associated with this review.

    Certainty of the evidence

    We assessed the certainty of the evidence using the GRADE approach at the outcome level. We classified the certainty of evidence of each outcome as high, medium, low or very low. The certainty of evidence was very low for all the investigated outcomes except retinal detachment in comparison 2 (low‐certainty evidence). 

    There was very low‐certainty evidence for our primary outcome, visual acuity, based on the results from Vasavada 2018. We downgraded this study one level because it was not possible to mask the personnel recording visual acuity in the RCT and two levels for imprecision due to very wide confidence intervals.

    The overall certainty of the evidence in this review is insufficient to comment on the most effective surgical intervention for bilateral congenital cataracts.

    Potential biases in the review process

    We followed standard Cochrane Review methods to minimise bias and MECIR standards to report this review. An information specialist conducted the search to identify eligible trials. None of the review authors have any financial conflicts of interest.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    In the following discussion, we decided to include prospective cohort studies and other RCTs that did not meet our inclusion criteria, since there were very few RCTs or controlled clinical trials in our population of interest. For the most part, we restricted the discussion to studies that were comparative in nature and evaluated children with bilateral cataracts who had surgery under the age of 2 years.

    (1) Primary IOL implantation versus aphakia

    After cataract surgery, a critical part of a child's visual rehabilitation is how their refractive error is managed. Surgeons can choose between contact lenses, spectacles and IOLs. There is agreement amongst surgeons that IOL implantation in children older than 2 years offers a good standard of care (Wilson 2017). For those under this age there is controversy, with some studies reporting a high complication rate with IOL lenses that need future reoperation. There is also debate in cases of IOL implantation about whether the formulas used to calculate the power of the lens in young children are accurate. Our literature search found one RCT that reported on the effect of IOL implantation versus aphakia in bilateral congenital cataracts (Vasavada 2018). It suggested there was no benefit of IOL implantation to visual acuity, but argued that the rate of complications was acceptable. The evidence base to make a judgement on this is limited.

    The IoLunder2 study inception cohort study was conducted at 31 sites in the UK and investigated IOL implantation versus aphakia in those who underwent cataract surgery in the first 2 years of life (Solebo 2018). The study found no statistically significant difference in visual acuity between those who had IOL implantation and those who were aphakic in a cohort of 158 children with bilateral cataracts at 5‐year follow‐up. The USA Infant Aphakia Treatment Study (IATS) is a RCT that compared the same procedure but in a population of children under 6 months old with unilateral cataracts (Lambert 2014). They reported no significant difference in visual acuity at 4.5 years follow‐up.

    IoLunder2 and IATS found a high rate of complications associated with IOL implantation over 5 years follow‐up. The most frequent complication of IOL implantation in both trials was VAO. IATS reported VAO in 40% of pseudophakic eyes compared to 4% in aphakic eyes, this often needed to be resolved by a second surgery. IoLunder2 found that IOL implantation was associated with a 5.5 times increased likelihood of reoperation. The rate of VAO in the Vasavada 2018 trial was markedly lower than the IATS trial. The reasons for this lower incidence could be that the patient population had a median age of 6 months compared to 1.8 months in the IATS study.

    There is no consensus on the effect of IOL implantation on glaucoma. Some non‐randomised trials suggest that it may have a protective effect against the incidence of glaucoma (Asrani 2000; Lawrence 2005). This was contested by the IoLunder2 study (Solebo 2018) and Vasavada 2018 which found no significant difference in the rate of glaucoma between the primary aphakia and IOL implanted groups. The lack of trials exploring the mechanism of open‐angle glaucoma preclude any meaningful comparison with the wider literature.

    (2) Posterior optic capture versus in‐the‐bag IOL implantation

    The current gold standard for reducing the incidence of VAO is in‐the‐bag implantation with vitrectomy. Although posterior continuous curvilinear capsulorhexis with optic capture and no anterior vitrectomy has shown to be successful in preventing secondary VAO in early studies (Gimbel 1997; Raina 2002), some surgeons found this is not always true for children younger than 5 years of age (Vasavada 2000).

    The design of the IOL is an important predictive factor for the occurrence of VAO (Gimbel 2004). IoLunder2 found that VAO was more common in eyes with single piece IOL models, although a RCT is needed to confirm this association (Solebo 2020). The use of new materials and structure of IOLs has offered another opportunity to evaluate approaches without vitrectomy. Our literature search found one RCT which reported posterior optic capture without vitrectomy to be as effective as in‐the‐bag implantation in preventing VAO (Kaur 2020), and had a lower risk of inflammation. The investigators used a non‐customised 3‐piece hydrophobic acrylic IOL. Another trial comparing the same interventions and using the same design of IOL by Vasavada 2017 found no statistically significant difference in the incidence of VAO or inflammation at 1‐year follow‐up. These findings suggest that posterior optic capture is a reasonable alternative to in‐the‐bag implantation at preventing VAO, but we found no trials with larger sample sizes and longer follow‐up to confirm this conclusion or to infer superiority.

    An emerging technique to prevent VAO is bag‐in‐the lens IOL (BIL‐IOL) implantation. This involves making an anterior and posterior capsulorhexis of similar size (5 mm), and following IOL insertion, the edges of the rhexes are placed into a groove around the circumference of the BIL‐IOL. The aim is to physically block the migration of lens epithelial cells to the posterior lens capsule, preventing posterior capsule opacification (PCO). This approach has shown success in Sweden (Nyström 2018), but has not been widely adopted in the absence of RCTs comparing it with the current gold standard.

    (3) Transcorneal versus pars plana route for lensectomy using a 25‐gauge vitrectomy system

    The 25‐gauge vitrectomy system has proved popular in recent years because it offers a minimal size surgical wound and the potential for sutureless surgery. Our review found one RCT that compares a transcorneal with a pars plana route using a 25‐gauge system (Raina 2016). The study investigators reported that both approaches have similar intraoperative stability and postoperative 1‐year outcomes. We did not find any other RCTs or cohort studies in the literature to compare these results with.
