Mailuoning for acute ischaemic stroke

  • Weimin Yang
  • Zhaobo Shi
  • Hong‐Qi Yang
  • Junfang Teng
  • Jun Zhao
  • Guoliang Xiang
Authors' declarations of interest

Version published: 09 January 2015 Version history

https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD007028.pub3
Collapse all Expand all

Abstract

available in
  • English
  • Español
  • فارسی

    • Background

    Mailuoning is widely used in the treatment of acute ischaemic stroke in China. Animal experimental studies and clinical pharmacological research indicate that mailuoning might improve blood circulation, prevent ischaemic injury, and protect heart and brain tissue. This review was last published in 2009. As new data have become available, it is necessary to reassess the evidence from randomised controlled trials.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects and safety of mailuoning agents (injection or oral liquid) in the treatment of people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Search methods

    We searched the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Register (May 2014), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL;2014 , Issue 4), MEDLINE (1966 to May 2014), Embase (1980 to May 2014), AMED (1985 to May 2014), the Chinese Stroke Trials Register (June 2014), the China Biological Medicine Database (CBM‐disc; 1979 to June 2014), China Science and Technology Journal database (CSTJ; 1979 to June 2014), Wanfang Data Chinese databases (1979 to June 2014), and the China National Knowledge Infrastructure (1979 to June 2014). We searched clinical trials and research registers, handsearched 10 Chinese journals including relevant conference proceedings, scanned reference lists, and contacted the pharmaceutical company that manufactures mailuoning. We also attempted to contact trial authors to obtain further data.

    Selection criteria

    Randomised controlled trials comparing mailuoning with placebo or mailuoning plus other treatment compared with that other treatment in people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Data collection and analysis

    Two review authors independently selected trials for inclusion, assessed trial quality, and extracted data.

    Main results

    We included 21 trials, involving 1746 participants, in this update; six trials were new. The included trials did not report the numbers of dead and dependent participants at the end of at least three months' follow‐up. Of the 12 trials that reported adverse events, five events occurred in two trials. There was no significant difference between the treatment group and the control group. We assessed 20 trials to be of a poor quality: When analysing these trials together, mailuoning was associated with a significant increase in the number of participants with an improved neurological deficit (risk ratio (RR) 0.31, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.23 to 0.42) and showed a significant improvement of neurological deficit with the European Stroke Scale (ESS) (mean difference (MD) (fixed) 8.29, 95% CI 3.44 to 13.15). One placebo‐controlled trial, assessed to be of a better methodological quality, failed to show a significant improvement of neurological deficit at the end of three months' follow‐up (MD (fixed) 2.49, 95% CI ‐1.45 to 6.43) or in quality of life. One trial, which reported cognitive function using the Montreal Cognitive Assessment as a continuous scale, showed a significant improvement of cognitive function (MD (fixed) 2.68, 95% CI 1.82 to 3.54). Two trials assessed activities of daily life: One trial showed a significant improvement, but the other did not.

    Authors' conclusions

    This review did not provide sufficient evidence to support the routine use of mailuoning for the treatment of people with acute ischaemic stroke. High‐quality large‐scale randomised controlled trials are needed to confirm the efficacy of mailuoning.

    • Mailuoning (a type of traditional Chinese medicine) for improving outcome in people with acute ischaemic stroke

    Review question: We wanted to compare the safety and effects of mailuoning treatment versus placebo or no treatment for reducing death and dependency in people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Background: Mailuoning, a type of Chinese traditional medicine, is widely used in the treatment of acute ischaemic stroke in China even though its effect is still uncertain. Animal experimental studies and clinical pharmacological research indicate that mailuoning might improve blood circulation, prevent ischaemic injury, and protect heart and brain tissue. We therefore sought to undertake a review to discover whether using mailuoning therapy was better or worse than placebo or no additional treatment for people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Study characteristics: We searched for trials up to June 2014 and included 21 randomised controlled trials, involving 1746 participants, of mailuoning for people with acute ischaemic stroke. Two trials followed up participants for three months after treatment ended, but the duration of the other trials was shorter, from 14 to 38 days. A government agency funded one of the 21 included trials.

    Key results: We assessed 20 trials to be of a low quality. When we analysed these trials together, there was no significant difference for adverse events between the mailuoning‐treatment group and the control group; mailuoning improved neurological impairment and cognitive function significantly. One trial, assessed to be of a higher quality, failed to show any significant improvement made by mailuoning on neurological deficit, activities of daily life, or quality of life. There was no convincing evidence from trials with sufficient methodological quality to support the routine use of mailuoning to promote recovery after stroke. High‐quality large‐scale randomised controlled trials are needed to confirm its effect.

    Quality of the evidence: We assessed 20 of the 21 included trials to be of a poor quality for their study design; we assessed one trial to be of a higher quality, but there were fewer participants in this trial. Overall, the quality of evidence was poor.

    Visual summary

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    This review did not provide sufficient evidence to support the routine use of mailuoning for the treatment of people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Implications for research

    This review suggests that mailuoning may improve neurological impairment after acute ischaemic stroke. However, with one exception, we assessed the trials included to be of a poor methodological quality. High‐quality large‐scale randomised trials are needed to confirm or refute these results. Future trials should overcome the limitations of the trials presented in this review. In particular, they should ensure adequate concealment of allocation, blinding of outcome assessors, and use a functional outcome as the primary outcome measured at long‐term follow‐up.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    The interruption of the blood supply to the brain, usually because a blood vessel bursts or is blocked by a clot, causes a stroke to occur. This cuts off the supply of oxygen and nutrients, causing damage to the brain tissue (WHO 2014a). Annually, 15 million people worldwide suffer a stroke. Of these, five million die, and another five million are left permanently disabled, placing a burden on families and the community (WHO 2014b). In China, with a population of 1.4 billion, the annual stroke mortality rate is approximately 1.6 million, or 157 per 100,000, exceeding heart disease as the leading cause of adult death and disability. In addition, China has 2.5 million new stroke cases each year and 7.5 million stroke survivors (Liu 2011; Wu 2013). Ischaemic stroke accounts for 43.7% to 78.9% of all strokes (Liu 2007; Sun 2013).

    Description of the intervention

    Despite studying many drug therapies, there is as yet no routine effective, generally accepted, specific treatment for acute ischaemic stroke, except for aspirin (Jauch 2013; Sandercock 2014). Some clinicians may wish to use thrombolytic therapy in highly selected patients under license conditions. Research carried out by the China National Stroke Registry suggested approximately one in five people with stroke presenting within three hours received thrombolytic therapy (Xu 2014); others who are concerned about the possible risks may choose not to use this treatment at all (Wardlaw 2014). Other strategies may improve cerebral microcirculation.

    In China, a survey of treatments used in routine practice showed that over 70% of doctors surveyed believed that Chinese herb products were effective treatments for acute ischaemic stroke, and 66% of doctors used them routinely for most patients (Chen 1997). Mailuoning is one such drug. It has been used in clinical practice since 1985 (Wang 1997), and in 1993, the State Administration of Traditional Chinese Medicine identified it as an emergency Chinese medicine (Zhao 1993).

    How the intervention might work

    Mailuoning is a compound agent; it is made by extracting herbal Dendrobium, Radix Scrophularia, Flos Lonicerae, and Radix Achyranthis Bidentatae; the major effective components are scoparone and ayapin (Zhu 1992). Animal experimental studies and clinical pharmacological research have shown that mailuoning inhibits platelet agglutination (Zhu 2000a), reduces erythrocyte aggregation in the microcirculation, lowers blood viscosity (Lai 2000; Zhu 2000b), controls blood pressure (Wang 2000a), dilates blood vessels (Zhang 2000), improves microcirculation (Wang 2000b; Wang 2000c), prevents ischaemic injury, and protects heart and brain tissue (Dai 2000; Li 1998; Yang 1998). Its mechanism is associated with blocking α‐adrenergic receptors, exciting histamine receptors (Lu 2000), improving neuronal function, and reducing excitatory amino acid release (Sun 2005). It also has the function of reducing serum total cholesterol‐triglyceride levels, increasing serum high‐density lipoprotein levels (Liu 1999; Liu 2002), reducing fibrinogen levels, increasing plasminogen levels (Zhu 2000c), and enhancing tissue plasminogen activator activity (Zhuang 1995). In addition, mailuoning can prevent free radical injury from ischaemic brain tissue by increasing the superoxide dismutase level and reducing lipid peroxide (Feng 2000; Luo 2006; Wang 2004); it can also reduce infarct volume and improve neurological deficit scores (Luo 2006; Wang 2004). All of these mechanisms give mailuoning neuroprotective properties (Luo 2006; Wang 2004).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Hundreds of clinical studies of the benefits and side‐effects of mailuoning and other drugs for the treatment of ischaemic stroke have been carried out. This review was first published in 2009 (Yang 2009), when we found no convincing evidence to support the routine use of mailuoning to promote recovery after stroke. Since then, new clinical studies of mailuoning for ischaemic stroke have been published. We have therefore updated this review to include the new evidence to determine whether mailuoning can be recommended for routine use in people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Objectives

    To determine the effects and safety of mailuoning agents (injection or oral liquid) in the treatment of people with acute ischaemic stroke.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs). Trials comparing mailuoning with placebo or open control (no placebo) or mailuoning plus other treatment compared with that other treatment for people with acute ischaemic stroke were eligible for inclusion irrespective of blinding, publication status, or language.

    Types of participants

    Trials that included people of any age or sex with acute ischaemic stroke (within 14 days of onset) were eligible. The clinical definition of ischaemic stroke is that of the World Health Organization criteria (Hatano 1976), with computerised tomography (CT) or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanning to exclude haemorrhagic stroke. We also included participants with probable ischaemic stroke (i.e., without confirmation by CT or MRI).

    Types of interventions

    We included trials evaluating any type of mailuoning (injection, oral liquid, etc.), regardless of length of treatment period and dosage of treatment. The control interventions were placebo or no treatment. We also included trials of mailuoning plus another treatment compared with that other treatment alone.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    1. Death or dependency at the end of long‐term follow‐up or the scheduled follow‐up. We defined dependency as dependent on others in activities of daily living ‐ for example, the Barthel Index score of 60 or less, the modified Rankin Scale graded 3 to 5 (Sulter 1999), or the trialist's own definition.

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Adverse events: nausea, vomiting, allergic reaction, tachycardia, haemorrhagic transformation of the infarct caused by mailuoning, and some unexplained organ abnormalities (e.g., hepatic, haematological, cardiac, respiratory). The number of participants developing at least one adverse event listed above would be evaluated at the end of treatment.

    2. The proportion of participants with marked neurological improvement after treatment. The measures can focus on specific impairment (e.g., motor deficit or cognitive impairment) or global neurological deficit (e.g., the National Institutes of Health Stroke Scale; Canadian Neurological Scale; European Stroke Scale; or the Scandinavian Stroke Scale, which involve motor, sensory, and other impaired neurological function).

    3. Death from all causes:

      1. within the first two weeks of treatment, and

      2. by the end of the scheduled treatment period.

    4. Quality of life if assessed by the included trials at the end of long‐term follow‐up or the scheduled follow‐up.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    See the 'Specialized register' section in the Cochrane Stroke Group module. We searched for trials in all languages and arranged translation of relevant papers if required.

    Electronic searches

    We searched the Cochrane Stroke Group Trials Register (which was last searched by the Managing Editor in May 2014), the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL; 2014 ,Issue 4) (Appendix 1), MEDLINE (Ovid) (1966 to May 2014) (Appendix 2), Embase (Ovid) (1980 to May 2014) (Appendix 3), the Allied and Complementary Medicine Database (AMED) (Ovid) (1985 to May 2014) (Appendix 4), the Chinese Stroke Trials Register (June 2014), the China Biological Medicine Database (CBM‐disc; 1979 to June 2014), China Science and Technology Journal database (CSTJ (www.cqvip.com); 1979 to June 2014), Wanfang Data Chinese databases (www.wanfangdata.com.cn; 1979 to June 2014), and the China National Knowledge Infrastructure (www.cnki.net; 1979 to June 2014).

    In an effort to identify further published, unpublished, and ongoing trials, we searched the following trials and research registers (June 2014).

    1. Stroke Trials Registry (www.strokecenter.org/trials/).

    2. National Center for Complementary and Alternative Medicine (NCCAM) (nccam.nih.gov/research/clinicaltrials).

    3. The US National Institutes of Health Ongoing Trials Register (www.clinicaltrials.gov/).

    4. HerbMed (www.herbmed.org).

    Searching other resources

    1. We scanned reference lists of relevant papers.

    2. We contacted the pharmaceutical company manufacturing mailuoning (Jinling Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd.) (January 2008 and August 2014).

    3. We attempted to contact trial authors to obtain further data.

    4. For the previous version of the review in 2007, we handsearched the following relevant journals and conference proceedings:

      1. China Journal of Chinese Materia Medica (2002 to 2007);

      2. Chinese Journal of Information on Traditional Chinese Medicine (1994 to 2007);

      3. Chinese Journal of Integrative Medicine (1998 to 2007);

      4. Chinese Journal of Neurology (1998 to 2007);

      5. Chinese Traditional Patent Medicine (2002 to 2007);

      6. Journal of Apoplexy and Nervous Diseases (2002 to 2007);

      7. Journal of Brain and Nervous Diseases (2002 to 2007);

      8. Journal of Emergency in Traditional Chinese Medicine (1992 to 2007);

      9. Research of Traditional Chinese Medicine (2002 to 2007); and

      10. Stroke and Nervous Diseases (2000 to 2007).

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    One review author (WY) screened the titles and abstracts of the records retrieved by electronic searches and excluded obviously irrelevant studies. We obtained the full text of the remaining studies, and two review authors (WY, ZS) independently assessed these for inclusion in the review according to the predefined inclusion criteria. We resolved any disagreements by discussion and, if necessary, in consultation with a third review author (JZ). We contacted study authors if we required further clarification for any study.

    Data extraction and management

    Two review authors (WY, ZS) independently extracted details of participant characteristics, methods, interventions, and outcomes using a data extraction form. We resolved disagreements by discussion. We obtained missing data from the study authors whenever possible.

    For dichotomous outcomes, we extracted the number of participants experiencing the event and the total number of participants in each arm of the trial. For continuous or ordinal variables, the main outcomes of interest were the final assessment score and the change in score from baseline to the final assessment; we extracted the mean value and standard deviation for the changes in each arm of the trial along with the total number in each group. The baseline assessment score was the first available score no longer than two months prior to randomisation.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (WY, ZS) independently evaluated the risk of bias for each study. We assessed the methodological quality of trials according to standard criteria of The Cochrane Collaboration (Higgins 2011). We resolved any disagreements by discussion between the same two review authors, and if they reached no consensus, we consulted a third review author (HY).

    We made judgements on the following:

    1. random sequence generation (selection bias);

    2. allocation concealment (selection bias);

    3. blinding of participants and personnel (performance bias);

    4. blinding of outcome assessment (performance bias);

    5. incomplete outcome data (attrition bias);

    6. selective reporting (reporting bias); and

    7. other possible biases.

    According to criteria of The Cochrane Collaboration (Higgins 2011), we divided studies into three categories:

    • low risk of bias: all quality criteria met;

    • high risk of bias: one or more quality criteria not met; or

    • unclear risk of bias: insufficient information to permit judgement of 'low risk' or 'high risk'.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We expressed results for dichotomous outcomes as risk ratios (RR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) and results for continuous outcomes as mean difference (MD) or standardised mean difference (SMD) as appropriate.

    Unit of analysis issues

    For each included study, we intended to review the level at which randomisation occurred and took this into consideration in the analysis. For studies with non‐standard designs (e.g., cross‐over trials, cluster‐randomised trials), we managed the data according to the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Dealing with missing data

    If data were missing, we documented the missing data for each study and attempted to contact the investigators or companies of the relevant trials for additional information. If data remained unavailable, we considered both best‐case and worst‐case scenarios.

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We carried out tests for heterogeneity by using the I² statistic to estimate total variation across studies due to heterogeneity: We considered less than 25% as low‐level heterogeneity, 25% to 50% as moderate‐level heterogeneity, and higher than 50% as high‐level heterogeneity (Higgins 2003).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We investigated potential publication bias by funnel plot (Egger 1997).

    Data synthesis

    We carried out meta‐analyses using Review Manager (RevMan 2014). We used the fixed‐effect approach throughout unless there was substantial heterogeneity ‐ in which case, we examined the potential causes of heterogeneity, but we did not use the random‐effects method.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    1. Timing of treatment after stroke onset (e.g., less than 12 hours, 12 to 48 hours, 48 hours to 14 days).

    2. Route of drug administration (intravenous or oral).

    We tested significant differences between two or more subgroups using the Chi² test, in which we computed the P value for Qint (Qint = Qall ‐ (Q₁ + ... + Qm)) on m‐1 degrees of freedom using Excel software. Qall is the Chi² statistic for heterogeneity for all included trials; Q₁ to Qm are Chi² statistics for heterogeneity for each subgroup.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to carry out the following sensitivity analyses by excluding studies with the following conditions:

    • inadequate concealment of allocation;

    • in which outcome evaluation was not blinded;

    • in which loss to follow up was not reported or at least 10%; and

    • not using placebo.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    We identified 3216 references through electronic searches and handsearches, of which 1089 references were identified since the previous searches in 2007. Of these, we excluded 1882 irrelevant or duplicate references by reading the titles and abstracts. We obtained the full texts of 106 papers and identified 45 potentially eligible studies, of which we included 21 (Figure 1).

    • Open in figure viewer
    Figure 1

    Mailuoning study flow diagram

    We included 21 trials, involving a total of 1746 participants ‐ all of whom were administered treatment within seven days after stroke onset (Ding 2005; Ding 2010; Guo 2002; He 2001; Huang 2006; Huo 2009; Huo 2012; Lv 2010; Mu 1998; Sun 2002; Wang 2007; Wang 2012; Xu 1999; Yan 2001; Yan 2003; Yang 2007; Yu 2006; Zang 2010; Zhang 1997; Zhang 2004; Zhou 2004). In this update, we included six new trials, involving 466 participants (Ding 2010; Huo 2009; Huo 2012; Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Zang 2010). We have provided summary details of these trials in the 'Characteristics of included studies' tables.

    We excluded 24 trials because they were not randomised trials, the type of participants did not comply with our inclusion criteria, or there were no eligible data for analysis (Bai 2001; Ban 2010; Chen 1998; Chen 2004; Fu 2007; Fu 2008; Gao 1998; He 1998; He 2012; Huang 1997; Jiang 2001; Li 2007; Li 2008; Liang 2001; Rozi 2011; Shangguan 2011; Shen 1994; Su 2001; Wang 2005; Wang 2010; Yang 2004; Yang 2014; Yu 2000; Zhu 2011). We excluded Fu 2008 because the major data were similar to that of the included trial Wang 2007. We excluded four trials that were previously awaiting assessment because they did not report the timing from onset to treatment of ischaemic stroke (Bai 2001; Chen 2004; Jiang 2001; Yang 2004) (see the 'Characteristics of included studies' tables).

    We did not identify any ongoing studies.

    In 2008, we wrote to the trial authors of 29 potentially eligible trials twice for supplementary data: Only Shen 1994 and Wang 2005 replied. In 2014, we wrote to the trial authors of 43 potentially eligible trials twice: Only Shangguan 2011 replied. There were few disagreements between the review authors in selecting studies for inclusion, assessing quality, or extracting data, and they were resolved by consensus.

    Included studies

    The 21 included trials were all conducted in China. The age of the participants ranged from 18 to 87 years. Each trial included more men than women, except Huo 2012, which did not provide information about the men:women ratio. All trials had distinct inclusion criteria: Ding 2010 only included acute lacune cerebral infarction. Nine trials reported the level of severity of acute ischaemic stroke (Guo 2002; He 2001; Huang 2006; Mu 1998; Wang 2007; Yan 2001; Yang 2007; Yu 2006; Zhang 1997). All trials reported the time period of the start of treatment after stroke onset: Yan 2003 included all participants within the first 24 hours of stroke onset, five trials included all participants within the first 48 hours of stroke onset (He 2001; Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Zang 2010; Zhou 2004), and the other 15 trials also included participants more than 48 hours (from three to seven days) after onset. Timing of the start of treatment after stroke onset did not influence the division of participants into groups. The baseline characteristics of the participants did not differ between treatment groups and control groups in these trials.

    Twenty included trials studied mailuoning injections, and one trial studied mailuoning oral liquid (Huo 2012). The treatment method, drug dose, and treatment period were different among the trials; we present this information in the 'Characteristics of included studies' tables.

    The intervals from the beginning of treatment to taking measurements of neurological deficit were different: In 10 trials, the interval was 14 days (Ding 2010; Guo 2002; He 2001; Huang 2006; Lv 2010; Wang 2007; Wang 2012; Yan 2003; Yu 2006; Zang 2010); in one trial, it was 15 days (Huo 2012); in three trials, it was 20 to 21 days (Huo 2009; Mu 1998; Zhou 2004); in five trials, it was 28 to 31 days (Ding 2005; Sun 2002; Xu 1999; Yan 2001; Yang 2007); and in two trials, it was 35 to 38 days (Zhang 1997; Zhang 2004). Two trials followed up the participants three months after treatment discontinued, measuring the activities of daily living scores at the end of follow‐up (Huo 2012; Yu 2006); Yu 2006 also measured neurological deficit again at the end of follow‐up. These trials reported no deaths. Twelve trials observed adverse events, but only two trials reported these events (He 2001; Zhou 2004); the other 10 trials reported no adverse events (Ding 2010; Huo 2009; Huo 2012; Lv 2010; Wang 2007; Wang 2012; Yan 2001; Yan 2003; Yang 2007; Zang 2010). The outcome measure used in 17 trials was the measurement of neurological deficit using the Modified Edinburgh‐Scandinavian Stroke Scale (MESSS); three trials measured neurological deficit using the European Stroke Scale (ESS); one trial measured cognitive function using the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) scale. Two trials assessed activities of daily living (ADL) (Huo 2012; Yu 2006). One trial assessed quality of life (Yu 2006). A government agency funded one of the included trials (Huang 2006).

    Excluded studies

    Of the 45 potentially eligible trials, we excluded 24 trials, of which eight trials were not randomised controlled trials (RCTs) (Gao 1998; He 1998; Liang 2001; Shangguan 2011; Wang 2005; Wang 2010; Yang 2014; Yu 2000), stage from onset to treatment was not clear in eight trials (Bai 2001; Ban 2010; Chen 2004; Huang 1997; Jiang 2001; Rozi 2011; Shen 1994; Yang 2004), there was no eligible data for analysis in two trials (Chen 1998; Fu 2007), eligible participants were more than 14 days from onset in three trials (He 2012; Li 2008; Su 2001), and stage from onset was not clear and there were no eligible data for analysis in two trials (Li 2007; Zhu 2011). We excluded one trial, Fu 2008, because the data were similar to that of included trial Wang 2007, so we considered them to be the same study.

    Risk of bias in included studies

    Risk of bias is summarised in Figure 2 and Figure 3.

    Allocation

    Yu 2006 used a stratified block randomised method; allocated participants by random number; used sealed and opaque envelopes for allocation concealment; and compared the effects of extract of Ginkgo biloba leaves, mailuoning, and puerarin with placebo, but the sample size was unbalanced (40 participants in the mailuoning group versus 15 participants in the control group); Yu 2006 did not report the reason for the imbalance. However, this trial was of a higher methodological quality and avoided bias effectively. Wang 2007 allocated participants by random number, but it did not report the method of allocation concealment, so we could not regard it as being of a high methodological quality. The other 19 trials did not describe the method of the sequence generation process, so we assessed the method of concealment to be at a high risk of bias.

    Blinding

    Only Yu 2006 used a blinded method for outcome assessment. The other trials did not describe the method of blinding, so we assessed them as at a high risk of bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Yu 2006 reported incomplete outcome data: Five participants in the treatment group and three in the control group were lost to follow up in this trial. In the final analysis, these participants were not included, and the trial did not use an intention‐to‐treat analysis, which introduced potential attrition bias and influenced the quality of the trial to some extent. None of the other 20 trials stated whether they used intention‐to‐treat analyses; these trials did not report any participants as lost to follow up, but the follow‐up period was short (14 to 38 days), except in Huo 2012, where the follow‐up period was three months.

    Selective reporting

    Ten trials reported adverse effects in their results (Ding 2010; He 2001; Huo 2009; Huo 2012; Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Yan 2001; Yan 2003; Yang 2007; Zhou 2004), but the methods section of the trials did not state whether adverse effects were to be observed. As the protocols of these included studies were not available, selective reporting bias was high.

    Other potential sources of bias

    Although we undertook extensive searches for eligible studies and went to considerable effort to obtain all results from pharmaceutical companies, there were no negative results available, so we could not exclude the possibility that studies with negative findings remain unpublished.

    There were no studies included more than once in each meta‐analysis, and there were no cross‐over studies, survival studies, or cluster‐randomised studies requiring special treatment in the meta‐analysis.

    Effects of interventions

    Primary outcomes

    Death or dependency at the end of long‐term follow‐up

    Two trials followed up participants for three months after the end of treatment (Huo 2012; Yu 2006). The methods section of one trial, Yu 2006, stated the Barthel Index, but the data about the Barthel Index presented in the results section were incorrect, so we could not analyse the data. Huo 2012 did not provide any information about dependency. Neither of the studies included death as an outcome in the methods and did not report death at the end of follow‐up.

    Secondary outcomes

    Adverse events

    Only two studies reported adverse events at the end of treatment (He 2001; Zhou 2004). Drowsiness occurred in three participants (one in the treatment group and two in the control group) in the first week, and two participants in the treatment groups presented with palpitations and chest tightness. There was no significant difference between the treatment group and the control group for adverse events (risk ratio (RR) 1.39, 95% confidence interval (CI) 0.28 to 6.76; I² statistic = 0%; Analysis 1.1).

    Improvement of neurological deficit at the end of treatment

    Meta‐analysis of the 16 relevant trials showed that mailuoning agents significantly increased the improvement of neurological deficit at the end of treatment (without follow‐up) between the two groups (RR 0.31, 95% CI 0.23 to 0.42; I² statistic = 0%; Analysis 2.1).

    Yu 2006, which we assessed to be of a better methodological quality and reported neurological improvement with the MESSS as a continuous scale, failed to show a significant improvement in neurological deficit between the two groups based on the change in score of MESSS from baseline to the end of follow‐up (mean difference (MD) (fixed) 2.49, 95% CI ‐1.45 to 6.43; Analysis 2.2). Three trials, Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Zang 2010, reporting neurological improvement with the ESS as a continuous scale, showed a significant improvement in neurological deficit based on the final scores at the end of treatment (without follow up) (MD (fixed) 8.29, 95% CI 3.44 to 13.15; Analysis 2.3). Ding 2010, which reported cognitive function using MoCA as a continuous scale, showed a significant improvement in cognitive function based on the change in MoCA scores from baseline to the end of treatment (MD (fixed) 2.68, 95% CI 1.82 to 3.54; Analysis 2.4).

    Death from all causes within the first two weeks of treatment and during the whole follow‐up period

    The 21 included trials did not report any deaths during the treatment period or during follow up after the treatment ended in all trials. This may mean that the trials included only mild strokes, or deaths occurred, but the trialists did not report them.

    Functional outcome and quality of life

    Two trials undertook assessment of activities of daily living function (Huo 2012; Yu 2006). Because heterogeneity was high (I² statistic = 75%), we were unable to conduct a meta‐analysis for activities of daily living function. Based on the final scores at the end of follow‐up, there was statistical difference between the treatment group and the control group in one trial (MD 9.03, 95% CI 6.57 to 11.49) (Huo 2012), but there was no difference in the other trial (MD ‐5.31, 95% CI ‐19.11 to 8.49) (Yu 2006) (Analysis 2.5).

    Yu 2006 assessed quality of life using the Spitzer Quality of Life Index at the end of three months' follow‐up. There was no significant difference between the treatment group and the control group (MD (fixed) ‐0.92, 95% CI ‐2.11 to 0.27; Analysis 2.6).

    Subgroup analyses

    One trial included participants within 24 hours from stroke onset to treatment (Yan 2003); five trials included participants within 48 hours from stroke onset to treatment (He 2001; Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Zang 2010; Zhou 2004); the other 15 trials included participants within three to seven days from stroke onset to treatment, but participants were not divided into groups by timing of the start of treatment after stroke onset. Furthermore, we were unable to get the primary data from the trialists, so we could not do this subgroup analysis. The route of mailuoning administration was intravenous in 20 trials and oral in one trial (Huo 2012). We did a subgroup analysis for the route of mailuoning administration. The results showed that the intravenous group benefited from a significant improvement of neurological deficit, but the oral group did not (Analysis 3.1). However, the test for subgroup differences was non‐significant (P value = 0.22), which means that there was insufficient evidence that the effect of mailuoning on neurological function varied by route of administration. Two trials measured the activities of daily living function (Huo 2012; Yu 2006); because heterogeneity was high (I² statistic = 75%), we could not get a reliable estimate of heterogeneity with just these two studies, so we could not undertake a subgroup analysis for activities of daily living function. The oral group (Huo 2012) reported a significant improvement of ADL score (MD 9.03, 95% CI 6.57 to 11.49), but the intravenous group (Yu 2006) did not (MD ‐5.31, 95%CI ‐19.11 to 8.49).

    Sensitivity analysis

    We did not perform the planned sensitivity analysis as only one trial had adequate allocation concealment, blinding, and used a placebo (Yu 2006). We investigated potential publication bias by funnel plot, which did not show potential publication bias in this review (Figure 4; Figure 5).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    This systematic review was initiated because mailuoning is widely used in clinical practice in China; it has even been considered as standard treatment in clinical practice and used as a standard control intervention in clinical trials, but its effectiveness and safety in acute ischaemic stroke are still uncertain. Compared with our previous systematic review of mailuoning for acute ischaemic stroke, we identified and included six new trials (Ding 2010; Huo 2009; Huo 2012; Lv 2010; Wang 2012; Zang 2010). Ding 2010, which only included people with acute lacune cerebral infarction, measured cognitive function using the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) scale; the other trials measured the improvement of neurological deficit. Furthermore, two trials observed the activities of daily living (ADL) score at three months (Huo 2012; Yu 2006). In our original protocol, we had planned to include quasi‐randomised controlled trials to have more trials for analysis. However, it is likely that studies using weaker methods of treatment allocation and concealment would be subject to bias and thus uninformative; hence, we changed the protocol to focus on randomised controlled trials (RCTs) only. We had also planned to perform random‐effects meta‐analysis if the data had high levels of heterogeneity (I² statistic > than 50%), but we could not get a reliable estimate of heterogeneity, so we used fixed‐effect meta‐analysis throughout.

    Results showed that one trial, which we regarded to be of a better methodological quality than the other studies and with longer follow‐up data from treatment start to measurement date, compared extract of Ginkgo biloba leaves, mailuoning, and puerarin with placebo, but failed to show that mailuoning was superior to placebo in the treatment of acute ischaemic stroke. However, this trial was very small, with an unbalanced sample size, and with a high loss of participants that reduced the power to detect a difference between the two groups (Yu 2006). Meta‐analysis of the other 20 included trials showed that the proportion of participants with improvement in neurological score at the end of the treatment period was significantly higher among those treated with mailuoning than in the control participants; three trials reporting neurological improvement with the European Stroke Scale (ESS) as a continuous scale also showed the same result. However, these results should be interpreted with caution because of the inferior methodological quality of the trials. One trial measuring cognitive function showed a significant improvement in the treatment group compared with the control group (Ding 2010). Of the two trials measuring ADL, one trial showed a significant improvement in the treatment group compared with the control group (Huo 2012); the other did not (Yu 2006). These results should also be interpreted with caution because of the inferior methodological quality of the trials.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    For the following reasons, the trials had limited usefulness.

    1. In the 21 trials, the period of follow‐up ranged from 14 to 38 days after the start of treatment, except in two trials where the period of follow‐up was three months. The follow‐up time was insufficient to assess the long‐term effects of mailuoning. Spontaneous recovery does not plateau until five to six months after stroke, especially in severe stroke (Duncan 2000). The short follow‐up cannot confirm the long‐term effect of the intervention. Furthermore, primary outcome measures should be at the level of disability (functional outcome) and ideally also quality of life; 18 of the 21 included trials focused on the level of impairment (neurological deficits); one trial focused on cognitive function over a short period. Impairment outcome measures were the surrogate end point.

    2. It was remarkable that none of the included trials reported any deaths. One reason may be that deaths occurred, but the trialists did not report them. It was also possible that the trials did not include participants who had severe strokes, although the inclusion criteria did not clearly state this. Since we could not assess whether there was differential mortality in the treatment groups that might have affected an assessment of impairment or disability in survivors, there is a strong suggestion that the results of the trials were not reported with sufficient rigour.

    3. Adverse effects are as important as the efficacy of the interventions in clinical trials. Twelve trials in this review observed adverse events, and two trials reported them. The two trials reported three participants with minor adverse events in the treatment group and two participants in the control group. The 21 trials did not report any major side‐effects, but adverse effects of mailuoning were not uncommon (Lu 2011; Wu 2009): One article reported the rate of adverse effects as 1.898% (Lu 2011). The China Food and Drug Administration also reported the adverse effects of mailuoning in 2012: The major adverse effects were respiratory system lesion, general lesion, and cardiovascular system lesion (CFDA 2012). Therefore, we need to assess the safety of mailuoning objectively.

    Quality of the evidence

    The limitations of the 21 trials were as follows.

    1. There was uncertainty as to whether or not the trials were truly randomised with adequate concealment of allocation, which allows the possibility of selection bias. Only two trials, Wang 2007; Yu 2006, allocated participants by random number (Yu 2006 concealed allocation with opaque sealed envelopes). Nineteen trials did not mention the method of allocation or concealment; therefore, concealment was not adequate. This could have led to selection bias. It has been reported that odds ratios are exaggerated by 41% for inadequately concealed trials and by 30% for unclearly concealed trials (Schulz 1995). It is therefore plausible that mailuoning is truly ineffective, and the apparent benefits are simply due to bias arising from the methodological weaknesses of the studies.

    2. Outcome assessment should preferably be blinded. Only one trial reported that they had blinded the results assessment (Yu 2006). Whether the other 20 trials used a blinded method to assess outcomes was unclear, leading to the possibility of measurement bias. As a result, the trials could have either exaggerated or minimised the effect of the intervention.

    3. Sample sizes were too small.

    Potential biases in the review process

    The experience of the present authors is that negative trial results are not easy to get published in China. Therefore, publication bias is probably high in the Chinese medical literature. Among the 21 included trials, all from China, only one trial reported negative results: It was a student thesis completed at the Nanjing University of Traditional Chinese Medicine in 2005 but not published in a peer‐reviewed journal (Yu 2006). We made considerable efforts to obtain all results from pharmaceutical companies ‐ there were no negative results available. Some level of publication bias is likely even though this was not revealed by the funnel plot (see Figure 5 for potential publication bias by funnel plot).

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We identified seven reviews about mailuoning for ischaemic stroke, all published in Chinese, but the criteria for considering studies for these reviews were different: Five reviews included RCTs (Chen 2010; Li 2006a; Li 2006b; Shi 2009; Zhang 2013), and two reviews included RCTs and quasi‐RCTs (Ma 2005; Zhang 2010). The types of participants were different: Three reviews included ischaemic stroke participants within 30 days of onset (Ma 2005; Li 2006a; Li 2006b); the others did not report the criteria from onset to treatment (Chen 2010; Shi 2009; Zhang 2010; Zhang 2013). The search methods were also different and incomplete among the reviews, so the included studies were different. All reviews had appraised neurological improvement and safety of mailuoning. Two reviews also appraised the laboratory index; the conclusions were that mailuoning was effective for ischaemic stroke, but their conclusions were not convincing.
