Pressure‐garment therapy for preventing hypertrophic scarring after burn injury

    • Background

    Burn damage to skin often results in scarring; however in some individuals the failure of normal wound‐healing processes results in excessive scar tissue formation, termed 'hypertrophic scarring'. The most commonly used method for the prevention and treatment of hypertrophic scarring is pressure‐garment therapy (PGT). PGT is considered standard care globally; however, there is continued uncertainty around its effectiveness.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of pressure‐garment therapy for the prevention of hypertrophic scarring after burn injury.

    Search methods

    We used standard, extensive Cochrane search methods. We searched CENTRAL, MEDLINE, Embase, two other databases, and two trials registers on 8 June 2023 with reference checking, citation searching, and contact with study authors to identify additional studies.

    Selection criteria

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) comparing PGT (alone or in combination with other scar‐management therapies) with scar management therapies not including PGT, or comparing different PGT pressures or different types of PGT.

    Data collection and analysis

    At least two review authors independently selected trials for inclusion using predetermined inclusion criteria, extracted data, and assessed risk of bias using the Cochrane RoB 1 tool. We assessed the certainty of evidence using GRADE.

    Main results

    We included 15 studies in this review (1179 participants), 14 of which (1057 participants) presented useable data. The sample size of included studies ranged from 17 to 159 participants. Most studies included both adults and children. Eight studies compared a pressure garment (with or without another scar management therapy) with scar management therapy alone, five studies compared the same pressure garment at a higher pressure versus a lower pressure, and two studies compared two different types of pressure garments. Studies used a variety of pressure garments (e.g. in‐house manufactured or a commercial brand). Types of scar management therapies included were lanolin massage, topical silicone gel, silicone sheet/dressing, and heparin sodium ointment.

    Meta‐analysis was not possible as there was significant clinical and methodological heterogeneity between studies. Main outcome measures were scar improvement assessed using the Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS) or the Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale (POSAS) (or both), pain, pruritus, quality of life, adverse events, and adherence to therapy. Studies additionally reported a further 14 outcomes, mostly individual scar parameters, some of which contributed to global scores on the VSS or POSAS. The amount of evidence for each individual outcome was limited. Most studies had a short follow‐up, which may have affected results as the full effect of any therapy on scar healing may not be seen until around 18 months.

    PGT versus no treatment/lanolin

    We included five studies (378 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on whether PGT improves scars as assessed by the VSS compared with no treatment/lanolin. The evidence is also very uncertain for pain, pruritus, adverse events, and adherence. No study used the POSAS or assessed quality of life. One additional study (122 participants) did not report useable data.

    PGT versus silicone

    We included three studies (359 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the effect of PGT compared with silicone, as assessed by the VSS and POSAS. The evidence is also very uncertain for pain, pruritus, quality of life, adverse events, adherence, and other scar parameters. It is possible that silicone may result in fewer adverse events or better adherence compared with PGT but this was also based on very low‐certainty evidence.

    PGT plus silicone versus no treatment/lanolin

    We included two studies (200 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on whether PGT plus silicone improves scars as assessed by the VSS compared with no treatment/lanolin. The evidence is also very uncertain for pain, pruritus, and adverse events. No study used the POSAS or assessed quality of life or adherence.

    PGT plus silicone versus silicone

    We included three studies (359 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the effect of PGT plus silicone compared with silicone, as assessed by the VSS and POSAS. The evidence is also very uncertain for pain, pruritus, quality of life, adverse events, and adherence.

    PGT plus scar management therapy including silicone versus scar management therapy including silicone

    We included one study (88 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the effect of PGT plus scar management therapy including silicone versus scar management therapy including silicone, as assessed by the VSS and POSAS. The evidence is also very uncertain for pain, pruritus, quality of life, adverse events, and adherence.

    High‐pressure versus low‐pressure garments

    We included five studies (262 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the effect of high pressure versus low pressure PGT on adverse events and adherence. No study used the VSS or the POSAS or assessed pain, pruritus, or quality of life.

    Different types of PGT (Caroskin Tricot + an adhesive silicone gel sheet versus Gecko Nanoplast (silicone gel bandage))

    We included one study (60 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the effect of Caroskin Tricot versus Gecko Nanoplast on the POSAS, pain, pruritus, and adverse events. The study did not use the VSS or assess quality of life or adherence.

    Different types of pressure garments (Jobst versus Tubigrip)

    We included one study (110 participants). The evidence is very uncertain on the adherence to either Jobst or Tubigrip. This study did not report any other outcomes.

    Authors' conclusions

    There is insufficient evidence to recommend using either PGT or an alternative for preventing hypertrophic scarring after burn injury. PGT is already commonly used in practice and it is possible that continuing to do so may provide some benefit to some people. However, until more evidence becomes available, it may be appropriate to allow patient preference to guide therapy.

    • Pressure garments for preventing hypertrophic scarring after burn injury

    Key messages

    – We do not know if pressure garment therapy is more effective than other scar treatment options (e.g. silicone gel/sheets, massage with lanolin) for preventing an abnormally thick and raised scar (hypertrophic scarring).

    – Future studies should include more participants who are monitored for longer; should clearly report on any other treatments that are offered alongside or as an alternative to pressure garment therapy; and measure outcomes that are most important to people with burn injuries.

    What are pressure garments used for?

    Pressure garments are tight elastic garments (such as dressings or bandages) that are commonly worn over a burn injury as soon as the wound is judged clinically to have sufficiently healed. They are worn for 23 hours a day, usually for 12 to 18 months. The tightness of the garment is thought to help reduce hypertrophic scarring.

    Hypertrophic scarring is when skin does not heal normally after an injury. This results in larger scars forming and can cause pain, itching, and reduced movement in affected areas. Many factors are involved in the development of hypertrophic scarring, such as age, ethnic group, and hormone levels, as well as the burn size, thickness, and location, but not much is known about why some people develop this type of scarring.

    What did we want to find out?

    We wanted to find out whether pressure garments are effective for preventing hypertrophic scarring compared with other scar treatment options such as silicone gels and sheets, massage with lanolin, ointment containing the medicine heparin sodium, or no treatment.

    What did we do?

    We searched for randomised controlled trials that compared:

    – pressure garments versus other treatment options; or

    – different pressures being applied by pressure garments;

    – different types of pressure garment.

    Randomised controlled trials are studies in which participants are assigned randomly to one of two or more treatment groups. This is the best way to ensure that groups of participants are similar, and that any differences between the groups are due to the treatment that is being evaluated.

    We compared and summarised the trial results, and rated our confidence in the evidence, based on factors such as study methods and sizes.

    What did we find?

    We found 15 studies that involved 1179 people with (mostly burn) injuries that resulted in scars; 14 of these (1057 participants) presented useable data. Most studies included adults and children. The largest study had 159 participants and the smallest study had 17 participants. The studies were conducted in countries around the world, but most were done in China (five) and the USA (five). Most studies lasted for around six months; only three studies lasted for 12 months or more. The studies assessed a wide range of scar‐related outcomes, for example: scar thickness, redness, itching, pain, complications from the treatment, or used a rating tool that included several scar characteristics. However, not all studies assessed the same outcomes.

    We are unsure whether pressure garments (with or without silicone) improve scar‐related outcomes compared with other treatments (with or without silicone), as the quality of the available evidence is very low. We did not find any evidence to suggest that pressure garments or silicone cause any serious harms, though a small amount of evidence suggested the pressure garments might cause more discomfort; however, we are unsure about this.

    What are the limitations of the evidence?

    We are very uncertain about the benefits or harms of pressure garment therapy compared with other scar management therapies for preventing hypertrophic scarring. Most studies involved only small numbers of people, and used methods that may mean some findings were biased. In some studies, people were aware of the treatment they were getting, which might have influenced how they rated any improvement of their scars. Some studies also included people who had scars resulting from injuries other than burns. Most studies were not long enough to show longer‐term changes (12 months or more) to scars.

    How up to date is this evidence?

    The evidence is up to date to June 2023.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is insufficient evidence to recommend either the use of pressure‐garment therapy (PGT) or an alternative for preventing hypertrophic scarring after burn injury, or for recommending a particular PGT pressure. We found no evidence of serious adverse events with any therapy.

    PGT is already commonly used in practice and it is possible that continuing to do so may provide some benefit to patients; however, it is also possible that using an alternative that includes silicone may provide some benefits. At present, the choice of therapy could be guided by patient preference.

    There is currently no evidence on whether there are particular patient groups that might benefit more or less from PGT (e.g. age group, severity of burn scar, location of scar, etc.) or which type of pressure garment should be used and at which pressure.

    Implications for research

    Given the continued uncertainty around the benefit of PGT, and the adverse effects on quality of life that hypertrophic scarring can have, further research is necessary.

    Many studies included in this review were limited by small sample sizes, and included participants with a range of injuries. Future studies should recruit larger numbers of participants and ensure that if mixed populations are included (both in terms of age and mechanism of injury), subgroup analysis is carried out and clearly reported.

    Future studies should have longer follow‐up periods. Many studies in this review had only short periods of follow‐up (six months or less) and, therefore, it was impossible to consider longer‐term outcomes, which are likely to be important in scarring following a burn injury. Any treatment arm in future trials should include the range of available standard care options, including silicone, so that the role of PGT in addition to comprehensive standard care can be assessed.

    Many included studies were limited by large numbers of participants withdrawing and inadequate reporting of these participants. Future research should attempt to minimise participants being lost to follow‐up and ensure reasons for withdrawal are well reported.

    Analysis in this review was complicated by the large number of outcomes reported by small numbers of included studies. The development and use of a set of core outcomes for scar healing would result in more studies consistently reporting the same important outcomes. Outcomes that we considered to be important were reported by few or very few studies (e.g. global scar assessment scales, quality of life, adverse events, and adherence).

    Future research may wish to consider other aspects in the use of PGT. For example, one feasibility study in a few participants evaluated the use of automated (three‐dimensional scan‐based) versus manual measurements for constructing the garments (Kisch 2021). Automated measurement can minimise contact with scarred skin and has the potential for greater customisation.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Burn damage to skin, caused by thermal, electrical, or chemical sources, is a common injury worldwide (Peck 2011). In 2004, approximately 11 million people experienced a burn requiring medical attention globally (WHO 2008). It is estimated that there are 180,000 deaths from burn injuries every year, with the vast majority (over 95%) occurring in low‐ and middle‐income countries (WHO 2018). One systematic review found that the incidence and severity of burns occurring in high‐income countries are on a downward trend attributed to advances in burn care, socioeconomic development, and burn prevention programmes, but evidence for low‐ and middle‐income countries is lacking (Smolle 2017). Most burns are not fatal, but the resultant scarring can cause both cosmetic and functional problems (Ripper 2009). Scar formation is an inevitable consequence of injury to the skin (Urioste 1999). Healing is a complex process dependent on several immunological responses, and three successive stages of healing must occur: inflammation, granulation tissue formation (proliferation), and remodelling (Markiewicz‐Gospodarek 2022). Maturation takes place during the remodelling stage when structural proteins are laid down creating an extracellular matrix; this matrix then remodels into scar tissue (Tiwari 2012). The maturation phase can last up to 24 months (Finnerty 2016). In some individuals, failure of normal wound‐healing processes results in excessive scar tissue formation, termed 'hypertrophic scarring' (Atiyeh 2007). The exact mechanism resulting in hypertrophic scar formation is poorly understood, but there is some evidence to suggest that dysregulated immunological responses are responsible for an exaggerated inflammatory phase early in the wound‐healing process (van der Veer 2009). Estimates suggest that hypertrophic scarring affects 1.5% to 4.5% of the general population, with hypertrophic scarring following a burn injury affecting 32% to 67% of people (Atiyeh 2007). Hypertrophic scarring is red, thick, and rigid, and causes a variety of problems including pain, itching, and reduced movement in affected areas (Bloemen 2009; Friedstat 2014). It tends to occur within four to eight weeks following injury (Gauglitz 2011). Keloid scarring may also occur after burn injury in genetically susceptible individuals, but is relatively rare. Keloid scarring is similar in appearance to hypertrophic scarring, but keloid scarring spreads beyond the margins of the original wound whereas hypertrophic scarring does not. Keloid scarring can develop later than hypertrophic scarring, up to several years after the initial injury (Gauglitz 2011). Keloid scars are treated very differently to hypertrophic scars (Gauglitz 2013), and as such are not the subject of this review.

    Many factors are involved in the development of hypertrophic scarring, such as age, ethnic group, and hormone levels, as well as the burn size, thickness, and location (Niessen 1999). The involvement of these factors in hypertrophic scar formation is poorly understood, and robust evidence is currently lacking (Butzelaar 2016). However, it is generally accepted that the most important factor in predicting the development of hypertrophic scarring is healing time, which is related to burn size and thickness (Chipp 2017; Miller 2005), and in particular whether the burn was partial or full thickness with the majority of full thickness burns resulting in hypertrophic scars (Schmieder 2023).

    There are many methods of assessing scars based on various factors such as colour, texture, and thickness. The most commonly used subjective assessment scales are the Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale (POSAS) (Draaijers 2004) and the Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS) (Bae 2014). Laser imaging, ultrasound imaging, cutometer, colorimetry, and three‐dimensional cameras can also be used for objective scar assessment (Lee 2016). Current recommendations are that a panel of devices should be used for comprehensive scar assessment (Lee 2016).

    Description of the intervention

    The most commonly used method for the prevention and treatment of hypertrophic scarring is pressure‐garment therapy (PGT) (Anzarut 2009; Engrav 2010; Ward 1991), which costs the UK National Health Service approximately GBP 2.2 million a year (Moiemen 2018). PGT involves the wearing of tight elastic garments in the area of the burn as soon as wound healing has occurred and pressure therapy can be tolerated. The garments should be worn for 23 hours a day (allowing removal for up to one hour for bathing and personal care), for an average of 12 to 18 months, depending on scar severity (Anzarut 2009; Yildiz 2007). Other estimates on the number of recommended months that PGTs should be worn to achieve an optimally treated scar range from six to 24 months (Moiemen 2018). There are two main types of pressure garments: ready‐to‐wear/pre‐sized garments and custom‐made garments. Ready‐to‐wear garments have the advantages of being immediately available and cheaper than custom‐made garments, but adjustments are usually required for a better fit and to ensure they exert enough pressure (Macintyre 2006). The exact amount of pressure required is not known (Atiyeh 2013; Huang 2013), but it is thought that less than 15 mmHg and more than 40 mmHg does not result in the required effect (Bloemen 2009; Park 2011). Most typically, pressures of 15 mmHg to 25 mmHg are used in clinical settings (Ai 2017; Sharp 2007). There is some evidence to indicate that pressures over 30 mmHg are harmful (Ai 2017). The exact amount of pressure exerted by a garment is often not known, particularly where the reduction method is used for manufacture. With this method, the dimensions of the body part with the burn wound are measured and the garment is made with a 10% to 20% reduction factor for that measurement (Atiyeh 2013). Exerted pressure may also not be uniform and depends on factors such as curvature of the body part, and the underlying type of tissue; there is also a loss of tension over time (Atiyeh 2013). Pressure monitors may be useful in ensuring consistency of pressures applied when wearing pressure garments, but this is not routinely undertaken (Syron‐Jones 2022).

    Wearing pressure garments can be challenging for several reasons, including discomfort (itching, pain, sweating), embarrassment at wearing the visible garments, and the demands of the duration and intensity of treatment (Johnson 1994; Macintyre 2006; Ripper 2009). Such challenges can result in treatment dropout rates of 30% to 59% in adults (Ripper 2009). Factors shown to help improve patient adherence to PGT include good social support and a good doctor–patient relationship (DiMatteo 2004; Moiemen 2018; Ripper 2009).

    Other forms of treatment, used independently of or in conjunction with PGT, include massage with moisturising creams or oils (Lin 2022), topical heparin (Manzoor 2019), silicone sheets/sprays/gels applied to the burn, corticosteroid injections, and laser therapy (Bloemen 2009). These treatments are thought to variously influence wound collagen remodelling, inflammation or angiogenesis or both, but the exact mechanisms of action have not been fully elucidated (Atiyeh 2013). They are most likely to be used in combination with PGT rather than as an alternative.

    How the intervention might work

    PGT is thought to work in two ways, by affecting collagen remodelling during the wound‐healing process. First, the tight pressure restricts blood flow, and therefore oxygen availability, to the scar, which is thought to accelerate the maturation of the scar (Clark 1996; Ripper 2009). Second, the constant pressure exerted on the scar tissue forces the collagen fibres of the new tissue to grow systematically and more in line with the normal pattern of healthy skin fibres (Clark 1996; Ripper 2009). Therefore, PGT is most effective if initiated when the wound is fully closed but the scar is still fragile and likely to break down (Bloemen 2009). For best results, the pressure garments need to be worn until scar maturation.

    Why it is important to do this review

    Scarring can have serious functional and cosmetic implications, and may negatively impact upon a person's quality of life (Atiyeh 2013; Ripper 2009). Scarring is very visible due to the differences in colour, pigmentation, and texture of affected tissue and can lead to psychological problems such as stigmatisation and low self‐esteem. Scarring can also be very uncomfortable in terms of pain, pruritus, and dryness (Ai 2017). Interventions aim to improve functionality in terms of both work and leisure activities (Atiyeh 2013).

    PGT in its currently used form originated in the 1970s from a leading burns centre in the USA where there was immediate improvement of hypertrophic scarring observed in individuals with major burns (Atiyeh 2013). The practice spread widely to other centres in the USA and beyond, despite the lack of well‐designed clinical trials to show the efficacy of PGT (Moiemen 2018). This was probably facilitated by the ready availability of the garments, and substantial industry involvement and drive towards commercialisation (Linares 1993). PGT is now standard care in almost all burn centres globally, but there is continued uncertainty around its effectiveness, including perceived benefit to its recipients (Moiemen 2018).

    Laser treatment and percutaneous needling are gaining recognition as viable options to treat hypertrophic scarring, but these treatments are used mainly after scar maturation (Finnerty 2016).

    One systematic review based on six studies was unable to draw definitive conclusions (Anzarut 2009). A more recent systematic review included 12 studies and found some evidence of improvement as measured by the VSS score and in pigmentation, redness, and scar hardness (Ai 2017). Inclusion was limited to studies using a pressure of 15 mmHg to 25 mmHg. There appeared to be insufficient consideration of heterogeneity in the analyses, with studies being pooled regardless of comparator (no and low pressure), age (adults and children), or co‐intervention (e.g. pressure therapy and silicone). It was also unclear if data included in meta‐analyses were based on intention‐to‐treat analyses, and whether studies not included in the meta‐analyses reported relevant outcomes. Adverse events and compliance were also insufficiently considered. Since the publication of this systematic review, there has been at least one other relevant study (Pegasus) (Moiemen 2018), and there are a number of ongoing studies.

    Therefore, a complete assessment of all the evidence, including full consideration of heterogeneity (including different levels of pressure), all comparator groups, and all relevant outcomes was warranted.

    Objectives

    To evaluate the benefits and harms of pressure‐garment therapy for the prevention of hypertrophic scarring after burn injury.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    Studies eligible for inclusion in this review were published and unpublished randomised controlled trials (RCTs), including 1. between‐participant comparisons where participants were either allocated to the intervention or to a comparator, and 2. split‐body designs, where different wounds within the same participant were allocated to different treatments. There were no restrictions on publication status or language. We excluded studies using quasi‐randomisation.

    Types of participants

    We included people in any population or care setting and with any size, depth, or cause of burn (thermal, electrical, or chemical) who were likely to develop hypertrophic scarring and who were suitable for hypertrophic scarring prevention therapy. People likely to develop hypertrophic scarring were those whose wounds had taken around three or more weeks to heal or who had received skin grafts as a result of their burn injury. We included studies with mixed populations (i.e. people with burn scars as well as scars from surgery or trauma).

    Types of interventions

    Eligible for inclusion were studies with PGT (any pressure) alone or in combination with other scar‐management therapies. We included any RCTs in which the use of PGT (any type or duration of treatment), a difference in pressure, or the type of pressure garment during the treatment period was the only systematic difference between treatment groups. We did not specify a minimum length of follow‐up.

    Types of comparison

    Any other type of scar‐management treatment not including PGT, or PGT using a different (lower) pressure, or a different type of pressure garment. We excluded studies comparing different durations of the same PGT.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    • Scar improvement assessed by scar‐rating scales such as the Vancouver Scar Scale (VSS) and the Patient and Observer Scar Assessment Scale (POSAS) or other verified assessment scale with results reported as mean and standard deviation (SD), or median and range. Scar‐rating scales attempt to quantify overall scar severity by measuring a range of scar parameters, such as thickness, vascularity, pigmentation, and pliability. Some, such as the POSAS, also include pain and pruritus. There is currently no 'gold standard' scar‐rating scale, as none are thought to include all aspects relevant to a patient's quality of life (e.g. cosmetic, functional, or psychological aspects) (Nguyen 2015). We reported all time points for each study.

    Secondary outcomes

    • Individual scar parameters (surface area/volume, texture, thickness, vascularity, pigmentation, and pliability), regardless of how measured (subjective (e.g. observer‐ or participant‐rated) or objective (e.g. using a measurement tool such as a colorimeter or tonometer), or both).

    • Range of movement (ROM) assessment (where the scar was over a joint), measured using a goniometer.

    • Pain measured by any relevant scale/tool.

    • Pruritus measured by any relevant scale/tool.

    • Adverse events/complications (e.g. wound breakdown and blistering), where this was reported as the proportion of participants in each group with an adverse event.

    • Participant health‐related quality of life/health status, measured using a standardised generic questionnaire such as EuroQol Five‐dimensions (EQ‐5D), 36‐item Short Form (SF‐36), 12‐item Short Form (SF‐12), or 6‐item Short Form (SF‐6), or wound‐specific questionnaires such as the Burn Specific Health Scale – Brief (BSHS‐B).

    • Adherence to therapy

    • Any other outcome measuring the impact of the burn/scar on the participant (e.g. the Brisbane Burn Scar Impact Profile).

    Some outcomes in paediatric trials were parent‐reported. Outcomes affecting the participant as a whole (such as quality of life) could not be measured where an RCT had a split‐body design.

    At present there are no defined minimal clinically important differences or thresholds for either the primary or secondary outcomes.

    Two review authors (IMH, JD or KCL) independently extracted data using a standardised and piloted data extraction form. We resolved disagreements through discussion or through consulting a third review author (KCL or NM). Key data extracted included:

    • study characteristics and methods: year of publication, study design, randomisation method and unit of randomisation, start date, country of study, setting, length of follow‐up;

    • participants: number, age, inclusion and exclusion criteria, percentage male, severity and cause of burn;

    • intervention: type of garment, time from 95% healing to starting PGT, duration of treatment period, pressure used, any other therapies used;

    • comparator therapy: standard care, other therapies, (lower) pressure used;

    • primary and secondary outcomes: method of scar improvement assessment, how measured, time points measured, scores at baseline/post‐treatment (intervention and control groups), change scores, summary scores;

    • funding and potential conflicts of interest amongst the study authors.

    We recorded the extracted data in RevMan Web (RevMan Web 2021). We contacted study authors to request missing outcome data.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (IMH, JD or KCL) independently assessed risk of bias of included RCTs using the Cochrane RoB 1 tool (Higgins 2017), which is displayed in Appendix 2. Domains related to RoB 1 include selection bias, performance bias, detection bias, attrition bias, reporting bias, and other potential forms of bias. Due to the nature of the intervention, it was difficult to blind participants or those administering the intervention. Blinding may be possible to an extent where different pressures are compared (e.g. high versus lower/ineffective pressure). As pressure garments were by design tight‐fitting, they can leave marks on the skin when removed, so blind outcome assessment of scars may also be difficult to achieve. Review authors made a decision of 'low', 'high', or 'unclear' risk of bias based on the criteria given for making risk of bias judgements. We assessed risk of bias separately for different outcomes where there were differences in, for example, blinding and loss to follow‐up. We recorded reasons underpinning the decisions. The review authors resolved disagreements though discussion or through consulting a third review author (KCL or NM).

    Measures of treatment effect

    Most outcomes were continuous (e.g. changes on scar‐rating or quality‐of‐life scales), and we used the mean difference (MD) and SDs to present findings in forest plots. When comparing the difference between groups, our preference was for a follow‐up score adjusted for baseline (using analysis of covariance (ANCOVA)) compared with a change score. Only one study specified use of ANCOVA (Moiemen 2018). As we did not undertake pooling of results, we did not consider the standardised mean difference (SMD) with 95% confidence intervals (CI) where studies measured the same outcome using different scales; instead, we presented these data separately or narratively. We did not present data using risk ratio (RR) for dichotomous data or hazard ratios (HR) for time‐to‐event data.

    Unit of analysis issues

    In some studies, the unit of analysis was the participant (one scar per participant) and, in other studies, the unit of analysis was the scar (more than one scar per participant). For the latter studies, we considered in the risk of bias assessment whether authors used analysis methods to account for such clustering. Some studies also measured within‐participant differences, where one half of a wound was treated differently to the other half. We considered the risk of bias likely to arise from such a design in our risk of bias assessment. As we have not undertaken meta‐analysis, we have presented data in forest plots where possible for illustrative purposes, regardless of method of analysis.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted study authors if relevant outcome data were insufficiently reported (e.g. requesting if any additional data were available for subgroups, requesting if data were available as means rather than medians). We also contacted authors of ongoing trials and relevant conference abstracts to request interim or additional data. Where measures of variance were missing, we calculated them where possible (e.g. calculating SD from 95% CIs using the method outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Lefebvre 2021)). For analyses, we used data based on an intention‐to‐treat analysis where possible (e.g. based on last observation carried forward for continuous data).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We considered clinical and methodological heterogeneity when deciding whether to combine data quantitatively. Sources of clinical heterogeneity related to the type of therapy (e.g. PGT with or without additional scar‐management techniques, level of pressure, type of pressure garments), comparator (e.g. alternative pressure, different therapy, no active therapy), and population (aged 16 years or under versus adults; all with burn wounds or mixed populations (burn, trauma, or surgical scars)). There were also differences in length of follow‐up and use of outcome measures (e.g. types of scar‐assessment tool). Methodological heterogeneity related to the type of randomisation (e.g. between participants or within participant, or between participant or scars).

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We tried to minimise reporting biases by searching multiple databases and clinical trial registries. As part of the risk of bias assessment, we looked for incompletely reported or missing outcome data. We were unable to produce funnel plots to assess the possible presence of reporting bias as we were unable to undertake meta‐analyses.

    Data synthesis

    We grouped findings first by comparison, then by outcome (PGT versus other scar management therapy; higher versus lower pressure; different types of pressure garment). Due to the considerable heterogeneity between studies, no meta‐analyses were undertaken. However, study effect estimates (mean difference and 95% CIs) were presented in forest plots for illustrative purposes where there were two or more studies reporting the same outcome and making the same comparison. Studies were subgrouped by comparison within forest plots. We presented all time points, including baseline, to highlight potential imbalances at the start of the study. As no pooling was undertaken, there was no investigation of statistical heterogeneity. Findings from individual studies not represented in analyses or summary of findings tables were described narratively and tabulated.

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    Formal subgroup analyses by age, location of burn, burn severity, length of follow‐up, or time from 95% healing to start of PGT were not possible. Findings were grouped by comparison and outcome, and we used footnotes in the forest plots to highlight additional differences between studies in terms of population (age and type of wound/scar (burn only or mixed)) and follow‐up times.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We used risk of bias assessments to guide interpretation of our findings. Formal sensitivity analyses were not possible as no meta‐analyses were undertaken.

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We presented the results for different comparators in the summary of findings tables for the following outcomes: scar improvement assessed by VSS or POSAS, pain, pruritus, adverse events, quality of life, and adherence. We did not include scar parameters in the summary of findings tables (Differences between protocol and review).

    We rated the certainty of the body of evidence as high, moderate, low, or very low, depending on the directness of the evidence in addressing the study question(s), the risk of bias in the included studies (methodological quality), the precision of effect estimates, the consistency of the evidence, and the risk of publication bias (GRADE Handbook).

    As no meta‐analysis was conducted, we have not presented risks in comparator groups, or summary estimates. Instead, the direction of effect has been described. We used the guide by Murad 2017 to assess certainty in the evidence in the absence of meta‐analysis. The absence of defined minimal clinically important differences for primary or secondary outcomes meant it was not possible to use this information in making decisions on rating of imprecision.

    Results

    Description of studies

    Results of the search

    The searches identified 273 records from electronic databases, and an additional four records were identified from existing systematic reviews. After excluding duplicate records, we screened the titles and abstracts of 261 records. We assessed the full texts of 24 articles for eligibility and included 15 studies (reported in 16 publications) in this review. Of the remaining eight records, four were ongoing studies, and four were excluded for the following reasons: duplicate population (Moore 2000), ineligible populations (ACTRN12610000127000; NCT01602458), and ineligible intervention (Kisch 2021). A summary of the search process is show in Figure 1.

    Included studies

    Study design and setting

    Studies were conducted in six countries: China (Candy 2010; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; Xu 2005), Australia (Wiseman 2020), Hong Kong (Li‐Tsang 2010a), the USA (Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990), the UK (Moiemen 2018), and Belgium (Moortgat 2019; van den Kerckhove 2005).

    Studies were set in specialist burn centres (Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Kealey 1990; Moortgat 2019; van den Kerckhove 2005), or hospitals (Candy 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020; Xu 2005).

    One study used a between‐scar design (Candy 2010), one study used a within‐wound comparison design (Engrav 2010), and the remaining 13 used a between‐participant design (Chang 1995; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Wiseman 2020; Xu 2005).

    Ten studies had one scar/wound per participant (Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; Xu 2005), and five studies had one or more scar/wounds per participant (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Moiemen 2018; van den Kerckhove 2005; Wiseman 2020).

    Length of follow‐up was between two and seven months for nine studies, up to 12 months for three studies (Engrav 2010; Moiemen 2018; Wiseman 2020), and 18 months for one study (Groce 2001). Two studies followed participants up to "scar maturation" (Chang 1995; Kealey 1990).

    One study had two publications contributing data (Wiseman 2020).

    Participants

    A total of 1179 participants (67.7% male) were randomised across all 15 studies. Sample sizes ranged from 17 (Candy 2010) to 159 (Wiseman 2020). Three studies had exclusively children (Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Wiseman 2020), two had exclusively adults (Candy 2010; van den Kerckhove 2005), one did not specify the age of the participants (Li‐Tsang 2010a), and the remaining nine studies included both children and adults (Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; Xu 2005).

    Eleven studies had participants who exclusively had burn injuries (Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Moiemen 2018; van den Kerckhove 2005; Wiseman 2020; Xu 2005), and four studies had mixed populations where the injuries had a variety of causes including burns, surgical scars, and other traumas (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004). These four studies provided no details about the number of participants with each type of injury. We contacted the authors of the mixed population studies to request subgroup data, but they either did not respond or were unable to provide such data.

    Interventions

    Five studies compared the same pressure garment (note: this was a different garment for each study, see below) at a high pressure versus a low pressure (Candy 2010; Engrav 2010; Groce 2001; Li 2009; van den Kerckhove 2005), eight studies compared a pressure garment with another scar management therapy as a comparator (Chang 1995; Groce 2000; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020; Xu 2005), and two studies compared two different types of pressure garments (Kealey 1990; Moortgat 2019).

    Types of pressure garments evaluated included in‐house manufactured garments (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010b), Medical Z Inc (Engrav 2010), JOBST (Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990), Tubigrip (Kealey 1990), Caroskin Tricot (Moortgat 2019), Gecko Nanoplast (Moortgat 2019), Tricolast (van den Kerckhove 2005), Anvarex (van den Kerckhove 2005), and Therapeutic Support Laboratory (Wiseman 2020). Four studies did not specify the type of pressure garment used (Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Tsang 2004; Xu 2005). One study used either in‐house manufactured garments or purchased garments depending on study centre (Moiemen 2018). Other scar management therapies used as comparators included heparin sodium ointment (Xu 2005), topical silicone gel (Wiseman 2020), silicone sheet/dressing (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), and lanolin massage (Li‐Tsang 2010b).

    We structured findings by the following comparisons:

    • PGT versus no treatment/lanolin massage;

    • PGT versus silicone;

    • PGT plus silicone versus no treatment/lanolin massage;

    • PGT plus silicone versus silicone;

    • higher versus lower pressure; or

    • different types of pressure garment.

    One study is represented independently (Moiemen 2018); here the comparators were PGT plus standard care which could include silicone versus standard care which could include silicone. This study did not explicitly plan to compare PGT with silicone, but rather aimed to have similar standard care in both arms with access to a range of standard treatments.

    Excluded studies

    We excluded four studies for the following reasons.

    • Duplicate population (Moore 2000). This study had the same population as an already included study (Engrav 2010), and was excluded after contact with the authors seeking clarification about the populations of each study.

    • Ineligible population (ACTRN12610000127000; NCT01602458). The populations of these studies did not have wounds directly caused by burns.

    • Ineligible intervention (Kisch 2021). This study used the same standard pressure garment in each arm (no difference in material or pressure used), and was comparing different methods for measuring people for the garment.

    Ongoing studies

    There are four ongoing studies (NCT03469830; NCT03628495; NCT04456543; NCT04643223).

    Allocation

    Random sequence generation

    Eight studies had low risk of bias for this domain. Four studies used computer‐generated random numbers or random number tables for randomisation (Kealey 1990; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Moiemen 2018; Wiseman 2020), one study used envelope randomisation (Li 2009), and one study used drawing of lots (Li‐Tsang 2010b). The two remaining studies, which had within‐participant or within‐wound randomisation, randomised by drawing lots (Candy 2010) and coin tossing (Engrav 2010).

    Seven studies had unclear risk of bias for this domain. These studies provided insufficient details to make a judgement (Chang 1995; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Xu 2005).

    Allocation concealment

    Three studies had low risk of bias for this domain. These studies used a telephone randomisation service (Moiemen 2018), or sealed, opaque, serially numbered envelopes prepared in advance (Moortgat 2019; Wiseman 2020).

    Twelve studies had unclear risk of bias for this domain. These studies provided insufficient details to make a judgement (Candy 2010; Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Xu 2005).

    Blinding

    Blinding of participants and personnel

    No studies had low risk of bias for this domain.

    Five studies had unclear risk of bias for this domain. For these studies, we judged that blinding may have been possible as the intervention and comparator were either different pressures of the same garment or different garments that may have looked the same; however, there was insufficient information (Candy 2010; Engrav 2010; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; van den Kerckhove 2005).

    Ten studies had high risk of bias for this domain. Eight studies reported that participants and personnel were not blinded (Chang 1995; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020), and for the remaining two studies, blinding was unlikely to have been possible as the comparators used were other scar management therapies with or without PGT (Li‐Tsang 2010a; Xu 2005).

    Blinding of outcome assessment

    Seven studies had a low risk of bias for this domain. These studies reported blinding of outcome assessors (Candy 2010; Chang 1995; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moortgat 2019; Wiseman 2020). Candy 2010 had a between‐scar study design whereas the other six studies had a between‐participant design.

    Six studies had an unclear risk of bias for this domain. These studies provided insufficient details to make a judgement (Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Xu 2005).

    Two studies had a high risk of bias for this domain. These studies reported the outcome assessors were not or could not be blinded (Engrav 2010; Moiemen 2018). Engrav 2010 had a within‐wound study design whereas Moiemen 2018 had a between‐participant design.

    Incomplete outcome data

    One study had a low risk of bias for this domain and they reported complete outcome data (Tsang 2004).

    Six studies had an unclear risk of bias for this domain. These studies provided insufficient details to make a judgement (Groce 2001; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Xu 2005).

    Nine studies had a high risk of bias for this domain. The studies had large numbers of participants withdraw or provided no further details about the withdrawn participants (or both) (Candy 2010; Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Kealey 1990; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Moortgat 2019; Wiseman 2020).

    Only two studies stated that they used intention‐to‐treat analysis (Li 2009; Wiseman 2020). Wiseman 2020 did not include participants with missing data or those lost to follow‐up in the analysis.

    Selective reporting

    All 15 studies had a low risk of bias for this domain, as none showed apparent selective outcome reporting (Candy 2010; Chang 1995; Engrav 2010; Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Kealey 1990; Li 2009; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moiemen 2018; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004; van den Kerckhove 2005; Wiseman 2020; Xu 2005).

    Other potential sources of bias

    We judged one study at high risk of other bias because it was unclear how many participants were randomised to each group (Groce 2000). We judged an additional three studies at high risk of other bias because it was unclear if these studies, where participants had one or more scars/wounds, had considered scar clustering in the analysis (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Moiemen 2018).

    The remaining studies were at low risk of other bias.

    Comparison 1: pressure‐garment therapy versus no treatment/lanolin

    Five studies (378 participants) compared PGT versus no treatment/lanolin (Groce 2000; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; Xu 2005; summary of findings Table 1). One additional included study (Chang 1995) reported no useable data.

    Scar improvement (VSS)

    It is unclear whether PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin has an effect on overall VSS scores (Analysis 1.1; Groce 2000; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Tsang 2004). The scores may be slightly lower with PGT, but the CIs included the null value and overall the evidence was very low certainty. We downgraded the evidence two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision, and one level for indirectness (one study had a mixed population with proportion of participants with burns unknown (Tsang 2004)).

    Pain

    The effect of PGT on pain (measured by visual analogue scale (VAS)) compared with no treatment/lanolin was very uncertain (at seven months: mean 2 (SD 2.79) in PGT group versus 1.36 (SD 1.74) in no treatment group; 1 study; very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010b). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias, one level for indirectness, and one level for imprecision.

    Pruritus

    The effect of PGT on pruritus (measured by VAS) compared with no treatment/lanolin was very uncertain (at seven months: mean 3.09 (SD 2.34) in PGT group versus 2.63 (SD 1.91) in no treatment group; 1 study; very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010b). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias, one level for indirectness, and one level for imprecision.

    Scar thickness

    It is unclear whether PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin has an effect on scar thickness (assessed by ultrasound) (Analysis 1.6; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004). One study reported a decrease in scar thickness with PGT at one time point (seven months) (Li‐Tsang 2010b), but the CIs for the other time points and studies all included the null value. All the evidence was very low‐certainty. We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness.

    Adverse events

    One study reported adverse event noting that "some patients reported discomfort from prolonged wearing of PGs in summer" (very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010b). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias, one level for indirectness, and one level for imprecision.

    Scar lightness/pigmentation

    It is unclear whether PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin has an effect on scar lightness (assessment with spectrocolorimeter; Analysis 1.12). There may be a slight increase in lightness with PGT but all the CIs included the null value (except for one study at one time point (seven months); Li‐Tsang 2010b). All the evidence was very uncertain (three studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision, and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness.

    The effect of PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin on scar pigmentation (as assessed by the VSS) was very uncertain (1 study; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.9; Groce 2000). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Scar redness

    It is unclear whether PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin had an effect on scar redness (assessment with spectrocolorimeter; Analysis 1.13). The evidence was very uncertain (three studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010a; Tsang 2004; Li‐Tsang 2010b). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness.

    Scar yellowness

    The effect of PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin on scar yellowness (assessment with spectrocolorimeter, Analysis 1.15) was very uncertain (3 studies; very low‐certainty evidence, Li‐Tsang 2010a; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness. The direction of effect across studies was inconsistent.

    Scar pliability

    Scar pliability scores may be similar with PGT and no treatment/lanolin, but the evidence was very uncertain (two studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.16; Groce 2000; Li‐Tsang 2010b). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias, one level for imprecision, and, for one study (Li‐Tsang 2010b), one level for indirectness.

    Scar height

    PGT may reduce scar height compared with no treatment/lanolin (as assessed by the VSS), but the evidence was uncertain (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.21; Groce 2000). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Vascularity

    The effect of PGT compared with no treatment/lanolin on scar vascularity (as assessed by the VSS) was very uncertain (mean 1.74 (SD 1.03) in PGT group versus 1.64 (SD 0.88) in no treatment group; 1 study; very low‐certainty evidence; Groce 2000). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Other assessment tools

    The effect of PGT with or without heparin sodium ointment compared with no treatment was very uncertain (at 6 months: mean 5.1 (SD 0.7) in PGT group versus 7.5 (SD 0.6) in no treatment group; 1 study; low‐certainty evidence; Xu 2005). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for risk of bias and one level for imprecision. This was based on a scar assessment tool developed by the study authors that included pain/itch, redness, scar surface irregularity, and pliability.

    Scar improvement (POSAS), quality of life, and adherence

    No studies measured scar improvement (POSAS), quality of life, or adherence.

    Comparison 2: pressure‐garment therapy versus silicone

    Three studies (359 participants) compared PGT versus silicone (summary of findings Table 2).

    Scar improvement (VSS)

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on the overall VSS score was very uncertain (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1; Tsang 2004). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence one level for risk of bias, one level for indirectness, and one level for imprecision.

    Scar improvement (POSAS)

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on the overall POSAS observer or participant/caregiver rating was very uncertain (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.2; Analysis 1.3; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Pain

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on pain was very uncertain (at 12 months assessed by POSAS: MD −0.11, 95% CI −0.32 to 0.10; 1 study; Analysis 1.4; Wiseman 2020; at 7 months assessed by VAS: mean score 2.0 (SD 2.79) in PGT group versus 0.10 (SD 0.45) in silicone group; 1 study; Li‐Tsang 2010b; very low‐certainty evidence). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for one study (Li‐Tsang 2010b), one level for indirectness.

    Pruritus

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on pruritus was very uncertain (at 12 months assessed by POSAS: MD −0.26, 95% CI −0.64 to 0.12; Analysis 1.5; Wiseman 2020; mean score at 7 months: 3.09 (SD 2.34) in PGT group versus 1.05 (SD 1.31) in silicone group; 1 study; Li‐Tsang 2010b; very low‐certainty evidence). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for one study (Li‐Tsang 2010b), one level for indirectness.

    Adverse events

    There may be a higher rate of adverse events (mainly "problems with the intervention" and skin irritation) with PGT compared with silicone (at 6 months: MD −1.36, 95% CI −2.66 to −0.06; 1 study, 159 participants; very low‐certainty evidence; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision. Li‐Tsang 2010b noted that "some patients reported discomfort from prolonged wearing of PGs in summer" (very low‐certainty evidence). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for one study (Li‐Tsang 2010b); one level for indirectness.

    Quality of life

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on quality of life (assessed by Child Health Utility‐9D and Brisbane Burn Scar Impact Profile) was very uncertain (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Adherence

    It is uncertain whether there was a difference in adherence between PGT and silicone (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision. Adherence was measured in different ways and variable numbers of caregivers provided data at different time points.

    Scar thickness

    There may be no difference in scar thickness with PGT or silicone (as assessed by ultrasound (Analysis 1.6) or POSAS (Analysis 1.7)), but the evidence was very uncertain (3 studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness.

    Scar lightness/pigmentation

    There may be no difference in scar lightness with PGT or silicone (as assessed by a spectrocolorimeter), but the evidence was very uncertain (three studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.12; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness.

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on scar pigmentation (Analysis 1.10) or colour (Analysis 1.11) was very uncertain (one study; very low‐certainty evidence; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias and one level for imprecision.

    Scar redness

    It is unclear whether PGT compared with silicone had an effect on scar redness as the evidence was very uncertain (three studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.13; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for two studies (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004), one level for indirectness. Two of the three studies found a decrease in scar redness at some time points with PGT (Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004).

    Scar yellowness

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on scar yellowness was very uncertain (two studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.15; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Tsang 2004). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence either one or two levels for risk of bias, one level for indirectness, and one level for imprecision. One of the two studies found a decrease in scar yellowness at two months with PGT (Tsang 2004).

    Scar pliability/texture/relief/vascularity

    The effect of PGT compared with silicone on scar texture, relief, vascularity, or pliability was very uncertain (two studies; very low‐certainty evidence; Analysis 1.16; Analysis 1.19; Analysis 1.20; Analysis 1.22; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Wiseman 2020). We downgraded the certainty of the evidence two levels for risk of bias; one level for imprecision; and, for one study (Li‐Tsang 2010b), one level for indirectness.

    Comparison 3: pressure‐garment therapy plus silicone versus no treatment/lanolin

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We included 15 studies comparing PGT with other scar management therapies, or different pressures or types of PGT. Fourteen of those (1057 participants) reported useable data. The studies reported 21 outcomes, with only a small number of studies (between one and five) contributing data to each outcome. Several outcomes related to individual scar parameters (e.g. thickness, redness, elasticity, etc.), whilst many of these are captured within the overall scar improvement scores (VSS or POSAS), many studies assessed only individual scar parameters. There was very limited evidence for quality of life in particular.

    We are uncertain about the effect of PGT (with or without silicone) for both adults and children compared with other scar management strategies (with or without silicone). Evidence from studies was very‐low certainty, and evidence was downgraded for risk of bias, and imprecision or indirectness (or both).

    Very limited (and low‐certainty) evidence suggests that PGT might be associated with more discomfort in children compared with other scar management therapies. Comparing adherence rates between PGT and other therapies was difficult due the different nature of the interventions and how adherence was measured. At present, there are no defined minimal clinically important differences or thresholds for either the primary or secondary outcomes.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    As a result of comprehensive literature searches, all relevant literature is likely to have been identified for this review. However, we acknowledge limitations in the completeness and applicability of the evidence. Most studies included in this review had small numbers of participants. The 15 included studies had a total of 1179 participants, and the median study sample size was 67 participants (range: 17 to 159). Only four studies had more than 100 participants (Chang 1995; Kealey 1990; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Wiseman 2020). Only one to five studies contributed to individual outcomes, and only seven studies (47%) reported a primary outcome.

    Not all participants had scars resulting from burn injuries. Four studies had mixed populations where the injuries had a variety of causes including burns, surgical scars, other trauma, etc. (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004). No results were provided by cause of scar, therefore results for the whole study population are included. Three studies enroled children (Groce 2000; Groce 2001; Wiseman 2020), two studies enroled adults (Candy 2010; van den Kerckhove 2005), and the remaining 10 studies either had mixed populations, or did not specify the age of participants. As such, it was not possible to subgroup studies by age, and conclusions regarding effectiveness of PGT for specific age groups were not possible. Other subgroup analyses by location of burn, burn severity, length of follow‐up, or time from 95% healing to start of PGT were also not possible.

    The studies used a variety of different types of PGT. Not all studies specified the make/type of PGT used, and very few studies used the same make/type of PGT. There was a broad range of comparators (other PGT, PGT at different pressure, silicone sheet/dressing, silicone gel, heparin sodium ointment, lanolin massage), and it was not always explicitly clear what had been used. For example, some studies stated "standard care" was offered, or stated that the comparator was "no PGT" without further details. One study offered silicone to participants in addition to standard care but it was unclear how many participants had received this (Moiemen 2018). Overall, we could not be sure about the similarity of comparators across some studies. Poor reporting on the rate of adherence (whether to PGT or components of standard care, or both) in many studies further added to the uncertainty of what was received as part of care.

    Finally, the length of follow‐up was likely to have been too short in most studies as the full effect of any therapy on scar healing may not be evident until around 18 months. Nine studies had a follow‐up of between two and seven months, and three studies up to 12 months. Only one study (comparing higher and lower pressure) had a follow‐up time of 18 months, and two studies followed participants until 'scar maturation'.

    Quality of the evidence

    Ten studies were at high risk of bias for the blinding of participants and personnel domain as they either stated that participants or personnel (or both) were not blinded, or we judged it was unlikely for blinding to have been possible given the nature of the intervention and comparators used (e.g. PGT versus silicone). This is important as most outcomes were of a subjective nature. Furthermore, nine studies reported a large number of participants lost to follow‐up with no further details provided about those who had withdrawn from the study.

    Poor reporting of risk of bias criteria was common and we made several 'unclear' risk of bias decisions across domains. In particular, only three studies provided sufficient details on how concealment of allocation was undertaken.

    There were additional risks of bias in four studies: one study did not clarify how many participants were randomised to each group; and three studies did not clarify if any scar clustering had been considered in the analysis where the participants had more than one scar/wound. Only one study clearly stated that ANCOVA had been used as a method for adjusting for baseline; as studies were generally small, it is possible that any baseline differences may have biased findings if not adequately considered.

    Based on the GRADE framework, we judged the certainty of the evidence for most outcomes/comparisons to be very low. This was mainly due to serious or very serious risk of bias (mainly lack of blinding and incompleteness of data); imprecision as the number of studies and participants contributing to each outcome was low (mostly just one study); and indirectness, as there were some studies with mixed populations (with trauma or surgery scars as well as burn scars). We were mostly unable to rate inconsistency as there was only one study contributing to most outcomes. We did not downgrade for publication bias as it was not possible to generate funnel plots, and we did not find evidence of small studies showing mostly positive findings in favour of one intervention. However, publication or other bias due to small‐study effects could not be ruled out.

    Potential biases in the review process

    To avoid any potential biases in our review process, we followed prespecified methods (Harris 2020).

    One potential source of bias was unavailable data. Where a study did not report data or we required clarification of details, we contacted study authors; however, not all responded. This meant that, for example, we were unable to obtain separate data for participants with burns injuries in studies that had mixed populations (Candy 2010; Li‐Tsang 2010b; Moortgat 2019; Tsang 2004).

    We chose not to include all individual scar parameter outcomes in the summary of findings tables. This was due to the large number of outcomes and the fact that some of the scar parameters are already encompassed within the VSS and POSAS (primary outcomes). However, findings are reported for all scar parameters and the very low level of certainty highlighted.

    We were unable to undertake any meta‐analyses and, therefore, were unable to explore whether increasing the sample size for a given outcome would result in pooled effect estimates showing differences in effect. We were also unable to conduct any sensitivity analyses.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    We are aware of three systematic reviews that evaluated the effectiveness of PGT for hypertrophic burn scars (Ai 2017; Anzarut 2009; De Decker 2023). Ai 2017 included 12 RCTs (710 participants) and concluded that treatment with PGT showed significant improvements in scarring on the VSS (global score), scar thickness, brightness, redness, pigmentation, and hardness, based on pooled estimates that included studies with a range of comparators, co‐interventions, age ranges, and follow‐up times. We did not pool studies as we considered heterogeneity to be substantial; therefore, we could not confirm these findings. Anzarut 2009 included six RCTs (316 participants) and concluded that treatment with PGT showed no improvement in global scar scores. They found a small improvement in scar height but thought this to be of questionable clinical importance. This review also did not separate out studies comparing PGT versus other management therapies, or studies comparing different pressures of PGT as we have done in our review. One more‐recent systematic review included 17 articles (five RCTs, seven prospective cohort studies, and five case series) (De Decker 2023). Four of the RCTs were also included in this review. The RCTs were rated at lower risk of bias compared with the ratings given here for the same RCTs and the GRADE framework was not used for judging the certainty of the evidence. There was no formal synthesis and results were reported on a study‐by‐study basis. The authors concluded that there was sufficient moderate‐ to good‐quality evidence to support the use of PGT.

    Our review has added an additional five relevant studies. We used rigorous methodology to evaluate all available evidence and have fully considered the impact of heterogeneity in our analyses. We have used additional Cochrane methodological requirements (GRADE assessment) that none of the previous reviews have used.
