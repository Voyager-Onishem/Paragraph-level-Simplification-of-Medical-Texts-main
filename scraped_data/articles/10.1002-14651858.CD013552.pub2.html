Parenteral versus enteral fluid therapy for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis

    • Background

    The main focus of treatment for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis is supportive, including oxygen supplementation, respiratory support, and fluid therapy. Up to half of infants hospitalised with bronchiolitis require non‐oral fluid therapy due to dehydration or concerns related to the safety of oral feeding. The two main modalities used for non‐oral fluid therapy are parenteral (intravenous (IV)) and enteral tube (nasogastric (NG) or orogastric (OG)). However, it is not known which mode is optimal in young children.

    Objectives

    To systematically review randomised clinical trials (RCTs) of the effectiveness and safety of parenteral and enteral tube fluid therapy for children under two years of age hospitalised with bronchiolitis.

    Search methods

    We conducted a search of the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL), MEDLINE, Embase, CINAHL, Web of Science, ClinicalTrials.gov, and the World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry Platform on 8 March 2021. We handsearched conference proceedings, conducted forward and backward searching of citation lists of relevant articles, and contacted experts.

    Selection criteria

    We included RCTs and quasi‐RCTs of children aged up to two years admitted to hospital with a clinical diagnosis of bronchiolitis who required fluid therapy. The trials compared enteral tube fluid therapy with parenteral fluid therapy. The primary outcome was difference in length of hospital stay in hours after each non‐oral fluid therapy modality. As actual time of discharge can be impacted by various factors, we also assessed theoretical length of stay (i.e. time when a patient is safe for discharge). We assessed several secondary outcomes.

    Data collection and analysis

    We used standard methodological procedures expected by Cochrane. 

    Main results

    The searches yielded 615 unique records, of which four articles underwent full‐text screening. We included two trials (810 children). Oakley 2013 was an open, non‐blinded RCT of infants aged two to 12 months admitted to hospitals in Australia and New Zealand with a clinical diagnosis of bronchiolitis during three bronchiolitis seasons. The trial enrolled 759 children, of which 381 were randomised to NG tube therapy and 378 to IV therapy. Risk of bias was low in most domains. Kugelman 2013 was an open, non‐blinded RCT that enrolled infants aged less than six months with a clinical diagnosis of "moderate bronchiolitis" at a single hospital in Israel. The study enrolled 51 infants, of which 31 were assigned to NG or OG tube therapy and 20 to IV therapy. Risk of bias was unclear in most domains.

    The application of enteral tube fluid therapy compared to IV fluid therapy probably makes little to no difference for actual length of hospital stay (mean difference (MD) 6.8 hours, 95% confidence interval (CI) −4.7 to 18.4 hours; 2 studies, 810 children, moderate certainty evidence). There was also little to no difference for theoretical length of stay (MD 4.4 hours, 95% CI −3.6 to 12.4 hours; 2 studies, 810 children, moderate certainty evidence). For the secondary outcomes, enteral tube fluid therapy probably makes little to no difference for time to resume full oral feeding compared to IV fluid therapy (MD 2.8 hours, 95% CI −3.6 to 9.2 hours; 2 studies, 810 children, moderate certainty evidence). The use of enteral tube for fluid therapy probably results in a large increase in the success of insertion of fluid modality at first attempt (risk ratio (RR) 1.52, 95% CI 1.36 to 1.69; 1 study, 617 children, moderate certainty evidence), and probably largely reduces the chances of change in fluid therapy modality (RR 0.52, 95% CI 0.38 to 0.71; 1 study, 759 children, moderate certainty evidence) compared to IV fluid. 

    Oakley 2013 reported 47 local complication events after discharge in the IV fluid group compared to 30 events in the NG tube group. They also evaluated parental satisfaction, which was high with both modalities. Enteral tube fluid therapy makes little to no difference to the duration of oxygen supplementation (MD 2.2 hours, 95% CI ‐5.0 to 9.5 hours; 2 studies, 810 children, moderate certainty evidence). Compared with the IV fluid therapy group, there was a 17% relative reduction in the number of intensive care unit admissions (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.47 to 1.46; 1 study, 759 children, moderate certainty evidence) and a 19% relative reduction in number of readmissions to hospital (RR 0.81, 95% CI 0.33 to 2.04; 1 study, 678 children, moderate certainty evidence) in the enteral tube fluid therapy group. Adverse events were uncommon in both trials, with likely little to no differences between groups.

    Authors' conclusions

    Based on two RCTs, enteral tube feeding likely results in little to no difference in length of hospital stay compared with the IV fluid group. However, enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in a large increase in the success of insertion of fluid modality at first attempt, and a large reduction in change in modality of fluid therapy. It also probably reduces local complications compared to the IV fluid group. Despite bronchiolitis being one of the most prevalent childhood conditions, we identified only two studies with under 1000 participants in total, which highlights the need for multicentre trials. Future studies should explore type of fluid administered, parent‐reported outcomes and preferences, and the role of shared decision‐making.

    • Intravenous fluids compared to nasogastric or orogastric tube hydration for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis

    Review question

    Is feeding tube or intravenous catheter better for hydrating children hospitalised with bronchiolitis?

    Background

    Bronchiolitis is a common respiratory infection in young children that makes feeding difficult due to increased breathing effort and mucous production. When infants are considered unsafe to feed by mouth (orally), there are two ways that fluid can be given: a feeding tube and/or an intravenous catheter. A feeding tube is inserted through the nose or mouth into a child’s stomach and can be used to give milk or clear fluids like rehydration solution. The intravenous cannula is inserted into a vein and gives medical hydration fluids into the vein. It is not clear if one way to give fluids is better than the other, and there is a lot of variation in which method is used in children hospitalised with bronchiolitis. Our review used standard Cochrane methods to identify studies that randomly assigned children to receive fluids either by a feeding tube or intravenous catheter, and then compared the effects of the two fluid therapy methods on the children. The aim of this review was to find out if one method is better than the other.

    Search date

    The evidence is current 8 March 2021.

    Study characteristics

    We found only two studies that compared using a feeding tube to an intravenous catheter for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis. The studies included 810 children in total who were younger than two years of age, and who were randomly assigned to either a feeding tube or an intravenous catheter.

    Study funding sources

    The authors of the Oakley trial indicated that the study was funded by the Australian National Health and Medical Research Council, Samuel Nissen Charitable Foundation (Perpetual), Murdoch Children’s Research Institute, and the Victorian Government, but that the funders had no role in the design and interpretation of the trial. The Kugelman trial did not describe the study funder.

    Key results

    The two studies found no differences in the number of hours children stayed in hospital, nor were there any differences in recovery time to resume full oral feeding. The main difference between groups was greater success in inserting a feeding tube on first try compared to an intravenous catheter, which often required several attempts. Also, children who received intravenous fluid were more prone to switching to an enteral tube feeding and experienced more side effects. One of the studies reported that parents were satisfied with both hydration methods. However, because there were only two included studies, it is hard to tell if enteral tube feeding is better or worse than intravenous fluids.

    Conclusions

    There is a need for more specific research on this topic, including a better understanding of parent and caregiver preferences related to giving non‐oral fluids and how to incorporate shared decision‐making, where both patient and doctor work together to make treatment decisions. 

    Certainty of evidence

    In one study, the methods used to randomly assign and allocate participants to treatment groups were unclear. It was unclear how this may have caused bias in the study. As one study had an unclear risk of bias, we rated the overall certainty of evidence for the primary outcome as moderate.  None of the study authors declared any conflict of interest.

    Authors' conclusions

    Implications for practice

    There is international variation in the mode of fluid therapy used for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis. While both enteral tube hydration and intravenous fluids each have benefits and risks, the available trial data do not provide evidence that one modality is superior to the other in terms of length of hospital stay and healthcare utilisation outcomes. However, data indicate that enteral tube insertion at first attempt is more often successful with much lower chances of needing to switch from enteral tube to intravenous fluids. Local complications were also less frequent in the enteral tube hydration group. There was limited information about parent or caregiver preferences for mode of therapy. These findings collectively suggest an overall potential for reduced harm with enteral tube hydration. 

    Implications for research

    There remains some uncertainty regarding the most effective mode of fluid therapy in children hospitalised with bronchiolitis. Whilst both modes of fluid therapy appear to be effective and safe, future high‐quality studies are needed to understand parent and caregiver, along with provider preferences regarding the potential risks and benefits of each treatment. Future studies should have objective criteria for duration of hospitalisation, and for intensive care unit admission, which currently vary between hospitals. The included trials involved a broad range of children younger than one year, but future studies should further explore differences in young infants under two months of age, where there are greater concerns of adverse events, children with chronic conditions or comorbidities, in addition to subgroup analyses of disease severity.

    Background

    Description of the condition

    Bronchiolitis is the most common cause of hospitalisation in children aged up to two years (Florin 2016; Ralston 2014), leading to over 100,000 hospitalisations per year in the USA (Ralston 2014 ), or 134 cases per 1000 person‐years during the first year of life (Koehoorn 2008). Bronchiolitis is a lower respiratory tract infection usually caused by viruses (e.g. respiratory syncytial virus), leading to airway inflammation, swelling, and necrosis of cells lining the small airways (Florin 2016). Peak bronchiolitis incidence is between December and March in the Northern Hemisphere, and between May and July in the Southern Hemisphere (Obando‐Pacheco 2018). Most children with bronchiolitis present with rhinorrhoea, cough, and low‐grade fever before developing rapid breathing, increased effort to breathe, and poor feeding (Florin 2016). Children with underlying health problems, in particular prematurity and congenital heart disease, are at increased risk of severe bronchiolitis (Murray 2014; Schuh 2018).

    Despite extensive research on possible interventions to modify the natural history of bronchiolitis, such as bronchodilators (Gadomski 2014), inhaled epinephrine (Hartling 2011), nebulised hypertonic saline (Zhang 2017), steroids (Fernandes 2013), and antibiotics (Farley 2014), none are effective in reducing complications or changing patient‐important outcomes. Whilst bronchiolitis is usually self‐limiting, a common reason for hospitalisation is to administer supplemental oxygen, and for respiratory support, such as heated high‐flow nasal cannula (Friedman 2014; NICE 2015; Ralston 2014). Furthermore, up to half of infants admitted to hospital with bronchiolitis require fluid maintenance therapy with non‐oral fluid (Mahant 2021; Saqib 2021; Srinivasan 2018; Unger 2008), because they are unsafe and/or unable to feed orally due to increased breathing effort, mucous production, and nasal congestion.

    Description of the intervention

    The two main modalities used for non‐oral fluid administration are intravenous (IV) fluid and enteral replacements with either a nasogastric (NG) or orogastric (OG) tube. Children require the insertion of an IV cannula to administer IV fluids. There are numerous compositions of IV fluids, ranging from 0.2% to 0.9% normal saline, which usually include dextrose (5% or 10%) with or without potassium. The choice of fluid is based on and adjusted for the child's serum electrolytes, age, and weight (Feld 2018; Neilson 2015). Acutely dehydrated children may require an IV bolus for resuscitation (10 mL/kg to 20 mL/kg) before IV maintenance therapy (Meyers 2009). The volume of fluid administered is calculated based on the child's expected total fluid intake, which is the estimated amount of fluid required per day. Total fluid intake calculations are based on weight and age (Feld 2018; Neilson 2015). Most hospitalised children are provided between 80% and 100% of their expected total fluid intake when given IV fluids (Oakley 2013b), which is decreased as the child's oral intake increases.

    To provide enteral hydration, either an NG tube is inserted through the nasopharynx directly into the stomach, or an OG tube is inserted via the mouth into the stomach. Children may be provided with oral rehydration solution (e.g. Pedialyte or Enfalyte), formula, or expressed breast milk, either at a continuous rate per hour, or with bolus feeds at prespecified time intervals to reproduce a regular feeding schedule. Both fluid therapy modes have been used for other childhood conditions associated with dehydration, such as gastroenteritis, where enteral hydration is associated with fewer adverse events and reduced length of hospital stay (Fonseca 2004).

    How the intervention might work

    IV hydration is often used for children who are unsafe to feed orally or if an IV is already inserted (e.g. for medication delivery). Insertion of an IV cannula is painful, requires a high level of skill, frequently becomes dislodged, and can damage tissue if extravasation occurs. IV fluids usually contain only electrolytes and dextrose, which can make children more irritable due to hunger. IV fluids can also create a catabolic state, given reduced calorie provision during a time of increased metabolic demand. Suboptimal caloric intake on day 2 of hospitalisation has been associated with prolonged length of stay amongst infants with bronchiolitis (Weisgerber 2013). There is also a risk of electrolyte abnormalities with IV fluids in a population already at increased risk of hyponatraemia (Hasegawa 2015).

    Enteral fluid maintenance is more physiologic, as fluids are deposited directly into the gastrointestinal tract. This modality can also provide the same nutrition and calories that the child would be receiving at home. However, children may experience discomfort on NG insertion; the NG tube may become dislodged; or children may pull the tube out if it is not well secured. Concerns that younger children with a high respiratory rate and an NG tube are at increased risk of choking, coughing, or aspiration events, Khoshoo 1999, have not been demonstrated in studies (Kugelman 2013b; Oakley 2013b; Oakley 2016). As small infants are obligate nasal breathers, an NG tube may obstruct the upper airway and compromise respiratory function (Kennedy 2005; Stocks 1980); however, no aspiration events were identified in a retrospective study of hospitalised infants aged up to two months with an NG tube (Oakley 2016). Furthermore, parents may prefer NG tubes to IV hydration: a survey of parents of children who required both NG and IV insertion reported that if parents had the opportunity to choose, 80% would select NG hydration over IV fluids (Srinivasan 2017).

    Why it is important to do this review

    Despite extensive research on bronchiolitis, it is not known which mode of fluid therapy is optimal in young children. North American and Australasian bronchiolitis clinical practice guidelines suggest that clinicians may use either NG or IV routes for fluid therapy (Friedman 2014; O'Brien 2019; Ralston 2014); however, British guidelines recommend NG/OG hydration over IV fluids (NICE 2015). The lack of clarity regarding the safest and most effective way to hydrate infants means there is substantial practice variation in the use of NG/OG compared with IV hydration (PREDIC 2010; Srinivasan 2018). No systematic review has evaluated the safety and effectiveness of both modes of fluid therapy, or identified the optimal indication, timing, and type of fluid to use. If one modality is found to be more effective, implementation of this evidence into practice will improve quality of care and could potentially result in cost savings. If both are equally effective, decision‐making should be informed by patient values and preferences, the adverse event profile of each treatment option, and available expertise.

    Objectives

    To systematically review randomised clinical trials of the effectiveness and safety of parenteral and enteral tube fluid therapy for children under two years of age hospitalised with bronchiolitis.

    Methods

    Criteria for considering studies for this review

    Types of studies

    We included randomised controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi‐RCTs (trials using methods of allocating participants to study arms that are not truly random). We included studies reported as full text, those published as abstract only, and unpublished data. We included RCTs irrespective of the language of publication, sample size, or risk of bias.

    Types of participants

    We included children aged up to two years admitted to hospital with a clinical diagnosis of bronchiolitis who required fluid therapy, irrespective of whether they were tested for specific viruses (e.g. respiratory syncytial virus). We excluded studies conducted exclusively in intensive care unit (ICU) settings (i.e. neonatal or paediatric). We excluded infants who weighed less than 2 kg due to the concern that nasogastric (NG) tubes could obstruct the nares of small infants (Kennedy 2005; Stocks 1980).

    Types of interventions

    We included trials comparing enteral tube fluid therapy (i.e. nasogastric (NG) or orogastric (OG)) with intravenous (IV) fluid therapy. We permitted any of the following, provided they were not part of the randomised treatment: type of fluid therapy (i.e. isotonic or hypotonic fluids, oral rehydration solution, formula, or breast milk), dose of fluid (i.e. partial or complete fluid replacement), rate of infusion (i.e. intermittent bolus or continuous infusion), length of therapy, or timing of treatment (i.e. initiated at the time of hospitalisation, or if required later during the course of hospital admission). We included any comparators, except placebo or control, because withholding fluids is unethical.

    Types of outcome measures

    Primary outcomes

    Length of hospital stay (defined in hours or days). We defined a difference in length of stay of 12 hours to be clinically meaningful. As length of hospital stay is affected by factors unrelated to hospitalisation (e.g. transportation home), we also described the time children were medically stable for discharge (as defined in each individual study).

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Duration of time requiring non‐oral fluid therapy (i.e. defined as time to resume full oral feeding).

    2. Proportion of children with successful insertion of either IV or NG/OG on first attempt.

    3. Number of attempts required of either IV or NG/OG before successful insertion.

    4. Proportion of children requiring change in modality of fluid therapy (e.g. NG to IV).

    5. Rate of local complications due to each modality of fluid therapy (e.g. NG – epistaxis, IV – extravasation injuries).

    6. Rate of complications due to type of fluid administered (e.g. hyponatraemia).

    7. Duration of time requiring oxygen supplementation.

    8. Proportion of children who required continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP).

    9. Proportion of children who required ICU admission.

    10. Proportion of children who had a return visit to a healthcare provider.

    11. Proportion of children who were readmitted to hospital.

    12. Parental and/or caregiver satisfaction.

    13. Rate of adverse effects and events (e.g. desaturation, bradycardia, apnoea, and aspiration events).

    The outcomes were based on prior work to develop a core outcome set for bronchiolitis (Fernandes 2015; Fernandes 2016). In a study to define clinically important outcomes amongst general practitioners and paediatricians, the most highly rated outcomes were hospital admission, length of hospital stay, respiratory distress, need for oxygen therapy, and pulmonary function (Fernandes 2016). Other work has evaluated the validity, reliability, and responsiveness of respiratory status instruments (e.g. Respiratory Distress Assessment Instrument (RDAI)) (Fernandes 2015). These tools are frequently used to evaluate response to therapy. However, because hydration therapy is not anticipated to change the natural history of the illness, it is unlikely that trials would include these or other instruments as an outcome measure. We instead included other clinical variables and complications, such as respiratory rate, apnoea, and bradycardia, as secondary outcomes.

    If scores or instruments that evaluate clinical severity were included, we would select the following time points: day 1, day 2, day 3, day 4, and day 5. Given that length of hospital stay is short, we planned to include additional measures of severity that were recorded more than once per day. For readmissions to hospital and return visits to a healthcare provider, we would have selected seven days or less and 7 to 30 days, if this information was available.

    Search methods for identification of studies

    Electronic searches

    We searched the following databases from inception to present.

    1. The Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) in the Cochrane Library (Issue 2, 2021); searched 8 March 2021.

    2. MEDLINE Ovid (from 1946 to 8 March 2021).

    3. Embase Ovid (from 1947 to 8 March 2021).

    4. CINAHL Plus via EBSCOhost (Cumulative Index to Nursing and Allied Health Literature) (from 1961 to 8 March 2021).

    5. Web of Science (from 1900 to 8 March 2021).

    We used the search strategy described in Appendix 1 to search MEDLINE. We combined the MEDLINE search with the Cochrane Highly Sensitive Search Strategy for randomised trials: sensitivity and precision‐maximising version (2008 revision) (Lefebvre 2011). We have also presented search strategies for Embase (Appendix 2), CENTRAL (Appendix 3), CINAHL (Appendix 4), and Web of Science (Appendix 5).

    We also conducted searches of ClinicalTrials.gov and the World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Platform (ICTRP) on 8 March 2021.

    Searching other resources

    We checked the reference lists of all primary studies and review articles for additional references. We also searched conference proceedings for abstracts. We contacted experts in the field to identify additional unpublished materials.

    Data collection and analysis

    Selection of studies

    Two review authors (MRA, EK) independently screened all titles and abstracts and keywords (when available) for inclusion of the studies identified by the search.

    We retrieved the full‐text study reports/publications of those studies deemed potentially relevant, and two review authors (MRA, EK) independently screened the full texts and identified studies for inclusion, and identified and recorded reasons for exclusion of the ineligible studies. Any disagreements were resolved through discussion or by consulting a third review author (PG) when required. We identified and excluded duplicates. We collated multiple reports of the same study so that each study, rather than each report, was the unit of interest in the review. If unpublished trials and abstracts did not include sufficient numerical details needed to determine study inclusion, they were classified as awaiting classification. We recorded the selection process in sufficient detail to complete a PRISMA flow diagram and Characteristics of excluded studies table (Moher 2009). We did not impose any language or publication restrictions.

    Data extraction and management

    We used a data collection form for study characteristics and outcome data that had been pilot tested on one study included in the review. Two review authors (MRA, EK) extracted study characteristics from the included studies. We extracted the following study characteristics.

    1. Methods: study design, total duration of study, details of any 'run‐in' period, number of study centres and location, study setting, withdrawals, and date of study.

    2. Participants: N, mean age, age range, sex, severity of condition, diagnostic criteria, inclusion criteria, and exclusion criteria.

    3. Interventions: intervention, comparison, concomitant medications, and excluded medications.

    4. Outcomes: primary and secondary outcomes specified and collected, and time points reported.

    5. Notes: funding for trial, and notable conflicts of interest of trial authors.

    Two review authors (MRA, EK) independently extracted outcome data from the included studies. We noted in the Characteristics of included studies table if outcome data were not reported in a useable way. Any disagreements were resolved by consensus or by involving a third review author (PG). One review author (MRA) entered data into Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2020). We double‐checked that data were entered correctly by comparing the data presented in the systematic review with the included study reports. A second review author (EK) spot‐checked the study characteristics.

    Assessment of risk of bias in included studies

    Two review authors (MRA, EK) independently assessed risk of bias for each study using the criteria outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011). Any disagreements were resolved by discussion or by involving a third review author (PG). We assessed risk of bias according to the following domains.

    1. Random sequence generation.

    2. Allocation concealment.

    3. Blinding of participants and personnel.

    4. Blinding of outcome assessment.

    5. Incomplete outcome data.

    6. Selective outcome reporting.

    7. Other bias.

    We graded each potential source of bias as low, high, or unclear and provided a quote from the study report together with a justification for our judgement in the risk of bias table. We summarised the risk of bias judgements across both included studies for each of the domains listed. We considered blinding separately for different key outcomes where necessary. Where information on risk of bias was related to unpublished data or correspondence with a trialist, we noted this in the risk of bias table. When considering treatment effects, we took into account the risk of bias for the studies that contributed to that outcome. We determined overall risk of bias as follows: low when all domains were judged as low risk; unclear when one or more domains were judged as unclear risk and no domains were judged as high risk; and high when one or more domains were judged as high risk.

    Assessment of bias in conducting the systematic review

    We conducted the review according to this published protocol and reported any deviations from it in the Differences between protocol and review section of the systematic review.

    Measures of treatment effect

    We entered the outcome data for each study into the data tables in Review Manager Web to calculate the treatment effects (RevMan Web 2020). We analysed dichotomous data using risk ratios (RRs) based on the prevalence of the disease. Dichotomous outcomes included successful insertion on first attempt, change in modality of fluid therapy, local complications due to modality of fluid therapy, complications due to type of fluid administered, required CPAP, ICU admission, children with a return visit to a healthcare provider, children readmitted to hospital, and adverse effects and events.

    We analysed continuous data as mean differences (MDs) or standardised mean differences (SMDs). We also considered a difference of medians analysis, depending on the data reported. Continuous outcomes included length of hospital stay (actual, and based on time ready for discharge), duration of time requiring non‐oral fluid therapy, duration of time requiring oxygen supplementation, and parental or caregiver satisfaction.

    We undertook meta‐analyses only where this was meaningful, that is if the treatments, participants, and the underlying clinical question were similar enough for pooling to make sense.

    Unit of analysis issues

    The unit of analysis was the individual participant. If an included study had multiple treatment arms, we combined the groups to facilitate a single pair‐wise comparison.

    Dealing with missing data

    We contacted study investigators to verify key study characteristics and to obtain missing numerical outcome data where possible (e.g. when a study was identified as abstract only). Where this was not possible, or if there was no response within eight weeks, and the missing data were thought to introduce serious bias, we explored the impact of including such studies in the overall assessment of results by a sensitivity analysis. When we were unable to obtain missing data and data were assumed to be missing at random, we imputed the missing data with plausible replacement values.

    When numerical outcome data were missing, such as standard deviations (SDs) or correlation coefficients, and they could not be obtained from the study authors, we calculated them from other available statistics such as P values, according to the methods described in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011).

    Assessment of heterogeneity

    We used the I² statistic to assess and report heterogeneity in the included trials, as outlined in the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions. We defined heterogeneity as follows: 

    • mild (0% to 30%); 

    • moderate (31% to 50%); 

    • substantial (51% to 80%); 

    • considerable (81% to 100%). 

    When we identified substantial or considerable heterogeneity, we reported it and explored possible causes by prespecified subgroup analysis. We also looked for clinical heterogeneity by assessing study characteristics and the forest plot of study results. When we identified substantial or considerable heterogeneity (I² > 50%) that could not be explained, we considered not conducting a meta‐analysis.

    Assessment of reporting biases

    We mitigated reporting biases by searching for unpublished studies in the grey literature. Had we pooled 10 or more trials, we would have created and examined a funnel plot to explore possible small‐study and publication biases. If trial protocols were available, we would check whether expected but unreported outcomes were prespecified in the study protocol.

    Data synthesis

    We pooled data from studies we judged to be clinically similar using Review Manager Web (RevMan Web 2020). When more than one study provided useable data in any single comparison, we performed a meta‐analysis using a random‐effects model due to the variation expected between studies. We performed a sensitivity analysis with a fixed‐effect model. All pooled effects were accompanied by 95% confidence intervals (CIs).

    Subgroup analysis and investigation of heterogeneity

    We planned to carry out the following subgroup analyses if data were available.

    1. Age (children aged up to 2 months, 2 to 12 months, or 12 to 24 months).

    2. Amount of fluid replacement (under 50% maintenance or over 50% maintenance for total fluid intake).

    3. Oxygen requirement (room air or supplemental oxygen), oxygen saturation target of 90% or greater (e.g. 92% or 94%).

    4. Respiratory support requirement (room air or CPAP).

    5. Type of NG/OG fluid (oral rehydration solution (e.g. Enfalyte) or formula or breast milk).

    6. Type of IV fluid (isotonic (e.g. 0.9% sodium chloride) or hypotonic (e.g. 0.45% sodium chloride)).

    7. Rate of NG/OG fluid therapy (intermittent bolus or continuous).

    However, as data were limited, no subgroup analyses were conducted.

    Sensitivity analysis

    We planned to carry out the following sensitivity analyses if a sufficient number of studies were available. We would evaluate the effect of excluding:

    1. a study if it had a very large weight in a forest plot;

    2. studies where missing data were imputed or calculated;

    3. studies where respiratory syncytial virus was not tested;

    4. studies with a high risk of bias for the primary outcome;

    5. unpublished data (if relevant).

    Summary of findings and assessment of the certainty of the evidence

    We created a summary of findings table comparing IV fluid therapy versus NG/OG tube fluid therapy, which included the following outcomes: length of hospital stay (actual); length of hospital stay (theoretical); duration of time requiring non‐oral fluid therapy; proportion of children with successful insertion of either IV catheter or NG/OG tube on first attempt; proportion of children requiring change in modality of fluid therapy; rate of local complications; and parental or caregiver satisfaction (see summary of findings Table 1).

    We used the five GRADE considerations (study limitations, consistency of effect, imprecision, indirectness, and publication bias) to assess the quality of a body of evidence as it relates to the studies which contribute data to the meta‐analyses for the prespecified outcomes (Atkins 2004). We used the methods and recommendations described in Section 8.5 and Chapter 12 of the Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions (Higgins 2011), employing GRADEpro GDT software (GRADEpro GDT ). We justified all decisions to down‐ or upgrade the certainty of the evidence using footnotes and made comments to aid the reader's understanding of the review where necessary.

    Included studies

    We identified two eligible trials (see Characteristics of included studies) (Kugelman 2013a; Oakley 2013a).

    Oakley 2013a was an open, non‐blinded RCT of infants aged 2 to 12 months admitted to hospitals in Australia and New Zealand with a clinical diagnosis of bronchiolitis during three bronchiolitis seasons (1 April to 31 October 2009 to 2011). The trial defined bronchiolitis as symptoms and signs of respiratory distress (tachypnoea, recessions, nasal flaring, or cyanosis) associated with symptoms of a viral respiratory tract infection. Seven hospital emergency departments participated in the trial which were members of the Paediatric Research in Emergency Departments International Collaborative (PREDICT) research network. A total of 759 participants were enrolled in the trial, of which 381 were randomised to receive NG tube hydration and 378 to IV fluids. Infants assigned to NG tube hydration received continuous oral rehydration solution for the first two hours, then their usual feed by bolus every one to two hours, with a total fluid volume of 80% of daily maintenance. Infants assigned to IV hydration received continuous IV fluids composed of 0.45% sodium chloride with either 2.5%, 4%, or 5% dextrose. The initial route, rate, and type of fluid administration at the time of randomisation were determined by the study protocol. Any further management decisions, such as changing the route of fluid therapy, were at the discretion of the treating clinician. The authors assessed the effects of NG tube hydration versus IV fluid therapy on both actual and theoretical length of hospital stay, duration of oxygen therapy, time to resume full oral feeding, ICU admission, impact on oxygen saturation, changes from one method of fluid therapy to another, success of initial intervention insertion at randomisation, local complications, and side effects. They also assessed parent/guardian satisfaction by conducting a telephone interview five to seven days after discharge from hospital. The trial authors did not declare any conflicts of interest, and the funding source had no role in the design and interpretation of the trial.

    Kugelman 2013a was also an open, non‐blinded RCT, which enrolled infants aged less than six months with a clinical diagnosis of “moderate bronchiolitis” at a single hospital in Israel. Moderate bronchiolitis was defined as the inability to sustain oral feeding (i.e. respiratory rate > 60 breaths/minute, supplemental oxygen requirement, and difficulties in breathing). Supplemental oxygenwas administered when the oximeter saturation was < 93% in room air, respiratory rate was > 60 breaths/minute, or there were difficulties in breathing and feeding. Infants were excluded from the trial if they showed signs of severe respiratory distress or of impending respiratory failure. The study enrolled 51 infants, of which 31 were assigned to gastric tube hydration and 20 to IV fluids. If infants admitted to hospital with bronchiolitis were unable to sustain oral feedings, the mode of fluid/nutritional support was randomly assigned as either IV fluid (glucose 5%, sodium chloride 0.33%, and potassium supplementation) or NG/OG tube hydration (of breast milk or infant formula). The trial did not outline any specific recommendation regarding fluid rate or amount. Both IV and NG/OG groups were allowed comfort nutritive sucking. The two primary outcomes were duration of supplemental oxygen and length of hospital stay (actual and theoretical). Other outcomes reported included time to resume full oral feeding, change in treatment modality, and clinical aspiration events. The investigators did not describe any potential conflicts of interest or external funding sources. As the number of infants in each group for each outcome was unclear in the published report, we contacted the study authors for additional details, of which some data were provided, but the date of data collection was not available.

The economic evaluation did not report any outcomes that were prespecified in our protocol (Gill 2020). For details on reasons for exclusion, see Characteristics of excluded studies.

    Allocation

    Random sequence generation

    Random sequence generation was adequately described in Oakley 2013a and therefore judged as low risk of bias. The trial authors randomly allocated infants to NG tube or IV hydration 1:1 using a computer‐generated allocation sequence. Randomisation occurred at admission or at any time during hospital stay. Kugelman 2013a only stated that infants were randomly assigned to NG tube and IV groups, providing no description on randomisation procedure. We therefore judged this domain as unclear risk of bias for Kugelman 2013a.

    Allocation concealment

    Oakley 2013a ensured allocation concealment by using three randomly allocated block sizes and opaque, sealed envelopes; we judged this domain as low risk of bias. Kugelman 2013a did not provide details on allocation concealment, therefore we judged this domain as unclear risk of bias.

    Blinding

    Blinding of participant and personnel (performance bias)

    In both studies, neither participants nor healthcare providers were blinded to the study interventions; however, it would not have been possible to blind participants and personnel to the interventions, therefore both studies were assessed as unclear risk of bias.

    Blinding of outcome assessment (detection bias)

    Neither study blinded outcome assessors. However, to minimise bias, for the primary outcome in Oakley 2013a, the study authors recorded both observed and theoretical length of stay. Kugelman 2013a used objective criteria and management protocols, which reduced the possibility of bias due to lack of blinding. Both studies did not blind outcome assessors for pragmatic reasons, therefore we judged this domain as unclear risk of bias.

    Incomplete outcome data

    Both studies reported complete data of all its participants. Oakley 2013a followed participants after discharge from hospital, and postdischarge data were available for 89% of participants. We assessed both studies as at low risk of bias for this domain.

    Selective reporting

    Oakley 2013a was registered with the Australian New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry (ACTRN12605000033640), and outcome data for the primary outcomes were available for all participants in both groups. Kugelman 2013a was registered with ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT01189149), and provided data for the primary outcomes of all participants as listed in the protocol, and most secondary outcomes. Given the availability of all information, we rated the risk of bias as low.

    Other potential sources of bias

    In Oakley 2013a, no other potential sources of bias were identified. In Kugelman 2013a, a large number of participants (n = 62) were excluded from the study for administrative rather than clinical reasons. The exclusion of these participants may have introduced bias into the study, thus we judged this domain as unclear risk of bias.

    Primary outcome

    Length of hospital stay

    Neither trial reported between‐group differences in mean length of hospital stay measured as either actual or theoretical length of stay (Kugelman 2013a; Oakley 2013a), with theoretical length of stay being the time when the child is able to tolerate oral feed and is deemed medically ready for discharge. The combined mean difference (MD) for actual length of hospital stay was 6.8 hours (95% confidence interval (CI) −4.7 to 18.4; P = 0.24, I2 = 16%; 2 studies, 810 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.1), whilst the combined MD for theoretical length of stay was 4.4 hours (95% CI −3.6 to 12.4; P = 0.28, I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 810 children; Analysis 1.1). The analysis suggests that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy probably makes little to no difference to the outcomes compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for both outcomes as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    Secondary outcomes

    1. Duration of time requiring non‐oral fluid therapy (i.e. defined as time to resume full oral feeding)

    Kugelman 2013a reported "time to resume full oral feeding", and Oakley 2013a reported "duration of non‐oral hydration". Neither study provided a precise definition of these terms, but because both referred to similar concepts, we combined the data in a meta‐analysis, which revealed an MD of 2.8 hours (95% CI −3.6 to 9.2; P = 0.40, I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 810 children; Analysis 1.2). The analysis suggests that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy probably makes little to no difference to the outcomes compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    2. Proportion of children with successful insertion of either IV or NG/OG tube on first attempt

    Oakley 2013a assessed the number of successful insertions of IV/NG at first attempt, finding that NG tube feeding probably has a higher success rate of insertion at first attempt compared with the IV fluid group (risk ratio (RR) 1.52, 95% CI 1.36 to 1.69; P < 0.001; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 617 children; Analysis 1.3). We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    3. Number of attempts required of either IV or NG/OG before successful insertion

    Oakley 2013a recorded number of insertion attempts for 323 infants from the NG group and 294 infants from the IV group. In the NG tube feeding group, 275 (85%) infants required one attempt for successful insertion compared to 165 (56%) in the IV group. Of the remaining infants, 30 (62%) of the 48 infants in the NG tube feeding group required two attempts compared to 69 (53%) of the remaining 129 infants in the IV group (RR 1.17, 95% CI 0.89 to 1.53; P = 0.26, I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 177 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4). Eleven (61%) infants in the NG group compared with 41 (68%) infants in the IV group required three attempts for successful insertion (RR 0.89,  95% CI 0.60 to 1.34; P = 0.59; 1 study, 78 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4). Of the remaining infants, 7 in the NG group and 19 in the IV group required between four and eight attempts before successful insertion (RR 1.00, 95% CI 0.83 to 1.21; P = 1.00; 1 study, 26 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.4). The analysis suggests that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy probably makes little to no difference to the outcome compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    4. Proportion of children requiring change in modality of fluid therapy (e.g. NG to IV)

    Oakley 2013a also evaluated change of therapy to the alternative hydration method, finding that it was significantly lower in the NG tube feeding group compared with the IV fluids group (RR 0.52, 95% CI 0.38 to 0.71; P < 0.001; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.5). This suggests that NG tube feeding likely results in a large reduction in change in modality of fluid therapy compared to IV fluid. The reasons provided for changing from NG tube to IV fluids included admission to ICU, IV bolus, IV drugs or blood tests required. The reasons provided for changing from IV fluids to NG tube included "unable to place IV line", IV fluid extravasation, or parental or medical staff requests. We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1). 

    5. Rate of local complications due to each modality of fluid therapy (e.g. NG – epistaxis, IV – extravasation injuries)

    Oakley 2013a described parent‐reported local complication events by conducting a telephone interview of children's parents or guardians five to seven days' postdischarge. Overall there were 30 events of local complications in the NG tube hydration group compared to 47 in the IV fluids group (RR 0.65, 95% CI 0.42 to 1.00; P = 0.05, I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). The analysis suggests that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in a large reduction in the rate of local complications compared to IV fluid therapy, although the upper bound of the CI is 1.00, which includes the possibility of no difference. 

    To further break down the events, there were three events of IV line site bruising in the NG tube hydration group, and 33 events in IV fluids group (RR 0.09, 95% CI 0.03 to 0.30; P < 0.001; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). There were no events of IV line site soreness in the NG tube hydration group, and nine events in the IV fluids group (RR 0.05, 95% CI 0.00 to 0.92; P = 0.04; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). There were no events of IV line site infection in either group.

    There were nine events of sore nose in the NG tube hydration group and one event in the IV fluids group (RR 9.16, 95% CI 1.2 to 72.9; P = 0.04; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). Furthermore, there were four events of epistaxis in the NG tube hydration group and one event in the IV fluids group (RR 4.07, 95% CI 0.46 to 36.24; P = 0.21; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). There were three events of nasal trauma (any) in the NG tube hydration group and no events in the IV fluids group (RR 7.12, 95% CI 0.37 to 137.40; P = 0.19; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). There were 11 events of other local complications reported in both groups, which included unspecified events, vomiting, worsened cough, rash, and crying, which occurred in a similar frequency in both groups (RR 1.02, 95% CI 0.45 to 2.32; P = 0.97; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.6). We graded the certainty of evidence for overall local complication outcomes as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1). We graded the certainty of evidence for specific individual local complication outcomes as low, downgrading for risk of bias (−1) and imprecision (−1). 

    6. Rate of complications due to type of fluid administered (e.g. hyponatraemia)

    This outcome was not reported in the included studies.

    7. Duration of time requiring oxygen supplementation

    Neither trial reported a between‐group difference in duration of supplemental oxygen. The combined data from the studies also did not demonstrate a between‐group difference: MD 2.2 hours (95% CI −5.0 to 9.5; P = 0.54, I2 = 0%; 2 studies, 810 children; Analysis 1.7), suggesting that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference in the outcome compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    8. Proportion of children who required continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP)

    Oakley 2013a reported the number of children requiring CPAP during the study period, which was similar between groups: 12 (3%) children in the NG tube feeding group required CPAP compared to 13 (3%) children in the IV fluid group (RR 0.92, 95% CI 0.42 to 1.98; P = 0.82; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.8). The analysis suggests that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference in the proportion of children who required CPAP compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    9. Proportion of children who required intensive care unit (ICU) admission

    Oakley 2013a reported ICU admission: 21 (6%) children in the NG tube feeding group were admitted to the ICU compared to 25 (7%) children in the IV fluids group (RR 0.83, 95% CI 0.47 to 1.46; P = 0.53; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.9). This suggests that the use of enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference in the proportion of children who required ICU admission compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    10. Proportion of children who had a return visit to a healthcare provider

    Only Oakley 2013a followed participants after discharge from hospital. The proportion of return visits to a healthcare provider was similar between groups (RR 0.99, 95% CI 0.66 to 1.49; P = 0.97; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; moderate certainty evidence; Analysis 1.10), suggesting that the application of enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference for this outcome compared to IV fluid therapy. 

    11. Proportion of children who were readmitted to hospital

    There were eight readmission events in the NG tube feeding group compared to 10 in the IV fluid group (RR 0.81, 95% CI 0.33 to 2.04; P = 0.66; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; Analysis 1.11). Oakley 2013a looked specifically at bronchiolitis‐related readmissions, observing four events in the NG tube feeding group and seven in the IV fluid group (RR 0.58, 95% CI 0.17 to 1.97; P = 0.38; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 678 children; Analysis 1.12). Both of these analyses suggest that enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference for these outcomes compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for these outcomes as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    12. Parental and/or caregiver satisfaction

    In Oakley 2013a, parental satisfaction was measured at the end of hospital stay using a five‐point Likert scale. The measure was a general feedback on the overall care/outcome of the episode. Successful follow‐up was carried out for 678 (89%) infants (342 in the IV fluids group and 336 in the NG tube hydration group), and the median response did not differ between groups: NG tube hydration group (5 (interquartile range (IQR) 4 to 5)) and IV fluids group (5 (IQR 4 to 5)). We graded the certainty of evidence for this outcome as moderate, downgrading for risk of bias (−1).

    13. Rate of adverse effects and events (e.g. desaturation, bradycardia, apnoea, and aspiration events)

    Kugelman 2013a did not report any adverse events. In Oakley 2013a, adverse events were reported for both NG tube hydration and IV fluid therapy. There were six events of bradycardia in the NG tube hydration group and four events of bradycardia in the IV fluids group (RR 1.49, 95% CI 0.42 to 5.23; P = 0.54; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.13). Oakley 2013a reported apnoea events amongst its participants, describing one event with each modality (RR 0.99, 95% CI 0.06 to 15.80; P = 1.00; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.13). Oakley 2013a also reported a single epistaxis event in the IV fluids group, and none in the NG tube hydration group (RR 0.33, 95% CI 0.01 to 8.09; P = 0.50; I2 = not applicable; 1 study, 759 children; Analysis 1.13). No aspiration events were reported in either study. The analysis suggests that enteral tube fluid therapy likely results in little to no difference in the rate of adverse effects and events compared to IV fluid therapy. We graded the certainty of evidence for all adverse event outcomes as low, downgrading for risk of bias (−1) and imprecision (−1).

    Discussion

    Summary of main results

    We aimed to assess the effectiveness and safety of enteral tube and IV fluid therapy for children hospitalised with bronchiolitis. We identified two RCTs that compared enteral tube fluid therapy to IV fluid therapy for hydrating children hospitalised with bronchiolitis (Kugelman 2013a; Oakley 2013a). Oakley 2013a compared these two interventions in infants aged 2 to 12 months, whilst Kugelman 2013a focused on infants aged six months and under. The studies compared the effects of both hydration modalities on length of hospital stay, length of time requiring non‐oral fluid therapy, oxygen supplementation, ICU admission, need for CPAP, successful insertion of IV or NG on first attempt, and need for change in treatment modality. Oakley 2013a, the larger study with 759 participants, evaluated most of the outcomes assessed in the review, whilst Kugelman 2013a included 51 participants and reported fewer outcomes. 

    Overall, we observed that there are likely little to no clinically important differences between the enteral tube and IV fluids feeding groups for the primary and most secondary outcomes. However, the main differences observed were that enteral tube insertion was probably more successful at first attempt compared to IV insertion, and that children in the IV fluid group were more likely to need a change in therapy modality (i.e. switch to NG tube). Local complications, such as soreness and bruising at insertion sites, were also likely more frequent in the IV fluids group than in the enteral tube group. Adverse events were uncommon, with the frequency of events similar between groups. Aspiration events were not reported.

    Overall completeness and applicability of evidence

    Despite being the most common reason for children under the age of two to be admitted to hospital, we identified only two RCTs that studied the effectiveness of enteral tube and IV fluid hydration modalities on hospitalised children with bronchiolitis. Based on the two studies, we did not find evidence to suggest that one modality of hydration is more effective or safer than the other. Our primary outcome was length of hospital stay, which was similar in both treatment groups for both actual and theoretical length of hospital stay. There are also probably little to no differences between hydration modalities when evaluating secondary outcomes such as duration of time requiring oxygen supplementation, proportion of children requiring ICU admission, or readmission to hospital. The main significant differences were in success of insertion on first attempt, local complications at insertion site, and rate of change in treatment modality, which were more likely favourable in the enteral tube group.

    Oakley 2013a reported that children randomised to IV fluids required several additional attempts at IV insertion, and more often had to switch to the NG tube hydration group (56% success for IV insertion versus 88% success for the NG tube insertion at first attempt). It is difficult to disentangle whether the increased rate of changing modality in the IV fluids group was due to technical failure in obtaining an IV or active treatment failure whilst receiving IV fluids. However, these study findings reinforce the results of previous studies where researchers have identified that skilled clinicians and nurses are sometimes unsuccessful with peripheral IV catheterisation at first attempt in children (Benkhadra 2012; Sabri 2013). It is worth noting that most of the outcomes observed in the review were based on only one trial (Oakley 2013a), and that lack of detailed data precluded subgroup analysis. Adverse events were reported in both studies, but were infrequent.

    Whilst both included studies conducted an intention‐to treat analysis, none of the physicians, participants, or outcome assessors were blinded to the study interventions. Although the study authors reported that individual clinicians’ preferences to one intervention over another were unlikely to have an important impact, the unmasked nature of the primary outcome (length of hospital stay) may be at risk of bias, as discharge times vary according to the time of the consultant ward round. However, similarities between the theoretical and actual length of hospital stay suggest that external influences had little effect on the primary outcome. Kugelman 2013a also acknowledged the non‐blinding nature of their pilot study, but we consider the risk of bias unclear as they used objective criteria and standardised management protocols. Furthermore, a large portion of eligible infants (n = 73) were not enrolled due to technical constraints related to heavy workload in the emergency department (n = 62). 

    Quality of the evidence

    We graded the certainty of the evidence for most outcomes as moderate given the unclear risk of bias in several domains, including for random sequence generation and allocation concealment. For a small number of secondary outcomes, we graded the certainty as low due to imprecision and risk of bias. Risk of bias was low for incomplete outcome data and selective reporting. 

    Potential biases in the review process

    We attempted to mitigate biases by conducting a comprehensive, detailed, and sensitive search strategy according to standard Cochrane methodology. Where information was not available in the published trial, we contacted study authors to obtain the additional information. There were no notable differences between protocol and review.

    Agreements and disagreements with other studies or reviews

    Whilst we identified only two RCTs that compared parenteral and enteral modes of fluid therapy in children hospitalised with bronchiolitis, several retrospective studies have evaluated this clinical question. A retrospective cohort study that included children younger than two years of age concluded that children fed enterally (i.e. orally, or via NG tube) whilst receiving heated high flow nasal cannula had a shorter length of hospital stay compared with those receiving IV fluids, regardless of the mode of feeding (Shadman 2019). Another recent retrospective study of hospitalised children with bronchiolitis on non‐invasive ventilation therapy found that children fed enterally had improved vital signs (Sochet 2021). Both studies suggest that enteral nutrition, which can be delivered via NG or OG tube, may play an important role in the care of hospitalised children with bronchiolitis. In our review, the superior effectiveness of either mode of therapy on length of hospital stay is uncertain, suggesting that both modalities may perform similarly, and limited data were available on differences in vital signs. 

    Other authors have evaluated the safety of enteral fluid therapy in young infants. Oakley 2016 conducted a retrospective cohort study of 211 infants younger than two months old admitted with bronchiolitis who required non‐oral hydration, of which 69% received NG tube hydration and 31% received IV fluid hydration (Oakley 2016). The study's primary outcome was the rate of adverse events (desaturation, bradycardia, apnoea, and pulmonary aspiration); secondary outcome measures were length of hospital stay, duration of non‐oral hydration, ICU admission, and duration of ICU admission. Oakley 2016 reported that IV fluid hydration was associated with an increased risk of ICU admission (risk difference (RD) 18.6, 95% CI 5.1 to 32.1; P = 0.004) and use of ventilation support (RD 12.6, 95% CI 0.3 to 23; P = 0.03). No significant differences in adverse events were noted between groups. Oakley 2016 also reported that length of hospital stay did not differ between groups, and change in hydration modality was significantly lower in the NG tube feeding group than in the IV fluids group (RD −35%, 95% CI −49% to −22%; P < 0.001). The findings from Oakley 2016 are similar to the overall findings of our review, which suggest an increased risk of complications in infants and children who receive IV fluids compared with enteral fluid. 

    The findings of this review are consistent with guideline recommendations for bronchiolitis care. The UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) and Norwegian Society of Pediatricians guidelines recommend preferentially giving hospitalised children NG or OG tube hydration, and reserving IV fluids for those who do not tolerate NG/OG tube hydration, or if they have pending respiratory failure (NICE 2015; NPA 2019). However, the Australasian Bronchiolitis Guideline recommends that when non‐oral hydration is necessary, both IV and NG tube hydration are appropriate (O'Brien 2019), whilst the position statement on bronchiolitis of the Canadian Paediatric Society (CPS) similarly supports both modalities of hydration as equally effective (Friedman 2014). The CPS statement also highlights that enteral tube insertion might have a higher success rate than IV placement, citing evidence from Oakley 2013a. Indeed, studies exploring the success of IV cannulation warn that IV insertion requires a skilled professional, and success of insertion at first attempt varies with the service provider (Carr 2016). In light of similar evidence of effectiveness, the greater risk of harms with IV fluids may warrant more frequent use or preferential initiation of enteral tube fluid therapy.
